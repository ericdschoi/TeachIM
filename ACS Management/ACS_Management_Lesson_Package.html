Learning Objectives

  1. Select appropriate early medical interventions for oxygenation, pain, antiplatelet, and anticoagulation in patients presenting with ACS.
  2. Determine timing of revascularization in STEMI vs NSTEMI, with and without high-risk features.
  3. Select appropriate discharge medical therapies for ACS based on ACS type, revascularization method, and comorbidities.

Pre-work send the day before

Three retrieval questions to prime the session. Try them before peeking.

1. A patient with STEMI arrives at a hospital with NO cath lab. What single time threshold decides whether you transfer for PCI vs give fibrinolytics?

If the patient can reach a PCI-capable hospital with a door-to-balloon time <120 min, transfer for primary PCI. If not, give fibrinolytics within 90 min (no contraindications), then transfer for delayed angiography (3–24 h).

2. Name the four "very high-risk" NSTEMI features that mandate immediate angiography (<2 h), regardless of GRACE/TIMI score.

Cardiogenic shock; hemodynamic instability (e.g., acute MR or VSD); refractory angina despite maximal medical therapy; unstable arrhythmia (VT/VF).

3. Which two ACS medications carry a 30-day mortality benefit but are started before discharge rather than acutely?

ACE inhibitor/ARB (all STEMI; NSTEMI with LVEF <40%, HTN, DM, or CKD) and an MRA (LVEF <40%, HF, or DM — once tolerating a beta-blocker + ACEi/ARB).

Teaching Script

Restructured into timed segments mapped to the three objectives. The original interactive board drives the visuals; this script supplies hooks, ASK pauses, and pearls.

Hook 2 min

"It's 2 a.m. A 58-year-old grabs his chest in triage. In the next 90 minutes you'll make four or five decisions that decide how much heart muscle he keeps. Let's build the timeline of ACS care — the same spine for STEMI, NSTEMI, and unstable angina — so you never freeze at the bedside." Project the timeline slide: O → A → H/E → Pain → DC, with 0 / 90 min / 2 h / 24 h / 48 h / 72 h tick marks.

Segment 2 — Timing of revascularization 9 min Objective 2

STEMI = time is myocardium. Click "risk stratification" only after the STEMI branch.

ASK — "STEMI at a PCI-capable hospital: what's the target, and when does the target change?"
  • PCI-capable hospital: primary PCI within 90 min (door-to-balloon), if symptom onset <12 h.
  • Non-PCI hospital: transfer for PCI if achievable in <120 min; otherwise fibrinolytics <90 min then transfer for delayed angiography (3–24 h). PCI beats lytics — lytics fail to reperfuse ~35% and carry higher death/MI/stroke/bleed.
  • Onset >12 h: still PCI unless totally occluded infarct with no ongoing ischemia.
ASK — "NSTEMI: you can't activate the lab for everyone. How do you decide who goes now vs who can wait?"
Risk tierTriggerStrategy
Very highCardiogenic shock, hemodynamic instability, refractory angina, VT/VFImmediate PCI <2 h
HighGRACE >140, TIMI 6–7, new ST depression, rising troponin, age >75Early invasive <24 h
Mod–lowGRACE 109–140, TIMI 3–5Delayed invasive 25–72 h (no mortality difference vs <24 h)
Very lowGRACE <109, TIMI 0–1Ischemia-guided — medical mgmt; angiography only if refractory angina, objective ischemia, or high prognostic risk
Pearl. GRACE estimates death within 6 months / in-hospital; TIMI estimates 14-day composite (death, MI, urgent revascularization). Use either to anchor the conversation — they sort, they don't dictate.

Retrieval close 2 min

ASK — Erase the board. "Rebuild the ACS timeline from memory: what's given to everyone, what decides revascularization timing for STEMI vs NSTEMI, and what gets added before discharge?" Have the team reconstruct the three take-home points aloud.

Cases click to reveal

Case 1 — 61 y/o, HTN/DM/HLD, chest pain. T 36.4, HR 114, BP 105/78, SpO₂ 94%. ECG: ST elevations I, aVL, V1–V4.

When should this patient undergo revascularization?

Emergently. Anterior LAD STEMI — activate the cath lab, primary PCI within 90 min at a PCI-capable hospital.

What if they were at a non-PCI-capable hospital?

Transfer if estimated door-to-balloon <120 min. Otherwise fibrinolytics <90 min → transfer for delayed PCI (<24 h). Weigh individual bleeding risk.

What therapy would you initiate while awaiting PCI?

Aspirin 325 mg chewed; heparin drip (PCI planned); hold P2Y12 in case CABG is needed; PO beta-blocker for angina; PRN nitrates/morphine.

After DES to LAD (HR 80, BP 135/90, EF 50%, Cr 0.9, K 4.0), what's on the discharge list?

Aspirin + ticagrelor (superior P2Y12 in STEMI); high-intensity statin (e.g., rosuvastatin 20); beta-blocker; ACE/ARB (all STEMI). No MRA — EF >40%.

Case 2 — 45 y/o, DM2, crushing substernal pain. HR 110, BP 82/45, SpO₂ 82% RA. ECG: ST depressions V2–V4.

How would you manage pain?

Nitrates, morphine, and beta-blockers are contraindicated with hypotension. Consider GP IIb/IIIa (eptifibatide) under interventionalist guidance.

When should this patient be revascularized?

Immediate/urgent (<2 h). This is an NSTEMI but with very high-risk features (cardiogenic shock, refractory angina since anti-anginals are contraindicated). Other immediate-PCI triggers: ventricular arrhythmia, persistent angina.

What would you initiate while awaiting PCI?

Oxygen (hypoxic on RA); aspirin 325 mg chewed; heparin drip; hold P2Y12 pending possible CABG; high-intensity statin.

After DES to proximal LAD (HR 80, BP 135/90, EF 28%, Cr 0.9, K 4.0), what's at discharge?

DAPT (e.g., aspirin + clopidogrel/ticagrelor); beta-blocker when euvolemic and compensated; ACE/ARB (DM + EF <40%); MRA (DM + EF <40%).

Case 3 — 41 y/o, anxiety, one episode of chest pain. HR 85, BP 145/85, SpO₂ 94% RA. ECG: precordial T-wave inversions. hs-troponin I 25 → 110 (H) → 15 ng/L.

What is your diagnosis?

NSTEMI — elevated/rising troponin + chest pain + ECG change. (Original case listed troponin in μg/dL; corrected to ng/L.)

How would you estimate mortality risk?

Very low risk. GRACE 53 → ~0.8% 6-month death; TIMI 1 → ~5% 14-day all-cause mortality.

What revascularization strategy?

Very low risk → an ischemia-guided strategy (stress test + medical management) is reasonable, especially with patient preference.

While awaiting stress testing, what therapy?

Aspirin; enoxaparin (superior to heparin in medical management); high-intensity statin; beta-blocker; nitro PRN. P2Y12 can be held until coronary anatomy is known.

Board-Style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 60-year-old man with chest pain has an ECG showing 2-mm ST elevation in II, III, aVF. He arrives at a hospital without a cath lab. The nearest PCI center is a confirmed 150-minute door-to-balloon transfer. SBP 130, no bleeding history. Best next step?

  1. Transfer immediately for primary PCI
  2. Administer fibrinolytics now, then transfer for angiography in 3–24 h
  3. Start heparin and observe for spontaneous reperfusion
  4. Give morphine and arrange outpatient stress test
Answer & explanation

B. Inferior STEMI; transfer would exceed the 120-min threshold, so fibrinolytics within 90 min (no contraindications) followed by transfer for delayed angiography is correct.

A — a 150-min transfer misses the <120-min window, so lytics first. C — "wait and see" abandons reperfusion in an evolving STEMI. D — outpatient management of an active STEMI is dangerous and morphine masks ischemia.

2. A 72-year-old woman with NSTEMI has GRACE 165, new ST depression, and a rising troponin. She is pain-free and hemodynamically stable on medical therapy. Optimal timing of angiography?

  1. Immediate (<2 h)
  2. Early invasive (<24 h)
  3. Delayed invasive (25–72 h)
  4. Ischemia-guided (no routine angiography)
Answer & explanation

B. High-risk NSTEMI features (GRACE >140, new ST depression, dynamic troponin) → early invasive within 24 h.

A — reserved for very-high-risk features (shock, instability, refractory angina, VT/VF), none present. C — for mod-low risk (GRACE 109–140). D — for very low risk (GRACE <109, TIMI 0–1).

3. A 55-year-old man is admitted with NSTEMI, BP 84/52, cool extremities, lactate 4.2. Echo shows a new large anterior wall motion abnormality. Most appropriate revascularization timing?

  1. Delayed invasive at 48 h after diuresis
  2. Ischemia-guided with stress testing
  3. Immediate angiography (<2 h)
  4. Fibrinolytics
Answer & explanation

C. Cardiogenic shock is a very-high-risk feature mandating immediate angiography regardless of risk score.

A/B — delaying revascularization in shock increases mortality. D — fibrinolytics are for STEMI when timely PCI is unavailable, not for NSTEMI, and are contraindicated in shock.

4. A 64-year-old undergoes successful DES to the RCA for STEMI. Discharge EF is 35%; he has type 2 diabetes, Cr 1.1, K 4.2, and tolerates a beta-blocker and lisinopril. Which agent provides additional 30-day mortality benefit and should be added before discharge?

  1. Amlodipine
  2. Spironolactone
  3. Digoxin
  4. Furosemide
Answer & explanation

B. An MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone) is indicated post-MI with LVEF <40%, HF, or diabetes once the patient tolerates a beta-blocker + ACEi/ARB, and confers a 30-day mortality benefit (EPHESUS).

A — amlodipine has no mortality benefit in ACS. C — digoxin is for rate/symptom control, not mortality. D — diuretics treat congestion, not mortality.

5. Per the 2025 ACC/AHA ACS guideline, which lipid strategy is recommended for a patient whose LDL-C is 92 mg/dL despite maximally tolerated high-intensity statin after MI?

  1. No change; statin alone is sufficient
  2. Switch to a moderate-intensity statin to reduce side effects
  3. Add a non-statin agent (e.g., ezetimibe or a PCSK9 inhibitor)
  4. Stop the statin and start niacin
Answer & explanation

C. The 2025 guideline recommends adding a non-statin (ezetimibe, evolocumab, alirocumab, inclisiran, or bempedoic acid) when LDL-C ≥70 mg/dL on maximally tolerated statin.

A — LDL ≥70 on max statin warrants escalation. B — de-escalating intensity post-ACS is wrong. D — niacin is not a recommended add-on; it lacks outcome benefit and the statin should continue.

2025 ACC/AHA ACS Guideline — Update Feb 2025

The original talk (Oct 2023) predates the 2025 ACC/AHA/ACEP/NAEMSP/SCAI ACS guideline, which consolidated STEMI and NSTE-ACS into a single document. The standing teaching above remains correct; these are the practice-shifting additions, also captured on the appended deck slide.

Antiplatelet. Ticagrelor or prasugrel is preferred over clopidogrel for STEMI/NSTE-ACS undergoing PCI (Class 1). Default DAPT is ≥12 months if not high bleeding risk. Bleeding-reduction options: PPI if GI-bleed risk; transition to ticagrelor monotherapy ≥1 month post-PCI; if long-term anticoagulation is needed, stop aspirin 1–4 weeks post-PCI and continue a P2Y12 inhibitor (preferably clopidogrel).
Lipids. High-intensity statin for all ACS, with the option of concurrent ezetimibe. Add a non-statin (ezetimibe, evolocumab, alirocumab, inclisiran, bempedoic acid) if LDL-C ≥70 mg/dL on maximally tolerated statin; further intensification is reasonable if LDL-C 55–<70.
Procedure & revascularization. Radial access preferred over femoral (↓ bleeding, vascular complications, mortality). Intravascular imaging for complex lesions (Class 1). Complete revascularization recommended in STEMI and NSTE-ACS; in cardiogenic shock, revascularize the culprit only (no routine non-IRA PCI at the same sitting). Microaxial flow pump reasonable in selected AMI-cardiogenic shock.
Supportive. RBC transfusion to a hemoglobin of 10 g/dL may be reasonable in ACS with anemia (not actively bleeding). Check a lipid panel 4–8 weeks after starting/adjusting therapy; refer to cardiac rehabilitation (home-based option acceptable).

Source: Rao SV, et al. 2025 ACC/AHA/ACEP/NAEMSP/SCAI Guideline for the Management of Patients With Acute Coronary Syndromes. Circulation 2025;151. doi:10.1161/CIR.0000000000001309.

Take-Home Points

  1. STEMI = emergent reperfusion. PCI <90 min if PCI-capable; transfer if door-to-balloon <120 min is achievable; otherwise fibrinolytics <90 min then transfer for delayed angiography.
  2. NSTEMI = risk-stratify, then time the cath. Very high → immediate (<2 h); high → early invasive (<24 h); mod-low → delayed (25–72 h); very low → ischemia-guided strategy is acceptable.
  3. Layer the meds. Early (all ACS): aspirin, anticoagulation, nitrates, beta-blocker — never delaying emergent revascularization. Within 24 h: high-intensity statin + P2Y12. Before discharge: ACEi/ARB and MRA where indicated.
