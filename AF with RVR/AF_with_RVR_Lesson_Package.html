Learning Objectives

  1. Recognize atrial fibrillation with a rapid ventricular rate on the ECG.
  2. Evaluate a patient with new AF for cardiac and non-cardiac triggers.
  3. Build a framework for rate control in AF with RVR — anchored on hemodynamic stability.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What three ECG features define AF with RVR? Irregularly irregular rhythm, narrow QRS (<120 ms), no discernible P waves — with a ventricular rate >100 bpm. (It can be wide-complex if there's an underlying bundle branch block or aberrancy.)
Q2. What makes a patient with AF with RVR "unstable," and what do you do about it? Sustained hypotension (SBP <90), or a SBP drop >30 mmHg with chest pain, altered mentation, or dyspnea — i.e., hemodynamic compromise attributable to the rhythm. Unstable → immediate synchronized cardioversion (120–200 J); sedation/analgesia if feasible but never let it delay shocking.
Q3. Name the four rate-control drug classes and which two are first-line. Beta-blockers (metoprolol) and non-dihydropyridine calcium-channel blockers (diltiazem) are first-line; digoxin and amiodarone are second-line for specific scenarios (BP-limited, heart failure, shock). Avoid non-DHP CCBs in HFrEF.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected presenter board (AF_RVR_Optimized.pptx) and click through, or rebuild the schematic on a whiteboard. The learner board (Afib-Learner-.pptx) has a more detailed meds table for self-study. Every cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Talk without cases: ~15–20 min. Cases: +10–15 min. Print the learner handout for the case pair-shares.

Hook (2 min)

Open with the board's case: "68-year-old with HTN, diabetes, and COPD, dyspneic and palpitating; here's his ECG." Have a learner read it cold. The whole talk hangs off two decisions you'll make in the first 30 seconds: is this AF, and is he stable?

Objective 1 — Recognize & the pathophysiology 4 min

ASK: "Walk me through this rhythm strip" — then click DIAGNOSIS. Narrow QRS (<120 ms), irregularly irregular, no P waves, rate >100.

Click the Pathophysiology tab: multiple atrial micro-reentry circuits fire at ~400 bpm; the AV node's refractory period is the gatekeeper that determines how many impulses reach the ventricle — which is exactly why AV-nodal blockers (BB, CCB, digoxin) are the levers for rate control.

Pearl: AF with RVR is usually narrow-complex, but it can be wide-complex with an underlying bundle branch block or rate-related aberrancy — don't let a wide QRS talk you out of the diagnosis when the rhythm is irregularly irregular.

Objective 2 — Evaluate the trigger 5 min

ASK: "New AF — what set it off? Sort your work-up into cardiac and non-cardiac." Click each category; emphasize testing in context, not a shotgun panel.
CardiacNon-cardiac
Valvular or structural heart disease → TTE; decompensated HF → ±BNP; new ischemia → ECG ± troponinPulmonary disease (incl. PE) → TTE, ±CXR, ±OSA screen; drugs/toxins → ±urine tox, EtOH level; metabolic → BMP+Mg, CBC; endocrine → TSH with reflex T4

The universal new-AF work-up the take-home emphasizes: TTE, a chemistry panel (BMP+Mg), and TSH — plus CBC and ECG/troponin as indicated.

Objective 3 — Management: stability first 7 min

ASK: "Before any drug — what one question decides everything?" Click "1": is the patient UNSTABLE?

Unstable = sustained hypotension (SBP <90) or a SBP drop >30 mmHg with chest pain, altered mentation, or dyspnea. Unstable → synchronized DCCV 120–200 J; give sedation/analgesia if you can but don't delay; start anticoagulation.

ASK: "Stable patient — what's the rate target, and IV or oral?" Click "2".

Goal HR <110. Acute: if HR >130 give IV agents; chronic/sub-acute: HR 110–130 can be brought down with oral agents.

ASK: "Four agents — where do they act and what's the catch for each?" Click "3", then each drug.
AgentLine / nicheBP effect & caution
Metoprolol (BB)First-line↓↓BP; caution in decompensated HFrEF, sepsis. IV 5 mg q5min ×3; PO 12.5–37.5 mg q6h
Diltiazem (non-DHP CCB)First-line↓↓BP; avoid in HFrEF, caution in sepsis. IV 10–20 mg bolus + drip; PO 30–90 mg q6h
DigoxinSecond-lineBP-neutral (↔) with positive inotropy — useful when BP won't tolerate a BB/CCB; caution in elderly and renal failure. IV 0.25–0.5 mg load then 0.25 mg q6h
AmiodaroneSecond-lineMild ↓BP; favored in shock; caution as a rate-control agent in chronic AF (it can chemically cardiovert). IV 150 mg bolus then drip; lung/liver/thyroid toxicity long-term

If one agent at escalating doses fails, add a second (e.g., a CCB on top of a BB). Start anticoagulation (heparin/enoxaparin) for anyone heading to cardioversion or with high thromboembolic risk by CHA2DS2-VASc.

2023 currency note: The acute approach above is unchanged in the 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS AF guideline — BB/CCB first-line, lenient resting HR <110 acceptable when asymptomatic with preserved LVEF (RACE II), DCCV for instability. Around cardioversion: if AF has lasted ≥48 h or for an unknown duration, give ≥3 weeks of therapeutic anticoagulation first OR do a TEE to exclude thrombus, then anticoagulate ≥4 weeks afterward regardless of score. Long-term stroke prophylaxis is still guided by CHA2DS2-VASc (2023 ACC); the 2024 ESC guideline shifted to CHA2DS2-VA (dropping the sex category).

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Have the group rebuild: the three ECG features, the universal new-AF work-up, the stability criteria + what to do when unstable, the rate target, and which agent they'd reach for in HFrEF, in shock, and when the BP is borderline.

Practice Cases 10–15 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Stable RVR

68-year-old man with HTN and DM, new AF with RVR, HR 160s, BP 120s/60s, dyspnea and palpitations.

How would you manage him?Stable AF with RVR. With HR >130, give IV metoprolol 5 mg or IV diltiazem 10 mg. Work-up: CBC, BMP/Mg, TSH, ECG, troponin, CXR.
He gets IV metoprolol 5 mg; HR improves to 140s, BP 110/70s. Next?HR still >130s with stable BP — give another IV metoprolol 5 mg.
HR now <130. Next step?Transition to PO metoprolol 12.5–25 mg q6h. His CHA2DS2-VASc is 3 — consider/start anticoagulation.

Case 2 — RVR in septic shock

54-year-old woman with HTN and alcohol use disorder, admitted with septic shock on norepinephrine 0.1, MAPs in the 70s. New AF, HR 150s.

How would you manage her?Despite the pressors, she is in stable AF with RVR (the rhythm isn't the cause of her shock). Given septic shock, amiodarone is preferred over metoprolol or diltiazem because it is more hemodynamically neutral. Treat the sepsis in parallel.

Case 3 — RVR with HFrEF

72-year-old man with DM, HTN, and HFrEF (EF 25%) with AF with RVR, HR 160s, BP 110s/60s, no orthopnea or PND.

How would you manage him?Stable AF with RVR. Avoid CCBs in HFrEF. A beta-blocker can be used in compensated HFrEF — give IV metoprolol 5 mg.
He gets metoprolol 5 mg ×3; HR 140s but BP drifts to 90/60s; palpitations only, no CP/dyspnea/AMS, normal BMP, adequate UOP. Next agent?He's stable but BP-limited. Digoxin is the BP-neutral agent with positive inotropy — the right next choice. Give an IV load of 0.25–0.5 mg.

Case 4 — The one who decompensates

68-year-old on an esmolol drip in the ICU; called for HR 160s, BP drops to 85/palp, and the patient is unresponsive.

How would you manage him?This patient is now UNSTABLE. Place pads and prepare for synchronized DCCV at 120–200 J. All cardioverted patients should receive anticoagulation.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 70-year-old man presents with palpitations. ECG shows an irregularly irregular narrow-complex tachycardia at 150 bpm with no P waves. BP is 132/78 and he is mentating normally with no chest pain. What is the most appropriate initial management?

  1. Immediate synchronized cardioversion
  2. IV diltiazem or metoprolol for rate control
  3. IV adenosine
  4. Amiodarone load for chemical cardioversion
Answer & explanationB. This is AF with RVR and he is hemodynamically stable, so the priority is rate control with a first-line AV-nodal blocker (IV diltiazem or metoprolol). Cardioversion (A) is for unstable patients. Adenosine (C) transiently blocks the AV node and can unmask atrial activity but does not treat AF and won't sustain rate control. Up-front chemical cardioversion (D) raises stroke risk if AF has lasted ≥48 h without anticoagulation and isn't the first move in a stable patient.

2. A 64-year-old woman with HFrEF (EF 30%) develops AF with RVR at 150 bpm, BP 108/70, no chest pain or hypoxia. Which rate-control agent should be avoided?

  1. Metoprolol
  2. Diltiazem
  3. Digoxin
  4. Amiodarone
Answer & explanationB. Non-dihydropyridine calcium-channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil) are negative inotropes and are contraindicated in HFrEF — they can precipitate decompensation. A beta-blocker (A) is appropriate once the patient is compensated; digoxin (C) and amiodarone (D) are reasonable, BP-friendly options in heart failure.

3. A 58-year-old man in septic shock on norepinephrine develops AF with RVR at 155 bpm; MAP is 68. Which agent best balances rate control and hemodynamics?

  1. IV diltiazem drip
  2. IV metoprolol boluses
  3. IV amiodarone
  4. Synchronized cardioversion
Answer & explanationC. In the setting of shock, amiodarone is the most hemodynamically neutral rate-control choice; diltiazem (A) and metoprolol (B) can drop an already marginal blood pressure. Cardioversion (D) is unlikely to hold while the underlying driver (sepsis, catecholamines) persists and is reserved for instability attributable to the rhythm — here the shock is septic, not rate-driven. Treat the sepsis concurrently.

4. A 75-year-old man with AF at 150 bpm becomes acutely hypotensive (SBP 80) with chest pain and confusion. What is the immediate next step?

  1. IV metoprolol 5 mg
  2. IV fluids and reassess in 30 minutes
  3. Synchronized direct-current cardioversion
  4. Oral diltiazem
Answer & explanationC. He meets criteria for instability (hypotension plus ischemia and altered mentation attributable to the tachyarrhythmia), so synchronized cardioversion is indicated immediately; sedation should not delay it. AV-nodal blockers (A, D) would further lower his blood pressure, and waiting (B) is unsafe in an unstable patient. Anticoagulate after cardioversion.

5. A patient with new AF of unknown duration is hemodynamically stable and you plan elective cardioversion. Regarding anticoagulation, which is correct?

  1. No anticoagulation is needed if CHA2DS2-VASc is 0
  2. Anticoagulate for ≥3 weeks beforehand or perform a TEE, then ≥4 weeks afterward
  3. Anticoagulate only if the post-procedure rhythm is AF
  4. Heparin during the procedure is sufficient with no further therapy
Answer & explanationB. When AF has lasted ≥48 hours or for an unknown duration, the embolic risk around cardioversion requires either ≥3 weeks of therapeutic anticoagulation beforehand or a transesophageal echo to exclude left-atrial thrombus, followed by ≥4 weeks of anticoagulation afterward regardless of the CHA2DS2-VASc score (A) — because atrial mechanical stunning persists after the rhythm normalizes (C). Peri-procedural heparin alone (D) is inadequate.

Take-Home Points

  1. AF with RVR is an irregularly irregular, narrow-complex (<120 ms) tachycardia with no P waves and a rate >100 — wide-complex only with BBB/aberrancy.
  2. Work up new AF for cardiac and non-cardiac triggers; the universal tests are TTE, a chemistry panel (BMP+Mg), and TSH.
  3. Decide stability first: unstable (hypotension or end-organ symptoms from the rhythm) → synchronized DCCV 120–200 J without delay.
  4. If stable, rate-control to HR <110 — IV agents if HR >130; beta-blockers and CCBs are first-line, digoxin/amiodarone second-line, and avoid CCBs in HFrEF.
  5. Anticoagulate everyone who is cardioverted (≥4 weeks after) and guide long-term therapy with CHA2DS2-VASc.
