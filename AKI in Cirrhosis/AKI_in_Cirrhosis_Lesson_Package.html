Learning Objectives

  1. Differentiate hepatorenal syndrome (HRS) from other causes of AKI in cirrhosis.
  2. Describe the systemic pathophysiology of compensated and decompensated cirrhosis in terms of kidney perfusion.
  3. Identify the initial treatment of HRS.
  4. Apply the framework through a series of short cases.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Q1. In a cirrhotic with new AKI, what is the single most common category — pre-renal, intrarenal, or post-renal — and what two things must you always rule out? Pre-renal (60–70% of AKI in cirrhosis). Always evaluate for infection — including a diagnostic paracentesis for SBP — and rule out GI bleeding. Post-renal/obstruction is <1%.
Q2. What defines hepatorenal syndrome physiologically, and how do you confirm it at the bedside? HRS is a pre-renal AKI that does NOT respond to volume. Confirm by stopping diuretics and giving an albumin challenge (1 g/kg/day, max 100 g/day, ×48 h): no improvement in creatinine, with no shock, no nephrotoxins, and no structural kidney disease, supports HRS.
Q3. What is the only definitive treatment for HRS? Liver transplant. Medical therapy (albumin + a vasoconstrictor) and HD/TIPS are bridges or temporizing measures.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the corrected interactive board (AKI_in_Cirrhosis_Optimized.pptx) and click through; every cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt. Navigate objectives with the key in the top-right. Print the learner case handout. ~30–45 min total.

Objective 1 — The differential 8 min

ASK: "Cirrhotic patient, creatinine just doubled. Before labs — what are your buckets?" Build pre-renal / intrarenal / post-renal, then click to reveal prevalence.
CategoryPrevalenceCauses
Pre-renal60–70%Volume responsive: GI bleed, over-diuresis, GI losses. Not volume responsive: HRS, cardiorenal. Plus sepsis (SBP, pneumonia, UTI), medications.
Intrarenal30–40%ATN, AIN, glomerulonephritis
Post-renal<1%Obstruction (intrinsic/extrinsic), neurogenic bladder
Pearl: AKI increases mortality 2–6 fold in cirrhosis. The mandatory early workup is a diagnostic paracentesis (SBP can present without abdominal pain) and ruling out GI bleed. FeUrea (not FeNa) when on diuretics — FeUrea <35% suggests pre-renal. Granular casts and low urine Na are unreliable for ATN in advanced cirrhosis (persistent RAAS activation reclaims urinary sodium).

Objective 2 — Pathophysiology 8 min

Click each number on the board to build the cascade. The core idea: cirrhosis is a vasodilatory state.

Compensated: ↓liver metabolism of vasodilators → ↓vascular tone, especially splanchnic → splanchnic pooling → ↓effective arterial volume (EAV) → heart raises CO to preserve perfusion.

Decompensated: the same vasodilation worsens; CO can no longer compensate → ↓BP → RAAS, ADH, and sympathetic activation → renal vasoconstriction and sodium/water retention → hyponatremia and pre-renal AKI. HRS is the kidney being squeezed by systemic vasodilation, not by intrinsic renal disease.

Objective 3 — Diagnosis & treatment of HRS 10 min

ASK: "Pre-renal AKI that won't budge with volume — how do you prove it's HRS?" Stop diuretics + 48-h albumin challenge (1 g/kg/day); no Cr improvement, no shock, no nephrotoxins, no structural disease.
Updated terminology (use this when you teach): The board labels HRS "Type 1 / Type 2." Since 2015 (ICA) and the 2021 AASLD guidance, HRS-1 → HRS-AKI (diagnosed by ICA-AKI criteria: ↑Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 h or ≥50% within 7 days — the old fixed "Cr >2.5" threshold was dropped) and HRS-2 → HRS-NAKI (non-AKI; includes HRS-CKD, often with refractory ascites). The board's schematic is still excellent for teaching the physiology — just name it with current terms.

Treatment targets the physiology — refill the arterial volume and restore tone:

StepDetail
AlbuminRaises effective arterial volume (up to ~50 g/day for ongoing expansion)
VasoconstrictorRestores vascular tone. US, 2026: terlipressin (FDA-approved 2022) or norepinephrine (ICU); the midodrine + octreotide + albumin regimen is the older, less effective outpatient/ward option (midodrine 5–7.5 mg TID, goal ↑MAP >15)
TIPSCan give delayed renal improvement; most HRS patients are too ill to qualify
HemodialysisBridge to transplant in selected patients
Liver transplantThe ONLY definitive therapy; some need combined liver-kidney transplant
2026 update — terlipressin: The original lesson says terlipressin is "available in other countries but not in the US." As of September 2022 (CONFIRM trial), the FDA approved terlipressin for HRS-AKI — the first drug ever approved for HRS in the US. It improves HRS reversal vs placebo. Avoid in ACLF grade 3, pre-existing hypoxia, or SCr >5 mg/dL (respiratory-failure safety signal; benefit unlikely at very high Cr).

Practice Cases from the original handout — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Fever + confusion in HCV cirrhosis

55 F, HCV cirrhosis, fever and increased confusion, more ascites, ↓UOP, on lactulose. Afebrile, HR 92, BP 92/60. Diffusely edematous, distended nontender abdomen. Na 127, BUN 30, Cr 2.4 (baseline 1.2); WBC 12k, Hct 30, Plt 90k; Tbili 4, alb 2; UA RBCs + hyaline casts; urine Na <10.

Workup?Low urine Na = pre-renal pattern; differential includes GI bleed, volume loss, sepsis, meds, HRS. She is decompensating (↑Cr, ↑Tbili, worse encephalopathy) — get a diagnostic paracentesis for SBP (can occur without pain): 500 cells, 90% PMNs. Start an albumin challenge while awaiting results.
Cause of AKI?SBP — >250 PMNs/µL on ascitic fluid. Start empiric treatment.
Management?IV cefotaxime or ceftriaxone ×5 days (IV fluoroquinolone if penicillin-allergic). Albumin 1.5 g/kg day 1 + 1 g/kg day 3, especially with Tbili >4, Cr >1, or BUN >30 — reduces renal dysfunction and mortality.

Case 2 — Listed for transplant, rising Cr

48 M, HCV/EtOH cirrhosis, actively listed, worsening Cr/Tbili/INR despite diuretics. HR 60, BP 92/45. Tight distended abdomen. Na 126, BUN 40, Cr 2.4 (baseline 1.0); Tbili 11, INR 5; UA 1+ protein, hyaline + occasional granular casts; urine Na >65, FeUrea 30%.

Workup?Use FeUrea (not FeNa) on diuretics — FeUrea <35% suggests pre-renal. Granular casts and low urine Na are unreliable for ATN in advanced cirrhosis. Paracentesis: 450 cells, 10% PMNs (no SBP). RUQ US with Doppler (PVT — negative). Bladder pressure 12 mmHg (normal). Albumin challenge: no Cr improvement after 1 g/kg ×48 h.
Cause of AKI?HRS — no response to albumin challenge. By the older labels this is "type II" (Cr <2.5, refractory ascites); in current terms, HRS-NAKI. Modern criteria diagnose HRS by AKI/temporal pattern, not the fixed Cr threshold.
Management?Stop diuretics (manage volume with periodic paracenteses); albumin up to 50 g/day; a vasoconstrictor to raise MAP >15 (in 2026: terlipressin or norepinephrine; the handout's midodrine 5–7.5 mg TID + octreotide 100 µg SQ TID is the older regimen). Too advanced for TIPS. Definitive: liver transplant (already listed); consult nephrology; some need combined liver-kidney transplant.

Case 3 — PSC flare, heavy NSAID use

45 M, PSC cirrhosis, week of worsening abdominal pain self-treated with Tylenol and ibuprofen, ↓UOP, dark urine. HR 90, BP 120/80. Mild RUQ tenderness, no Murphy's, no CVA tenderness. Na 132, K 5.8, Cr 3.6 (baseline 0.9); UA hyaline + WBC casts, no bacteria; urine Na 28, urine Cr 60.

Likely cause?Intrinsic/intrarenal AKI. FeNa ~1.5%; WBC casts + heavy NSAID use → high suspicion for AIN (acute interstitial nephritis). (Note FeNa is interpretable here as he is not on diuretics.)
Management?Stop the offending drug (NSAIDs). A cautious volume challenge is reasonable without volume overload — NSAIDs also cause pre-renal AKI and he may have had poor intake during the flare.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 58-year-old man with alcohol-associated cirrhosis and ascites has a creatinine rise from 0.9 to 2.3 mg/dL over 5 days. Diuretics are stopped and albumin 1 g/kg/day is given for 48 hours with no improvement. BP 96/58, no shock; UA bland, no proteinuria; renal ultrasound normal; no recent contrast or nephrotoxins. Diagnostic paracentesis shows 120 PMNs/µL. Which is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Diagnose HRS-AKI and start a vasoconstrictor plus albumin
  2. Start empiric cefotaxime for SBP
  3. Diagnose ATN and provide supportive care
  4. Proceed directly to hemodialysis
Answer & explanationA. He meets HRS-AKI criteria: AKI in cirrhosis with ascites, no response to diuretic withdrawal + 48-h albumin, no shock, no nephrotoxins, no structural disease, and <250 PMNs so no SBP (B is wrong). Bland sediment argues against ATN (C). HD (D) is a bridge, not the first step. Begin albumin + a vasoconstrictor (terlipressin or norepinephrine).

2. Which statement about terlipressin for HRS-AKI is correct in the United States in 2026?

  1. It is not approved in the US; use midodrine and octreotide instead
  2. It is FDA-approved (2022) but should be avoided in ACLF grade 3, hypoxia, or SCr >5 mg/dL
  3. It is FDA-approved and is the definitive cure for HRS
  4. It is approved only for type 2 HRS
Answer & explanationB. The FDA approved terlipressin for HRS-AKI in September 2022 (so A is outdated — exactly the original lesson's error). No drug cures HRS; only liver transplant is definitive (C wrong). It is indicated for HRS-AKI, not the old "type 2"/HRS-NAKI (D). Key safety limits: avoid in ACLF grade 3, pre-existing hypoxia, or SCr >5 mg/dL because of a respiratory-failure signal and low likelihood of benefit.

3. A 60-year-old woman with cirrhosis is admitted with SBP (ascitic PMNs 480/µL), Tbili 5, Cr 1.4. In addition to cefotaxime, which intervention most reduces her risk of HRS and death?

  1. Furosemide infusion
  2. IV albumin 1.5 g/kg on day 1 and 1 g/kg on day 3
  3. Prophylactic TIPS
  4. Fresh frozen plasma
Answer & explanationB. In SBP, albumin (1.5 g/kg day 1 + 1 g/kg day 3) reduces HRS and mortality, with greatest benefit at Tbili >4, Cr >1, or BUN >30 (Sort et al., NEJM 1999) — she qualifies. Diuretics (A) worsen the pre-renal state. TIPS (C) and FFP (D) have no role here.

4. A cirrhotic on furosemide and spironolactone has AKI. Urine Na is 70 mEq/L. Why is FeNa misleading here, and what is more useful?

  1. FeNa is always accurate in cirrhosis; no substitute is needed
  2. Diuretics raise urinary sodium, falsely elevating FeNa; use FeUrea (<35% suggests pre-renal)
  3. FeNa is low because of contrast nephropathy
  4. Urine Na is irrelevant in AKI
Answer & explanationB. Loop and thiazide-type diuretics force renal sodium excretion, inflating urine Na and FeNa regardless of volume status. FeUrea is not affected the same way; FeUrea <35% supports a pre-renal cause. Conversely, in advanced cirrhosis a low urine Na/FeNa can occur even in ATN because persistent RAAS activation reclaims urinary sodium — interpret these indices cautiously.

5. A patient with cirrhosis develops AKI after a week of ibuprofen for pain. UA shows WBC casts and no bacteria; FeNa 1.5% (not on diuretics). The most likely diagnosis and best initial step are:

  1. HRS-AKI; start terlipressin
  2. Pre-renal azotemia; give large-volume normal saline boluses
  3. Acute interstitial nephritis from NSAIDs; stop the NSAID
  4. Post-renal obstruction; place a Foley and consult urology
Answer & explanationC. WBC casts plus a clear NSAID exposure point to AIN; the elevated FeNa (interpretable off diuretics) argues against a purely pre-renal HRS picture (A, B). The first and most important step is removing the offending drug. A cautious volume challenge is reasonable since NSAIDs also cause pre-renal injury, but aggressive saline (B) is not the answer and there's no obstruction (D).

Take-Home Points

  1. AKI in cirrhosis is usually pre-renal and almost never obstructive — always tap the belly (SBP) and rule out GI bleed.
  2. HRS is a pre-renal AKI that does not respond to volume; confirm with diuretic withdrawal + a 48-hour albumin challenge, after excluding shock, nephrotoxins, and structural disease.
  3. Treat with albumin + a vasoconstrictor — in the US (2026) terlipressin (FDA-approved 2022) or norepinephrine; midodrine/octreotide is the older regimen. The only definitive therapy is liver transplant.
  4. In SBP, albumin (1.5 g/kg day 1 + 1 g/kg day 3) reduces HRS and death.
