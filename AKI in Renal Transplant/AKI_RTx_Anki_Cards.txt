What two complementary frameworks organize the differential for AKI in a kidney transplant recipient?	(1) Anatomic — pre-renal, intra-renal, post-renal; and (2) timing from transplant. Always keep BOTH transplant-specific and usual AKI causes on the list.
Name the 5 transplant-specific causes of allograft dysfunction.	CNI (calcineurin-inhibitor) toxicity — acute and chronic; renal artery stenosis/thrombosis; viral infection (BK, CMV, adenovirus); acute rejection; ureteral stenosis.
By what mechanism does ACUTE CNI toxicity cause AKI, and when does it typically occur?	Vasoconstriction of both afferent and efferent arterioles (a pre-renal/hemodynamic insult). Most common in the first 3 months post-transplant.
What are the clinical and lab clues to acute CNI toxicity?	Neurotoxicity (headache, tremor, paresthesias, seizures), hypertension, rarely HUS; labs show ↑K and ↑uric acid; usually an elevated CNI trough level.
Does a CNI trough within the goal range exclude chronic CNI toxicity?	No. Chronic CNI toxicity causes a gradual, asymptomatic rise in Cr and is diagnosed on biopsy (glomerulosclerosis, interstitial fibrosis, adventitial nodule — all nonspecific); a normal trough does NOT exclude it.
When does hyperacute rejection occur and what mediates it?	Within the first 24 hours; antibody-mediated by pre-formed donor-specific antibodies (DSAs).
When does acute rejection most commonly occur, and what late trigger should you suspect?	Within the first 6 months; later episodes suggest medication non-adherence. Types: acute cellular (T-cell mediated) and acute humoral/antibody-mediated.
How is acute rejection diagnosed?	Biopsy is the standard. Acute cellular → biopsy; acute antibody-mediated → biopsy + DSAs (± donor-derived cell-free DNA). Clues: graft pain, fever, hypertension, proteinuria (esp. >1 g/d), hematuria.
What is the typical timing and progression of BK polyomavirus nephropathy?	Can occur anytime but peaks ~10–13 months post-transplant. Progression: BK viruria → viremia → BK nephropathy.
What is the preferred test to screen for and diagnose BK, per the 2024 consensus?	Plasma (serum) BK PCR is the preferred screening/diagnostic test — screen monthly until ~9 months, then q3 months to 2 years. Urine BK PCR/decoy cells are a sensitive early screen; biopsy confirms nephropathy.
Which CMV donor/recipient serostatus carries the highest risk of CMV disease?	CMV donor-positive / recipient-negative (D+/R−). CMV nephritis typically occurs after 3 months; clues: fever, malaise, arthralgias, ↓WBC and ↓platelets; diagnose with CMV PCR ± biopsy.
When does renal artery stenosis typically present in a transplant, and how is it diagnosed?	Most common between 3 months and 2 years (atherosclerosis, trauma, ischemia; risk rises with CMV infection). Clues: hypertension, edema, ↓RBCs. Diagnosis: renal duplex ultrasound.
When does vascular (renal artery/vein) thrombosis occur after transplant?	In the immediate post-transplant period; relatively rare. Artery: kinking or rejection. Vein: external compression or extension of a DVT. Diagnosis: renal duplex.
Name the post-renal transplant-specific causes of AKI and the key test.	Ureteral stenosis (surgical anastomosis stenosing over time) and extrinsic compression from lymphocele, perinephric hematoma, or urinoma → obstructive uropathy. Diagnose with renal ultrasound (hydronephrosis).
What tests make up the "toolbox" for working up AKI in a transplant?	Renal duplex ultrasound, viral studies (plasma + urine BK/CMV PCR), CNI trough level, and allograft biopsy with DSAs (± donor-derived cell-free DNA). Involve transplant nephrology early.
A transplant patient on tacrolimus develops AKI, ↑K, gout flare, and tremor after starting fluconazole. Mechanism?	Acute CNI toxicity. Azole antifungals (e.g., fluconazole) inhibit CYP3A4 and raise tacrolimus/cyclosporine levels, precipitating neurotoxicity, hypertension, ↑K and AKI. Always review for CNI drug interactions.
What is the role of donor-derived cell-free DNA (dd-cfDNA) in 2024 practice?	A non-invasive adjunct that rises before creatinine; used to help exclude rejection (especially antibody-mediated) in acute allograft dysfunction or stable function (ASTS, Oct 2024). It supports, but does not replace, biopsy + DSAs.
Why must "usual" AKI causes still be evaluated in a transplant recipient?	Transplant patients still develop pre-renal (volume depletion, NSAIDs/ACEi, sepsis, cardiorenal/hepatorenal), intra-renal (ATN, AIN, contrast, GN), and post-renal (calculi, clot, cancer, BPH) AKI — transplant-specific causes are ADDED to, not substituted for, the standard differential.
