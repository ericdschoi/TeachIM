Learning Objectives

  1. Differentiate pre-renal, intra-renal, and post-renal causes of AKI in a kidney-transplant recipient — keeping both transplant-specific and usual causes on the list.
  2. Name the transplant-specific complications that cause allograft dysfunction and how timing from transplant narrows the differential.
  3. Evaluate acute allograft dysfunction with a focused "toolbox" (renal duplex US, viral PCR, CNI trough, biopsy + DSAs) and know when to involve transplant nephrology.

Pre-work send the day before

Three retrieval questions to prime the session.

1. A transplant recipient develops AKI. Before you reach for transplant-specific tests, what standard framework should still structure your differential?

The same pre-renal / intra-renal / post-renal framework used in any AKI. Transplant-specific causes are added to — not substituted for — volume depletion, NSAIDs/ACEi, sepsis, ATN, AIN, contrast, and obstruction.

2. List as many transplant-specific causes of allograft dysfunction as you can.

CNI toxicity (acute & chronic), renal artery stenosis/thrombosis, viral infection (BK, CMV, adenovirus), acute rejection (hyperacute, cellular, antibody-mediated), and ureteral stenosis (± lymphocele/hematoma/urinoma).

3. Which single test rules post-renal obstruction in or out, and which best evaluates vascular causes?

Renal ultrasound detects hydronephrosis (obstruction); adding duplex assesses arterial/venous flow for renal artery stenosis or thrombosis.

Teaching Script 30–45 min

Hook (2 min)

Put the case on the board: "A 33-year-old man, 6 months out from a kidney transplant for IgA nephropathy (CMV D+/R+), on tacrolimus, prednisone, and mycophenolate, now has a Cr of 3.4 (baseline 1.5)." Ask the room: "His nephrologist is worried about rejection — but what would you miss if rejection is the only thing you think about?" The point of the talk: a transplant patient gets every cause of AKI a non-transplant patient does, plus five transplant-specific ones.

ASK — "What's your one-line framework for any AKI?" Elicit pre/intra/post-renal before revealing the board's categories.

Segment 1 — The two-axis differential (Objective 1) 8 min

Use the board's first slide. Reveal that each compartment splits into typical/non-transplant and transplant-specific causes. Have learners rapid-fire the usual causes first (≤2 min), then build the transplant-specific list.

CompartmentUsual causesTransplant-specific
Pre-renalVolume depletion, sepsis, cardiorenal, hepatorenal, NSAIDs/ACEiAcute CNI toxicity; renal artery stenosis / artery-or-vein thrombosis
Intra-renalATN, AIN, contrast nephropathy, glomerulonephritisAcute rejection; viral (BK, CMV, adenovirus); chronic CNI toxicity; recurrent/de novo GN
Post-renal3 C's — calculi, clot, cancer; bladder dysfunction; BPHUreteral stenosis; extrinsic compression (lymphocele, perinephric hematoma, urinoma)
Pearl: Acute CNI toxicity is a pre-renal/hemodynamic lesion — it constricts both the afferent and efferent arteriole. Chronic CNI toxicity is an intra-renal structural lesion (fibrosis). Same drug, two compartments.

Segment 2 — Timing narrows it (Objective 2) 12–18 min

ASK — "It's post-op day 1 and the new graft makes no urine. What two things top your list?" (Hyperacute rejection from pre-formed DSAs; vascular thrombosis.)
TimingLead withKey clue / test
< 24 hHyperacute rejection; renal artery/vein thrombosisPre-formed DSAs; renal duplex
First 3 monthsAcute CNI toxicity; acute rejection (cellular/humoral)↑CNI trough; biopsy + DSAs (± dd-cfDNA)
3 months – 2 yearsRenal artery stenosis; BK nephropathy (peak ~10–13 mo); CMV nephritis (>3 mo)Duplex; plasma BK PCR; CMV PCR
Any timeLate rejection (esp. non-adherence); ureteral stenosis; all usual causesBiopsy; renal US
ASK — "Patient is 11 months out, asymptomatic, creeping creatinine, bland-ish urine. What virus, and what's the single best test?" (BK; plasma BK PCR.)
Pearl: BK progresses viruria → viremia → nephropathy. Per the 2024 Second International Consensus, plasma (serum) BK PCR is the preferred screening/diagnostic test — screen monthly to ~9 months, then q3 months to 2 years. Sustained DNAemia or biopsy-proven nephropathy → reduce immunosuppression, the primary treatment.
ASK — "Which CMV serostatus is highest risk?" (Donor-positive / recipient-negative, D+/R−.)

Segment 3 — The toolbox & calling for help (Objective 3) 5 min

Reveal the "toolbox" icon: renal duplex US (hydronephrosis + flow), viral studies (plasma + urine BK/CMV PCR), CNI trough, and allograft biopsy with DSAs (± donor-derived cell-free DNA). Emphasize: a normal CNI trough does NOT exclude chronic CNI toxicity, and biopsy + DSAs remain the standard for rejection. Involve transplant nephrology early.

Pearl: Always reconcile the med list. Azole antifungals (fluconazole, voriconazole), diltiazem, and macrolides inhibit CYP3A4 and raise CNI levels — a classic precipitant of acute CNI toxicity (see Case 1).

Retrieval close (3 min)

Erase the board. Ask learners to rebuild, from memory: the five transplant-specific causes, the timeline buckets, and the four toolbox tests. Whoever names the azole–CNI interaction unprompted wins.

Cases click to reveal

All three share the stem: 33M, transplant 6 months ago for IgA nephropathy (CMV D+/R+), on tacrolimus/prednisone/mycophenolate, Cr 3.4 from baseline 1.5.

Case 1 — Numbness, hot toe, recent fluconazole

ROS: new numbness/tingling in hands & feet; pain and redness of the left great toe. Meds: fluconazole 2 weeks ago for a yeast infection. Exam: BP 160/92, no graft tenderness. Labs: Na 135, K 6.9, FeNa 0.7%; UA clear, hyaline casts, no protein/WBC/RBC.

Work the toolbox — what do you send, and what's the diagnosis?

CNI trough elevated at 14 (goal 6–8). Diagnosis: acute CNI toxicity causing neurotoxicity, a gout flare, hypertension, and hyperkalemia. Fluconazole inhibits CYP3A4 → raised tacrolimus levels. Hold/adjust the CNI, treat hyperkalemia and gout, and manage the interaction.

Case 2 — Fevers, dysuria, graft tenderness; post-biopsy hematuria

ROS: 2 days of fevers/chills, abdominal pain, dysuria, nausea/vomiting. Exam: T 39.4 °C, HR 115, BP 160/92, tenderness over the graft. (Renal biopsy yesterday for uptrending Cr, complicated by hematuria.) Labs: FeNa 1.2%, urine Pr:Cr 2.6; UA cloudy, + leukocyte esterase, + nitrite, +WBC/RBC/protein/bacteria.

What does the workup show?

CNI trough in range; renal duplex normal flow, no hydronephrosis, perinephric stranding; biopsy shows acute cellular rejection, no DSAs; viral studies negative. Diagnosis: acute cellular rejection plus sepsis from acute graft pyelonephritis — two processes at once. Treat the infection and coordinate rejection management with transplant nephrology.

Case 3 — Sudden suprapubic pain, anuria for 12 h

ROS: acute suprapubic/abdominal pain, no urine output for 12 hours. Exam: VSS, abdomen tender over graft/suprapubic region. Labs: FeNa 5.7%, urine Pr:Cr 0.6; UA not collected (anuric).

What's the unifying diagnosis?

Renal duplex: normal arterial flow but graft hydronephrosis with 700 cc in the bladder and retained clot. Diagnosis: acute clot retention → obstructive uropathy, on a background of chronic CNI toxicity (biopsy: glomerulosclerosis + interstitial fibrosis). The post-biopsy clot obstructs outflow — relieve the obstruction (catheter/irrigation).

Board-Style MCQs click for answer

1. A 40-year-old woman is 5 weeks post-deceased-donor kidney transplant on tacrolimus, mycophenolate, and prednisone. She started clarithromycin for bronchitis 4 days ago. Cr rises from 1.3 to 2.4; she has a fine tremor and headache. K 5.8. Which is the most likely cause?

  1. Acute cellular rejection
  2. Acute CNI toxicity from a drug interaction
  3. BK virus nephropathy
  4. Renal artery stenosis
  5. Ureteral obstruction
Answer

B. Macrolides (clarithromycin) inhibit CYP3A4, raising tacrolimus levels; tremor, headache, hypertension, hyperkalemia, and a hemodynamic AKI within the first months are classic acute CNI toxicity. Check a trough (expected high). A is possible but the temporal link to clarithromycin and neuro symptoms point to CNI toxicity; rejection would more typically show graft pain/proteinuria. C peaks later (~10–13 mo) and is usually asymptomatic. D presents with refractory HTN over months, not acutely after an antibiotic. E would show hydronephrosis and ↓UOP.

2. An 11-month-post-transplant man has an asymptomatic Cr rise from 1.4 to 2.0. UA is relatively bland. Plasma BK PCR is markedly elevated. What is the most appropriate next step in management?

  1. Pulse high-dose corticosteroids
  2. Start IV ganciclovir
  3. Reduce immunosuppression
  4. Start an ACE inhibitor
  5. Begin hemodialysis
Answer

C. The cornerstone of BK nephropathy treatment is reducing immunosuppression (typically the antiproliferative ± CNI), often confirmed with biopsy. A treats rejection and would worsen BK. B targets CMV, not BK. D is not the acute management. E is unnecessary at this level of dysfunction. Per the 2024 consensus, sustained plasma DNAemia or biopsy-proven nephropathy triggers stepwise immunosuppression reduction.

3. A patient is anuric 12 hours after an allograft biopsy and has new suprapubic pain. Vitals stable. What is the best initial test?

  1. Allograft biopsy
  2. CNI trough level
  3. Renal ultrasound
  4. Plasma CMV PCR
  5. Urine eosinophils
Answer

C. Anuria plus suprapubic pain after a biopsy screams obstruction — renal US will show hydronephrosis and a clot-filled bladder (clot retention/obstructive uropathy), which is rapidly reversible. The others address intra-renal causes and miss the time-sensitive post-renal lesion. Always rule out obstruction first when UOP drops abruptly.

4. Which donor/recipient CMV serostatus carries the highest risk of CMV disease after transplant?

  1. D−/R−
  2. D−/R+
  3. D+/R+
  4. D+/R−
  5. Risk is independent of serostatus
Answer

D. A CMV-seronegative recipient receiving a seropositive organ (D+/R−) has no prior immunity and the highest risk of primary CMV disease. CMV nephritis typically occurs after 3 months and can present with fever, malaise, arthralgias, leukopenia, and thrombocytopenia; diagnose with CMV PCR ± biopsy.

5. A transplant recipient with a slowly rising creatinine over a year has a tacrolimus trough squarely within goal range. Biopsy shows arteriolar hyalinosis, interstitial fibrosis, and tubular atrophy. What does this most likely represent?

  1. Acute antibody-mediated rejection
  2. Chronic CNI toxicity
  3. BK nephropathy
  4. Recurrent IgA nephropathy
  5. Acute tubular necrosis
Answer

B. Gradual, asymptomatic decline with hyalinosis/fibrosis is classic chronic CNI toxicity — and crucially, a therapeutic trough does not exclude it. A would show peritubular capillaritis/C4d with DSAs. C shows viral cytopathic changes with positive plasma BK PCR. D shows mesangial IgA with hematuria/proteinuria. E is acute, not a year-long course. Findings are nonspecific, so correlate clinically.

Take-Home Points

  1. Keep both lists — a transplant patient gets every usual AKI cause plus five transplant-specific ones.
  2. Five transplant-specific causes: CNI toxicity (acute + chronic), renal artery stenosis/thrombosis, viral (BK, CMV, adenovirus), rejection, ureteral stenosis.
  3. Timing narrows it: <24 h hyperacute/thrombosis; first 3 mo acute CNI tox & rejection; 3 mo–2 yr RAS, BK (peak ~10–13 mo), CMV.
  4. Toolbox: renal duplex US, plasma + urine BK/CMV PCR, CNI trough, biopsy + DSAs (± dd-cfDNA). Loop in transplant nephrology early.
  5. Reconcile meds — azole antifungals and other CYP3A4 inhibitors raise CNI levels and trigger acute toxicity. A normal trough does not exclude chronic CNI toxicity.
