Learning Objectives

  1. Define abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB) using the four FIGO bleeding parameters.
  2. Build a differential for AUB with the PALM-COEIN mnemonic, separating structural from non-structural causes.
  3. Order the correct baseline and cause-specific work-up — including when to add transvaginal ultrasound and endometrial biopsy.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient says her periods are "heavy and long." Which four parameters define a normal menstrual cycle, and what are the rough FIGO cutoffs for each? Frequency 24–38 days (abnormal = <24 frequent, >38 infrequent, none = amenorrhea) · Duration ≤8 days · Regularity cycle-to-cycle variation ≤7–9 days (age-dependent) · Volume subjective (interferes with life) or objective (soaking a pad/tampon every 2 h, flooding, overnight changes). Any deviation = AUB.
Q2. Expand PALM-COEIN. Which letters are structural vs non-structural, and what does the "I" stand for? PALM = structural: Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma (fibroid), Malignancy/hyperplasia. COEIN = non-structural: Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic (hormonal contraception/IUD/implant, anticoagulants, SERMs, dopamine antagonists), Not yet classified. The "I" is Iatrogenic — not "idiopathic."
Q3. Which three labs (plus one exam) does EVERY patient with AUB get, before any cause-specific testing? Pelvic exam + three labs: pregnancy test, CBC, and iron studies. Pregnancy is always ruled out first; CBC screens for anemia/thrombocytopenia; ferritin <50 ng/mL is low in pre-menopausal patients and warrants treatment even without anemia.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (Abnormal_Uterine_Bleeding_Optimized.pptx) or reproduce it on a whiteboard using the Learner Worksheet as the set-up. Click through the What-is-AUB → Causes → Work-up → Cases (→ Bonus HPO axis) tabs. Every cursor icon on the board is a built-in click-to-reveal prompt — pose the ASK before clicking. Core talk ~20 min; the two cases add 5–10 min.

Hook (2 min)

"A 32-year-old tells you her periods are 'heavier than they used to be.' Before you order a single test — what four questions tell you whether this is even abnormal, and what's the one test you must send no matter what the story is?" (The four FIGO parameters; the must-send is a pregnancy test.) This launches Objectives 1 and 3 at once.

Objective 1 — Define AUB: the four parameters 5 min

ASK: "What features of a menstrual history would make you call a bleeding pattern abnormal?" Collect answers, then click each button (Duration / Frequency / Regularity / Volume) to reveal the cutoffs.
ParameterNormalAbnormal
Duration≤8 daysProlonged >8 days
FrequencyEvery 24–38 daysFrequent <24 d, infrequent >38 d, absent (amenorrhea = no menses ×3 mo)

Pearl: Ask about all four every time — patients (and notes) collapse them into "heavy," but the parameter that's off steers the differential. Heavy+regular suggests a structural or coagulopathy cause; irregular/infrequent points to ovulatory dysfunction.

Objective 2 — Build the differential: PALM-COEIN 7 min

ASK (first, always): "Before any mnemonic — what is the one diagnosis you rule out in every reproductive-age patient with AUB?" Click "Pregnant?" → reveal. Then: go letter by letter; have the group guess a cause for each before you click.
PALM — structuralCOEIN — non-structural
P Polyp (endometrial > cervical)C Coagulopathy (vWD, thrombocytopenia, platelet dysfunction from liver/renal disease)
A Adenomyosis (endometrium into myometrium; "boggy," tender uterus)O Ovulatory dysfunction (PCOS, thyroid, hyperprolactinemia, perimenopause, POI, HPA-axis stress)
L Leiomyoma (fibroid)E Endometrial (endometritis/PID, STI; local hemostatic disorders)
M Malignancy / hyperplasia (endometrial in at-risk patients; also cervical/vaginal/vulvar)I Iatrogenic — hormonal contraception (IUD, implant/Nexplanon), anticoagulants, SERMs (tamoxifen, raloxifene), steroids, dopamine antagonists
N Not yet classified (AVMs, isthmocele)
Corrected here: The classic teaching error is calling the "I" idiopathic. FIGO's "I" is Iatrogenic — the board now reflects this. The textbook example is new-Nexplanon spotting.

Objective 3 — Work it up: baseline → cause-specific 8 min

ASK: "What does EVERY patient with AUB get, before you go down any one branch?" Click Exam + Labs to reveal.

Baseline for all-comers: pelvic exam (visualize vulva/vagina/cervix for a non-uterine source; bimanual for an enlarged/tender uterus or adnexal mass) plus three labs — pregnancy test, CBC, iron studies (ferritin <50 ng/mL is low pre-menopause).

Structural branch: transvaginal ultrasound to image uterus/ovaries/endometrial stripe. Add endometrial biopsy when hyperplasia/cancer risk is present — AUB is the most common symptom of endometrial cancer.

ASK: "Who needs the endometrial biopsy?" Click to reveal the risk factors.

Biopsy triggers: any postmenopausal bleeding; age >45 with AUB; chronic anovulation/obesity; unopposed-estrogen exposure (incl. tamoxifen); persistent AUB despite treatment in younger patients with risk factors.

Non-structural branch: directed by history — personal/family bleeding history, surgical/dental bleeding, medication and procedure review, full ROS.

Clue on history/examSuspectTargeted test
Heavy menses since menarche; family bleeding hx; postpartum/dental/surgical bleedingCoagulopathy (≈20% of HMB)vWF panel, PT/INR, PTT, factor activity

Bonus (HPO axis): Hypothyroidism → ↑TSH provides negative feedback → ↓GnRH → ↓FSH/LH → anovulation. Perimenopause → failing ovarian estradiol → loss of feedback → ↑GnRH → ↑FSH (why FSH rises).

Retrieval close (2 min)

Board erased: have the group rebuild, from memory — (1) the four parameters, (2) PALM vs COEIN with the "I" = Iatrogenic, (3) the universal exam + 3 labs, (4) the two triggers for endometrial biopsy.

Cases original cases, click-to-reveal

Case 1 — 32-year-old with "heavy periods" ×12 mo

Periods every 26–28 days lasting 8–9 days; soaks a pad every 2 hours on heavy days, with blood soaking through clothing. Previously 4–5 days and lighter. Pelvic exam: mildly enlarged uterus, otherwise normal. Labs: iron deficiency without anemia, negative pregnancy test.

Is this AUB? What is the bleeding pattern?Yes — it is high volume (q2h soaking, flooding) and prolonged duration (>8 days). Frequency and regularity are normal.
Most likely cause?A structural cause — fibroids or adenomyosis — given the enlarged uterus and heavy-but-regular pattern.
Next evaluation?Transvaginal ultrasound. Treat the iron deficiency. (If this patient were peri-/post-menopausal, add endometrial biopsy.)

Case 2 — 39-year-old with "irregular periods" ×2 yr

Increasingly infrequent periods every 40–60 days, variable volume. FHx celiac disease and lupus. ROS: fatigue, hair loss, 15-lb unintentional weight gain over 2 years. Pelvic exam: unremarkable. Labs: negative pregnancy test, normal CBC and coagulation, no iron deficiency.

Bleeding pattern?Infrequent (oligomenorrhea, cycles >38 d) and irregular (variable cycle length).
Most likely cause?Ovulatory dysfunction, most likely endocrine — the fatigue, hair loss, and weight gain point to hypothyroidism. Given age, premature ovarian insufficiency is also on the list.
Next evaluation?Repeat pregnancy test, TSH, prolactin, ± FSH. Add total testosterone if PCOS features are present.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 28-year-old woman reports menses every 27 days lasting 9 days, soaking a pad every 1–2 hours on her two heaviest days. She has bled heavily since menarche, had a postpartum hemorrhage, and her mother has frequent nosebleeds. Pregnancy test negative; Hgb 10.8, platelets normal. Which is the most appropriate next test?

  1. Transvaginal ultrasound
  2. Endometrial biopsy
  3. von Willebrand panel
  4. Pelvic MRI
  5. TSH and prolactin
Answer & explanationC — von Willebrand panel. Heavy menstrual bleeding since menarche plus a personal (postpartum hemorrhage) and family bleeding history strongly suggest an inherited bleeding disorder (≈20% of HMB; vWD most common). A is for structural suspicion (enlarged uterus, focal findings) — absent here. B is for hyperplasia/cancer risk (age >45, postmenopausal bleeding, anovulation/obesity, unopposed estrogen) — this patient has none. D MRI is not a first-line AUB test. E thyroid/prolactin fit ovulatory dysfunction (irregular cycles), but hers are regular.

2. A 58-year-old woman who reached menopause at 51 has one episode of vaginal spotting. Exam is unremarkable. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Reassure; bleeding this remote from menopause is benign
  2. Transvaginal ultrasound and/or endometrial biopsy
  3. Start cyclic progesterone
  4. Repeat pelvic exam in 6 months
  5. Check FSH to confirm menopause
Answer & explanationB — transvaginal ultrasound and/or endometrial biopsy. Any postmenopausal bleeding is endometrial cancer until proven otherwise and mandates evaluation (TVUS for endometrial stripe; biopsy if stripe >4 mm or bleeding persists/recurs). A and D dangerously delay diagnosis. C treats without excluding malignancy. E menopause is already established clinically; FSH won't change the need for evaluation.

3. A 24-year-old with a levonorgestrel IUD placed 6 weeks ago reports irregular light spotting. Pregnancy test negative; exam normal. Under PALM-COEIN, which category best fits, and what is the next step?

  1. Endometrial (E) — STI testing
  2. Iatrogenic (I) — counsel and continue; spotting typically settles by 3–6 months
  3. Malignancy (M) — endometrial biopsy
  4. Ovulatory (O) — check TSH and prolactin
  5. Coagulopathy (C) — von Willebrand panel
Answer & explanationB — Iatrogenic. New-IUD/implant spotting is the prototypical iatrogenic cause; it is expected and usually resolves within 3–6 months — reassure and continue. (This is precisely the "I" the corrected board now labels Iatrogenic, not "idiopathic.") The other categories don't fit a low-risk young patient with a clear temporal trigger; STI testing would be added only with discharge/dyspareunia or risk factors.

4. A 35-year-old with obesity, oligomenorrhea (cycles 45–70 days), hirsutism, and acanthosis nigricans has AUB. Beyond pregnancy test, CBC, and iron studies, which test best targets the most likely cause?

  1. Transvaginal ultrasound
  2. von Willebrand panel
  3. Total testosterone
  4. FSH
  5. Coagulation factor activity
Answer & explanationC — total testosterone. The picture is androgen excess / PCOS (ovulatory dysfunction): obesity, oligomenorrhea, hirsutism, insulin resistance. Workup also includes TSH and prolactin. Of note, chronic anovulation = unopposed estrogen, so this patient is also at risk for endometrial hyperplasia and may warrant biopsy. A/B/E target structural or bleeding-disorder causes; D FSH fits perimenopause/POI, less likely at 35 with these features.

5. A 47-year-old with AUB has a transvaginal ultrasound showing a thickened endometrium. Which single factor most strongly mandates endometrial biopsy?

  1. Cycle-to-cycle variation of 8 days
  2. Age >45 with AUB (and chronic anovulation/unopposed-estrogen risk)
  3. Iron deficiency without anemia
  4. A negative pregnancy test
  5. Menses lasting 8 days
Answer & explanationB. Endometrial sampling is indicated for AUB with hyperplasia/cancer risk — age >45, postmenopausal bleeding, chronic anovulation/obesity, unopposed estrogen (incl. tamoxifen), or persistent bleeding despite treatment. A thickened stripe in a 47-year-old with these risks crosses the threshold. The other options are routine findings that don't, by themselves, drive biopsy.

Take-Home Points

  1. AUB is defined by abnormality in any of four parameters — duration (>8 d), frequency (<24 or >38 d), regularity (variation >7–9 d by age), or volume.
  2. After ruling out pregnancy, use PALM-COEIN: PALM = structural (Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy/hyperplasia); COEIN = non-structural (Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not yet classified).
  3. Every patient gets a pelvic exam plus three labs: pregnancy test, CBC, and iron studies.
  4. Add transvaginal ultrasound for the structural branch; endometrial biopsy for hyperplasia/cancer risk (postmenopausal bleeding, age >45, chronic anovulation/obesity, unopposed estrogen).
  5. ~20% of heavy menstrual bleeding reflects a bleeding disorder — screen when bleeding dates to menarche or there's a personal/family bleeding history.
