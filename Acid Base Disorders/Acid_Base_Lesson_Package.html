Learning Objectives

  1. Identify the components of a standard ABG and their normal values.
  2. Apply a step-wise approach to interpreting acid-base disorders.
  3. Generate a differential diagnosis for metabolic acidosis, respiratory acidosis, metabolic alkalosis, and respiratory alkalosis.
  4. Calculate expected compensation for each primary disorder — and recognize a mixed disorder when compensation doesn't match.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient's pH is exactly 7.40. Can they still have an acid-base disorder? Yes — a normal pH with abnormal PCO2 or HCO3 means two (or more) opposing primary processes. Compensation never fully normalizes the pH, so a "perfect" pH with deranged values is a mixed disorder until proven otherwise.
Q2. Why must you calculate an anion gap on every chemistry panel, even when the pH is alkalemic? An anion gap metabolic acidosis can hide behind any primary disorder — and an elevated AG is never compensatory. Classic example: salicylate poisoning presents as respiratory alkalosis + concurrent AGMA.
Q3. Your patient's albumin is 2.0 g/dL and the anion gap is 11. Is that normal? No — expected AG ≈ 2.5 × albumin ≈ 5. An AG of 11 is ~6 points above expected: a real anion gap acidosis hidden by hypoalbuminemia.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected presenter board (Acid_Base_Optimized.pptx); prepare with the learning board (ABG-Learner.pptx), which carries more detail and pop-ups. Print the worksheet so learners take notes. ~15 min for the framework, 10–15 min for cases; cases 1–3 are simpler, 4–5 complex — pick by learner level.

Hook (2 min)

Write 6.98 / 41 / 58 / 10 on the board. "This ABG has three primary acid-base disorders hiding in it, and by the end of this talk every one of you will find all three in under two minutes." (It's Case 5 — return to it at the close.)

Objective 1 — Components of the ABG 3 min

ASK: "What's the format of an ABG, and what are the normal values?" — click each component to reveal.
ComponentNormalDerangement
pH7.40 ± 0.05<7.35 acidemia; >7.45 alkalemia
PCO240 ± 4>44 respiratory acidosis; <36 respiratory alkalosis
PO2>80 (RA)see the hypoxemia talk (A–a gradient)
HCO324 ± 2<22 metabolic acidosis; >26 metabolic alkalosis
Base excess0 ± 2>+2 metabolic alkalosis; <−2 metabolic acidosis (H+ needed to restore pH at normal PCO2)
ASK: "What's the difference between acidemia and acidosis?" — -emia is the blood pH; -osis is a process. Several -oses can coexist with any pH.

Objective 2 — The step-wise approach 4 min

  1. Acid-base status: pH → acidemia / normal / alkalemia.
  2. Primary process: which deranged value (PCO2 respiratory, HCO3 metabolic) explains the pH? Both deranged in the pH's direction → concurrent processes. Normal pH + deranged values → mixed disorder.
  3. Anion gap — in everyone: AG = Na − (Cl + HCO3); expected AG ≈ 2.5 × albumin. If AGMA → delta-delta (step 3a).
  4. Compensation: calculate the expected compensatory change; mismatch = another primary process.
Pearl — the "residual HCO3" delta-delta: take measured AG − expected AG and add that difference back to the measured HCO3. It asks: "what's left after the AGMA is accounted for?" Normal → pure AGMA. High → concurrent metabolic alkalosis. Low → concurrent NAGMA.

Objective 3 — Differentials 5 min

ASK: before each reveal — "Build me the differential." Start with acidemia.
DisorderDifferential
AGMAMUDPILES — Methanol, Uremia, DKA/ketoacidosis, Paraldehyde, INH/Iron, Lactic acidosis, Ethylene glycol (& EtOH), Salicylates
NAGMAGut HCO3 loss (diarrhea, ileostomy), renal loss (RTA, early renal failure, ↓aldosterone), excess Cl (NS). Urinary AG: "neGUTive" = GI loss; positive = renal
Respiratory acidosis↓CO2 elimination (obstructive/intrinsic lung disease, neuromuscular weakness, ↓drive — opioids/benzos) or ↑production (sepsis, overfeeding, hyperthyroidism)
Metabolic alkalosisIVF/Cl-responsive: vomiting/NGT, thiazide/loop diuretics, contraction. Non-responsive: ↑aldosterone, genetic disorders, milk-alkali, hypokalemia
Respiratory alkalosisAnxiety/pain, fever/sepsis, CNS disease, salicylates, liver disease, PE

Objective 4 — Compensation 4 min

ASK: "Which organ compensates for which, and how fast?" Lungs for metabolic (minutes); kidneys for respiratory (2–3 days). PCO2 and HCO3 always move in the same direction.
Primary disorderExpected compensation
Metabolic acidosisWinter's: PCO2 = 1.5 × HCO3 + 8 (±2) ≈ thumb rule: PCO2 ≈ last two digits of pH
Metabolic alkalosisPCO2 = 0.7 × HCO3 + 21
Respiratory acidosis (per ↑10 PCO2)HCO3 ↑1 acute · ↑4 chronic
Respiratory alkalosis (per ↓10 PCO2)HCO3 ↓2 acute · ↓5 chronic
Pearl: compensation never overcompensates and never fully corrects pH to 7.40. Don't memorize the whole table — metabolic acidosis is the most common derangement, so own Winter's formula and the thumb rule; look up the rest.

Close — retrieval (2 min)

Return to the hook ABG (6.98/41/10 + chemistry) and have learners run the four steps aloud to find all three disorders (AGMA + NAGMA + respiratory acidosis). Then have them rebuild the step-list from memory.

Cases from the original boards — answers click-to-reveal

Basic pattern-recognition cases (the six-panel grid) precede these. Remind learners: the grid identifies patterns; real interpretation requires calculating expected compensation. Cases 1–3 simple; 4–5 complex.

Case 1

65M with COPD, fever and cough. SaO2 90% on 30% FiO2. ABG 7.36/60/60/36. Na 142, Cl 97, Cr 1.2, HCO3 32 (panel), albumin 3.0.

Acid-base status?pH is low-normal.
Primary process?Respiratory acidosis — PCO2 and HCO3 both elevated, pH low-normal, so the respiratory acidosis is primary.
Anion gap?142 − (97 + 36) = 9; expected AG = 2.5 × 3.0 = 7.5 — no AGMA.
Compensation?Yes — metabolic alkalosis (compensatory). Normal-range pH suggests chronic: for chronic ↑PCO2 of 20, expect HCO3 ↑8 (to ~32). Diagnosis: chronic, compensated respiratory acidosis. Bonus: A–a difference 72 — elevated; V/Q mismatch or shunt.

Case 2

45M with IBD, abdominal pain, multiple loose bowel movements. ABG 7.34/35/–/18. Na 127, Cl 100, HCO3 18, Cr 2.4, albumin 4.0.

Acid-base status / primary process?Acidemia (borderline). PCO2 and HCO3 both low → the metabolic acidosis is primary.
Anion gap?127 − (100 + 18) = 9 — a NAGMA. (UAG would be inaccurate given renal dysfunction.)
Compensation?Yes — respiratory alkalosis: Winter's = 1.5 × 18 + 8 = 35 = measured PCO2 (thumb rule agrees). Diagnosis: non-gap metabolic acidosis from diarrhea with appropriate respiratory compensation.

Case 3

34F found down, SaO2 89% on RA, pinpoint pupils. ABG 7.25/60/60/26, base excess +1. Na 136, Cl 100, HCO3 26, Cr 1.4, albumin 4.0.

Acid-base status / primary process?Acidemia (7.25). PCO2 60 elevated, HCO3 upper-normal → respiratory acidosis.
Anion gap?136 − (100 + 26) = 10 — normal.
Compensation?None yet — HCO3 normal, no base excess; for an acute ↑PCO2 of 20 expect HCO3 ↑2 (~26: buffering only). Diagnosis: acute respiratory acidosis with hypoxemia from opioid-induced hypoventilation. A–a difference 15 — normal for age.

Case 4

24-year-old man with a history of depression, brought in unresponsive. RR 34, SaO2 99% RA. ABG 7.56/22/110/17. Na 137, Cl 99, HCO3 18, Cr 1.2, albumin 4.0, lactate 1.2, bland urine.

Acid-base status / primary process?Alkalemia — PCO2 low → respiratory alkalosis.
Anion gap?137 − (99 + 18) = 20 — elevated. An AGMA is never compensatory → concurrent primary AGMA.
Delta-delta?ΔAG = 20 − 12 = 8; residual HCO3 = 18 + 8 = 26 — upper-normal → pure AGMA.
Diagnosis?Primary respiratory alkalosis + AGMA — classic for salicylate poisoning.

Case 5

72F with Roux-en-Y gastric bypass: 3 days of dyspnea, abdominal pain, nausea, diarrhea; somnolent after IV morphine. ABG 6.98/41/58/10. Na 146, Cl 121, HCO3 7, K 18/98 panel, lipase 3290, lactate 2.7, albumin 2.1, urine + ketones.

Acid-base status / primary process?Severe acidemia. HCO3 very low → metabolic acidosis; PCO2 "normal" at 41 is inappropriate (see compensation) → concurrent respiratory process.
Anion gap?146 − (121 + 7) = 18 — elevated (MUDPILES; here ketoacidosis + lactate).
Delta-delta?Expected AG with albumin 2.1 ≈ 5–6; ΔAG = 18 − 6 = 12; residual HCO3 = 7 + 12 = 19 — still low → concurrent NAGMA (diarrhea).
Compensation?Winter's: expected PCO2 = 1.5 × 10 + 8 = 23... measured 41 → concurrent respiratory acidosis (opioid hypoventilation). Diagnosis: AGMA (ketoacidosis + lactic) + NAGMA (diarrhea) + respiratory acidosis (narcotics) — three primary disorders.

Board-style MCQs NEW — 5 ABIM-format questions

1. A 68-year-old man with severe COPD is seen for a routine visit. He feels at baseline. ABG: pH 7.36, PCO2 58, PO2 62, HCO3 32. Which best describes his acid-base status?

  1. Acute respiratory acidosis
  2. Chronic (compensated) respiratory acidosis
  3. Respiratory acidosis with concurrent metabolic acidosis
  4. Primary metabolic alkalosis with respiratory compensation
  5. Mixed respiratory and metabolic alkalosis
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. PCO2 ↑18 with HCO3 ↑~8 (24→32) matches chronic compensation (↑4 per 10) and a near-normal pH. A: acute compensation would raise HCO3 only ~2 (to 26) and the pH would be much lower (~7.27). C: HCO3 is high, not low. D: primary metabolic alkalosis would push pH above 7.45 and PCO2 compensation tops out lower (0.7×32+21≈43, not 58). E: pH is not alkalemic.

2. A 30-year-old woman presents with tinnitus, vomiting, and hyperpnea (RR 32). ABG: pH 7.51, PCO2 20, PO2 105, HCO3 15. Na 140, Cl 102, albumin 4.0. Which best describes her acid-base status?

  1. Primary respiratory alkalosis with appropriate renal compensation
  2. Primary metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation
  3. Primary respiratory alkalosis with a concurrent anion gap metabolic acidosis
  4. Pure anion gap metabolic acidosis
  5. Metabolic alkalosis from vomiting with respiratory compensation
Answer + why each distractor is wrongC. Alkalemic pH with PCO2 20 = primary respiratory alkalosis; AG = 140 − (102+15) = 23 — and an elevated AG is never compensatory, so there is a concurrent AGMA. Classic salicylate toxicity. A: acute renal compensation drops HCO3 ~2 per 10 (expect ~20); 15 with AG 23 is an acidosis, not compensation alone. B: pH 7.51 means the alkalosis dominates — compensation can't make the pH alkalemic. D: pure AGMA can't produce pH 7.51. E: HCO3 is low, not high.

3. A 28-year-old man with type 1 diabetes has 2 days of vomiting and polyuria. ABG: pH 7.32, PCO2 28. Na 134, Cl 88, HCO3 14, albumin 4.0, glucose 480. After calculating the anion gap and delta-delta, which combination is present?

  1. Pure anion gap metabolic acidosis
  2. AGMA + concurrent non-gap metabolic acidosis
  3. AGMA + concurrent metabolic alkalosis
  4. NAGMA + respiratory acidosis
  5. AGMA + respiratory acidosis
Answer + why each distractor is wrongC. AG = 134 − (88+14) = 32; ΔAG = 32 − 12 = 20; residual HCO3 = 14 + 20 = 34 > 26 → hidden metabolic alkalosis (vomiting). A: residual HCO3 would be ~24. B: residual HCO3 would be <22. D: the AG is frankly elevated. E: Winter's = 1.5×14+8 = 29 ≈ measured 28 — respiratory compensation is appropriate, no respiratory acidosis.

4. A 45-year-old man with a high-output ileostomy: pH 7.31, PCO2 30. Na 138, Cl 111, HCO3 15, albumin 4.0. Urinary anion gap is negative. Which best describes the disorder?

  1. AGMA from lactic acidosis
  2. NAGMA from GI bicarbonate loss with appropriate respiratory compensation
  3. NAGMA from renal tubular acidosis
  4. NAGMA with concurrent respiratory acidosis
  5. Metabolic alkalosis with respiratory compensation
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. AG = 138 − (111+15) = 12 (normal) → NAGMA; Winter's = 1.5×15+8 = 30.5 ≈ measured 30 (appropriate); negative UAG = "neGUTive" = GI loss, matching the ileostomy. A: AG is normal. C: RTA gives a positive UAG. D: measured PCO2 matches Winter's — no concurrent respiratory process. E: HCO3 is low and pH acidemic.

5. A 56-year-old with cirrhosis and septic shock: lactate 6.0, albumin 2.0. Chemistry: Na 132, Cl 100, HCO3 18 (AG 14). The resident notes "anion gap only mildly elevated at 14 (normal 8–12)." What is the most accurate interpretation?

  1. The AG of 14 is essentially normal; no AGMA is present
  2. Expected AG here is ~5, so an AG of 14 represents a significant anion gap acidosis
  3. An AG cannot be interpreted in liver disease
  4. The low albumin falsely elevates the measured AG
  5. A urinary anion gap is needed to confirm AGMA
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Expected AG ≈ 2.5 × albumin = 5; the measured 14 is ~9 above expected — a substantial AGMA (lactic acidosis) masked by hypoalbuminemia. A: ignores the albumin correction. C: AG remains interpretable with the correction. D: backwards — hypoalbuminemia lowers the AG. E: UAG distinguishes causes of NAGMA, not AGMA.

Take-Home Points

  1. Same routine every time: pH → primary process → anion gap in every patient → compensation. A normal pH with abnormal PCO2/HCO3 is a mixed disorder.
  2. Expected AG ≈ 2.5 × albumin — hypoalbuminemia hides anion gaps. If AGMA, run the residual-HCO3 delta-delta to find the second metabolic process.
  3. PCO2 and HCO3 move in the same direction when compensating; lungs compensate in minutes, kidneys in days; compensation never overshoots.
  4. Winter's formula (1.5 × HCO3 + 8) ≈ the thumb rule (PCO2 ≈ last two digits of pH) — the one compensation calculation to memorize.
  5. A measured value that doesn't match the expected compensation is a second primary diagnosis — like Case 5's "normal" PCO2 of 41 hiding a respiratory acidosis.
