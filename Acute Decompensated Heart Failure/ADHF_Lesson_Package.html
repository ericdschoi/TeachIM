Learning Objectives

  1. Profile a decompensated HF patient using the congestion (wet/dry) × perfusion (warm/cold) framework rather than EF alone.
  2. Dose and titrate IV loop diuretics using a measured natriuretic response (DOSE / spot urine Na).
  3. Initiate and continue the four pillars of GDMT during the hospitalization, knowing the hold parameters.
  4. Assemble a discharge bundle that lowers 30-day readmission (euvolemia, education, early follow-up).

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient on 80 mg PO furosemide daily is admitted wet. What IV furosemide dose should you start?At least the same total daily dose given IV, and the DOSE trial favored the high-intensity arm of ~2.5× the home dose. Reasonable: 80 mg IV BID (160 mg/day). Then reassess natriuretic response at 2 hours and double if inadequate.
Q2. Why give loop diuretics IV rather than PO in acute decompensation?Splanchnic/gut wall edema reduces oral bioavailability and slows absorption; IV dosing bypasses this and produces a reliable, brisk natriuresis.
Q3. The creatinine rises 0.3 mg/dL on day 2 of effective diuresis in a still-congested patient making good urine. Stop diuresing?No — a modest creatinine bump during decongestion (often hemoconcentration / WRF) is not an indication to stop if the patient remains volume-overloaded and is producing urine. Persistent congestion is the bigger driver of bad outcomes.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"Three patients are admitted 'for CHF.' One is a 72-year-old who stopped her diuretic for a beach trip; one is hypotensive with cold mottled knees and a lactate of 4; one came in with a new fast atrial fibrillation. Same chief complaint — but the EF on the chart tells you almost nothing about what to do in the next hour. Two bedside questions do." Set up the 2×2.

Step 1 — Profile: congestion × perfusion 7 min

ASK: "Forget the EF for a second. At the bedside, how do you decide if this patient is WET, and separately whether they are COLD?"

Draw the Stevenson 2×2. Wet = congestion: orthopnea, JVD, edema, weight gain, B-lines on POCUS, S3. Cold = hypoperfusion: narrow pulse pressure (<25% of systolic), cool/mottled extremities, rising lactate and creatinine, mental slowing. Emphasize hypoperfusion is NOT the same as a low cuff pressure — a patient can be 110/95 and cold. Most ward admits are warm-and-wet and need diuresis alone. Cold-and-wet patients belong in a higher-acuity setting and may need an inotrope or vasodilator.

Pearl: The profile, not the EF, drives the first orders. A preserved-EF (HFpEF) patient who is warm-and-wet still gets IV loop diuretics.

Step 2 — Decongest with IV loops 8 min

ASK: "Your patient takes 80 of torsemide at home. What's your starting IV furosemide order, and how do you know in two hours whether it worked?"

Anchor on the DOSE trial: IV beats PO; bolus and infusion are equivalent; the high-intensity strategy (~2.5× the home oral dose, given IV) relieved symptoms faster with a transient, reversible creatinine bump. Convert home dose, give IV, and measure the response — a spot urine sodium > 50–70 mmol/L at 2 hours, or urine output > 100–150 mL/h, means you dosed adequately. If not, double the dose now rather than waiting a day. Inadequate despite a high loop dose → sequential nephron blockade with a thiazide (metolazone or IV chlorothiazide), with acetazolamide (ADVOR) and early SGLT2i as evidence-based add-ons. Track daily weights, strict ins-and-outs, BMP every 12–24 h for K and Cr.

Pearl: "Diuretic resistance" is usually under-dosing or too-infrequent dosing — not a true ceiling. Reassess and escalate the same day.

Step 3 — Start GDMT before discharge 7 min

ASK: "The patient is decongesting nicely on day 2. Which long-term medications should already be going in — and which one do you NOT start yet?"

For HFrEF, the four pillars: ARNI (or ACEi/ARB), evidence-based beta-blocker, MRA, and SGLT2 inhibitor. The STRONG-HF trial showed that rapid in-hospital initiation and up-titration with close follow-up improves outcomes; EMPULSE showed in-hospital SGLT2i is safe and adds clinical benefit. Start the SGLT2i and MRA early. Continue a chronic beta-blocker unless the patient is cold/hypoperfused — do NOT newly start or up-titrate a beta-blocker until the patient is euvolemic and off inotropes, because it is negatively inotropic. Mind the hold parameters: SBP, potassium, and renal function.

Pearl: The hospitalization is the intervention. Every pillar you start inpatient is one the outpatient world often never gets to.

Close — precipitant + retrieval (2 min)

Always name the precipitant before discharge (the FAILURE mnemonic). Hide the board; learners rebuild the 2×2, the DOSE dosing rule, and the four pillars from memory. Finish: "Which pillar do you hold in a cold patient, and why?" (beta-blocker — negative inotropy.)

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — warm & wet

72F with HFrEF (EF 30%) on furosemide 80 mg PO daily stopped her meds on vacation. BP 138/82, warm extremities, JVD to the jaw, 3+ edema, lungs with bibasilar crackles. Cr 1.1 (baseline 1.0).

Profile?Warm-and-wet — perfused but congested. The classic ward admission.
Starting diuretic order?IV furosemide ≥ her home daily dose, dosed for high intensity per DOSE: ~80 mg IV BID. Reassess natriuretic response at 2 h; double if UOP/urine Na inadequate.
What else by day 2?Confirm the precipitant (med nonadherence), restart/continue all four GDMT pillars, daily weights, low-Na diet teaching, 7-day follow-up.

Case 2 — cold & wet

64M, EF 20%, BP 88/72 (pulse pressure 16), cool mottled knees, lactate 3.8, Cr up from 1.3 to 2.1, somnolent. JVD elevated, anasarca.

Profile + disposition?Cold-and-wet — congested AND hypoperfused. Higher-acuity/ICU setting; this is cardiogenic-shock-spectrum.
Pitfall to avoid?Don't start or continue a beta-blocker, and don't diurese aggressively into a hypoperfused kidney without addressing output — may need an inotrope (e.g., dobutamine/milrinone) or vasodilator to restore perfusion so diuresis works. Cardiology involvement.

Case 3 — the creatinine bump

68F admitted warm-and-wet, down 4 L over 48 h, still with JVD and 2+ edema, good urine output, Cr risen from 1.0 to 1.4. The night team held her diuretic "for AKI."

Right call?No. She is still congested and making urine; the modest Cr rise is expected hemoconcentration/worsening renal function during effective decongestion and is not, by itself, a reason to stop. Resume diuresis, monitor electrolytes, continue toward euvolemia.

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. A 70-year-old man with HFrEF (EF 25%) on torsemide 40 mg PO daily is admitted with orthopnea, JVD, and 3+ edema. BP 130/80, warm extremities. What is the most appropriate initial diuretic strategy?

  1. Oral torsemide 40 mg daily continued unchanged
  2. IV furosemide at roughly 1–2.5× his home oral dose, reassessing response at 2 hours
  3. Fluid restriction alone for 24 hours before diuretics
  4. Low-dose dopamine to augment diuresis
  5. Immediate ultrafiltration
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. DOSE trial: IV dosing (bypassing gut edema) at an adequate, often high-intensity dose, with a measured natriuretic response, is first-line for the warm-and-wet patient. A: PO is unreliably absorbed when congested and his current regimen failed. C: delays decongestion with no benefit. D: low-dose 'renal' dopamine does not improve diuresis or outcomes (ROSE-AHF). E: ultrafiltration is reserved for true diuretic resistance, not first-line (CARRESS-HF showed no renal benefit).

2. Two days into effective IV diuresis, a still-congested HF patient making 150 mL/h of urine has a creatinine rise from 1.1 to 1.5 mg/dL. The patient feels much better. What is the best next step?

  1. Stop diuretics for acute kidney injury
  2. Give a 1 L normal saline bolus
  3. Continue diuresis toward euvolemia while monitoring electrolytes
  4. Start dopamine for renal protection
  5. Order urgent dialysis
Answer + why each distractor is wrongC. A modest creatinine rise during decongestion (hemoconcentration / WRF) in a patient still wet and making urine is expected and not an indication to stop — residual congestion predicts worse outcomes than this transient bump. A: stopping perpetuates congestion. B: fluid worsens the overload. D: no benefit. E: no indication.

3. A euvolemic, normotensive HFrEF patient is ready for discharge on day 4. He is on furosemide and lisinopril only. Which change most reduces his risk of death and rehospitalization?

  1. Add a non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker
  2. Initiate the remaining GDMT pillars (switch to ARNI, add beta-blocker, MRA, and SGLT2 inhibitor) with early follow-up
  3. Discharge now and defer all medication changes to the outpatient clinic
  4. Add an NSAID for his knee pain
  5. Double the furosemide indefinitely
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. STRONG-HF: rapid in-hospital initiation/up-titration of guideline therapy with close follow-up improves outcomes; in-hospital SGLT2i (EMPULSE) is safe and beneficial. A: non-DHP CCBs are negatively inotropic and contraindicated in HFrEF. C: deferral is the status quo that STRONG-HF improved upon — many patients never get titrated. D: NSAIDs cause sodium retention and worsen HF. E: chronic over-diuresis risks hypovolemia/AKI without disease modification.

4. Which finding best indicates hypoperfusion (a 'cold' profile) rather than simply a low blood pressure reading?

  1. Bibasilar crackles
  2. Narrow pulse pressure with cool extremities, rising lactate and creatinine
  3. Elevated BNP
  4. Pedal edema
  5. Jugular venous distension
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Hypoperfusion is defined by end-organ signs: narrow pulse pressure (<25% of systolic), cool/mottled skin, rising lactate/creatinine, altered mentation — independent of the cuff number. A, D, E are congestion (wet) signs; C reflects wall stress, not perfusion.

5. In a patient admitted with decompensated HFrEF who is warm and wet and already taking carvedilol at home, what should you do with the beta-blocker?

  1. Stop it immediately because it is negatively inotropic
  2. Continue it at the home dose while decongesting, since the patient is well-perfused
  3. Double the dose to control heart rate
  4. Switch to IV metoprolol
  5. Hold it until the day of discharge then restart at half dose
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. A chronic beta-blocker should be continued during decompensation in a warm (well-perfused) patient — abrupt withdrawal worsens outcomes. You avoid NEW initiation or up-titration until euvolemic, and you would only reduce/hold it if the patient becomes cold/hypoperfused. A/E: unnecessary withdrawal harms. C: up-titration during decompensation is unsafe. D: no role for routine IV beta-blockade here.

Take-Home Points

  1. Profile every patient on two axes — congestion and perfusion — before you reach for the EF; warm-and-wet gets IV loop diuretics.
  2. Dose IV loops to a measured natriuretic response (DOSE / spot urine Na at 2 h) and double promptly if inadequate; don't wait a day.
  3. A modest creatinine rise during effective decongestion is usually hemoconcentration — keep diuresing the still-wet patient who is making urine.
  4. Start the four GDMT pillars in-house (STRONG-HF, EMPULSE); hold beta-blocker initiation only while wet or cold.
  5. Discharge euvolemic with the precipitant named, GDMT documented, weight teaching, and 7-day follow-up to cut readmission.
