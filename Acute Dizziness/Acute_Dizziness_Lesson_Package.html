Learning Objectives

  1. Use timing and triggers (modified TiTrATE) to sort acute dizziness into four limited differential categories.
  2. Perform a bedside Dix-Hallpike maneuver to diagnose episodic, positionally-triggered dizziness (BPPV).
  3. Perform the HINTS exam to identify concerning features in continuous, spontaneous dizziness (acute vestibular syndrome).
  4. Construct a four-step history-and-exam process to separate benign peripheral from dangerous central causes.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Patients describe dizziness vaguely ("lightheaded," "woozy"). Why does the modern approach use timing and triggers instead of asking "what kind of dizziness?" Symptom-quality categories (vertigo / presyncope / disequilibrium / lightheadedness) are unreliable — patients give inconsistent, overlapping descriptions, and quality does not map cleanly to cause. Timing (episodic vs continuous, and how long) plus triggers (positional, spontaneous, trauma/toxin) is reproducible and points directly to the right bedside test. This is the TiTrATE approach (Newman-Toker, Neurol Clin 2015).
Q2. A patient has continuous vertigo for 2 days. The head impulse test shows a corrective (catch-up) saccade. Is that reassuring or concerning? Reassuring — in a patient with continuous (acute vestibular) syndrome, an abnormal head impulse (a catch-up saccade) localizes to the peripheral vestibular nerve and points away from stroke. The dangerous finding is a normal/intact head impulse (no saccade) in someone with ongoing vertigo. This is the counter-intuitive heart of HINTS.
Q3. Which is more dangerous to miss: a positive Dix-Hallpike, or a normal head impulse test in a patient with continuous vertigo? The normal head impulse. A positive Dix-Hallpike diagnoses benign BPPV. A normal head impulse in continuous vertigo is one of three HINTS findings that flag a central cause (posterior-circulation stroke) — time-sensitive and potentially fatal.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (Acute_Dizziness_Optimized.pptx) and click through the animated buttons, or reproduce the algorithm on a whiteboard. Each cursor icon on the board is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Framework only: ~20 min. With the three cases: +10–15 min. (Can be split into two 20-min talks: framework first, cases second.)

Hook (2 min)

"Two patients, same complaint: 'the room is spinning.' One has been spinning for 10 seconds every time she rolls over in bed; the other has been spinning continuously for two days. Same symptom — but they get completely different exams and one might be having a stroke." Ask the room which is which, then build the framework. (These become Cases 1 and 3.)

Objective 1 — Timing + Triggers narrow the field 6 min

ASK: "Before you touch the patient — what two questions sort dizziness into a short list?" Click "Timing," then "Trigger." Timing splits episodic (seconds–hours) vs continuous (hours–days); triggers split positional, spontaneous, and trauma/toxin.

The four working categories: (1) Episodic, positionally triggered (lasts seconds–minutes → BPPV, orthostasis); (2) Spontaneous episodic (minutes–hours → vestibular migraine, Ménière, vasovagal/panic, TIA); (3) Continuous from trauma/toxin (meds, barotrauma); (4) Continuous spontaneous = acute vestibular syndrome (vestibular neuritis vs stroke).

Episodic, positionalSeconds–minutesDix-Hallpike; orthostaticsBPPV; orthostatic hypotension
Spontaneous episodicMinutes–hoursHistory; Weber/RinneVestibular migraine, Ménière, vasovagal/panic, TIA

Pearl: Always probe cardiovascular risk factors, hearing loss/tinnitus, focal neuro symptoms, substance use, and medication changes — these reset your pre-test probability before the exam even begins.

Objective 2 — Dix-Hallpike for positional dizziness 6 min

For episodic + positionally-triggered symptoms, the Dix-Hallpike diagnoses posterior-canal BPPV (canalith debris in the semicircular canal). A positive test (transient upbeat-torsional nystagmus + vertigo on lying back with the head turned 45°) = BPPV. A negative test → check orthostatics and reconsider other causes.

ASK: "Your Dix-Hallpike is positive — what do you do right now?" Click to reveal: treat at the bedside with the Epley canalith-repositioning maneuver.
Pearl (2023 GRACE-3): For pc-BPPV diagnosed by a positive Dix-Hallpike, perform the Epley at the time of diagnosis — strong recommendation, moderate-certainty evidence. The half-somersault maneuver is a reasonable home option but is less effective than Epley in small head-to-head trials (better patient adherence is its trade-off). Test characteristics of Dix-Hallpike: sensitivity ~79%, specificity ~75% — better at ruling in than ruling out.

Objective 3 — HINTS for continuous spontaneous dizziness 8 min

Reserve HINTS for the patient with acute, persistent (continuous), spontaneous vertigo and nystagmus — the acute vestibular syndrome. It is not a test for triggered or episodic dizziness. Three components:

ComponentReassuring (peripheral)Concerning (central)
Head ImpulseCorrective catch-up saccade present (abnormal VOR = nerve problem)Normal/intact head impulse — no saccade (a brain problem)
I: NystagmusHorizontal, unidirectionalBidirectional (direction-changing) or vertical/torsional
Test of SkewNo vertical realignment on cover-uncoverVertical skew deviation (disconjugate)

Interpretation: ANY concerning finding → central (stroke/TIA) until proven otherwise. ALL three reassuring → peripheral (vestibular neuritis / labyrinthitis). The mnemonic INFARCT captures the dangerous trio: Impulse Normal, FAst-phase Alternating, Refixation on Cover Test.

ASK: "Your patient with 2 days of continuous vertigo has a normal head impulse and bidirectional nystagmus. MRI is read as negative at hour 4. Are you reassured?" Reveal: No. HINTS performed by a trained examiner out-performs early MRI-DWI, which misses up to ~15–20% of posterior-fossa strokes in the first 24–48 h (Kattah, Stroke 2009).
Pearl (2023 GRACE-3 caution): HINTS is only reliable in the correct patient (continuous AVS with nystagmus) and in trained hands. Applied to the wrong patient or by an untrained clinician it is inaccurate and can falsely reassure. Don't HINTS a patient without nystagmus or with merely positional symptoms.

Objective 4 — Put it together; don't reach for the CT 5 min

The four-step process: (1) Timing → (2) Triggers → (3) the matching targeted exam (Dix-Hallpike or HINTS) → (4) diagnosis/disposition.

Pearl (2023 GRACE-3): Strong recommendation against routine noncontrast head CT to distinguish central from peripheral causes — CT is insensitive for acute posterior-circulation ischemia. If a central cause is suspected, the right test is MRI with DWI (and MRA), plus stroke-pathway activation — not a reflexive CT.

Retrieval close (3 min)

Close the laptop. Ask learners to rebuild the algorithm from memory: the two sorting questions, the test that matches each branch, and the single most dangerous finding (normal head impulse in continuous vertigo). Then have them state the take-home points before you show them.

Cases Original cases, click-to-reveal

All three are 60-year-old women, former smokers with HTN/CAD on atorvastatin 80 and lisinopril 20 — identical vascular risk, different timing/triggers. That is the teaching point: the history, not the risk factors, drives the exam.

Case 1 — Episodic, positionally triggered

Two days of brief spinning (<1 min) triggered by turning her head, with nausea, interfering with daily activities. No hearing change, tinnitus, headache, vision change, weakness, or falls.

How do you categorize her symptoms?Episodic, positionally triggered.
What exam will you focus on?Dix-Hallpike maneuver.
Diagnosis?BPPV (positive Dix-Hallpike with transient upbeat-torsional nystagmus).
Management?Epley canalith-repositioning at the bedside (GRACE-3 strong rec); half-somersault as a home option; consider vestibular PT for refractory cases. No imaging needed.

Case 2 — Continuous, spontaneous (the dangerous one)

Sudden onset of persistent vertigo ~1 hour ago; cannot do anything because of severity. History of BPPV but couldn't do Epley at home this time. No hearing change, tinnitus, or vision change. Adds DM to her risk profile.

How do you categorize her symptoms?Continuous, spontaneous — acute vestibular syndrome.
What exam will you focus on?HINTS exam (she has continuous vertigo with nystagmus).
Diagnosis?Acute cerebrovascular accident — HINTS shows a central pattern (normal head impulse, bidirectional nystagmus, ± skew).
Management?Activate stroke pathway: MRI/MRA (not reflexive noncontrast CT to rule out — it is insensitive early), antiplatelet and statin, and — depending on time window, deficits, and local resources — IV thrombolysis or thrombectomy. (See the companion Ischemic CVA lesson for the full hyperacute algorithm.)

Case 3 — Continuous, spontaneous (the benign mimic)

Three days of continuous room-spinning, lying in bed unable to work, nausea without vomiting. Tried half-somersault and Epley at home without relief. No hearing change, tinnitus, headache, vision change, weakness, or falls.

How do you categorize her symptoms?Continuous, spontaneous — acute vestibular syndrome (same category as Case 2).
What exam will you focus on?HINTS exam.
Diagnosis?Vestibular neuritis — HINTS is entirely reassuring (abnormal head impulse with catch-up saccade, unidirectional horizontal nystagmus, no skew).
Management?Symptomatic: antiemetics and short-course vestibular suppressants/anticholinergics (limit to a few days to avoid impairing central compensation). Steroids only via shared decision-making within 3 days of onset (GRACE-3, weak/very-low-certainty); evidence rests on small trials with weak endpoints. Acute idiopathic sensorineural hearing loss does warrant steroids.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 58-year-old man reports 3 days of continuous vertigo, nausea, and unsteadiness. He has spontaneous left-beating horizontal nystagmus. On horizontal head impulse testing there is no corrective saccade in either direction. Cover-uncover shows a small vertical realignment. Which is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Reassure and discharge with meclizine
  2. Epley canalith-repositioning maneuver
  3. Noncontrast head CT
  4. MRI brain with DWI/MRA and stroke-pathway activation
  5. Audiometry
Answer + explanationD. A normal/intact head impulse (no catch-up saccade) plus a skew deviation in a patient with continuous vertigo is a central HINTS pattern — posterior-circulation stroke until proven otherwise. The right test is MRI-DWI/MRA, not CT. A falsely reassures a central pattern. B treats BPPV, which is positional/episodic, not continuous. C — noncontrast CT is insensitive for early posterior-fossa ischemia (GRACE-3 strong rec against using it to discriminate). E doesn't address the time-critical stroke concern.

2. A 34-year-old woman has recurrent 20-second episodes of spinning whenever she rolls over in bed or tips her head back, for one week. Between episodes she is asymptomatic. Neuro exam is normal. Which test best establishes the diagnosis?

  1. HINTS exam
  2. Dix-Hallpike maneuver
  3. MRI brain
  4. Orthostatic vital signs only
  5. Electronystagmography
Answer + explanationB. Brief, positionally-triggered, episodic vertigo is the classic profile of posterior-canal BPPV, diagnosed by Dix-Hallpike. A HINTS is for continuous vertigo with nystagmus and would be inappropriate (and inaccurate) here. C imaging is unnecessary with a typical history and normal exam. D orthostatics are a reasonable adjunct if Dix-Hallpike is negative, but won't diagnose BPPV. E is not a first-line bedside test.

3. The Dix-Hallpike in the patient above is positive with transient upbeat-torsional nystagmus. What is the best immediate management?

  1. Start meclizine scheduled for 4 weeks
  2. Perform the Epley maneuver now
  3. Refer to neurosurgery
  4. Prescribe a benzodiazepine
  5. Order an MRI of the internal auditory canals
Answer + explanationB. GRACE-3 gives a strong recommendation to perform Epley canalith-repositioning at the time of diagnosis for pc-BPPV. A/D chronic vestibular suppressants/benzodiazepines impair central compensation and don't treat the mechanical cause; meclizine for weeks is inappropriate. C/E are unnecessary for typical BPPV.

4. Which patient is the appropriate candidate for a HINTS exam?

  1. A patient with 15-second spinning episodes triggered by head movement
  2. A patient with lightheadedness on standing and a 25-mmHg orthostatic SBP drop
  3. A patient with continuous vertigo for 36 hours and spontaneous nystagmus
  4. A patient with brief presyncope and palpitations
  5. A patient with chronic imbalance and no nystagmus
Answer + explanationC. HINTS is validated only for the acute vestibular syndrome: continuous, spontaneous vertigo with nystagmus. A is positional/episodic (Dix-Hallpike). B is orthostatic hypotension. D suggests a cardiac/presyncopal cause. E — without nystagmus, HINTS is uninterpretable; GRACE-3 warns it is inaccurate when applied to the wrong patient.

5. A 47-year-old woman with continuous vertigo for 2 days has an entirely reassuring HINTS (abnormal head impulse with catch-up saccade, unidirectional horizontal nystagmus, no skew) and normal hearing. She is diagnosed with vestibular neuritis. Regarding corticosteroids, which statement is most accurate?

  1. Steroids are strongly indicated and clearly improve quality-of-life outcomes
  2. Steroids are contraindicated in vestibular neuritis
  3. Steroids may be offered via shared decision-making within 3 days of onset, but evidence is weak
  4. Steroids are only useful after 2 weeks of symptoms
  5. High-dose steroids for 6 weeks are standard of care
Answer + explanationC. GRACE-3 suggests shared decision-making on short-term steroids for vestibular neuritis presenting within 3 days — a conditional recommendation on very-low-certainty evidence (trials used weak endpoints like caloric recovery, not quality of life). A overstates the data; B is wrong (not contraindicated); D/E are not supported. Note: acute idiopathic sensorineural hearing loss is a clear steroid indication.

Take-Home Points

  1. Timing + triggers (modified TiTrATE) sort acute dizziness into four categories and tell you which bedside test to do — before you touch the patient.
  2. Episodic + positional → Dix-Hallpike; if positive (pc-BPPV), treat with Epley at the bedside.
  3. Continuous + spontaneous (with nystagmus) → HINTS; ANY concerning finding (normal head impulse, bidirectional/vertical nystagmus, skew) = central until proven otherwise.
  4. HINTS by a trained examiner out-performs early MRI-DWI for posterior stroke — but only in the right patient, and only with nystagmus present.
  5. Don't reach for noncontrast CT to exclude posterior-circulation stroke — it's insensitive (2023 GRACE-3 strong rec against).
