What is the KDIGO definition of AKI?	An abrupt drop in GFR, defined as ↑Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 h, OR ↑Cr ≥1.5× baseline within the prior 7 days, OR urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h. Characterize as non-oliguric, oliguric, or anuric.
Why is eGFR not meaningful during an AKI?	eGFR equations assume a steady-state (stable) GFR. In AKI, GFR is changing rapidly, so the reported eGFR does not reflect true kidney function.
What are the three anatomic categories of AKI?	Pre-renal (decreased effective arterial blood volume), intra-renal/intrinsic (glomerular, tubulointerstitial, vascular), and post-renal (obstruction).
How is pre-renal AKI diagnosed at the bedside?	Suggestive history (volume loss, low EABV), a bland urinalysis, no hydronephrosis on US, and — if oliguric — a FeNa <1% (FeUrea <35% is more reliable if the patient is on diuretics).
What three features point toward an intra-renal etiology?	"Active" urine sediment (supports intra-renal), an elevated FeNa while oliguric (rules out pre-renal), and a normal renal ultrasound (rules out post-renal).
What are the causes of decreased effective arterial blood volume (pre-renal)?	True hypovolemia, sepsis (distributive), heart failure (cardiorenal), and cirrhosis (hepatorenal). Also altered vasculature: renal vein thrombosis, renal venous hypertension (CHF), and intra-abdominal hypertension.
What is "vasogenic" pre-renal AKI and its classic drug causes?	A change in arteriolar tone reducing GFR: afferent constriction from NSAIDs and calcineurin inhibitors; efferent dilation from ACE inhibitors and ARBs.
List the three subcategories of intra-renal AKI.	Glomerular (glomerulonephritis), tubulointerstitial (acute tubular necrosis, acute interstitial nephritis, tubular obstruction), and vascular (MAHA/TTP-HUS, cholesterol emboli).
How is acute glomerulonephritis recognized on urinalysis?	Dysmorphic RBCs and/or RBC casts, proteinuria, leukocyturia, ± hypertension. GN is grouped into immune-complex, pauci-immune, and anti-GBM; crescentic forms = RPGN.
What is the most common intra-renal cause of AKI, and its urine finding?	Acute tubular necrosis (ATN) — ischemic (prolonged hypoperfusion) or toxic (including iodinated contrast). Urine shows muddy-brown granular casts.
What drugs and findings suggest acute interstitial nephritis (AIN)?	Antimicrobials (esp. beta-lactams), NSAIDs, PPIs, immunotherapy; also infection and systemic disease (Sjögren's, sarcoidosis). UA: pyuria, WBC casts, proteinuria; eosinophils are insensitive/nonspecific.
What causes tubular obstruction within the kidney?	Pigment/cast nephropathy (myoglobin, hemoglobin, light chains/paraproteins) and crystal nephropathy (uric acid, acyclovir, ethylene glycol, methotrexate).
How is post-renal AKI diagnosed and why look early?	Hydronephrosis on renal ultrasound. Rule it out early because obstruction is typically rapidly reversible once relieved.
Does unilateral ureteral obstruction cause AKI?	No — not unless it obstructs a single functioning kidney. AKI from ureteral obstruction requires bilateral obstruction (or obstruction of the only working kidney).
A patient on diuretics has a FeNa of 2% but seems volume-depleted. What test is more reliable?	Fractional excretion of urea (FeUrea); a value <35% supports a pre-renal state even when diuretics raise the FeNa.
A rhabdomyolysis patient (CK 20,000) has dipstick 3+ blood but no RBCs on microscopy. Mechanism of AKI?	Pigment (myoglobin) cast nephropathy — heme-pigment tubular obstruction/toxicity. Dipstick detects myoglobin as "blood" without RBCs. Treat with aggressive IV fluids and stop nephrotoxins (e.g., NSAIDs).
What are the management principles by AKI category?	Pre-renal → restore perfusion (volume/hemodynamics). Intra-renal → treat the cause, remove nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, contrast, ACEi/ARB), supportive care. Post-renal → relieve the obstruction.
In ATN that is now anuric, what fluid pitfall should you avoid?	Avoid overhydration — once anuric, aggressive fluids cause volume overload. Maintain euvolemia, monitor electrolytes, and avoid nephrotoxins.
