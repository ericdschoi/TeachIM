Learning Objectives

  1. Build a framework that differentiates the cause of AKI as pre-renal, intra-renal, or post-renal.
  2. Diagnose and manage common AKI presentations using that framework and the bedside tests (history, urinalysis, FeNa, renal ultrasound).

Pre-work send the day before

1. Give the KDIGO definition of AKI and explain why eGFR shouldn't be reported during one.

AKI = ↑Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 h, OR ↑Cr ≥1.5× baseline in 7 days, OR urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h. eGFR equations assume a steady-state GFR, which is false during AKI, so the number is invalid.

2. A patient is oliguric with a bland UA, normal renal US, and FeNa 0.3%. Which bucket, and why?

Pre-renal: bland sediment + no hydronephrosis + FeNa <1% while oliguric all point to a perfusion problem rather than intrinsic injury or obstruction.

3. Name the three intra-renal compartments and one classic urine finding for each.

Glomerular — dysmorphic RBCs / RBC casts; Tubular (ATN) — muddy-brown granular casts; Interstitial (AIN) — pyuria with WBC casts. (Vascular is the fourth sub-bucket: MAHA / cholesterol emboli.)

Teaching Script 25–35 min

Hook (2 min)

Ask the room: "How do you know a patient has an AKI?" Steer to an abrupt rise in Cr (often with falling urine output) and define it formally with KDIGO. Then drop the key point that anchors the whole talk: eGFR is meaningless during AKI because the equations assume a stable GFR.

ASK — "What's your framework for an AKI differential?" If they don't say pre/intra/post, introduce it and build from there.

Segment 1 — Pre- and post-renal first (Objective 1) 8 min

The board deliberately covers the simpler left side before the busy intra-renal middle. Pre-renal = low effective arterial blood volume; post-renal = obstruction.

BucketDiagnosed byCauses
Pre-renalHistory + bland UA + no hydronephrosis + FeNa <1% (if oliguric)Hypovolemia, sepsis, HF (cardiorenal), cirrhosis (hepatorenal); vasogenic (NSAIDs/CNIs constrict afferent; ACEi/ARB dilate efferent); altered vasculature (RVT, IAH)
Post-renalHydronephrosis on renal USUrethra (stricture, BPH), bladder (clot, malignancy, neurogenic), ureter (calculi, malignancy/LAN, retroperitoneal fibrosis)
Pearl: Unilateral ureteral obstruction does not cause AKI unless it's the single functioning kidney — you need bilateral obstruction. Rule out obstruction early; it's the most reversible cause.
ASK — "Your patient is on furosemide and the FeNa is 2%. Can you trust it?" (No — use FeUrea <35% on diuretics.)

Segment 2 — Intra-renal by compartment (Objective 1) 10 min

Break the intra-renal bucket anatomically: glomerular → tubulointerstitial → vascular.

CompartmentKey entitiesUrine clue
GlomerularGN — immune-complex, pauci-immune, anti-GBM; crescentic = RPGNDysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, proteinuria
TubularATN (ischemic / toxic incl. contrast) — most common; tubular obstruction (myoglobin, light chains, crystals)Muddy-brown granular casts
InterstitialAIN — beta-lactams, NSAIDs, PPIs, immunotherapy; infection; Sjögren's/sarcoidPyuria, WBC casts (eosinophils insensitive)
VascularMAHA (TTP/HUS, scleroderma renal crisis, malignant HTN, HELLP); cholesterol emboliSchistocytes on smear; ± eosinophilia (emboli)
ASK — "Septic patient, fluid-resuscitated, still rising Cr at 24 h, now anuric, muddy-brown casts. Diagnosis?" (Ischemic ATN.)

Segment 3 — Apply it (Objective 2) 10 min

Run the six cases below. For each, ask for the bucket, the diagnosis/etiology, and the next management step before revealing. Reinforce the management-by-bucket rule: pre-renal → perfusion; intra-renal → treat cause + pull nephrotoxins; post-renal → relieve obstruction.

Retrieval close (3 min)

Erase the board. Have learners rebuild the three buckets, the three discriminating tests, and the four intra-renal compartments from memory.

Cases click to reveal

Case 1 — 35F, 1 week of vomiting/diarrhea, low UOP

Cr 1.5 (baseline 0.7), BUN 45, FeNa <0.2%; UA hyaline casts; renal US normal.

Diagnosis & next step?

Pre-renal azotemia from hypovolemia (BUN:Cr >20, very low FeNa, bland UA). Control nausea and give aggressive rehydration.

Case 2 — 70M, worsening obstructive voiding after starting allergy meds

Cr 2.0 (baseline 0.8), BUN 40, FeNa 1.5%; UA normal; renal US bilateral moderate hydronephrosis.

Diagnosis & next step?

Post-renal AKI from BPH plus anticholinergic-induced retention. Stop the anticholinergic, start tamsulosin, and consider a urinary catheter to decompress.

Case 3 — 23-year-old transman, SLE off therapy, arthralgias + dark urine

Cr 3.6 (baseline 0.9), BUN 30, FeNa 2.8%; UA dysmorphic RBCs and RBC casts; renal US normal.

Diagnosis & next step?

Acute glomerulonephritis from a lupus flare. Induction therapy with steroids plus cyclophosphamide or mycophenolate (coordinate with nephrology/rheumatology).

Case 4 — 40F, day 5 of amoxicillin/clavulanate, rash, low UOP

Cr 2.5 (baseline 0.9), BUN 30, FeNa 2.2%; UA proteinuria, WBCs & WBC casts, no eosinophils; renal US normal.

Diagnosis & next step?

Acute interstitial nephritis from the beta-lactam. Discontinue the antibiotic, repeat labs in ~3 days, and consider biopsy ± steroids if not improving. (Absence of urine eosinophils does not exclude AIN.)

Case 5 — 42M, DM2, pneumonia with severe sepsis, rising Cr despite fluids, now anuric

Cr 3.4 (2.5 on admission, baseline 1.2), FeNa 2.3%; UA muddy-brown casts; renal US normal.

Diagnosis & next step?

Ischemic ATN from prolonged hypoperfusion (hypovolemia + sepsis). Monitor electrolytes, maintain euvolemia, avoid overhydration now that he's anuric, and avoid nephrotoxins.

Case 6 — 36M, diffuse myalgias + dark urine after a new intense workout

Cr 3.6 (baseline 1.2), BUN 30, FeNa 3%, CK 20,000; dipstick 3+ blood with no RBCs on micro; renal US normal.

Diagnosis & next step?

Pigment (myoglobin) tubular obstruction from rhabdomyolysis, plus a hemodynamic hit from NSAIDs. Dipstick reads myoglobin as "blood" without RBCs. Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation and stop the NSAIDs.

Board-Style MCQs click for answer

1. A 68-year-old man on chronic furosemide presents with AKI. He looks dry. Cr 2.4 (baseline 1.1), FeNa 2.5%, BUN:Cr 28, bland UA, no hydronephrosis. Which test best clarifies the etiology?

  1. Repeat FeNa
  2. Fractional excretion of urea
  3. Renal biopsy
  4. Urine eosinophils
  5. CK
Answer

B. Diuretics drive sodium wasting and falsely raise FeNa, so it can't distinguish pre-renal here. FeUrea <35% supports a pre-renal state on diuretics. The clinical picture (dry, high BUN:Cr, bland UA) already favors pre-renal; the others don't address the diuretic confounder.

2. A 75-year-old woman with new anuria and suprapubic fullness has Cr 3.0 (baseline 1.0). What is the single most important first test?

  1. FeNa
  2. Renal ultrasound
  3. Urine protein:creatinine
  4. ANA
  5. Renal biopsy
Answer

B. Abrupt anuria with suprapubic fullness screams obstruction — renal US shows hydronephrosis (and a distended bladder), and relief is curative. Rule out the reversible post-renal cause before pursuing intrinsic workup.

3. A 30-year-old develops AKI 5 days into a course of a PPI and an NSAID, with low-grade fever, a rash, pyuria, and WBC casts. Which is most likely?

  1. Acute tubular necrosis
  2. Pre-renal azotemia
  3. Acute interstitial nephritis
  4. Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis
  5. Post-renal obstruction
Answer

C. Drug exposure (NSAID, PPI), fever, rash, sterile pyuria, and WBC casts are classic AIN. A shows muddy-brown casts without the allergic features. D shows dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts. B/E have bland sediment. Stop the offending drug; consider steroids if no improvement.

4. A 50-year-old man has AKI after a CT with iodinated contrast and a stormy ICU course with hypotension. UA shows muddy-brown granular casts; FeNa 3%. Best description?

  1. Pre-renal azotemia
  2. Acute tubular necrosis
  3. Acute interstitial nephritis
  4. Glomerulonephritis
  5. Obstructive nephropathy
Answer

B. Ischemic plus toxic (contrast) injury with muddy-brown granular casts and an elevated FeNa while injured is ATN. Pre-renal would have FeNa <1% and bland urine; the other options have distinct sediment or imaging findings. Supportive care; remove ongoing nephrotoxins.

5. Which statement about evaluating AKI is correct?

  1. eGFR accurately reflects function during AKI
  2. A unilateral obstructed ureter reliably causes AKI
  3. FeNa <1% in an oliguric patient suggests a pre-renal state
  4. Absence of urine eosinophils excludes AIN
  5. Renal US is unnecessary if the FeNa is elevated
Answer

C. A low FeNa while oliguric supports avid sodium retention (pre-renal). A is false — eGFR assumes steady state. B is false — you need bilateral obstruction (or a single functioning kidney). D is false — eosinophils are insensitive and nonspecific. E is false — always rule out obstruction with US.

Take-Home Points

  1. AKI = an abrupt rise in Cr (KDIGO thresholds); eGFR is meaningless during AKI.
  2. Three buckets: pre-renal (↓EABV), intra-renal (glomerular / tubulointerstitial / vascular), post-renal (obstruction).
  3. Three quick discriminators: history + bland UA + FeNa <1% (if oliguric) → pre-renal; active sediment → intra-renal; hydronephrosis on US → post-renal.
  4. Rule out obstruction early with renal US — it's the most reversible cause; unilateral obstruction needs the single functioning kidney to cause AKI.
  5. ATN (ischemic/toxic) is the most common intra-renal cause; remove nephrotoxins and maintain euvolemia.
