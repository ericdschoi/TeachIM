Three required criteria for ALF?	Acute liver injury (<26 wks, no chronic liver disease) + coagulopathy (INR >1.5) + hepatic encephalopathy.
What separates ALF from acute liver injury?	Hepatic encephalopathy.
Exceptions allowing ALF despite preexisting liver disease?	HBV, autoimmune hepatitis, Wilson's disease.
Most common ALF cause in the US?	Acetaminophen (drugs/toxins ~50%).
Two categories causing >50% of ALF?	Drugs/toxins and viral hepatitis.
Does alcohol cause ALF?	No — but it can worsen another insult (e.g., acetaminophen).
West Haven HE grades I–IV?	I altered sleep-wake/attention; II lethargy + asterixis; III stupor/gross disorientation; IV coma.
Empiric drug for all ALF?	N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — improves transplant-free survival even in non-APAP ALF.
When is NAC benefit greatest in non-APAP ALF?	Early-stage disease with low-grade (I–II) hepatic encephalopathy.
Why avoid correcting INR in ALF?	INR is a key prognostic marker; reverse only for active bleeding (vitamin K is ok).
Why minimize IV fluids in ALF?	Can precipitate/worsen cerebral edema (even with rising creatinine).
Ammonia threshold predicting intracranial hypertension?	Arterial ammonia >150–200 µmol/L.
HE grade prompting intubation?	Grade III–IV (airway protection).
King's College Criteria for acetaminophen ALF?	pH <7.3, OR all of: INR >6.5, Cr >3.3 mg/dL, grade III–IV HE.
King's College Criteria for non-APAP ALF?	INR >6.5, OR ≥3 of: age <10/>40; HAV/HBV/drug; jaundice >7 d before HE; INR >3.5; Tbili >18.
Wilson's clue in acute ALF?	ALP:total-bilirubin ratio <4, young age, Coombs-negative hemolysis; ceruloplasmin may be normal acutely.
Most important ALF disposition step?	Early transfer to a liver-transplant center before progression precludes safe transfer.
