Learning Objectives

  1. Define acute liver failure (ALF) and distinguish it from acute liver injury (ALI).
  2. Evaluate a patient with ALF and identify the most common causes by category.
  3. Build a framework for the initial ICU management of ALF — complications, NAC, prognostication, and transfer.

Pre-work send the day before

Three retrieval questions to prime the session. Answers hidden — try first, then check.

1. What three criteria, all required, define acute liver failure?

(1) Acute liver injury (onset <26 weeks, no chronic liver disease — except HBV, AIH, Wilson’s), (2) coagulopathy (INR >1.5), and (3) hepatic encephalopathy. The encephalopathy is what converts "acute liver injury" into "acute liver failure."

2. What are the two most common causes of ALF in the US, and which single drug leads?

Drugs/toxins and viral hepatitis (>50% combined). Acetaminophen is the single most common cause in the US/Europe (~50% of cases). Alcohol alone does NOT cause ALF.

3. One empiric drug should be started in essentially every ALF patient. What is it, and why even in non-acetaminophen cases?

N-acetylcysteine (NAC). It replenishes glutathione in acetaminophen toxicity and improves transplant-free survival even in non-acetaminophen ALF, especially early-stage disease with low-grade encephalopathy.

Teaching Script restructured into timed segments

Hook (0:00–0:02)

"A previously healthy 29-year-old comes in with AST in the 9,000s and an INR of 2.3 — but they're awake and oriented. Do they have acute liver failure? Not yet. The moment they get confused on hospital day 2, everything changes — the clock, the ICU, the transplant call." The talk turns on that single distinction and what to do once you cross it.

ASK — "What's the one finding that separates acute liver injury from acute liver failure?" Let them arrive at hepatic encephalopathy.

Objective 1 — Definition (0:02–0:08) 6 min

ALF is rare but lethal (20–80% mortality by etiology). Reveal the three required criteria one at a time:

CriterionDetail
1. Acute liver injury↑AST/ALT and bilirubin; onset <26 weeks; no chronic liver disease (exceptions: HBV, autoimmune hepatitis, Wilson’s).
2. CoagulopathyINR >1.5 (no anticoagulant). Marker of synthetic dysfunction and a key prognostic factor.
3. Hepatic encephalopathyUsually the last to appear; the criterion that defines ALF. Graded I–IV (West Haven); higher grade = higher cerebral-edema risk.
Pearl. West Haven grades: I = altered sleep-wake/attention; II = lethargy + asterixis; III = somnolence/stupor, gross disorientation; IV = coma. Grade III–IV warrants intubation for airway protection.

Objective 2 — Etiologies & evaluation (0:08–0:14) 6 min

ASK — "Give me a differential by category, most to least common in the US." Then reveal each category and its work-up.
CategoryCausesInitial work-up
Drugs/toxins (~50%)Acetaminophen (lead cause), supplements, Amanita mushrooms; statins/AEDs/antibioticsAPAP & salicylate level, tox screen, med/supplement review vs LiverTox
ViralHepatitis A, B, D, E; rarely HSV/VZVViral hepatitis serologies + PCRs; HIV (co-transmission risk)
Ischemic/vascularBudd-Chiari, hepatic artery thrombosis, shock liverAssess for shock/hypoperfusion; liver US with Doppler
OtherAutoimmune hepatitis, HELLP / acute fatty liver of pregnancy, Wilson’sANA, anti-smooth-muscle Ab, IgG; pregnancy test; ceruloplasmin, 24-h urine copper; liver biopsy if unrevealing
Pearl. Alcohol does NOT cause ALF, though it can worsen another insult (e.g., acetaminophen). For Wilson’s in a young patient, an alkaline phosphatase : total-bilirubin ratio <4 is a clue; ceruloplasmin can be normal acutely.

Objective 3 — Management (0:14–0:24) 10 min

All ALF goes to the ICU. Four steps:

ASK — "What complications kill ALF patients, and which one makes us avoid IV fluids and avoid correcting the INR?"
StepKey actions
1. Address complicationsCerebral edema — q1–2h neuro checks, intubate for grade III–IV HE, head-of-bed >30°, hypertonic saline/mannitol, hyperventilate if intubated, minimize IVF; arterial ammonia >150–200 flags high ICH risk. Hypoglycemia — q1–2h glucose, dextrose infusion. Multiorgan failure — vent/pressors/RRT; TTE to exclude cardiogenic shock. Coagulopathy — vitamin K ok; give FFP/factors ONLY for active bleeding (preserves INR’s prognostic value). Infection — high risk; some give prophylactic abx/antivirals.
2. Give NACStart empirically in all ALF — improves transplant-free survival even in non-acetaminophen cases (greatest in early/low-grade HE). Low threshold for acyclovir if febrile/HSV suspected.
3. PrognosticateWith hepatology, apply King’s College Criteria (± MELD) to identify those unlikely to recover spontaneously → transplant candidates.
4. TransferSend to a transplant-capable center EARLY — before progression precludes safe transfer.
Pearl. Two counterintuitive ALF rules: don’t reflexively correct the INR (it’s your prognostic compass, and these patients are often not truly "auto-anticoagulated"), and go easy on IV fluids even with rising creatinine — volume can worsen cerebral edema.

King’s College Criteria reference

Acetaminophen ALFNon-acetaminophen ALF

Retrieval close (0:24–0:25) 1 min

Board off: "Rebuild the three ALF criteria, the top two etiology categories, the one empiric drug, and the two counterintuitive ICU rules." Fill gaps from what they reconstruct.

Currency Note (2026)

The standing teaching is current per AASLD acute-liver-failure practice guidance. These refinements are surfaced in the HTML and on the appended deck slide:

  • NAC nuance: the transplant-free survival benefit in non-acetaminophen ALF is greatest in EARLY-stage disease with low-grade (I–II) HE; benefit is not established in advanced (III–IV) HE (Lee et al., Gastroenterology 2009). Still give NAC empirically to all ALF.
  • Cerebral edema marker: arterial ammonia >150–200 µmol/L predicts intracranial hypertension and severe HE.
  • Prognostication: MELD is increasingly used alongside (sometimes instead of) King’s College Criteria. KCC is specific but not sensitive — a negative KCC should NOT defer transplant-center transfer.

Cases original cases · sub-questions click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Acetaminophen

29-year-old with recurrent pancreatitis, taking 1000 mg acetaminophen q4h, presents with 24 h of nausea, vomiting, anorexia, RUQ pain. Exam: stable, alert & oriented ×4, no asterixis. Labs: AST 9290, ALT 4550, ALP 118, Tbili 2.3, INR 2.3. On HD2: confused, oriented to self only, asterixis.

At presentation, what is the diagnosis?

Acute liver INJURY — not ALF. Despite massive transaminases and INR 2.3, there is no encephalopathy at presentation, so the third criterion isn’t met. On HD2 the new confusion/asterixis completes the triad → acute liver failure.

Which test is NOT part of the ALF work-up?

Ethanol level — ethanol does not cause ALF. Acetaminophen level, liver US with Doppler, and viral hepatitis serologies are all appropriate.

Which lab represents synthetic dysfunction, and what is the best next step?

The elevated INR reflects synthetic dysfunction. Best next step: administer NAC (improves transplant-free survival), alongside the rest of the work-up.

Case 2 — Fulminant hepatitis B with rising ICP

35-year-old with ALF from fulminant hepatitis B; initially grade 1 HE, normal head CT. On HD2: new nausea/vomiting unresponsive to antiemetics. VS: HR 58, BP 160/88, RR 20, SpO2 94% on 2L. Exam: somnolent but arousable, oriented to self, mild asterixis. Labs: glucose 180; ABG 7.2/54/80/23; ammonia 202.

What is the next step in evaluation?

Repeat head CT to evaluate for elevated ICP. The bradycardia + hypertension (Cushing reflex) and ammonia >200 predict intracranial hypertension. CT shows early cerebral edema without midline shift or herniation.

What is the most appropriate next step in management?

Treat intracranial hypertension: q1h neuro checks, hypertonic saline or mannitol, elevate head of bed >30°, consider direct ICP monitoring. Do not reflexively give FFP (reverse INR only for bleeding); acyclovir only if febrile/HSV suspected.

Case 3 — Shock liver

69-year-old with CHF, HTN, T2DM admitted with abdominal pain and fatigue. VS: HR 145, BP 82/52. Exam: moderate distress, cold extremities, oriented to self only, asterixis. Labs: Cr 2.4 (baseline 0.9), ALT 3600, AST 5600, INR 2.2; troponin <0.04, BNP 2400; TTE LVEF 10–20%. Hepatitis serologies negative.

Most likely cause of the elevated LFTs?

Cardiogenic shock → shock liver (ischemic hepatitis). The hypotension, cold extremities, and severely reduced LVEF point to hypoperfusion; massive AST/ALT with AST > ALT is classic for ischemic injury.

Next step in management?

Vasopressors/inotropy to restore perfusion — treat the shock state driving the liver injury. Paracentesis and INR reversal are not indicated here.

Case 4 — Non-APAP ALF meeting KCC

59-year-old, ESRD s/p kidney transplant on chronic immunosuppression, presents to a small community hospital with fever, chills, and ALF. VS: T 38.8 °C, HR 98, BP 117/58. Exam: jaundiced, lethargic, mild asterixis. Labs: WBC 13K, AST 2300, ALT 1960, Tbili 22, INR 4.2, Cr 1.8. APAP level and urine tox negative.

Besides NAC, what treatment is warranted while awaiting results?

Empiric acyclovir — the patient is febrile and immunosuppressed; cover HSV until the PCR returns. (Not Tylenol, not prednisone, not charcoal.)

How do you risk-stratify and where should this patient be cared for?

Apply King’s College Criteria (or MELD) with hepatology. This patient meets non-APAP KCC (INR >6.5? no — but ≥3 of: age >40, drug/viral, jaundice before HE, INR >3.5, Tbili >18 — here INR 4.2, Tbili 22, age >40 = 3 criteria) → high risk for transplant. Transfer early to a liver-transplant-capable center. Use LiverTox to review hepatotoxic medications/supplements.

Board-style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 24-year-old woman presents 30 h after an acetaminophen overdose. AST 6200, ALT 5800, INR 2.1, Cr 1.0, alert and fully oriented, no asterixis. Which statement is correct?

  1. She meets criteria for acute liver failure
  2. She has acute liver injury; she will meet ALF criteria only if encephalopathy develops
  3. NAC is not indicated this late after ingestion
  4. FFP should be given to correct the INR
  5. Alcohol co-ingestion is the likely driver
Answer & explanation

B. Without hepatic encephalopathy she has acute liver injury, not failure. A wrong: HE is required. C wrong: NAC is still indicated in established acetaminophen hepatotoxicity regardless of timing. D wrong: correct the INR only for active bleeding — it’s a prognostic marker. E wrong: alcohol does not cause ALF.

2. A patient with ALF and grade III encephalopathy has worsening somnolence. Arterial ammonia is 210 µmol/L. Which intervention is most appropriate to address the leading concern?

  1. Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation
  2. Fresh frozen plasma to normalize INR
  3. Hypertonic saline and head-of-bed elevation >30°
  4. Lactulose titrated to 6 stools/day as sole therapy
  5. Immediate hemodialysis for ammonia clearance
Answer & explanation

C. Ammonia >150–200 with worsening HE signals intracranial hypertension/cerebral edema — treat with hypertonic saline/mannitol, HOB >30°, q1h neuro checks, and airway protection. A wrong: minimize IVF — volume worsens cerebral edema. B wrong: don’t correct INR without bleeding. D wrong: lactulose alone is inadequate for ICH in ALF. E wrong: RRT is used for renal failure/severe metabolic derangement, not as first-line ammonia clearance here.

3. Which patient with ALF should be referred to a transplant center earliest?

  1. A 30-year-old with acetaminophen ALF, arterial pH 7.36, INR 3.0, grade I HE
  2. A 45-year-old with acetaminophen ALF, arterial pH 7.25 after resuscitation
  3. A 28-year-old with acute liver injury, INR 1.4, no encephalopathy
  4. A 50-year-old with stable chronic hepatitis C cirrhosis
  5. A 35-year-old with mild transaminitis and normal INR
Answer & explanation

B. In acetaminophen ALF, an arterial pH <7.3 after adequate resuscitation alone meets King’s College Criteria — a strong predictor of death without transplant. A wrong: pH normal and the full alternative KCC set isn’t met. C/E wrong: acute injury without HE is not ALF. D wrong: stable chronic cirrhosis is a different pathway, not ALF.

4. A 26-year-old presents with ALF; ALP 90, total bilirubin 28 mg/dL (ALP:Tbili <4), Coombs-negative hemolytic anemia, and a low uric acid. Which diagnosis should you pursue?

  1. Autoimmune hepatitis
  2. Wilson’s disease
  3. Hepatitis A
  4. Budd-Chiari syndrome
  5. Acetaminophen toxicity
Answer & explanation

B — Wilson’s disease. Young patient, very high bilirubin with a low alkaline phosphatase (ALP:Tbili ratio <4), Coombs-negative hemolysis, and low uric acid are classic for acute Wilsonian ALF. Send ceruloplasmin (may be normal acutely), 24-h urine copper, and slit-lamp exam. The other options don’t fit this constellation.

5. Which statement about NAC in acute liver failure is most accurate?

  1. NAC is only beneficial in acetaminophen-induced ALF
  2. NAC should be withheld until the etiology is confirmed
  3. NAC improves transplant-free survival in non-acetaminophen ALF, especially with low-grade encephalopathy
  4. NAC is contraindicated once encephalopathy develops
  5. NAC reliably reverses established cerebral edema
Answer & explanation

C. NAC improves transplant-free survival in non-acetaminophen ALF, with the greatest benefit in early-stage disease and low-grade HE; it should be started empirically in all ALF. A/B/D wrong: give it early and broadly. E wrong: NAC does not treat cerebral edema.

Take-Home Points

  1. ALF = acute liver injury (<26 wks, no chronic liver disease*) + coagulopathy (INR >1.5) + hepatic encephalopathy. HE is what makes it "failure." (*exceptions: HBV, AIH, Wilson’s.)
  2. Drugs/toxins (esp. acetaminophen) and viral hepatitis cause >50%; also screen ischemic, autoimmune, pregnancy-related, and Wilson’s. Alcohol alone does NOT cause ALF.
  3. ICU care: manage complications (cerebral edema, hypoglycemia, multiorgan failure, coagulopathy, infection); reverse coagulopathy only for bleeding; minimize IV fluids.
  4. Start NAC early in all ALF — benefit extends to non-acetaminophen cases.
  5. Prognosticate with King’s College Criteria (± MELD) and TRANSFER to a transplant center early.
