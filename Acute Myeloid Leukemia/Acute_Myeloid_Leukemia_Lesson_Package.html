Learning Objectives

  1. Identify normal bone-marrow differentiation and where the pathologic mutation sits in AML.
  2. Diagnose AML using common clinical and laboratory findings.
  3. Recognize and initiate management of the four emergent complications of AML.
  4. Bonus: Understand the basics of risk stratification and treatment.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What two things are required to diagnose AML? (1) ≥20% blasts in bone marrow or peripheral blood, and (2) those blasts are of myeloid origin (Auer rods, MPO positivity, or myeloid immunophenotype). Certain AML-defining genetic abnormalities make the diagnosis regardless of blast count — and under WHO/ICC/ELN 2022 the 20% threshold no longer applies when such a lesion is present.
Q2. Name the four life-threatening complications of AML. Leukostasis, tumor lysis syndrome (TLS), disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), and febrile neutropenia. A generalist often sees these before heme/onc does — early recognition saves lives.
Q3. What empiric antibiotic do you start for febrile neutropenia, and what defines it? Anti-pseudomonal monotherapy — cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam. Febrile neutropenia = a single oral temp >38.3°C (or ≥38.0°C sustained ≥1 h) with ANC <500 (or expected to fall below 500 within 48 h). Add vancomycin only for a suggestive source (line, skin/soft tissue, pneumonia) or instability.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the interactive board (Acute_Myeloid_Leukemia_Optimized.pptx) and click through the navigation tabs (Epidemiology → Pathology → Diagnosis → Complications → Management → Cases), or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. Each cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt. Modular — Complications alone is a great short talk. Core ~20–30 min. Print the learner handout so learners take notes.

Hook (2 min)

"Untreated, how long does someone with AML live?" Days to weeks. "And with our best treatment?" Only about 1 in 3 reach five years. The gap between those two numbers — and the fact that a general medicine team usually sees these patients first — is why this talk exists.

Epidemiology (optional) 3 min

~1% of new cancers (~20k/yr), a disease of the elderly (median age 68, ~59% >65). Rare — but the complications are emergencies.

Objective 1 — Pathophysiology 5 min

ASK: "Walk the marrow tree — where's the lesion in AML?" Click Normal → AML. Hematopoietic stem cell → myeloid vs lymphoid; myeloid stem cell → myeloblast → leukocytes (also RBCs, platelets). AML = overproliferation of myeloid stem cells OR myeloblasts.
ASK: "Given that, what CBC will you see?" Reveal that it depends where the lesion sits:
PatternWhy
Isolated leukocytosisMyeloblast affected → ↑WBC (very high or very low depending on mutation)
Leukocytosis + cytopeniasBlasts crowd out / disrupt other lines (e.g., via IL-6); blast % doesn't have to track with cytopenias
PancytopeniaMyeloid stem cell affected → proportionate decline across lines

Objective 2 — Diagnosis 6 min

Diagnostic criteria — both required: (1) ≥20% blasts in marrow aspirate OR peripheral blood, and (2) myeloid origin (Auer rods, MPO+, or myeloid immunophenotype). An AML-defining cytogenetic abnormality [t(6;9), core-binding factor] is diagnostic regardless of blast count.
2022 update: WHO, ICC, and ELN 2022 dropped the ≥20% requirement when an AML-defining genetic lesion is present (e.g., PML::RARA, CBF), and made NPM1-mutated AML a distinct entity (≥10% blasts). This is the deck's "several nuanced exceptions," now formalized.
ASK: "Which CBC findings should make you think AML?" Reveal the line-by-line:
LineFindingsExam clue
WBCMedian 15k; hyperleukocytosis >100k in ~10–20%; leukopenia in ~25%; blasts on smear; Auer rods diagnosticGingival hyperplasia, leukemia cutis, Sweet syndrome
HgbAnemia common; anemia + thrombocytopenia → think DICConjunctival/palmar pallor
PlateletsThrombocytopenia in nearly all; ~25% present with plt <25kPetechiae, purpura

Objective 3 — The four critical complications 8 min

ASK: For each clinical vignette on the board, have learners name the complication, its diagnosis, and first management step before revealing.
ComplicationRecognizeDiagnoseTreat
LeukostasisHeadache, vision change, dyspnea, confusion (neuro + respiratory)Clinical; WBC >100k (or >50k + symptoms)Cytoreduction (chemo/hydroxyurea) ± leukapheresis. No leukapheresis in APL.

Pearls: Leukapheresis is contraindicated in APL (worsens coagulopathy). Avoid acetaminophen in hospitalized neutropenic patients — it can mask the fever that may be the only sign of impending sepsis.

Bonus — Risk & treatment 4 min

ELN sorts patients by cytogenetics + NGS into favorable / intermediate / adverse — and the spread is dramatic: ~46% vs ~18% vs ~4% 5-year OS. Treatment is systemic only (no surgery/radiation backbone), so the framework is induction (7+3 = 7 days cytarabine + 3 days anthracycline; Ven/HMA for unfit/elderly; ATRA/arsenic for APL) → consolidation (allogeneic SCT or further chemo) → maintenance, with salvage/targeted therapy or hospice for relapsed/refractory disease.

2022–25 update: NGS increasingly drives targeted therapy — quizartinib added to 7+3 for FLT3-ITD+ disease (QuANTUM-First; FDA 2023; OS 31.9 vs 15.1 mo); ivosidenib+azacitidine for newly-diagnosed unfit IDH1-mutated AML (AGILE); olutasidenib for relapsed/refractory IDH1; oral azacitidine (Onureg) as maintenance (QUAZAR); CPX-351 for therapy-related / myelodysplasia-related AML.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Have the group rebuild: the diagnostic criteria, the three CBC patterns, and the four complications with one management step each — then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases 8 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Fever and pancytopenia

50-year-old with 6 months of worsening fatigue and recent malaise. T 38.5°C. CBC: WBC 2.3 (ANC 450), Hgb 9.2, plt 72.

What is the diagnosis?Neutropenic fever — a single oral T >38.3°C (or ≥38°C sustained ≥1 h) with ANC <500. Start empiric cefepime or pip-tazo and an infectious workup. Marrow ultimately shows favorable-risk AML; being young and favorable, he'd likely get 7+3.

Case 2 — Bleeding and Auer rods

62-year-old with 6 months of fatigue and new gingival bleeding. WBC 2.3 (ANC 320), Hgb 7.2, plt 16. Smear shows Auer rods and schistocytes.

What does the smear show, and what does it mean?Auer rods (→ AML) and schistocytes (→ intravascular hemolysis). The combination with pancytopenia is worrisome.
What additional workup?Coagulation panel, fibrinogen, D-dimer. These return: ↑PT/INR and PTT, ↑D-dimer, low fibrinogen → DIC. The likely diagnosis is APL.
Next step in management?Supportive transfusions, then ATRA + arsenic for APL. APL has an excellent long-term prognosis — the acute phase is the dangerous window. (Do not leukapherese.)

Case 3 — Confusion, vision changes, very high WBC

75-year-old with altered mentation, headache, blurry vision, and 30-lb weight loss. WBC 153k (23% blasts), Hgb 8.2, plt 152. BMP: K 6.2, Cr 1.5, Ca 6.9, phos 5.8; uric acid 10.2.

Cause of the confusion and vision changes?Leukostasis — AML patients are at risk with WBC >50k; seen in 10–20% of new diagnoses.
What do the electrolytes suggest, and what else to check?Tumor lysis syndrome (↑K, ↑phos, ↓Ca, ↑uric acid) — check/confirm uric acid; TLS occurs in ~10% of patients with leukostasis.
Management?Leukostasis → consider leukapheresis + start cytoreduction. TLS → IV fluids, allopurinol, ± rasburicase. For his AML: at age >75 he's unlikely to tolerate 7+3 — Ven/azacitidine is the typical choice (favorable cytogenetics may still not warrant transplant).

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 70-year-old presents with fatigue and bruising. WBC 30k with 28% circulating blasts; smear shows cells with Auer rods. Which finding would confirm acute myeloid leukemia regardless of the blast percentage?

  1. Hemoglobin of 7 g/dL
  2. A core-binding factor rearrangement, e.g., t(8;21) or inv(16)
  3. Platelet count <25k
  4. Splenomegaly
Answer & explanationB. AML-defining genetic abnormalities — core-binding factor rearrangements [t(8;21), inv(16)], t(6;9), PML::RARA — are diagnostic of AML regardless of blast count, and under WHO/ICC/ELN 2022 the ≥20% threshold is waived in their presence. Anemia (A), thrombocytopenia (C), and splenomegaly (D) are common but nonspecific and do not establish the diagnosis.

2. A patient with newly diagnosed AML has WBC 180k and presents with confusion and hypoxia with diffuse pulmonary infiltrates. Which is the most appropriate immediate intervention?

  1. Packed red blood cell transfusion to improve oxygen delivery
  2. Cytoreduction with leukapheresis and/or hydroxyurea/chemotherapy
  3. Diuresis with furosemide
  4. Empiric heparin for presumed PE
Answer & explanationB. This is leukostasis (neuro + respiratory symptoms with hyperleukocytosis). Treatment is urgent cytoreduction — leukapheresis and/or hydroxyurea/induction chemotherapy. RBC transfusion (A) increases viscosity and can worsen leukostasis — avoid until the WBC is reduced. Diuresis (C) doesn't address viscosity and risks volume depletion in a patient who may also have TLS. Heparin (D) is wrong and dangerous given thrombocytopenia/possible DIC. (If APL is suspected, do NOT leukapherese.)

3. A patient newly started on induction chemotherapy for AML develops K 6.4, phosphorus 6.8, uric acid 11, calcium 7.0, and a rising creatinine. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. IV fluids and rasburicase
  2. Calcium gluconate and oral calcium supplementation
  3. Phosphate binders alone
  4. Hold all therapy and observe
Answer & explanationA. This is tumor lysis syndrome — aggressive IV fluids plus rasburicase (for a markedly elevated uric acid with renal compromise) are first-line. Repleting calcium (B) is generally avoided in TLS unless the patient is symptomatic, because it can precipitate calcium-phosphate in the setting of hyperphosphatemia. Binders alone (C) are insufficient for this severity. Observation (D) risks fatal arrhythmia and AKI.

4. A 58-year-old receiving AML induction has T 38.6°C and ANC 200 with no localizing symptoms. Blood pressure is normal. Which empiric regimen is most appropriate?

  1. Cefepime monotherapy
  2. Vancomycin monotherapy
  3. Ceftriaxone plus azithromycin
  4. No antibiotics; repeat the temperature in 4 hours
Answer & explanationA. Febrile neutropenia requires immediate empiric anti-pseudomonal coverage — cefepime (or piperacillin-tazobactam) monotherapy is standard. Vancomycin (B) is not adequate Gram-negative/pseudomonal coverage and is added only for a suggestive source (line, SSTI, pneumonia) or instability. Ceftriaxone/azithromycin (C) lacks pseudomonal coverage. Delaying antibiotics (D) in neutropenic fever can be rapidly fatal — door-to-antibiotic time matters.

5. A 38-year-old with AML has pancytopenia, schistocytes, prolonged PT/PTT, elevated D-dimer, and low fibrinogen; the smear shows promyelocytes with Auer rods. Which statement is correct?

  1. Leukapheresis should be performed urgently for cytoreduction
  2. This is likely APL; start ATRA without waiting for genetic confirmation
  3. DIC here is treated definitively with heparin
  4. The prognosis is uniformly poor
Answer & explanationB. The picture — DIC plus promyelocytes/Auer rods — strongly suggests acute promyelocytic leukemia; because early death is from coagulopathy, start ATRA immediately on clinical suspicion, before confirmatory PML::RARA testing returns. Leukapheresis (A) is contraindicated in APL — it worsens the coagulopathy. DIC management (C) is supportive (cryoprecipitate/fibrinogen, platelets) plus treating the leukemia, not heparin. APL prognosis (D) is actually excellent with treatment once the acute phase is survived.

Take-Home Points

  1. AML is a rare but high-mortality cancer of the elderly: untreated survival is days to weeks; with treatment only ~1 in 3 reach 5 years.
  2. It arises from aberrant overproliferation of myeloid precursors that disrupts the other cell lines — expect any CBC pattern.
  3. Diagnose with ≥20% myeloid-origin blasts in marrow or blood (Auer rods, MPO+, immunophenotype) — or an AML-defining genetic abnormality regardless of blast count (WHO/ICC/ELN 2022).
  4. Know the four emergencies cold — leukostasis, TLS, DIC, febrile neutropenia — because the generalist usually sees them first.
