Learning Objectives

  1. Distinguish the physiology of primary, secondary, and tertiary adrenal insufficiency.
  2. Know when to test and how to interpret cortisol levels (and the ACTH stimulation test) to diagnose AI.
  3. Apply the basic principles and initial dosing of exogenous hormone replacement, including crisis management.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Why is primary AI more likely to cause adrenal crisis than central (secondary/tertiary) AI? Primary (adrenal) AI loses BOTH cortisol and aldosterone. The aldosterone (mineralocorticoid) deficiency causes salt wasting → hypovolemia, hyperkalemia, and hyponatremia, which precipitate hemodynamic collapse. Central AI spares the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone axis (aldosterone is regulated by RAAS, not ACTH), so mineralocorticoid function is preserved.
Q2. What 8 AM cortisol values rule AI in versus out, and what do you do with values in between? A very low morning cortisol rules it in; a clearly high value rules it out; indeterminate values require an ACTH (cosyntropin) stimulation test. The exact numeric cutoff is assay-dependent (see the update section).
Q3. A hypotensive patient you suspect has adrenal crisis needs a definitive cortisol/ACTH work-up. Do you wait for results before treating? No. Adrenal crisis is treated empirically with stress-dose hydrocortisone and IV fluids immediately; you can draw cortisol/ACTH first if it doesn't delay therapy, but never withhold steroids awaiting confirmation. (Dexamethasone is an option if you must preserve a pending stim test, as it doesn't cross-react in the cortisol assay.)

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the interactive presenter board (AdrenalInsufficiency_Optimized.pptx) and click through the AI / CAUSES / TEST / TREATMENT buttons, or reproduce the HPA + RAAS schematic on a whiteboard. Advance the opening animations to build the normal HPA and RAAS axes first. Print the learner handout so residents take notes.

Hook (2 min)

"A patient on chronic prednisone is admitted septic and becomes refractory to fluids and pressors. Their adrenals can't mount a stress response — and the fix is a cheap, fast dose of hydrocortisone." Frame AI as a diagnosis you must treat before you can confirm.

Objective 1 — Physiology & the three levels 7 min

ASK: "Build the HPA axis with me: hypothalamus → ? → ? → ?" — CRH → ACTH → cortisol, with negative feedback. Separately, the RAAS axis (kidney JGA → renin → angiotensin → aldosterone) controls mineralocorticoid tone independent of ACTH.
LevelLesionACTHAldosterone / RAAS
Primary (adrenal)Adrenal cortexHighLost → salt wasting, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia, hypovolemia (also hyperpigmentation from ↑ACTH/MSH)
Secondary (pituitary)Pituitary ACTHLow/normalPreserved (RAAS intact)
Tertiary (hypothalamic)Hypothalamic CRHLow/normal (low even after CRH)Preserved
ASK: "Name causes at each level." — Primary: autoimmune adrenalitis (most common in resource-rich settings), infectious adrenalitis (TB, fungi, syphilis), metastases/tumor, hemorrhage, drugs (azoles, etomidate, mitotane, checkpoint inhibitors). Central: mass lesions/craniopharyngioma/metastases, radiation, infiltrative (hemochromatosis, sarcoid), infection, infarction (Sheehan), trauma/surgery — and, by far most common overall, exogenous glucocorticoids (tertiary).
Pearl — drug causes: Etomidate transiently suppresses adrenal cortisol synthesis even after a single intubation dose; azoles and mitotane inhibit steroidogenesis; rifampin/phenytoin/phenobarbital accelerate cortisol metabolism. And immune checkpoint inhibitors now cause both hypophysitis (central) and primary adrenalitis.

Objective 2 — Diagnosis 7 min

ASK: "When do you test, and what's the first test?" — test when the clinical picture (fatigue, weight loss, hypotension, hyponatremia/hyperkalemia, or refractory shock) fits. First test: an 8 AM serum cortisol (cortisol is highest in the morning).

Interpretation (board values, older assay): <3 µg/dL rules AI in; <10 is suspicious; >18 rules it out. Indeterminate → ACTH (cosyntropin) stimulation test: 250 µg cosyntropin IV/IM, cortisol at baseline and 30–60 min; a subnormal peak confirms AI. Then ACTH level localizes: high ACTH = primary; low/normal = central. See the update section for modern assay-specific cutoffs (~14–15 µg/dL).

Pearl — early central AI can pass a stim test: Recent-onset central AI (e.g., just after pituitary surgery) may give a normal cosyntropin response because the adrenals haven't yet atrophied. If suspicion is high, recheck or use a different test, and treat empirically.

Objective 3 — Treatment & crisis 7 min

SettingReplacement
Primary AI (chronic)Hydrocortisone 20–25 mg/24h (glucocorticoid, split dosing, larger AM dose) + fludrocortisone 0.1 mg qAM (mineralocorticoid) ± DHEA 25–50 mg qAM
Central AI (chronic)Hydrocortisone 15–20 mg/24h split — no fludrocortisone needed (RAAS intact)
Adrenal crisisHydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus → 200 mg/24h (or 50 mg IV q6h) + aggressive IV fluids; treat empirically
ASK: "Your patient with known AI is having surgery / a serious illness — what changes?" — Stress dosing: increase glucocorticoid for physiologic stress (sick-day rules: double/triple oral dose; IV hydrocortisone for major stress). Under-replacement during stress is what precipitates crisis. Every AI patient needs an emergency injectable hydrocortisone kit and a medical-alert ID.
Pearl — at stress doses, you don't need separate mineralocorticoid: Hydrocortisone >~50 mg/day provides enough mineralocorticoid activity, so fludrocortisone can be held during high-dose stress dosing and resumed as the hydrocortisone is tapered.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild: the three levels and which one loses aldosterone, the cortisol/stim-test pathway, and the one-line crisis treatment.

Guideline Update Assay-specific cortisol cutoffs & GC-induced AI

The standing physiology and crisis teaching are unchanged and correct. Two practice refinements to surface:

TopicCurrent guidance
Cortisol cutoffs are assay-specificThe classic 8 AM "rule-out" ≥18 µg/dL and ACTH-stim peak ≥18 µg/dL derive from older polyclonal assays. Modern specific monoclonal immunoassays and LC-MS/MS read ~20–30% lower, so the rule-out / peak threshold is ~14–15 µg/dL. Apply your lab's assay-specific cutoff.
Most common causeExogenous glucocorticoids (tertiary AI). The 2024 ESE/Endocrine Society joint guideline gives a structured approach to diagnosing and safely tapering glucocorticoid-induced AI.
Adrenal crisisTreat empirically: hydrocortisone 100 mg IV then 200 mg/24h (or 50 mg q6h) + IV fluids — do not wait for confirmatory testing. Provide every AI patient an emergency hydrocortisone kit and medical-alert ID.

ESE/ES joint guideline on glucocorticoid-induced AI, JCEM 2024. Clinician-reviewed before teaching.

Practice Cases NEW — within original scope, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Refractory shock

A 58-year-old on chronic prednisone 10 mg daily for polymyalgia rheumatica is admitted with pneumonia and becomes hypotensive, not responding to fluids and norepinephrine. Na 130, K 4.2, glucose 64.

What kind of AI is most likely, and why?Tertiary (HPA-axis suppression from chronic exogenous glucocorticoids) — the most common cause of AI overall. RAAS is intact, so potassium is near-normal here despite the crisis.
What is the immediate management?Stress-dose hydrocortisone (100 mg IV, then 200 mg/24h) plus continued fluids — empirically, before any confirmatory testing. Refractory septic shock in a steroid-exposed patient is adrenal crisis until proven otherwise.

Case 2 — Hyperpigmentation and salt craving

A 34-year-old woman has months of fatigue, weight loss, salt craving, and darkening of palmar creases and buccal mucosa. Na 128, K 5.6, 8 AM cortisol 2 µg/dL.

Primary or central? What confirms it?Primary AI — hyperpigmentation (high ACTH/MSH) plus hyperkalemia and hyponatremia signal mineralocorticoid loss. A morning cortisol of 2 rules AI in; a high ACTH localizes it to the adrenal. Check 21-hydroxylase antibodies for autoimmune adrenalitis.
What replacement does she need?Hydrocortisone (split, AM-weighted) and fludrocortisone 0.1 mg qAM (she lacks aldosterone) ± DHEA. Counsel on sick-day stress dosing and an emergency injection kit.

Case 3 — The indeterminate cortisol

A 47-year-old with fatigue has an 8 AM cortisol of 9 µg/dL. ACTH is pending.

What is the next step?An indeterminate morning cortisol needs an ACTH (cosyntropin) stimulation test: 250 µg IV/IM, cortisol at baseline and 30–60 min. Interpret the peak against your lab's assay-specific cutoff (~14–15 µg/dL with modern specific assays, vs the older 18).
The peak is 16 µg/dL on a modern LC-MS/MS assay. AI or not?Adequate — a peak ≥~14–15 µg/dL on a specific modern assay rules out AI. Using the old 18 cutoff here would over-diagnose AI. This is exactly why the assay matters.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 40-year-old woman with fatigue, hyperpigmentation, hyponatremia, and hyperkalemia is found to have a low cortisol and a high ACTH. Which additional hormone deficiency explains her electrolyte abnormalities?

  1. Growth hormone
  2. Aldosterone
  3. Thyroxine
  4. Antidiuretic hormone
Answer & explanationB. Primary AI destroys the entire adrenal cortex, so aldosterone (a mineralocorticoid governed by RAAS) is lost along with cortisol. Aldosterone deficiency causes salt wasting → hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, and hypovolemia. This is why primary AI causes crisis more readily than central AI, which spares aldosterone. The other hormones (A, C, D) do not produce this electrolyte pattern.

2. A patient on chronic high-dose prednisone is admitted with hypotension and a serious infection. Which statement about their adrenal axis is correct?

  1. They have primary adrenal insufficiency with hyperkalemia
  2. They have tertiary (HPA-suppression) AI with an intact RAAS axis
  3. Fludrocortisone is the cornerstone of acute management
  4. Hyperpigmentation is expected
Answer & explanationB. Exogenous glucocorticoids suppress the hypothalamic–pituitary axis (tertiary AI) — the most common cause of AI. Aldosterone (RAAS-driven) is preserved, so significant hyperkalemia is not expected (A) and fludrocortisone is not the acute priority (C). Hyperpigmentation (D) requires high ACTH, which is suppressed here. Treat with stress-dose hydrocortisone and fluids.

3. A hypotensive patient with suspected adrenal crisis needs a cosyntropin stimulation test, but you must treat now. Which steroid lets you start treatment without invalidating the pending test?

  1. Hydrocortisone
  2. Prednisone
  3. Dexamethasone
  4. Methylprednisolone
Answer & explanationC. Dexamethasone does not cross-react in the serum cortisol assay, so it treats the crisis while preserving the ability to perform a stimulation test shortly after. Hydrocortisone (A) is the usual crisis drug but is measured as cortisol and would confound the test. Prednisone and methylprednisolone (B, D) also interfere with cortisol assays. Regardless of choice, never delay treatment for testing.

4. A patient's 8 AM cortisol is 16 µg/dL measured on a modern monoclonal (specific) immunoassay. How should this be interpreted regarding adrenal insufficiency?

  1. Diagnostic of AI because it is below 18 µg/dL
  2. Likely sufficient — modern specific assays use a lower (~14–15 µg/dL) threshold
  3. Requires an insulin tolerance test regardless
  4. Indicates Cushing syndrome
Answer & explanationB. Newer specific monoclonal immunoassays and LC-MS/MS read ~20–30% lower than the older polyclonal assays that gave the historical 18 µg/dL cutoff, so ~14–15 µg/dL is an appropriate rule-out threshold. A value of 16 on such an assay is reassuring. Applying the old cutoff (A) over-diagnoses AI. There is nothing here to suggest a need to escalate to insulin tolerance testing (C) or Cushing (D).

5. A patient with established primary adrenal insufficiency on hydrocortisone and fludrocortisone is scheduled for major abdominal surgery. What is the most appropriate perioperative steroid plan?

  1. Continue the usual home dose unchanged
  2. Hold all steroids the morning of surgery
  3. Give stress-dose hydrocortisone (e.g., 100 mg IV then 50 mg q6h or 200 mg/24h)
  4. Switch to fludrocortisone only
Answer & explanationC. Major physiologic stress requires stress-dose glucocorticoid; under-replacement precipitates adrenal crisis. Continuing the home dose (A) or holding steroids (B) is dangerous. At stress doses of hydrocortisone the mineralocorticoid effect is adequate, so a mineralocorticoid-only plan (D) is wrong. Resume the home regimen as the patient recovers.

Take-Home Points

  1. Primary AI causes adrenal crisis more readily than central AI because it also loses aldosterone (mineralocorticoid) → hypovolemia, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia. Central AI spares the RAAS axis.
  2. Diagnose with an 8 AM cortisol — very low rules in, clearly high rules out; indeterminate values need a cosyntropin stimulation test, then ACTH to localize. Cutoffs are assay-specific (~14–15 µg/dL with modern assays, vs the older 18).
  3. Adrenal crisis (hypotension/shock ± fever, abdominal pain, nausea, fatigue) is an emergency: give stress-dose hydrocortisone and IV fluids immediately, before confirmatory testing.
