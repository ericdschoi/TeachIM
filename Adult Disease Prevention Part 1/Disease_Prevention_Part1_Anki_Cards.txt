Four conditions that make a disease worth screening for?	(1) Common in the population; (2) a good test exists (acceptable sensitivity/specificity, low-cost, easy, low-harm); (3) a detectable asymptomatic period; (4) early treatment beats late. Bottom line: benefits must outweigh harms/cost.
What patient factors modify the decision to screen?	Comorbidities and life expectancy, patient preference, and whether diagnosis/treatment would actually be pursued if positive. Individualize by age, sex, and risk factors.
Five domains of adult disease prevention?	Cancer, Infectious Disease, Cardiovascular, Psychosocial, and Family/Safety.
Which cancers have established screening?	Only five: breast, colon, cervical, lung, and prostate.
Breast cancer screening (USPSTF 2024)?	All average-risk women ages 40–74, biennial mammography (Grade B). Stop at >74 or life expectancy <10 years. Clinical/self breast exams no longer routine.
Colon cancer screening (USPSTF)?	Average-risk adults 45–79 (45–49 Grade B; 50–75 Grade A; 76–85 Grade C, selective). Options: colonoscopy q10y, FIT/gFOBT q1y, stool FIT-DNA q3y, CT colonography or sigmoidoscopy q5y. Colonoscopy is the gold standard (needed if any other test is positive).
Cervical cancer screening — USPSTF 2025 update?	Ages 21–29: cytology alone every 3 years. Ages 30–65: high-risk HPV PRIMARY screening every 5 years is now PREFERRED; self-collected (patient-collected) HPV samples are endorsed for the first time. Alternatives 30–65: cytology q3y or HPV+cytology co-test q5y. (ACS starts at 25.)
When can cervical cancer screening stop?	After 65 with adequate normal prior screening (within 10 years), or after hysterectomy with cervix removal and no history of high-grade lesion/cervical cancer.
Lung cancer screening criteria?	Adults 50–80 with ≥20 pack-year history who currently smoke or quit <15 years ago (Grade B). Annual low-dose CT. Stop if quit >15 years or limited life expectancy. Medicare requires a documented shared-decision-making visit before the first scan.
How do you calculate pack-years?	Packs per day × years smoked (e.g., 1 ppd × 20 years = 20 pack-years).
Prostate cancer screening?	Individualized PSA-based screening in men 55–69 (Grade C, shared decision-making) — discuss harms (false positives, biopsy complications, overdiagnosis/overtreatment). Stop after 70. Digital rectal exam is NOT recommended for screening.
Adult immunizations everyone should receive?	Influenza annually; COVID-19 (at least each fall); Tdap once then Td/Tdap every 10 years.
Pneumococcal vaccination in adults (2024 ACIP)?	PCV21 for all adults ≥50, and adults 19–49 with chronic conditions/immunocompromise. If PCV15 is used instead, follow with PPSV23 (~1 year later).
Hepatitis B and HPV vaccination in adults?	Hep B: universal for adults 19–59 (≥60 only if risk factors). HPV: ages 19–26 (preferred), or up to 45 with shared decision-making.
Shingles and RSV vaccination?	Recombinant zoster (Shingrix): age ≥50, 2 doses 2–6 months apart. RSV: one dose for adults ≥75 (and 60–74 at increased risk) via shared decision-making (high relative efficacy but NNT >700).
Infection screening recommended for all adults?	HIV once for ages 15–65; hepatitis C once for ages 18–79. Others (syphilis, gonorrhea/chlamydia, hepatitis B, latent TB) are risk-based.
Gonorrhea/chlamydia screening — who?	All sexually active women <24 (Grade B); older women with risk factors (new/multiple partners, partner with an STI or concurrent partners, inconsistent barrier use).
Why does lung cancer screening uniquely require a shared-decision-making visit?	Medicare mandates a documented SDM conversation (eligibility, decision aid, counseling on radiation/overdiagnosis/false positives and on smoking cessation) before the first low-dose CT — a response to direct-to-consumer CT advertising.
