Learning Objectives

  1. Describe the disease and test characteristics that make screening worthwhile.
  2. Use a framework (five domains) for prevention and screening in primary care.
  3. Apply current cancer-screening recommendations and adapt them to individual patients.
  4. Identify appropriate infectious-disease screening and immunizations.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Name the four conditions that make a disease worth screening for. Common in the population; a good test exists (acceptable sensitivity/specificity, low-cost, easy, low-harm); a detectable asymptomatic period; and early treatment beats late. Net benefit must outweigh harms and cost.
Q2. List the only five cancers with recommended screening and one population detail for each. Breast (women 40–74, q2y), colon (45–79), cervical (21–65), lung (50–80, ≥20 pack-years, quit <15y), prostate (men 55–69, shared decision).
Q3. What 2025 change did the USPSTF make to cervical cancer screening? For ages 30–65, primary HPV testing every 5 years is now preferred, and the USPSTF endorsed self-collected (patient-collected) HPV samples for the first time. Ages 21–29 stay on cytology q3y.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the interactive board (Disease_Prevention_Part1_Optimized.pptx) and click through each reveal — every cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt. Print the learner handouts beforehand. Content is important but can be dry — prompt learners to fill in guidelines before you click. Talk: ~20–30 min; cases: +10–15 min.

Hook (2 min)

"More screening isn't better care — some of what we order does nothing but generate false positives and biopsies." Ask: which cancers actually have screening, and which ones don't? Park the list — there are only five, and you'll build them all.

Objective 1 — When to screen 3 min

ASK: "What makes a disease worth screening for?" Take answers, then reveal disease vs test characteristics.

Screen when the disease is common, has an asymptomatic detectable window, and early treatment beats late; and the test is accurate, low-cost, easy, and low-harm. Bottom line: benefits > risks/harms/cost. Always weigh comorbidities, life expectancy, and whether you'd act on a positive result.

Objective 2 — A framework 2 min

Five domains of adult prevention: Cancer, Infectious Disease, Cardiovascular, Psychosocial, Family/Safety (CV/psychosocial/family-safety are covered in Part 2). Prioritize by age, sex, and risk factors. When you can't keep guidelines straight, use the USPSTF tool — and remember societies often disagree.

Pearl — Colonoscopy is the gold standard because any other positive test needs a colonoscopy to diagnose. For breast, clinical/self exams are no longer routine. For lung, lung-cancer screening is the only cancer screen that requires a documented shared-decision-making visit for Medicare coverage — and if they still smoke, cessation matters more than the scan.
Pearl — Prostate: the message is not "we never check a PSA." Individualize 55–69 with shared decision-making — PSA increases detection but a clear mortality benefit hasn't been shown, against real harms (false positives, biopsy complications, overtreatment).

Beyond the five, counsel lifestyle risk reduction: avoid tobacco, limit alcohol, healthy weight/diet, physical activity, sun protection, and STI prevention.

Objective 4 — Immunizations & infection screening 5 min

ASK: "Which vaccines does everyone get, and which are age- or risk-based?"

Everyone: influenza annually; COVID-19 (at least each fall); Tdap once then Td/Tdap q10y. Age/condition-based: HPV 19–26 (preferred) or up to 45 with SDM; Hep B universal 19–59 (≥60 if risk factors); PCV21 for adults ≥50 (and 19–49 with chronic disease/immunocompromise) — or PCV15 → PPSV23; recombinant zoster ≥50 (2 doses); RSV for ≥75 (and 60–74 at higher risk) via SDM.

InfectionScreen whom
HIVOnce for all ages 15–65 (more if at risk)
Hepatitis COnce for all ages 18–79
Gonorrhea/chlamydiaSexually active women <24; older women with risk factors
Syphilis / Hep B / latent TBRisk-based (MSM, HIV, IVDU, incarceration, high-prevalence origin, congregate settings)

Retrieval close (2 min)

Cover the board. Have learners rebuild: the five screenable cancers with start ages, the cervical 2025 update, and the "everyone" vaccines.

Cases original cases — sub-questions click-to-reveal

Case 1 — 54-year-old woman, current smoker, establishing care

54F, HTN on amlodipine, 1 ppd since age 19 (not ready to quit), normal colonoscopy 4 years ago, mammogram 2 years ago, unsure of last (always-normal) Pap, sexually active with husband, no family cancer history.

What cancer screening is recommended?(1) Breast — bilateral screening mammogram (due, q2y). (2) Lung — LDCT after a shared-decision-making discussion (age 54, ~35 pack-years, current smoker). (3) Cervical — primary hrHPV or HPV+cytology co-test. (Colon not yet due.)
How would you counsel her on lung screening?She qualifies (50–80, ≥20 pack-years, current smoker). Document a shared-decision-making visit (eligibility, decision aid, radiation/overdiagnosis/false-positive counseling) and emphasize tobacco cessation.

Case 2 — 32-year-old woman, routine physical

32F, hypothyroid on levothyroxine, IUD in place, last Pap 3 years ago (normal, no HPV testing done), unknown family cancer history.

What cervical screening is recommended now?She's 30–65: primary hrHPV testing (preferred) or HPV+cytology co-test. (Her last test was cytology-only 3 years ago.)
Her cytology returns LSIL with negative HPV — next step?Repeat testing in 1 year (LSIL with negative HPV is low-risk; per ASCCP, repeat in 12 months rather than immediate colposcopy).

Case 3 — 56-year-old man, homeless, no care in 8 years

56M, no PMH, ½ ppd ×20 years (10 pack-years), quit alcohol 2 years ago, no family cancer history, not currently sexually active.

What cancer screening is recommended?Colon cancer (age 45–79) — offer FIT, gFOBT, or FIT-DNA (or colonoscopy). Discuss prostate screening (55–69 shared decision) — but with limited life expectancy/competing needs, likely recommend against at this time. Lung: 10 pack-years does NOT meet the ≥20 pack-year threshold → not indicated.
What else matters for this patient?Risk-based infection screening (HIV, HCV, syphilis, Hep B, latent TB given homelessness/congregate exposure) and catch-up immunizations. Address social determinants and follow-up access.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. A healthy 41-year-old woman at average risk asks about mammography. Per the 2024 USPSTF recommendation, what do you advise?

  1. No screening until age 50
  2. Annual mammography starting now
  3. Biennial mammography starting now (age 40)
  4. Shared decision-making, screening optional in her 40s
  5. Breast MRI annually
Answer & explanationC. In 2024 the USPSTF moved to recommend biennial mammography for all average-risk women 40–74 (Grade B), replacing the prior "individualized in the 40s" stance (D, the old recommendation). A delays screening unnecessarily. B (annual) is offered by some societies but the USPSTF interval is every 2 years. E (MRI) is reserved for high-risk patients.

2. A 35-year-old woman at average risk is due for cervical cancer screening. Per the USPSTF 2025 update, which is now the preferred strategy?

  1. Cytology alone every 3 years
  2. HPV + cytology co-testing every 5 years
  3. Primary high-risk HPV testing every 5 years (clinician- or self-collected)
  4. Annual cytology
  5. No screening until age 40
Answer & explanationC. For ages 30–65, the 2025 USPSTF update makes primary hrHPV testing every 5 years the preferred strategy and, for the first time, endorses self-collected HPV samples. A and B remain acceptable alternatives but are not "preferred." D over-screens; E is wrong (screening continues through 65).

3. Which patient meets criteria for lung cancer screening with low-dose CT?

  1. 52-year-old, 10 pack-years, current smoker
  2. 60-year-old, 25 pack-years, quit 18 years ago
  3. 58-year-old, 30 pack-years, quit 5 years ago
  4. 82-year-old, 40 pack-years, current smoker, metastatic pancreatic cancer
  5. 45-year-old, 30 pack-years, current smoker
Answer & explanationC. Criteria: age 50–80, ≥20 pack-years, and currently smoking or quit within 15 years. C fits. A fails the ≥20 pack-year threshold; B quit >15 years ago; D has a limited life expectancy (don't screen); E is younger than 50.

4. A 58-year-old man asks about prostate cancer screening. What is the most appropriate approach?

  1. Order PSA and a digital rectal exam routinely
  2. Do not discuss PSA — screening is not recommended
  3. Shared decision-making about PSA (men 55–69), discussing benefits and harms
  4. Start PSA only after age 70
  5. PSA every year is mandatory
Answer & explanationC. For men 55–69 the USPSTF gives a Grade C — individualize via shared decision-making (PSA increases detection but mortality benefit is uncertain against real harms). A is wrong: DRE is not recommended for screening. B overstates (the message isn't "never check a PSA"). D is backwards (stop, not start, at 70). E ignores shared decision-making.

5. Which vaccine recommendation is correct for an average-risk 52-year-old adult in 2025?

  1. PPSV23 alone is preferred
  2. PCV21 (single dose) is appropriate for adults ≥50
  3. Pneumococcal vaccination isn't indicated until age 65
  4. Hepatitis B is not recommended at this age
  5. Zoster vaccine is a single dose
Answer & explanationB. Per 2024 ACIP, PCV21 (a single dose, no follow-on needed) is appropriate for all adults ≥50 (the age was lowered from 65). A is wrong (PPSV23 alone isn't preferred; if PCV15 is used it's followed by PPSV23). C reflects the old age threshold. D is wrong — Hep B is universal for 19–59. E is wrong — recombinant zoster is a 2-dose series.

2024–2025 Practice Update NEW — projected on the appended deck slide

The deck is the June-2025 build, so most content is already current: breast (40–74 q2y, 2024 USPSTF), colon (45–79), lung (50–80/20py/15y), prostate (55–69 Grade C), PCV21 for ≥50 (2024 ACIP), and universal Hep B 19–59 were all verified. The practice-relevant addition since the deck's review:

  • USPSTF cervical cancer screening (finalized 2025), Grade A: ages 21–29 cytology alone every 3 years (unchanged); ages 30–65 high-risk HPV primary screening every 5 years is now preferred, and the USPSTF endorses self-collected (patient-collected) HPV samples for the first time (clinician- or patient-collected accepted). Alternatives at 30–65 remain cytology q3y or HPV+cytology co-test q5y.
  • Caveats: ACS still begins at 25; ASCP/CETC have raised concerns about treating self-collected and clinician-collected samples as equivalent and about the 5-year interval for self-collection. Confirm your institution's protocol.
  • RSV (reinforced): CDC now recommends RSV vaccine for all adults ≥75 and for 60–74 at increased risk (shared decision-making) — narrower than the original "≥60 SDM" framing.

Take-Home Points

  1. Screening pays off only when the disease is common with a detectable asymptomatic phase, early treatment beats late, and the test is accurate, cheap, and low-harm — and benefits exceed harms and cost.
  2. Use the five-domain framework and individualize by age, sex, risk, comorbidities, and life expectancy.
  3. Only five cancers have screening: breast (40–74 q2y), colon (45–79), cervical (21–65), lung (50–80, 20 pack-yr, quit <15y), prostate (55–69, shared decision).
  4. Immunizations: PCV21 for ≥50; RSV for ≥75 (and 60–74 at risk) via SDM; Hep B universal 19–59; flu/COVID/Tdap for all.
  5. 2025: USPSTF endorses self-collected HPV and prefers primary HPV q5y at 30–65. Use the USPSTF tool and know societies differ.
