Learning Objectives

  1. Apply the five-domain prevention framework in primary care.
  2. Screen for substance use and mental-health conditions.
  3. Apply cardiovascular screening — lipids, diabetes, AAA, obesity, hypertension.
  4. Identify patients at risk for intimate partner violence, falls, and osteoporosis (plus family planning and advance-care planning).

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Which three substance-use domains should you screen all adults for, and with what question for alcohol? Alcohol, tobacco, and unhealthy drug use. For alcohol, the Single Alcohol Screening Question: "How many times in the past year have you had 5 (4 for women) or more drinks in a day?"
Q2. Who gets one-time AAA ultrasound screening? Men 65–75 who have ever smoked (one-time abdominal ultrasound). Selective in never-smoking men in that range; not recommended for women.
Q3. What CV-risk calculator does the 2026 ACC/AHA guideline now recommend, and why? PREVENT (ages 30–79) — it replaces the Pooled Cohort Equations, which overestimated 10-year risk by roughly 40–50%.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the interactive board (Disease_Prevention_Part2_Optimized.pptx) and click through each reveal. Print the learner worksheet. Prompt learners to fill in guidelines before you click. Talk: ~30 min; cases: +10–15 min. (This is Part 2 — Part 1 covered Cancer and Infectious Disease.)

Hook (2 min)

"Most of what changes outcomes in a well visit isn't a scan — it's catching the drinking, the blood pressure, the fall risk, and the unsafe home." Ask: which screens would you actually run on a healthy-looking 67-year-old? Park the list; you'll fill the three remaining domains.

Objective 1 — Framework 2 min

Recall the five domains (Cancer, Infectious Disease — Part 1; Psychosocial, Cardiovascular, Family/Safety — here). These are average-risk recommendations; family history/comorbidities shift you from screening toward diagnostic testing.

Objective 2 — Psychosocial 7 min

ASK: "How do you screen for alcohol, tobacco, and drugs in clinic?" Take answers, then reveal the tools.

Substance use (all adults, annually): Alcohol — SASQ ("How many times in the past year have you had 5 [4 for women] or more drinks in a day?") or AUDIT-C; CAGE assesses dependence, not screening. Tobacco — assess at every visit (Grade A); ask if they ever smoke and about all products. Drugs — the single question: "How many times in the past year have you used an illegal drug or a prescription medication for non-medical reasons?"

Pearl — NIAAA "unhealthy use": men 21–64 >4/day or >14/week; men ≥65 and all women >3/day or >7/week.

Mental health: USPSTF — screen all adults for depression (PHQ-2/4/9) and adults <65 for anxiety (GAD-7); no defined interval.

Objective 3 — Cardiovascular 10 min

ASK: For each — HTN, obesity, diabetes, lipids, AAA — ask who and how often before clicking.
TargetWho / how
HypertensionOffice BP for all; annually if ≥40 or at risk. Confirm with ambulatory/home BP before treating.
ObesityBMI at visits (Grade B); BMI ≥30 → intensive behavioral intervention. Classes 1 (30–34.9), 2 (35–39.9), 3 (≥40).
DiabetesADA: all at 35, or any age if overweight/obese + ≥1 RF. USPSTF: 35–70 overweight/obese. A1c/FPG/OGTT; repeat ≥q3y (yearly if prediabetes).
LipidsUSPSTF: 40–75 + ≥1 RF and 10-yr risk >10% (statin; 7.5–10% selective). Repeat ~q5y low-risk, q3y high-risk; non-fasting OK to screen.
AAAOne-time abdominal US in men 65–75 who ever smoked; selective in never-smokers; not for women.
Pearl (2026 update) — The 2026 ACC/AHA dyslipidemia guideline now uses the PREVENT calculator (ages 30–79) instead of the Pooled Cohort Equations and pushes "earlier and lower, for longer" LDL targets. Refer CV-risk patients to diet/activity counseling (≥150 min/week moderate aerobic + strengthening ≥2 days/week).

Objective 4 — Family & Safety 8 min

ASK: "Which safety screens do you owe a reproductive-age woman? An 80-year-old?"

Family planning: assess reproductive goals; folic acid 0.4–0.8 mg daily for anyone who may become pregnant. Falls: screen >65 (or risk factors) annually with 3 questions (unsteady? worry about falling? fallen in the past year?) → offer exercise (CDC STEADI). Osteoporosis: DEXA in women ≥65 (and postmenopausal <65 at risk); USPSTF finds insufficient evidence to screen men. IPV: screen all reproductive-age women, privately, with HITS/HARK/PVS/WAST. Advance-care planning: for adults of all ages, more so with comorbidities/older age.

Retrieval close (2 min)

Cover the board. Have learners rebuild the three domains and one screen each, plus the PREVENT update and AAA criteria.

Cases original cases — sub-questions click-to-reveal

Case 1 — 26-year-old woman, annual exam, planning pregnancy

26F, immunizations up to date except declined COVID, wants to conceive. BMI ~32 (175 lb, 5'2"), BP 138/82.

What screening/prevention is indicated, and what would you prioritize?Cervical cancer screening; STI/HIV/HCV (± syphilis by risk); COVID (± influenza by season); substance-use and depression/anxiety screening; HTN and obesity screening; diet/exercise counseling; family-planning counseling + folic acid 0.4–0.8 mg daily; IPV screening; advance-care planning. Prioritize preconception items (folic acid, BP, substance use) given her pregnancy plans.

Case 2 — 67-year-old woman, establishing care

67F, HTN on amlodipine, lives with son who manages her finances, former smoker (quit 20 years ago), normal colonoscopy 7 years ago, COVID vaccine done, tetanus ~5 years ago.

What screening/prevention is indicated?Tdap (if not had within recommended window), PCV21 (≥50; PCV20 also acceptable), recombinant zoster, ± influenza; breast cancer screening; substance-use (ask alcohol/drugs) and depression/anxiety screening; obesity, diabetes and lipid screening; diet/exercise counseling; fall and osteoporosis (DEXA) screening; advance-care planning; and given a relative managing her finances, consider elder abuse/vulnerable-adult assessment.

Case 3 — 69-year-old man, history of homelessness

69M, prior homelessness, smoked in his 20s, ~2 drinks nightly, colonoscopy 7 years ago (told to repeat in 5), no meds.

What screening/prevention is indicated?Colon cancer screening (colonoscopy — overdue); ± prostate screening (shared decision); Tdap, zoster, PCV21 (± influenza); HIV/HCV if not done; substance-use (his alcohol exceeds NIAAA limits) and mental-health screening; HTN, obesity, diabetes, lipid screening; AAA ultrasound (man 65–75, ever-smoked); diet/exercise counseling; fall screening; advance-care planning.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. Which patient should receive one-time AAA screening with abdominal ultrasound?

  1. A 60-year-old man who smoked for 30 years
  2. A 70-year-old man who has never smoked
  3. A 68-year-old man who smoked 1 ppd for 25 years
  4. A 72-year-old woman with a 40 pack-year history
  5. A 50-year-old man who smokes
Answer & explanationC. USPSTF recommends one-time AAA ultrasound in men 65–75 who have ever smoked. C fits. A is younger than 65; B is in range but never smoked (selective, not routine); D is a woman (not recommended regardless of smoking); E is younger than 65.

2. A healthy 36-year-old with no risk factors and a normal BMI asks about diabetes screening. Per ADA guidance, what do you advise?

  1. No screening until age 45
  2. Screen now — ADA recommends screening all adults starting at age 35
  3. Screen only if symptomatic
  4. Screen only if BMI ≥30
  5. Annual fasting glucose starting at 30
Answer & explanationB. ADA recommends screening all adults beginning at age 35 regardless of risk (and at any age if overweight/obese with ≥1 risk factor). A reflects the older threshold; C and D miss the universal age-35 trigger; E starts too early without basis. Repeat every ≥3 years if normal (yearly for prediabetes).

3. Per the 2026 ACC/AHA dyslipidemia guideline, which tool should you use to estimate primary-prevention CV risk, and why?

  1. Pooled Cohort Equations — they remain the standard
  2. Framingham Risk Score
  3. The PREVENT equations — Pooled Cohort overestimated risk ~40–50%
  4. CHA₂DS₂-VASc
  5. ASCVD risk is no longer calculated
Answer & explanationC. The 2026 guideline recommends the PREVENT calculator (ages 30–79) because the older Pooled Cohort Equations overestimated 10-year ASCVD risk by ~40–50%. A is outdated; B (Framingham) predates Pooled Cohort; D (CHA₂DS₂-VASc) is for AF stroke risk, not lipids; E is false.

4. Which screening does the USPSTF recommend for a 30-year-old woman at a routine visit?

  1. Anxiety screening and intimate-partner-violence screening
  2. AAA ultrasound
  3. DEXA scan
  4. Annual EKG
  5. Routine vitamin D level
Answer & explanationA. USPSTF recommends anxiety screening for adults <65 and IPV screening for all reproductive-age women. AAA (B) is for men 65–75 who smoked; DEXA (C) is for women ≥65 (or younger if high risk); routine EKG (D) and vitamin D (E) are not recommended for low-risk asymptomatic adults.

5. A 40-year-old man asks for "the best alcohol screen." Which is the most appropriate first-line screening approach?

  1. CAGE questionnaire
  2. The Single Alcohol Screening Question (or AUDIT-C)
  3. GGT and MCV
  4. Random blood alcohol level
  5. No screening unless he reports a problem
Answer & explanationB. The SASQ ("How many times in the past year have you had 5 [4 for women] or more drinks in a day?") or AUDIT-C are validated, sensitive screens. CAGE (A) assesses dependence, not screening. Labs (C) and a blood alcohol level (D) are not screening tools. E is wrong — USPSTF recommends screening all adults.

2024–2026 Practice Update NEW — projected on the appended deck slide

The deck's standing teaching (substance-use and mental-health screening, HTN/obesity/diabetes/lipid/AAA screening, folic acid, falls, DEXA in women, IPV, advance-care planning) was verified current. The practice-relevant additions:

  • 2026 ACC/AHA Multisociety Dyslipidemia Guideline: use the PREVENT calculator (ages 30–79) for primary-prevention CV risk — it replaces the Pooled Cohort Equations (which overestimated 10-year risk ~40–50%). Risk tiers: low <3%, borderline 3–<5%, intermediate 5–<10%, high ≥10%. Theme: "earlier and lower, for longer," with LDL-C targets of <55 (very-high-risk), <70 (high-risk), and <100 mg/dL (intermediate on statin), and earlier statins in familial hypercholesterolemia, those ≤30 with LDL ≥160, strong family ASCVD history, or high 30-year risk. The USPSTF statin threshold (40–75 + ≥1 RF, 10-yr risk >10%) is unchanged.
  • Pneumococcal (2024 ACIP): PCV21 is now an option for adults ≥50 (age lowered from 65) and 19–49 with chronic disease; the cases list "PCV20," which remains valid. If PCV15 is used, follow with PPSV23.
  • Mental health (USPSTF 2023, finalized): screen all adults for depression and adults <65 for anxiety — matches the board's framing.
  • RSV vaccine: recommended for adults ≥75, and 60–74 at increased risk, by shared decision-making.
  • Osteoporosis in men: USPSTF still finds insufficient evidence to screen men (the deck's "as of 2022" footnote remains accurate).

Take-Home Points

  1. Work all five prevention domains every visit; this talk covers psychosocial, cardiovascular, and family/safety. Individualize by age, sex, and risk.
  2. Psychosocial: screen all adults annually for alcohol (SASQ/AUDIT-C), tobacco (every visit), and drug use; depression (all) and anxiety (<65).
  3. Cardiovascular: HTN (office BP), obesity (BMI), diabetes (ADA all at 35), lipids (40–75 + risk), and one-time AAA ultrasound in men 65–75 who ever smoked.
  4. Family/Safety: folic acid for possible pregnancy, fall screening ≥65, DEXA in women ≥65, annual IPV screening in reproductive-age women, and advance-care planning for all.
  5. 2026 ACC/AHA: use PREVENT (not Pooled Cohort) for CV risk, with "earlier and lower for longer" LDL targets.
