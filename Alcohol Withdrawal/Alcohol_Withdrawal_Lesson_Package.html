Learning Objectives

  1. Risk-stratify alcohol withdrawal with PAWSS and place a patient on the syndrome timeline.
  2. Choose between symptom-triggered, fixed, and front-loaded benzodiazepine strategies and know when CIWA is invalid.
  3. Escalate to phenobarbital for severe or benzodiazepine-resistant withdrawal, and use adjuncts correctly.
  4. Apply supportive care — thiamine before glucose, electrolyte repletion — and link to addiction treatment.

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Why must thiamine be given before glucose in a malnourished patient with alcohol use disorder?A glucose load consumes thiamine as a cofactor; in a thiamine-deficient patient, giving dextrose first can precipitate or worsen Wernicke encephalopathy. Give parenteral thiamine before or with glucose.
Q2. A confused patient who cannot communicate has a high CIWA-Ar score. Should you escalate benzodiazepines based on that score?No — CIWA-Ar requires a patient who can be validly assessed/communicate. In a confused or non-communicative patient the score is unreliable; use a fixed-dose or front-loaded (or phenobarbital) strategy and look for other causes of altered mentation.
Q3. What distinguishes alcoholic hallucinosis from delirium tremens?Alcoholic hallucinosis features hallucinations with a clear sensorium and relatively normal vital signs; delirium tremens involves an altered sensorium (delirium) plus autonomic instability (tachycardia, hypertension, fever, diaphoresis) and is far more dangerous.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"Your patient admitted with pancreatitis is now day two, tremulous and anxious, heart rate 118. The night float gives glucose-containing fluids and a dose of an antipsychotic for 'agitation.' Both choices can hurt him — one risks Wernicke's, the other does nothing to prevent the seizure that's coming. Let's build the right reflex." Set up predict → treat → escalate → support.

Step 1 — Predict and place on the timeline 6 min

ASK: "When does each withdrawal syndrome appear — tremor, seizure, DTs?"

Use PAWSS on admission (≥4 flags risk of complicated withdrawal). The clock: minor symptoms at 6–24 h; withdrawal seizures 12–48 h; alcoholic hallucinosis 12–48 h (clear sensorium); delirium tremens 48–96 h (altered sensorium + autonomic storm). Stress the hallucinosis-vs-DT distinction: hallucinations with clear sensorium and normal vitals is not DTs.

Pearl: The patient who 'sees bugs' but is otherwise lucid and stable has hallucinosis, not DTs — but watch them, because the storm can still come.

Step 2 — Treat: benzodiazepines first-line 8 min

ASK: "Symptom-triggered or fixed dosing — and when is CIWA the wrong tool?"

Benzodiazepines are first-line and reduce seizures and DTs. Symptom-triggered CIWA-Ar dosing uses less drug and shortens treatment in patients who can be validly scored. Front-loading a long-acting benzo suits high-risk/severe presentations. Long-acting agents (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide) self-taper; switch to lorazepam in significant liver disease (no active metabolites). The key safety point: CIWA is invalid in a patient who can't communicate or whose confusion has another cause — don't titrate off a meaningless number.

Pearl: A high CIWA in a non-communicative patient is noise — find the real cause of the delirium and switch to a fixed/front-loaded protocol.

Step 3 — Escalate: phenobarbital 7 min

ASK: "The patient keeps needing benzos and isn't settling. What's next, and what won't fix it?"

For severe or benzodiazepine-resistant withdrawal, phenobarbital is effective alone or as an adjunct — long half-life, self-tapering, and acting on both GABA and glutamate. ICU-level adjuncts (dexmedetomidine, propofol) control autonomic symptoms but do not prevent seizures by themselves. Transfer to ICU for DTs, airway risk, or escalating requirements.

Pearl: Clonidine, dexmedetomidine, and antipsychotics treat the autonomic and agitation symptoms but do NOT prevent seizures or DTs — always keep a GABAergic agent on board.

Step 4 — Support 2 min

Thiamine before glucose; replete magnesium, potassium, and phosphate. Hunt for coexisting infection, GI bleed, head injury, and pancreatitis. Before discharge, treat the underlying alcohol use disorder — offer naltrexone or acamprosate and link to care.

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — the glucose trap

48M with alcohol use disorder, malnourished, brought in obtunded. The team hangs D50 for a glucose of 55.

What was missed?Thiamine before/with glucose — a dextrose load in a thiamine-deficient patient can precipitate Wernicke encephalopathy. Give parenteral thiamine.
Other repletion?Magnesium, potassium, phosphate; magnesium also supports the seizure threshold.

Case 2 — invalid CIWA

60F, post-op, intubated and sedated, 'CIWA 18' charted by protocol; team about to escalate benzodiazepines.

Right move?No — CIWA-Ar is invalid in a patient who cannot be validly assessed. Use a fixed-dose/front-loaded or phenobarbital approach and investigate other causes of the abnormal exam.

Case 3 — benzo-resistant withdrawal

52M, day 3, escalating tachycardia, agitation, and tremor despite repeated high benzodiazepine doses; now febrile and confused.

Diagnosis + next step?Severe withdrawal/early DTs with benzodiazepine resistance — escalate to phenobarbital (alone or adjunctive) and transfer to ICU; consider dexmedetomidine for autonomic control but keep a GABAergic agent on board.

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. A malnourished patient with alcohol use disorder presents with hypoglycemia. What is the correct order of administration?

  1. Glucose first, then thiamine
  2. Thiamine before or with glucose
  3. Glucose only
  4. Thiamine only after 24 hours
  5. Magnesium before everything else
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Thiamine must be given before or with a glucose load — dextrose consumes thiamine and can precipitate Wernicke encephalopathy in a deficient patient. A/C: glucose-first risks Wernicke. D: delaying thiamine is harmful. E: magnesium repletion matters but isn't the Wernicke-prevention step.

2. Which dosing strategy generally uses the least total benzodiazepine and shortens treatment in alcohol withdrawal for an assessable patient?

  1. Fixed-schedule dosing
  2. Symptom-triggered dosing guided by CIWA-Ar
  3. As-needed antipsychotics
  4. Clonidine monotherapy
  5. Dexmedetomidine infusion
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Symptom-triggered (CIWA-Ar) dosing reduces total benzodiazepine use and treatment duration versus fixed schedules in patients who can be validly scored. A: fixed dosing uses more drug. C/D/E: adjuncts that don't prevent seizures and aren't first-line.

3. A patient has worsening withdrawal despite repeated, escalating benzodiazepine doses. What is the most appropriate escalation?

  1. Add an antipsychotic as the primary agent
  2. Phenobarbital (alone or adjunctive)
  3. Clonidine monotherapy
  4. Stop all sedatives and observe
  5. Switch to oral diazepam taper only
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Benzodiazepine-resistant or severe withdrawal is effectively treated with phenobarbital, which acts on GABA and glutamate and self-tapers. A/C: antipsychotics/clonidine don't prevent seizures or DTs. D: dangerous undertreatment. E: inadequate for resistant severe withdrawal.

4. Which feature best distinguishes delirium tremens from alcoholic hallucinosis?

  1. Presence of visual hallucinations
  2. Altered sensorium with autonomic instability
  3. Onset within 6 hours
  4. A normal heart rate and blood pressure
  5. A clear sensorium
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. DTs are defined by an altered sensorium (delirium) plus autonomic instability (tachycardia, hypertension, fever, diaphoresis); hallucinosis has hallucinations with a clear sensorium and relatively normal vitals. A: both can have hallucinations. C: DTs typically appear at 48–96 h. D/E describe hallucinosis.

5. Which statement about adjunctive agents (clonidine, dexmedetomidine, antipsychotics) in alcohol withdrawal is correct?

  1. They reliably prevent withdrawal seizures
  2. They treat autonomic and agitation symptoms but do not prevent seizures or DTs
  3. They should replace benzodiazepines as first-line
  4. Antipsychotics lower the seizure threshold and are therefore preferred
  5. Dexmedetomidine alone is sufficient for severe withdrawal
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. These adjuncts help control autonomic hyperactivity and agitation but do not prevent seizures or DTs — a GABAergic agent (benzodiazepine or phenobarbital) must remain on board. A/C/E overstate their role; D is backwards — antipsychotics can lower the seizure threshold.

Take-Home Points

  1. Risk-stratify with PAWSS and know the timeline: seizures at 12–48 h, delirium tremens at 48–96 h.
  2. Benzodiazepines are first-line; symptom-triggered CIWA dosing uses less drug — but CIWA is invalid in a patient who can't be assessed.
  3. Escalate to phenobarbital for severe or benzodiazepine-resistant withdrawal.
  4. Adjuncts (clonidine, dexmedetomidine, antipsychotics) treat symptoms but do not prevent seizures or DTs — keep a GABAergic agent on board.
  5. Give thiamine before glucose, replete magnesium, and link the patient to addiction treatment at discharge.
