Alcohol Withdrawal — Inpatient Management

Predict severity (PAWSS), treat symptoms (CIWA-driven or front-loaded), benzodiazepines first-line · phenobarbital for severe/refractory · thiamine before glucose · don't miss DTs

1 · Predict & recognize the timeline

Risk-stratify on admission with the PAWSS (Prediction of Alcohol Withdrawal Severity Scale); a score ≥4 flags risk of complicated withdrawal. Know the clock:

OnsetSyndrome
6–24 hMinor: tremor, anxiety, ↑HR/BP, diaphoresis
12–48 hWithdrawal seizures (generalized, tonic-clonic)
12–48 hAlcoholic hallucinosis (clear sensorium)
48–96 hDelirium tremens (confusion, autonomic storm)
Hallucinosis = hallucinations with a CLEAR sensorium and normal vitals; DTs = altered sensorium + autonomic instability. The distinction drives acuity.

2 · Treat — benzodiazepines first-line

  • Benzodiazepines reduce seizures and DTs and are first-line (ASAM).
  • Symptom-triggered (CIWA-Ar) dosing uses less total drug and shortens treatment vs fixed schedules — use it in patients who can be validly scored.
  • Front-loading (large early doses of a long-acting benzo, e.g., diazepam) for high-risk/severe presentations.
  • Long-acting (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide) self-tapers; use lorazepam with significant liver disease (no active metabolites).
CIWA-Ar is invalid in patients who can't communicate or are confused from another cause — don't drive dosing off a meaningless score; switch to fixed/front-loaded or phenobarbital protocols.

3 · Severe / refractory — phenobarbital

For severe withdrawal or benzodiazepine-resistant cases, phenobarbital is effective (alone or adjunctive) — long half-life, self-tapering, acts on GABA and glutamate.

EscalationNote
PhenobarbitalSevere/refractory; reduces ICU/benzo needs in many protocols
Dexmedetomidine / propofolAdjuncts in ICU — do NOT prevent seizures alone
ICU transferDTs, airway risk, escalating requirements
Adjuncts (clonidine, dexmedetomidine, antipsychotics) treat autonomic/agitation symptoms but do NOT prevent seizures or DTs — keep a GABAergic agent on board.

Support — vitamins & the basics

  • Thiamine BEFORE glucose — giving dextrose first can precipitate Wernicke encephalopathy. Give parenteral thiamine generously in malnourished drinkers.
  • Replete magnesium, potassium, phosphate; magnesium supports seizure threshold.
  • Screen for and treat coexisting problems: infection, GI bleed, trauma/head injury, pancreatitis, liver disease.
  • Treat the substance-use disorder: offer naltrexone/acamprosate and link to treatment at discharge.

Do/Avoid

  • DO risk-stratify with PAWSS and pick a dosing strategy that fits the patient
  • DO give thiamine before glucose and replete magnesium
  • DO escalate to phenobarbital for benzo-resistant or severe withdrawal
  • AVOID driving benzo dosing off a CIWA score in a patient who can't be validly assessed
  • AVOID relying on clonidine/dexmedetomidine/antipsychotics alone — they don't prevent seizures
  • AVOID under-treating: untreated DTs carry meaningful mortality
