The spectrum of ALD, in order?	Hepatic steatosis → steatohepatitis (ASH) → fibrosis → cirrhosis.
Which ALD stages are reversible with abstinence?	Steatosis and inflammation regress; established fibrosis/cirrhosis is largely irreversible.
Prevalence of hepatic steatosis in heavy drinkers?	~90%.
Single most important factor improving survival in ALD?	Complete alcohol abstinence.
First-line FDA-approved agents for relapse prevention in ALD?	Naltrexone and acamprosate.
Which AUD medication is avoided in ALD?	Disulfiram (hepatotoxicity).
Acamprosate caution?	Avoid in severe renal dysfunction (renally cleared).
Who to screen for ALD?	All adults >18 with current or prior unhealthy alcohol use (CAGE, AUDIT-C).
Initial ALD work-up?	RUQ ultrasound + CMP, CBC, INR.
What clinches alcohol vs MASLD steatosis?	Clinical history — they are indistinguishable on US/biopsy.
Non-invasive fibrosis tests in ALD?	FIB-4, APRI, FibroSure/FibroTest, ELF; elastography; MRI.
Lab signature of alcohol-related hepatitis (AH)?	AST:ALT >2, both usually <400 IU/L; total bilirubin >3 mg/dL; recent heavy use; jaundice ≤8 wks.
Transaminases >400–500 in a drinker — implication?	Look for another cause (ischemic, acetaminophen, viral) — not pure AH.
Severity threshold for severe AH?	MDF ≥32 (or MELD >20).
Pharmacotherapy for severe AH without contraindications?	Prednisolone 40 mg/day (short-term survival benefit); IV NAC may add short-term benefit.
Contraindications to steroids in AH?	Active infection, AKI Cr >2.5, uncontrolled GI bleed, multiorgan failure/shock, alternative cause.
Lille score: when and cutoff?	Day 4–7; <0.45 = responder (continue 28-day course, ~85% 6-mo survival); ≥0.45 = stop steroids.
Nutrition in ALD?	High-protein diet + thiamine, folate, B-complex; low-sodium if portal HTN; consider zinc in AH.
