Learning Objectives

  1. Identify the spectrum of alcohol-related liver disease (ALD) and the risk factors for developing it.
  2. Select diagnostic tests that differentiate hepatic steatosis, alcohol-related hepatitis (AH), and cirrhosis.
  3. Build a paradigm for managing the spectrum of ALD — anchored on abstinence, nutrition, and transplant referral.
  4. Diagnose and risk-stratify acute alcohol-related hepatitis (AH) and decide who warrants corticosteroids.

Pre-work send the day before

Three retrieval questions to prime the session. Answers hidden — try first, then check.

1. Name the four stages in the spectrum of ALD, in order.

Hepatic steatosis (fatty liver) → alcohol-related steatohepatitis (ASH) → fibrosis → cirrhosis. Steatosis and inflammation can regress with abstinence; fibrosis/cirrhosis is largely irreversible.

2. What single intervention most improves survival across the entire ALD spectrum?

Complete alcohol abstinence. Nothing else — not steroids, not transplant criteria — matches its effect on the natural history of the disease.

3. A patient with heavy alcohol use has AST 220, ALT 95, total bilirubin 4.0. Which diagnosis does the lab pattern point to, and why?

Alcohol-related hepatitis. AST:ALT >2 with both transaminases <400 IU/L plus hyperbilirubinemia (>3 mg/dL) in the right clinical setting is the classic AH signature. Transaminases >400–500 should make you look for another cause (e.g., ischemic or toxic injury).

Teaching Script restructured into timed segments

Hook (0:00–0:02)

"Two patients, same six-pack-a-night history. One has a fatty liver that will normalize if he stops; the other is dying of alcohol-related hepatitis with a 30–50% chance of being dead in a month. Same exposure — what separates them, and what can we actually change?" The answer threads the whole talk: where on the spectrum they sit, and whether they stop drinking.

ASK — Before the board: "What's the single most powerful lever we have in any patient with alcohol-related liver disease?" Let them land on abstinence before you reveal anything.

Objective 1 — The spectrum & risk factors (0:02–0:08) 6 min

Click through the spectrum animation: normal liver → hepatic steatosis → steatohepatitis (ASH) → cirrhosis, each transition driven by continued alcohol use. Anchor the two big ideas: (1) progression is stepwise with ongoing intake, and (2) the early stages are reversible while fibrosis is not.

StageWhat it isReversible?
Hepatic steatosisEarliest, benign; seen in ~90% of heavy drinkers. A marker of altered metabolism and susceptibility.Yes, with cessation
Steatohepatitis (ASH)Inflammatory intermediary; can be indolent or present fulminantly as acute AH. Higher cirrhosis risk than steatosis alone.Inflammation yes; established fibrosis no
CirrhosisEnd-stage fibrosis from the maladaptive scarring response.Largely irreversible
ASK — "What raises a given person's risk of progressing?" Collect answers, then reveal.

Risk factors: quantity (≥2 drinks/day women, ≥3/day men), pattern (binging), genetic predisposition, obesity, female sex, smoking, and concurrent liver disease (viral hepatitis, MASLD).

Pearl. Steatosis is not just "a fatty liver" — it is biological evidence the liver is already responding to alcohol, and it flags who is set up to progress if drinking continues.

Objective 2 — Diagnostic evaluation (0:08–0:13) 5 min

Who to screen: every adult with current or prior unhealthy alcohol use (screen with CAGE / AUDIT-C). How to screen: RUQ ultrasound plus basic labs — CMP, CBC, INR.

ASK — "You can't tell alcohol-related steatosis from MASLD on ultrasound or biopsy. So what clinches it?" → the clinical history of alcohol use.
EntityKey diagnostic features
Hepatic steatosisOften incidental on imaging; LFTs normal or mildly elevated; indistinguishable from MASLD — history is decisive.
Alcohol-related hepatitisClinical diagnosis: AST:ALT typically >2, both usually <400 IU/L; elevated bilirubin. Biopsy rarely needed.
Fibrosis / cirrhosisNon-invasive scores (FIB-4, APRI, FibroSure/FibroTest, ELF), elastography, or MRI; small nodular liver on US. Biopsy now usually avoidable.
Pearl. Transaminases in ALD are usually modest (<400). AST or ALT in the high hundreds or thousands should redirect you to ischemia, acetaminophen, or viral causes — not "just alcohol."

Objective 3 — Managing the spectrum (0:13–0:18) 5 min

Four pillars, reveal one at a time:

ASK — "What pharmacotherapy can we offer to support abstinence, and which agent should we avoid in liver disease?"
PillarSpecifics
Alcohol cessationThe single most important factor for survival. Naltrexone (PO daily or monthly IM; hepatically metabolized, can be hepatotoxic, not studied in AH/cirrhosis) and acamprosate (PO TID; no hepatotoxicity but avoid in severe renal dysfunction) are first-line FDA-approved agents. Avoid disulfiram in ALD. Pair with psychosocial/behavioral treatment (counseling, mutual-help groups, residential programs).
NutritionHigh-protein diet; thiamine, folate, and B-complex supplementation for anyone still drinking or newly sober; low-sodium if portal hypertension; consider zinc, especially in AH.
Manage complicationsTreat ascites/SBP, varices, encephalopathy; vaccinate and screen (HCC surveillance, EGD for varices).
Transplant evaluationRefer cirrhosis that doesn't recover with abstinence — and, increasingly, carefully selected severe AH (see 2024 update).

Objective 4 — Acute alcohol-related hepatitis (0:18–0:24) 6 min

Clinical diagnosis (AASLD): heavy recent use (≥3 drinks/day women, ≥4/day men for ≥60 days), onset of jaundice within 8 weeks, AST >50 with AST:ALT typically >2 (both <400 IU/L), total bilirubin >3 mg/dL. Often leukocytosis with neutrophilia and tender hepatomegaly. Evaluate for infection in every patient.

ASK — "Your AH patient has an MDF of 40 and no infection or bleeding. What's the decision, and how will you know in a week whether it's working?"
  1. Risk-stratify: Maddrey Discriminant Function (MDF, from bilirubin + INR) is most common; MDF ≥32 (or MELD >20) = severe (deck quotes 30–50% 30-day mortality).
  2. Treat: prednisolone 40 mg/day if no contraindication — short-term (28-day) survival benefit only. Adding IV NAC may further improve short-term survival (2024 ACG).
  3. Contraindications to steroids: active/known infection, AKI with Cr >2.5 mg/dL, uncontrolled GI bleed, multiorgan failure/shock, or an alternative cause of injury. (AKI is relative — reconsider if it resolves.)
  4. Reassess at day 4–7 with the Lille score: <0.45 = responder, complete the 28-day course (≈85% 6-month survival); ≥0.45 = non-responder, stop steroids and pivot to palliative care / transplant evaluation.
Pearl. Steroids in AH buy weeks, not years, and only in responders. The Lille score at day 4–7 is your honesty check — if the bilirubin isn't moving, the risks of immunosuppression have stopped being worth it.

Retrieval close (0:24–0:25) 1 min

Pens down, board off: "Rebuild the take-home points from memory — the spectrum, the one intervention that matters most, the lab signature of AH, and the steroid decision rule." Fill gaps from what they reconstruct.

2024 Guideline & Nomenclature Update

The standing teaching is unchanged and remains current. Surface these newer points so the projected board reflects 2024 practice (also captured on the appended deck slide):

  • 2024 ACG Clinical Guideline on Alcohol-Associated Liver Disease (Am J Gastroenterol 2024;119:30–54) reaffirms the framework taught here: abstinence is the cornerstone; MDF ≥32 or MELD >20 defines severe AH for steroid consideration; Lille at day 4–7 guides continuation.
  • Prednisolone + NAC: adding IV N-acetylcysteine to prednisolone 40 mg/day may improve short-term (28–30 day) survival in severe AH — a reasonable adjunct, still no long-term benefit.
  • Early liver transplantation for carefully selected severe AH non-responsive to medical therapy is now endorsed (ACG/AASLD), with outcomes comparable to other indications — no longer purely experimental.
  • 2023 multi-society nomenclature change (Hepatology 2023;78:1966): NAFLD → MASLD, NASH → MASH; new MetALD category for metabolic dysfunction plus moderate alcohol use. Read the deck's "indistinguishable from NAFLD" lines as "indistinguishable from MASLD."

Case 1 — Diagnosis & evaluation of ALD

60-year-old man, HTN on lisinopril, drinks a 6-pack of beer nightly, never-smoker, no drug use. Exam: BP 129/78, P 75, SpO2 94%, BMI 28, hepatomegaly. Labs: WBC 8, Hgb 14, plts 208; AST 80, ALT 55, ALP 110, Tbili 0.9, INR 1.1; HCV antibody negative within the year.

What are the next best steps for evaluating his abnormal LFTs?

RUQ ultrasound (assess degree of liver disease and complications) and calculate APRI (screens for cirrhosis in ALD). Counsel on alcohol cessation. Routine hepatology referral is not yet required; repeating labs after a period of abstinence is reasonable, but here the elevated APRI/INR warrants imaging now.

He is abstinent for 3 months and returns. Repeat labs: plts 108, AST 87, ALT 65, ALP 118, Tbili 0.9, INR 1.5. US: small nodular liver with splenomegaly, no ascites.

What is your next step in management?

Findings now suggest cirrhosis (nodular liver, falling platelets, rising INR, splenomegaly). Refer to hepatology. Continue abstinence counseling and nutrition (high-protein diet, vitamin supplementation). Start health maintenance: viral hepatitis testing/vaccination, EGD for varices, and routine HCC surveillance.

Case 2 — Diagnosis & management of AH

35-year-old woman, 1 week of progressive jaundice, new ascites, and dyspnea on exertion. Drinks 3–4 drinks/night, occasional binges; last drink ~24 h ago. Exam: T 37.9 °C, HR 90, BP 120/88, RR 20, SpO2 94% RA; jaundiced, distended abdomen with fluid wave, 2+ LE edema to the knees, confused with asterixis. Labs: Cr 0.82, WBC 14, Hgb 11, plts 108; AST 224, ALT 110, ALP 118, Tbili 3.4, INR 2.1. US: enlarged fatty liver, moderate ascites.

What is the most likely diagnosis?

Alcohol-related steatohepatitis (acute AH). AST:ALT >2 with transaminases in the low hundreds, elevated bilirubin, recent heavy use, and the complications (ascites, encephalopathy). The enlarged liver argues against established cirrhosis; rule out SBP and other infection (here CXR, UA, and SBP work-up are negative).

What is the next best step in management?

MDF is ~94 (high short-term mortality). With no contraindications (normal renal function, no GI bleed, infection excluded), prednisolone 40 mg/day can be started, alongside abstinence, nutrition, and complication management.

She is started on prednisolone 40 mg daily.

When should she be re-evaluated for response, and what does it show?

Repeat CMP and coags at day 4–7 to calculate the Lille score. At day 7: Cr 0.78, WBC 12, Hgb 11, plts 98; AST 204, ALT 78, ALP 130, Tbili 4.4, INR 1.8; albumin 2.7. Lille is <0.45 → good prognosis and steroid response (≈85% 6-month survival). Continue prednisolone for a full 28-day course.

Board-style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 52-year-old man with heavy daily alcohol use is admitted with 2 weeks of jaundice. Labs: AST 180, ALT 70, total bilirubin 9.2 mg/dL, INR 1.9, Cr 0.9, WBC 13.5. CXR, UA, and ascitic fluid are negative for infection. MDF is 48. Which is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Liver biopsy to confirm the diagnosis before treatment
  2. Prednisolone 40 mg daily
  3. Pentoxifylline 400 mg three times daily
  4. Defer pharmacotherapy and reassess in 2 weeks
  5. Naltrexone IM now
Answer & explanation

B — Prednisolone 40 mg daily. This is severe AH (MDF ≥32) with no contraindication (no infection, AKI, or bleed), so corticosteroids are indicated for a short-term survival benefit.

A wrong: AH is a clinical diagnosis; biopsy is rarely needed and would delay treatment. C wrong: pentoxifylline does not improve survival (STOPAH) and is not recommended over steroids. D wrong: delaying steroids in eligible severe AH forgoes the only short-term mortality benefit. E wrong: naltrexone supports abstinence but is hepatically metabolized, not studied in AH, and treats neither the acute hepatitis nor mortality.

2. The patient in Question 1 starts prednisolone. On day 6, repeat labs are obtained and the Lille score is 0.62. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. Continue prednisolone to complete the 28-day course
  2. Increase prednisolone to 60 mg daily
  3. Stop prednisolone and focus on supportive care / transplant evaluation
  4. Add pentoxifylline to prednisolone
  5. Switch to IV methylprednisolone
Answer & explanation

C — Stop prednisolone. A Lille score ≥0.45 marks a non-responder; bilirubin has not improved, so continued steroids add infection risk without benefit. Pivot to supportive care and transplant evaluation.

A wrong: completing the course is for responders (Lille <0.45). B/E wrong: escalating or changing the steroid does not rescue a Lille non-responder. D wrong: adding pentoxifylline to steroids does not confer a survival benefit.

3. A 45-year-old woman with alcohol use disorder is found to have hepatic steatosis on ultrasound and AST 60, ALT 48, normal bilirubin and INR. She asks what will most improve her long-term liver outcome. Which is the best answer?

  1. Vitamin E 800 IU daily
  2. A low-fat diet
  3. Complete alcohol abstinence
  4. Ursodeoxycholic acid
  5. Prophylactic prednisolone
Answer & explanation

C — Complete alcohol abstinence. It is the single most important factor for survival across the ALD spectrum; at the steatosis stage, the liver can fully recover with cessation.

A wrong: vitamin E is studied in MASH, not ALD, and not for survival here. B wrong: nutrition matters (high-protein diet, vitamins) but a low-fat diet is not the key lever. D wrong: ursodeoxycholic acid has no role in ALD. E wrong: steroids are for severe acute AH, not bland steatosis.

4. A 58-year-old man with alcohol-related cirrhosis wants help staying sober. He has stage 3 CKD (eGFR 38) and decompensated cirrhosis. Which pharmacotherapy is most appropriate?

  1. Disulfiram
  2. Acamprosate
  3. Naltrexone
  4. Topiramate
  5. None — counseling only is safest given his liver disease
Answer & explanation

C — Naltrexone is the better choice here. Acamprosate is renally cleared and should be avoided in severe renal dysfunction; naltrexone is the more appropriate FDA-approved agent, with monitoring (it is hepatically metabolized and not formally studied in advanced disease, so use with care plus psychosocial support).

A wrong: disulfiram is avoided in ALD (hepatotoxicity). B wrong: acamprosate is contraindicated in severe renal dysfunction. D wrong: topiramate is non-FDA-approved second-line. E wrong: withholding all pharmacotherapy abandons an effective relapse-prevention tool — combine medication with counseling.

5. A 40-year-old man with heavy alcohol use presents with jaundice. Labs: AST 540, ALT 610, total bilirubin 6.0, INR 1.4. Which statement is most accurate?

  1. This lab pattern is classic for alcohol-related hepatitis
  2. The AST:ALT ratio confirms cirrhosis
  3. The very high transaminases should prompt evaluation for another cause
  4. Prednisolone is indicated based on these values alone
  5. A normal INR excludes severe liver injury
Answer & explanation

C — Look for another cause. In AH transaminases are usually <400 IU/L with AST:ALT >2. Values in the 500–600 range with ALT > AST point away from pure AH toward ischemic, toxic (e.g., acetaminophen), or viral hepatitis — check an APAP level and acute hepatitis panel.

A wrong: the magnitude and AST:ALT pattern don't fit AH. B wrong: the ratio doesn't confirm cirrhosis. D wrong: you cannot commit to steroids before establishing the diagnosis and excluding contraindications. E wrong: a near-normal INR does not exclude serious injury.

Take-Home Points

  1. ALD is a spectrum — steatosis → steatohepatitis → fibrosis/cirrhosis. Early stages regress with abstinence; fibrosis does not.
  2. Abstinence is the single most important factor for survival. Offer naltrexone or acamprosate (first-line) plus psychosocial treatment; avoid disulfiram.
  3. AH is a clinical diagnosis: heavy recent use, jaundice ≤8 wks, AST:ALT >2 with both <400 IU/L, bilirubin >3. Rule out infection in everyone.
  4. Risk-stratify AH with MDF ≥32 (or MELD >20); consider prednisolone 40 mg/day (± NAC) without contraindications, and use the day 4–7 Lille score to continue (<0.45) or stop (≥0.45).
  5. Nutrition and complication management matter throughout; refer non-recovering cirrhosis — and selected severe AH — for transplant evaluation.
