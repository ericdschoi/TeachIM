Learning Objectives

  1. Identify the bacterial pathogens commonly responsible for pneumonia, UTI, intra-abdominal infection, skin/soft-tissue infection, and meningitis — and how community vs. hospital acquisition shifts the "bugs."
  2. Differentiate CAP, HAP/VAP, and aspiration pneumonia by pathogens and empiric coverage, including when to add MRSA and Pseudomonas coverage.
  3. Diagnose catheter-associated UTI and distinguish simple cystitis, complicated UTI, and CAUTI by host risk and microbiology.
  4. Differentiate nonpurulent, purulent, and necrotizing soft-tissue infections by pathogen and treatment.
  5. Differentiate community- vs. hospital-acquired intra-abdominal infection and choose first- and second-line regimens, always paired with source control.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. The 2019 IDSA/ATS CAP guideline retired "healthcare-associated pneumonia (HCAP)." So which two patient features now actually warrant empiric MRSA/Pseudomonas coverage in a patient with CAP? (1) Prior respiratory isolation of MRSA or Pseudomonas, and (2) hospitalization with receipt of IV antibiotics within the last 90 days — ideally validated against local risk factors. Otherwise, treat as routine CAP (ceftriaxone + azithromycin, or a respiratory fluoroquinolone).
Q2. A patient with a chronic indwelling Foley grows 10⁵ CFU of E. coli but has no symptoms. Do you treat? No. CAUTI requires symptoms or signs of UTI plus the bacteriuria and a catheter (or one within 48 h). Asymptomatic bacteriuria in a catheterized patient is not treated (exceptions: pregnancy, pre-urologic procedure with mucosal trauma).
Q3. Which soft-tissue infection is a surgical emergency, and what three exam findings should raise the alarm? Necrotizing soft-tissue infection (NSTI). Red flags: pain out of proportion / tenderness extending beyond the erythema, rapid progression, and systemic toxicity (also crepitus, bullae, "woody" edema, skin necrosis). Antibiotics do not substitute for urgent surgical debridement.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive "Antibiotic Man" board (Antibiotics_Sites_of_Infection_Optimized.pptx) and click through each site, or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. Hand out the blank fill-in worksheet so learners complete the Bugs/Drugs grid as you go. The board is built around one repeating move: name the bugs → propose empiric drugs → narrow to culture data. Full talk ~45 min; cases add ~15 min. You can also deliver any single site as a 15–20 min stand-alone.

Hook (2 min)

"Two patients, same drug question, opposite answers: a healthy 40-year-old with a productive cough, and a ventilated ICU patient on hospital day 6 with a new infiltrate. Both have pneumonia — why does only one get vancomycin and Zosyn?" The whole talk is that question, repeated across five body sites: the site tells you the likely bugs, and where the patient caught it tells you which resistant bugs to add.

ASK (frame the session): "Across all of these infections, what three organisms does hospital exposure keep adding to the list?" Reveal: MRSA, Pseudomonas, Enterococcus. That trio is the spine of the whole talk.

Objective 1 & 2 — Pneumonia 20 min · cases 1, 4b

ASK: "Empiric bugs for CAP?" before clicking. Reveal: S. pneumoniae, GNRs (H. flu, Moraxella), atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila, Legionella). Empiric: ceftriaxone + azithromycin (or doxycycline), or a respiratory fluoroquinolone — regardless of severity.

Pearl — HCAP is dead. The 2019 IDSA/ATS guideline abandoned HCAP. Add MRSA/Pseudomonas coverage to CAP only for (1) prior MRSA/Pseudomonas, or (2) hospitalization + IV antibiotics within 90 days. Draw cultures so you can de-escalate.
Pearl — anaerobes in aspiration. Newer evidence says acute aspiration events rarely involve anaerobes. Don't reflexively add metronidazole/clindamycin — reserve anaerobic coverage for lung abscess or empyema.
ASK: "A negative MRSA nares PCR — does it help?" Reveal: yes, its high negative predictive value lets you stop empiric vancomycin (most useful in low-prevalence settings).

Objective 3 — Urinary tract infections 20 min · case 4a

ASK: "One organism causes 75–95% of cystitis — which?" Reveal: E. coli. Others: Proteus, Klebsiella, S. saprophyticus; Staph/Enterococcus/Pseudomonas appear with healthcare exposure.
SyndromeDrugs
Simple cystitis (1st line)Nitrofurantoin 100 mg BID ×5 d, or TMP-SMX ×3 d (if local resistance <20%), or fosfomycin 3 g ×1
Simple cystitis (2nd line)Urinary FQ ×3 d, or beta-lactam (Augmentin/cefpodoxime) ×3–7 d
Pyelonephritis / complicated UTICeftriaxone (no MDR risk) → Zosyn or meropenem (MDR risk/critically ill); add vanc/dapto/linezolid if GPCs / Enterococcus
Pearl — never nitrofurantoin or fosfomycin for pyelonephritis. They don't achieve tissue/renal levels. They are bladder-only drugs.
Pearl — CAUTI is a clinical diagnosis. Symptoms/signs of UTI + bacteriuria + an indwelling/suprapubic/intermittent catheter (or one within 48 h). Colonization is the rule in catheterized patients — do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria. Long-term catheters trend polymicrobial (Proteus common); replace the catheter and culture from a fresh one.

Objective 4 — Skin & soft-tissue infections 20 min · cases 2, 5

ASK: "Nonpurulent vs purulent cellulitis — which bug drives each?" Reveal: nonpurulent = Strep (treat Strep + MSSA); purulent = S. aureus, mostly MRSA (treat MRSA).
CategoryBugsDrugs
NonpurulentStrep >> S. aureusCephalexin (PO) / cefazolin or ceftriaxone (IV)
PurulentMSSA/MRSAI&D + MRSA coverage: vanc/dapto (IV) or Bactrim/doxycycline (PO) ± cephalexin
NSTIGAS > S. aureus, GNRs, Clostridium; often polymicrobialSurgery + (vanc/dapto) + (Zosyn/meropenem) + clindamycin
Pearl — purulent SSTI is a drainage problem first. I&D is the primary treatment; culture the pus. Routine added Strep coverage is usually unnecessary. In toxic-shock-picture patients, add penicillin + clindamycin (antitoxin effect).
ASK: "Why clindamycin in NSTI and strep toxic shock?" Reveal: ribosomal protein-synthesis inhibition shuts off exotoxin production (the "Eagle effect" / antitoxin activity) — independent of its antibacterial role.

Objective 5 — Intra-abdominal infections 15 min · case 3

ASK: "Community-acquired intra-abdominal infection — three bug categories?" Reveal: enteric GNRs (Enterobacterales), streptococci, and anaerobes (B. fragilis). Enterococcus and Pseudomonas are not routine in community infection.
SettingBugsDrugs

Pearl — antibiotics never substitute for source control. Abscess drainage, cholecystectomy/cholecystostomy, or perforation repair is half the treatment. Avoid ampicillin-sulbactam empirically for IAI given high E. coli resistance.

Retrieval close (3 min)

Erase the board (or close the deck). Have learners rebuild the take-home grid from memory: for each of the five sites, name the baseline bugs, then say what hospital exposure adds and which drug you'd add for it. Whoever offers a regimen has to name the one organism it's covering.

Cases original board cases — click to reveal

From the deck's case slides. Map to sections: Pneumonia → Cases 1, 4b; UTI → Case 4a; SSTI → Cases 2, 5; Intra-abdominal → Case 3.

Case 1 — Pneumonia

70-year-old, 4 days of productive cough, malaise, fevers. T 100.9 °F, HR 110, BP 99/53, RR 33, SpO₂ 90%. WBC 17k, lactate 2.1. CXR: focal RLL consolidation.

Diagnosis and disposition?CAP. By vital signs and age (CURB-65), manage in the hospital.
Bugs?S. pneumoniae (most common), GNRs (H. flu, Moraxella), atypicals.
Drugs?Ceftriaxone + azithromycin, or a respiratory FQ (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin).
What if his QTc were 560?Ceftriaxone + doxycycline — doxycycline gives atypical coverage without QT prolongation.
What if he had a history of MRSA colonization?Ceftriaxone/azithromycin ± vancomycin (add vanc especially if severe). Prior MRSA/Pseudomonas — or hospitalization + IV abx within 90 d — makes him MDR-risk.

Case 2 — Skin/soft tissue

30-year-old with IVDU, painful swollen L arm 2 days after injecting. T 99 °F, HR 100s, BP 100/60. L arm red, warm, tender, with a 2×2 cm area of fluctuance.

Diagnosis?Purulent SSTI (abscess with surrounding cellulitis).
Bugs?S. aureus, MRSA > MSSA.
Drugs?Antibiotics + I&D. PO MRSA coverage with Bactrim or doxycycline (generally preferred over clindamycin). Added Strep coverage with cephalexin can be considered in patients at high risk of endocarditis.

Case 3 — Intra-abdominal

21-year-old, abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting. T 103 °F, HR 140s, BP 95/55, SaO₂ 99% RA. Rigid abdomen with rebound. CT: appendicitis.

Bugs?Enteric GNRs, streptococci, enteric anaerobes (Bacteroides).
Drugs?Ceftriaxone + metronidazole (community-acquired IAI), plus source control (appendectomy).
Two weeks after appendectomy + antibiotics, returns with fevers, abdominal pain, and an intra-abdominal abscess. Now what?This is now hospital/healthcare-associated: Pseudomonas, Enterococcus, and S. aureus are more likely. Broaden (e.g., Zosyn ± vancomycin) and drain the abscess (source control).

Case 4a — UTI

65-year-old with prior stroke (L hemiparesis, residual dysphagia) presents from SNF with confusion. T 100.5 °F, HR 110s, BP 110/60s. Grimaces with suprapubic and L-flank palpation. WBC 17k, lactate 2.1. CXR clear. UA: +LE, 50 WBCs, many bacteria.

Diagnosis?Suspected pyelonephritis / complicated UTI.
Bugs?E. coli (75–95% of UTIs); other enteric GNRs and some Staph spp.
Drugs?Ceftriaxone. Although the SNF stay is an MDR risk factor, it is reasonable to start narrower here and broaden by cultures.
What if he had a chronic indwelling catheter?This is a CAUTI. Add Pseudomonas and Enterococcus to the differential: IV ceftriaxone, or IV Zosyn for broader coverage.

Case 4b — Pneumonia (same patient, later)

The same 65-year-old (prior stroke) is readmitted 2 months later with cough and dyspnea, admitted to the ICU. CXR: RLL consolidation.

Diagnosis?CAP vs. aspiration pneumonia (dysphagia history).
Bugs?Hospitalization + IV abx within 90 d is a risk factor for MRSA/Pseudomonas; CAP bugs (S. pneumoniae, GNRs, atypicals) and anaerobes are also possible.
Drugs?Vancomycin + cefepime + azithromycin. Severe pneumonia gets empiric MRSA + Pseudomonas coverage; routine anaerobic coverage is not necessary.
What if CT showed a cavitary lesion concerning for lung abscess?Add metronidazole (or switch to vancomycin + Zosyn + azithromycin) to cover anaerobes — anaerobic coverage is indicated for lung abscess or empyema.

Case 5 — Skin/soft tissue (the trap)

33-year-old, rapidly ascending L-leg erythema starting 12 h prior. T 103 °F, HR 150s, BP 80s/40s. Deep erythema ankle → mid-thigh with duskiness; tenderness beyond the erythema; no fluctuance. Na 123, Cr 3.5 (baseline 1.0), WBC 25k, lactate 6.7.

Diagnosis?Necrotizing soft-tissue infection — systemic toxicity, rapid spread, pain/tenderness beyond the visible margin, duskiness, and a high LRINEC-type lab profile.
Bugs?GAS, S. aureus, GNRs, anaerobes (Clostridium); may be polymicrobial.
Drugs?Vancomycin + Zosyn/meropenem + clindamycin — and consult surgery immediately. Clindamycin adds antitoxin activity. Definitive diagnosis and treatment is surgical exploration/debridement.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 68-year-old man is admitted with CAP. He has no history of MRSA or Pseudomonas and has not been hospitalized or received IV antibiotics in the past year. His nephew, a student, suggests "HCAP coverage" because he lives in an assisted-living facility. What is the most appropriate empiric regimen?

  1. Vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam
  2. Ceftriaxone + azithromycin
  3. Meropenem
  4. Vancomycin + cefepime + azithromycin
Answer & explanationB. The 2019 IDSA/ATS guideline abolished HCAP; residence in a facility is no longer, by itself, a reason for broad-spectrum coverage. Empiric MRSA/Pseudomonas coverage (A, D) is reserved for prior MRSA/Pseudomonas isolation or hospitalization + IV antibiotics within 90 days. Carbapenem monotherapy (C) is excessive and misses atypicals. Standard inpatient CAP therapy is a beta-lactam + macrolide (or a respiratory fluoroquinolone).

2. A 74-year-old woman with a chronic indwelling Foley is admitted for a hip fracture. Routine urine culture grows 10⁵ CFU/mL E. coli. She is afebrile with no urinary symptoms and a normal WBC. What is the best next step?

  1. Ceftriaxone for 7 days
  2. Nitrofurantoin for 5 days
  3. No antibiotics; do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria
  4. Exchange the catheter and start fosfomycin
Answer & explanationC. CAUTI requires symptoms or signs of infection; this is asymptomatic catheter-associated bacteriuria, which is not treated (treating it drives resistance and C. difficile, without benefit). Exceptions are pregnancy and pre-urologic procedures with anticipated mucosal trauma — neither applies. All antibiotic options (A, B, D) are therefore wrong; nitrofurantoin/fosfomycin would also be inappropriate if true pyelonephritis were present.

3. A 40-year-old man has rapidly spreading leg erythema, pain out of proportion to exam extending beyond the visible margin, a dusky bulla, and hypotension. Lactate is 6. What single intervention most improves survival?

  1. Adding clindamycin to vancomycin and piperacillin-tazobactam
  2. Urgent surgical exploration and debridement
  3. IVIG
  4. Hyperbaric oxygen
Answer & explanationB. NSTI is a surgical emergency; early, aggressive debridement is the dominant determinant of survival. Broad antibiotics with clindamycin for antitoxin effect (A) are necessary but adjunctive. IVIG (C) and hyperbaric oxygen (D) are at best unproven adjuncts and must never delay the OR. Antibiotics cannot sterilize dead, non-perfused tissue.

4. A 60-year-old with diverticulitis and a contained perforation is started on empiric antibiotics. He was hospitalized last month and received 6 days of broad-spectrum antibiotics. Which pathogens must your empiric regimen now additionally cover compared with a first-presentation community case?

  1. Atypical bacteria (Mycoplasma, Legionella)
  2. Pseudomonas and Enterococcus
  3. MRSA only
  4. Anaerobes only
Answer & explanationB. Recent hospitalization and broad-spectrum antibiotic exposure make this healthcare-associated IAI: empiric coverage must add Pseudomonas and Enterococcus (e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam, ± vancomycin for Enterococcus/MRSA risk). Atypicals (A) are respiratory pathogens, irrelevant to IAI. Anaerobes (D) are already covered in any IAI regimen. MRSA alone (C) misses the key additions. Source control remains essential.

5. A 25-year-old woman has dysuria and frequency for 2 days, no fever or flank pain, and is otherwise healthy. Local E. coli resistance to TMP-SMX is 12%. Which is the most appropriate first-line therapy?

  1. Ciprofloxacin for 7 days
  2. Nitrofurantoin for 5 days
  3. Amoxicillin for 5 days
  4. Ceftriaxone IV once, then oral cephalexin
Answer & explanationB. Uncomplicated cystitis — first-line options are nitrofurantoin ×5 d, TMP-SMX ×3 d (appropriate here since resistance <20%), or fosfomycin ×1. Fluoroquinolones (A) are effective but reserved as second-line due to collateral harm and longer-than-needed course. Amoxicillin alone (C) has unacceptable resistance. IV/parenteral therapy (D) is unnecessary for simple cystitis. Note: 2024 WikiGuidelines make these definitions non-sex-dependent, but management of simple cystitis is unchanged.

Take-Home Points

  • Healthcare-associated infections add MRSA, Pseudomonas, and Enterococcus — escalate empiric coverage only with risk factors, then de-escalate by culture.
  • CAP = S. pneumoniae + GNRs + atypicals → ceftriaxone + azithromycin (or respiratory FQ). HAP/VAP (>48 h) additionally covers MRSA and Pseudomonas.
  • Aspiration pneumonia and former-"HCAP" patients are generally treated as routine CAP; add anaerobic coverage only for lung abscess or empyema.
  • CAUTI is symptomatic bacteriuria with a catheter (or one within 48 h); E. coli predominates, plus S. aureus/Pseudomonas/Enterococcus. Don't treat asymptomatic bacteriuria; never use nitrofurantoin/fosfomycin for pyelonephritis.
  • Nonpurulent SSTI = Strep; purulent SSTI = S. aureus/MRSA (I&D first). NSTI is a surgical emergency.
  • Community IAI = enteric GNRs, strep, gut anaerobes; hospital IAI adds Pseudomonas and Enterococcus. Always pair antibiotics with source control.
