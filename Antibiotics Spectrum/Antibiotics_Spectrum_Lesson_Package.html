Learning Objectives

  1. Identify the spectrum of coverage for the most commonly used inpatient antibiotics, class by class.
  2. Use the "4 questions" framework (GPC including MRSA/Enterococcus; GNR including Pseudomonas/ESBL; atypicals; anaerobes including B. fragilis) to reason about any antibiotic.
  3. Optional: Recognize the agents that specifically cover MRSA and how to choose among them.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What single difference separates ampicillin-sulbactam (Unasyn) from piperacillin-tazobactam (Zosyn) in spectrum? Piperacillin-tazobactam adds Pseudomonas coverage. Both cover GPCs, many enteric GNRs, and gut anaerobes; pip-tazo is the broadest penicillin and the antipseudomonal one.
Q2. Which carbapenem does NOT cover Pseudomonas, and what's its niche? Ertapenem — no Pseudomonas coverage, but once-daily dosing makes it a convenient first-line agent for ESBL E. coli infections. Meropenem/imipenem/doripenem add Pseudomonas.
Q3. Why should daptomycin never be used for MRSA pneumonia? It is inactivated by pulmonary surfactant, so it doesn't work in the lung. Use vancomycin or linezolid (often preferred) for MRSA pneumonia instead.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the corrected interactive antibiogram board (Antibiotics_Spectrum_Optimized.pptx) and click through each class, or reproduce the antibiogram grid on a whiteboard. Hand learners the blank worksheet so they fill in the grid as you reveal each row. Talk ~20–25 min. This is Part 1 (spectrum); Part 2 covers sites of infection.

Hook (2 min)

"Your patient needs antibiotics. Before you reach for Zosyn-and-vanc reflexively — what four yes/no questions should decide the drug?" Build the framework, then hang every class on it. The whole talk is reading one antibiogram, column by column.

The 4 questions 3 min

ASK: "What four coverage questions do you ask of any antibiotic?" Reveal the spine of the antibiogram:
  1. Gram positives (Staph, Strep)? → and does it cover MRSA? Enterococcus?
  2. Gram negatives (enteric GNRs)? → and Pseudomonas? ESBLs?
  3. Atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila, Legionella)?
  4. Anaerobes? → and specifically B. fragilis (gut anaerobes)?

Penicillins 4 min

General rules: no MRSA, no atypicals; GN coverage increases across the classes.

Natural penicillin (Pen G/VK) — 1st-line for Strep (GAS, susceptible S. pneumoniae), syphilis, many oral anaerobes; high Staph resistance. Antistaphylococcal (nafcillin, dicloxacillin, oxacillin) — 1st-line MSSA. Aminopenicillin + beta-lactamase inhibitor (amp-sulbactam, amox-clav) — adds Staph, many enteric GNRs (E. coli, H. flu, Moraxella), gut anaerobes; 1st-line for E. faecalis. Pip-tazo — broadest, adds Pseudomonas.

Mnemonic: E. faecaLESS is LESS resistant (~90% ampicillin-sensitive, rarely VRE); E. faecium is more resistant (~10% ampicillin-sensitive, more often VRE).

Cephalosporins 4 min

General rules: no Enterococcus, no atypicals; GN coverage rises by generation (except the special 5th).

1st gen (cefazolin/cephalexin) — good GP, MSSA, minimal GN. 2nd gen (cefuroxime, cefotetan, cefoxitin) — more GN; cephamycins (cefotetan, cefoxitin) add some anaerobes. 3rd gen — ceftriaxone/cefotaxime/cefpodoxime (Strep + GNR, no anaerobes) vs ceftazidime (adds Pseudomonas, no Staph, poor CNS — not for meningitis). 4th gen cefepime — Pseudomonas + AmpC. Ceftaroline ("5th gen") — the one cephalosporin with MRSA activity.

Pearl: No oral cephalosporin covers Pseudomonas.

Carbapenems 3 min

Broadest class (GP, GN, anaerobe), most reliable ESBL coverage; no MRSA/atypical, poor Enterococcus. Ertapenem — once-daily, no Pseudomonas, first-line for ESBL E. coli. Meropenem/imipenem/doripenem — add Pseudomonas.

Fluoroquinolones 3 min

Agent"Type"Key spectrum
CiprofloxacinUrinaryGN/PsA; poor Strep → not for respiratory infections; 2nd anti-PsA / atypical
LevofloxacinUrinary + respiratoryAdds GP (MSSA, S. pneumoniae); good for low-risk respiratory/GU

Class harms: QT prolongation, aortic aneurysm/dissection, tendon rupture, encephalopathy, C. difficile — reserve FQs.

Anti-pseudomonal (non-beta-lactam) & macrolides 2 min

Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, plazomicin) — aerobic GNR + PsA, no anaerobe/atypical; oto-/nephrotoxic; gentamicin adds GP synergy for enterococcal endocarditis. Aztreonam — monobactam, aerobic GN + PsA only; safe in penicillin allergy except ceftazidime (shared side chain). Macrolides (azithromycin) — atypicals; rising Strep resistance; fidaxomicin for C. diff.

Anti-MRSA agents 3 min

ASK: "Community vs hospital MRSA — name the agents for each." Reveal:
SettingAgents & notes
CA-MRSA (PO)Clindamycin (antitoxin; C. diff risk; B. fragilis resistant); TMP-SMX (S. aureus/GN; variable Strep — add a beta-lactam for nonpurulent cellulitis); doxycycline (Staph/atypical; minimal Strep)
HA-MRSA (IV)Vancomycin (1st-line; also Enterococcus/CoNS); daptomycin (not for pneumonia — surfactant inactivation; rhabdo); linezolid (PO/IV; 1st-line VRE; often preferred for MRSA pneumonia; myelosuppression, serotonin syndrome)

Retrieval close (3 min)

Erase the grid. Call out an organism (MRSA, Pseudomonas, ESBL E. coli, B. fragilis, Enterococcus faecium) and have learners name two agents that cover it and one common drug that does not. Then run the take-home escalation ladders from memory.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A patient with a complicated intra-abdominal infection needs empiric coverage including Pseudomonas and gut anaerobes with a single agent. Which is most appropriate?

  1. Ceftriaxone
  2. Ertapenem
  3. Piperacillin-tazobactam
  4. Ampicillin-sulbactam
Answer & explanationC. Piperacillin-tazobactam covers GPCs, enteric GNRs, gut anaerobes, and Pseudomonas in one agent. Ceftriaxone (A) lacks anaerobic and Pseudomonas coverage. Ertapenem (B) covers anaerobes but not Pseudomonas. Ampicillin-sulbactam (D) covers anaerobes but not Pseudomonas (and has rising E. coli resistance).

2. A patient with MRSA pneumonia is started on daptomycin. The pharmacist pages you to change it. Why?

  1. Daptomycin doesn't cover MRSA
  2. Daptomycin is inactivated by pulmonary surfactant
  3. Daptomycin causes nephrotoxicity
  4. Daptomycin has no IV formulation
Answer & explanationB. Daptomycin is inactivated by surfactant and is ineffective in the lung — never use it for pneumonia. It does cover MRSA elsewhere (A wrong). Its characteristic toxicity is rhabdomyolysis, not nephrotoxicity (C). It is IV (D). Use vancomycin or linezolid (often preferred) for MRSA pneumonia.

3. A urine culture grows ESBL E. coli in a stable patient without Pseudomonas risk. Which is the most appropriate first-line agent?

  1. Ceftriaxone
  2. Piperacillin-tazobactam
  3. Ertapenem
  4. Cefepime
Answer & explanationC. Carbapenems are the most reliable ESBL coverage; ertapenem (once-daily, no Pseudomonas needed here) is a first-line choice for ESBL E. coli. ESBLs are by definition resistant to 3rd-gen cephalosporins like ceftriaxone (A). Pip-tazo (B) and cefepime (D) are less reliable against ESBLs and generally avoided for serious ESBL infection.

4. A patient with severe penicillin anaphylaxis needs Gram-negative/Pseudomonas coverage. Which agent is safest, and what is the one cross-reactivity caveat?

  1. Cefepime; cross-reacts with all penicillins
  2. Aztreonam; avoid if a ceftazidime allergy due to a shared side chain
  3. Meropenem; no caveats
  4. Ampicillin-sulbactam; safe in penicillin allergy
Answer & explanationB. Aztreonam (a monobactam) covers aerobic GNs and Pseudomonas with minimal beta-lactam cross-reactivity — except ceftazidime, which shares an identical side chain. Cefepime (A) and ampicillin-sulbactam (D) are beta-lactams to avoid in true anaphylaxis; carbapenems (C) carry a low but real cross-reactivity risk.

5. Which fluoroquinolone is the best choice when reliable anaerobic coverage (e.g., aspiration) is desired, and which should NOT be used for a respiratory infection?

  1. Moxifloxacin best for anaerobes; ciprofloxacin not for respiratory infections
  2. Ciprofloxacin best for anaerobes; levofloxacin not for respiratory
  3. Levofloxacin best for anaerobes; moxifloxacin not for respiratory
  4. All fluoroquinolones cover anaerobes equally
Answer & explanationA. Moxifloxacin has the broadest anaerobic coverage of the FQs (useful for GI/aspiration) — but weak Pseudomonas and poor renal penetration. Ciprofloxacin has poor Strep coverage, so it is not used empirically for respiratory infections. Levofloxacin and moxifloxacin are the "respiratory" FQs; ciprofloxacin is "urinary."

Spectrum-reasoning and escalation template. * = wildcard to edit at the bedside; brackets = pick-lists. 

#Abx spectrum reasoning: target * [GPC / GNR / atypical / anaerobe], +MRSA? * , +Enterococcus? * , +PsA? * , +ESBL? * , +gut anaerobe (B.frag)? * . PCN allergy * [none / rash / anaphylaxis]. renal/wt dose-adjust * .
- GPC ladder (MRSA/Enterococcus): MSSA/Strep [nafcillin/cefazolin] -> +MRSA [vancomycin] -> [daptomycin (NOT lung)] -> [linezolid (PO/IV, VRE, MRSA-PNA)] -> [ceftaroline (only BL w/ MRSA)]. E.faecalis [ampicillin]; E.faecium/VRE [linezolid/daptomycin].
- GNR ladder (PsA/ESBL): [ceftriaxone] -> +PsA [ceftazidime/cefepime/pip-tazo] -> ESBL [ERTAPENEM (no PsA) / meropenem (+PsA)] -> CRE/MDR ID-only [ceftazidime-avibactam, meropenem-vaborbactam, cefiderocol]. severe PCN allergy -> [aztreonam (avoid if ceftazidime allergy) / aminoglycoside].
- atypicals: [azithromycin / doxycycline / respiratory FQ (levo/moxi)]. cipro = poor Strep, NOT respiratory.
- anaerobes: gut/B.frag [metronidazole / pip-tazo / carbapenem / cefotetan-cefoxitin / moxifloxacin]. oral [pen G / clindamycin / amox-clav].
- key traps: ertapenem NO PsA; no oral cephalosporin covers PsA; daptomycin NOT for pneumonia; ceftazidime poor CNS (not meningitis); cipro not for respiratory; carbapenems poor Enterococcus; aztreonam cross-reacts w/ ceftazidime.
- de-escalate to culture/sensitivities ASAP; consult ID for advanced agents. confirm vs local antibiogram.

Take-Home Points

  • For any antibiotic, ask the 4 questions: GPCs (incl. MRSA, Enterococcus), GNRs (incl. Pseudomonas, ESBL), atypicals, anaerobes (incl. gut/B. fragilis).
  • GPC / MRSA / Enterococcus escalation: vancomycin → daptomycin → linezolid → ceftaroline.
  • GNR / Pseudomonas / ESBL escalation: levofloxacin → ceftazidime → cefepime → piperacillin-tazobactam → meropenem; use an aminoglycoside or aztreonam for severe penicillin allergy.
  • Remember the traps: ertapenem has no Pseudomonas coverage; no oral cephalosporin covers Pseudomonas; daptomycin fails in the lung; ceftazidime shouldn't treat meningitis; ciprofloxacin isn't for respiratory infections.
  • Always confirm empiric choices against local susceptibility data, and consult ID for advanced/MDR agents.
