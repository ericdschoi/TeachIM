Learning Objectives

  1. Identify risk factors for C. diff infection (CDI), including community-acquired CDI.
  2. Diagnose CDI and recognize the value and limitations of each stool test.
  3. Differentiate non-severe, severe, fulminant, and recurrent CDI.
  4. Manage non-severe, severe, fulminant, and recurrent CDI.

Pre-work send the day before

Three retrieval questions to prime the session. Answers hidden — try first, then check.

1. Why shouldn’t you test an asymptomatic patient for C. diff?

Colonization is common (4–15% of healthy adults, ~21% of hospitalized adults, 15–30% of LTC residents). A positive test in an asymptomatic patient reflects colonization, not infection — test only patients with ≥3 unformed stools/24 h (or ileus with clinical concern).

2. What two lab values define SEVERE CDI?

WBC >15,000 OR creatinine ≥1.5 mg/dL. Fulminant adds hypotension/shock or ileus/megacolon.

3. What is the preferred first-line antibiotic for an initial episode of CDI in current guidelines?

Fidaxomicin (preferred over vancomycin per the 2021 IDSA/SHEA focused update). PO vancomycin remains an acceptable first-line alternative. Metronidazole is reserved for low-risk non-severe disease when neither is available.

Teaching Script restructured into timed segments

Hook (0:00–0:02)

"Your hospitalized patient has a positive C. diff PCR. Treat or not? It depends entirely on one thing the lab can’t tell you — are they actually having diarrhea? Up to a fifth of inpatients are colonized." The talk builds the discipline of testing the right patient, classifying severity, and picking the right drug.

ASK — "Who should we test for C. diff, and who should we NOT?" Anchor on symptomatic-only testing.

Objective 1 — Epidemiology & risk factors (0:02–0:05) 3 min

Distinguish colonization (detection without symptoms — common) from infection. Community-acquired CDI is rising (~40% of cases).

Classic risk factorsNotable for community-acquired CDI
Age >65, healthcare exposure, antibiotic useIBD, cardiac disease, CKD — and younger patients without recent hospitalization
Pearl. A positive test never makes the diagnosis by itself — symptoms do. Sending C. diff on formed stool or in an asymptomatic patient generates false "infections" and unnecessary treatment.

Objective 2 — Diagnosis (0:05–0:09) 4 min

Test when: new diarrhea (≥3 unformed stools/24 h) OR ileus (a rectal swab works in ileus). Two-step algorithm:

TestDetectsPerformance
GDH antigenEnzyme made by ALL C. diff strains (toxigenic or not)High sensitivity (94–96%); can’t distinguish toxin vs non-toxin strains
Toxin A/B (EIA/PCR)Toxins from toxigenic strainsHigh specificity (94–98%); false negatives with poor specimen handling
C. diff NAATGene encoding toxinConfirms a toxigenic strain; can’t distinguish colonization from infection — use to resolve discordant GDH/toxin results

Algorithm: GDH + / toxin + → CDI. GDH − / toxin − → rule out. Discordant/indeterminate → confirm with NAAT.

Pearl. Do NOT repeat stool tests within 7 days, and do NOT test for cure — shedding persists in ~56% after symptoms resolve, and post-infectious IBS occurs in up to 25%.

Objective 3 — Severity classification (0:09–0:11) 2 min

ASK — "What separates severe from fulminant CDI?"
ClassDefinition
Non-severeAbsence of severe criteria
SevereWBC >15,000 OR Cr ≥1.5 mg/dL
FulminantSevere PLUS hypotension/shock OR ileus/megacolon
RecurrentRecurrent diarrhea + confirmatory test within 8 weeks of the initial episode (~20% of patients)

Objective 4 — Treatment (0:11–0:15) 4 min

ScenarioTreatment
Non-severe / severe (initial)Fidaxomicin 200 mg BID ×10 d (preferred) or PO vancomycin 125 mg q6h ×10 d. PO metronidazole only if neither available (low-risk non-severe).
FulminantPO vancomycin 500 mg q6h (PR if ileus) + IV metronidazole 500 mg q8h; surgical consult if no improvement; FMT if not a surgical candidate.
1st recurrenceFidaxomicin (preferred) or tapered/pulsed PO vancomycin, or standard PO vancomycin.
≥2 recurrencesTapered/pulsed vancomycin, vancomycin→rifaximin, or FMT / microbiota-based therapy (see update). Refer.
ASK — "Once you’ve treated, how do you know they’re cured?" (Trick: you don’t re-test — judge clinically.)

Prevention: contact + enteric precautions until 48 h after diarrhea resolves; consider low-dose PO vancomycin 125 mg daily prophylaxis in high-risk patients on systemic antibiotics. Probiotics and stopping PPIs are NOT recommended as preventive measures.

Retrieval close (0:15) 1 min

Board off: "Rebuild who to test, the two-step algorithm, the severe/fulminant definitions, and the preferred first-line drug." Fill gaps from what they reconstruct.

2023–2025 Treatment Update

The standing teaching (fidaxomicin-preferred, severity definitions, two-step testing) is current. Two material changes since publication are surfaced here and on the appended deck slide:

  • Bezlotoxumab discontinued. Merck discontinued bezlotoxumab (Zinplava) effective January 31, 2025 — it is no longer commercially available in the US. Where the deck lists "± bezlotoxumab" for recurrence prevention, treat that option as unavailable.
  • FDA-approved live biotherapeutics for preventing recurrent CDI (given after the CDI antibiotic course completes) now standardize what the deck calls "FMT": Rebyota (fecal microbiota, live-jslm; rectal; approved Nov 2022) and Vowst (fecal microbiota spores, live-brpk; oral; approved Apr 2023). Conventional FMT remains an option, usually by referral.
  • Unchanged: fidaxomicin preferred (2021 IDSA/SHEA; 2021 ACG); severity thresholds; symptomatic-only testing; no test-of-cure.

Cases original cases · sub-questions click-to-reveal

Case 1 — From acute to severe to recurrent CDI

33-year-old with fever, abdominal pain, and 4–5 watery BMs/day for 4 days, recently treated for a UTI with Bactrim. VS: T 37.4 °C, HR 110, BP 100/50s. Na 128, BUN 18, Cr 1.7, WBC 18. Stool: GDH Ag +, toxin PCR − (culture pending).

What is the diagnosis and what test resolves it?

Likely CDI, but GDH+/toxin− is discordant/indeterminate → send C. diff NAAT to confirm or exclude. (NAAT returns positive.)

How is the severity classified?

Severe CDI — Cr >1.5 (1.7) and WBC >15k (18). Treat with fidaxomicin (preferred) or PO vancomycin.

Treated with a prolonged PO vancomycin course; 2 weeks after completion, diarrhea returns.

What is the diagnosis now, and the management?

Recurrent (non-severe) CDI — symptoms with a positive test within 8 weeks of the initial episode. Manage with fidaxomicin (preferred) or tapered/pulsed PO vancomycin.

Case 2 — Fulminant CDI

82-year-old with recurrent UTIs admitted with fever, abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting, and constipation/obstipation ×2 d. VS: T 38.4 °C, HR 110, BP 80/50s. Na 128, BUN 18, Cr 1.7, WBC 18. KUB shows ileus.

How would you diagnose CDI here, and what is the classification?

Use a rectal swab (the patient has ileus). This is fulminant CDI — severe criteria plus hypotension and ileus.

What is the management?

PO vancomycin 500 mg q6h (can give by NG tube) + IV metronidazole 500 mg q8h + PR vancomycin 500 mg q6h (because of ileus). Obtain a surgical consult; consider FMT if not a surgical candidate.

Case 3 — Recurrence and prevention

33-year-old recently treated for severe CDI with 10 days of PO vancomycin returns 5 weeks later with recurrent watery diarrhea, no interval antibiotics. C. diff testing positive; afebrile, stable vitals, normal CBC/BMP.

What is the diagnosis and management?

Recurrent (non-severe) CDI. Treat with fidaxomicin (preferred) or tapered/pulsed PO vancomycin.

Two months later they’re readmitted for a UTI. How do you prevent another recurrence?

They’re high-risk for recurrence with new antibiotics — consider PO vancomycin 125 mg daily for secondary prophylaxis while on systemic antibiotics (continue 5 days after finishing them). For durable prevention after the CDI course, microbiota-based therapy (Rebyota/Vowst/FMT) is now available. Bezlotoxumab is no longer available.

Board-style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 70-year-old inpatient on day 4 of ceftriaxone has one formed stool and a routine C. diff PCR sent by the night team returns positive. He has no diarrhea. What is the best next step?

  1. Start fidaxomicin
  2. Start oral vancomycin
  3. No CDI treatment — this likely reflects colonization
  4. Send C. diff NAAT to confirm
  5. Start IV metronidazole
Answer & explanation

C. Without diarrhea, a positive test reflects colonization, not infection — don’t treat, and don’t have sent the test in the first place. A/B/E wrong: treating colonization is inappropriate. D wrong: more testing won’t change that there are no symptoms to treat.

2. A 60-year-old with CDI has WBC 19,000 and Cr 1.8 but normal blood pressure and no ileus. How is this classified and treated?

  1. Non-severe — PO metronidazole
  2. Severe — fidaxomicin (preferred) or PO vancomycin
  3. Fulminant — PO + PR vancomycin and IV metronidazole
  4. Severe — IV vancomycin
  5. Recurrent — FMT
Answer & explanation

B. WBC >15k or Cr ≥1.5 = severe; without hypotension/shock or ileus/megacolon it is not fulminant. Treat with fidaxomicin (preferred) or PO vancomycin. A wrong: not non-severe, and metronidazole isn’t first-line. C wrong: no fulminant features. D wrong: IV vancomycin doesn’t reach the colonic lumen — useless for CDI. E wrong: this is a first episode.

3. A patient with fulminant CDI has an ileus. Which regimen is most appropriate?

  1. IV vancomycin + IV metronidazole
  2. PO vancomycin 125 mg q6h alone
  3. PO vancomycin 500 mg q6h + PR vancomycin 500 mg q6h + IV metronidazole
  4. Fidaxomicin 200 mg BID alone
  5. Oral metronidazole alone
Answer & explanation

C. Fulminant CDI with ileus needs high-dose PO vancomycin (500 mg q6h) PLUS PR vancomycin (drug may not reach the colon orally with ileus) PLUS IV metronidazole; consult surgery. A wrong: IV vancomycin doesn’t enter the gut lumen. B/D/E wrong: inadequate for fulminant disease.

4. A patient finishes treatment for CDI and feels well, but is about to start an unrelated course of antibiotics. Which is most appropriate for preventing recurrence?

  1. Repeat C. diff testing to confirm cure first
  2. Add a probiotic
  3. Stop the patient’s PPI
  4. Consider PO vancomycin 125 mg daily prophylaxis while on the antibiotics
  5. Give a single dose of bezlotoxumab
Answer & explanation

D. Low-dose vancomycin prophylaxis during systemic antibiotics is reasonable for high-risk patients. A wrong: never test for cure (shedding persists). B/C wrong: probiotics and PPI discontinuation are not recommended preventive measures. E wrong: bezlotoxumab was discontinued (Jan 2025) and is unavailable.

5. A patient has had three episodes of recurrent CDI despite appropriate antibiotics. Which durable option is now FDA-approved for preventing further recurrence?

  1. Bezlotoxumab infusion
  2. An oral fecal microbiota spore product (Vowst) or rectal fecal microbiota (Rebyota)
  3. Long-term daily metronidazole
  4. IV immunoglobulin
  5. Rifaximin monotherapy indefinitely
Answer & explanation

B. Vowst (oral, 2023) and Rebyota (rectal, 2022) are FDA-approved live biotherapeutics for preventing recurrent CDI after an antibiotic course; conventional FMT is also an option. A wrong: bezlotoxumab is discontinued. C/E wrong: chronic metronidazole or rifaximin monotherapy is not a standard durable prevention strategy. D wrong: IVIG is not standard.

Take-Home Points

  1. Colonization is common — test ONLY symptomatic patients, and never test for cure.
  2. Diagnose with a two-step algorithm: GDH + toxin A/B; confirm discordant results with NAAT.
  3. Severe = WBC >15k or Cr ≥1.5; fulminant = severe + hypotension/shock or ileus/megacolon; recurrence = symptoms + positive test within 8 weeks.
  4. Fidaxomicin is preferred first-line for non-severe, severe, and first-recurrence CDI; PO vancomycin is an acceptable alternative. Fulminant: high-dose PO ± PR vancomycin + IV metronidazole ± surgery.
  5. For durable recurrence prevention, use microbiota-based therapy (Rebyota, Vowst, or FMT). Bezlotoxumab is no longer available.
