Learning Objectives

  1. Recognize and triage a COPD exacerbation, including the indications for admission and for NIV.
  2. Deliver the core bundle — bronchodilators, a 5-day oral steroid course, criteria-based antibiotics, and controlled oxygen to 88–92%.
  3. Identify NIV as the intervention with the largest mortality benefit in acute hypercapnic respiratory failure, and know when to intubate.
  4. Exclude mimics (pneumonia, HF, PE) and build a discharge plan that lowers recurrence.

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A COPD patient on 4 L nasal cannula has an SpO2 of 99% and is becoming drowsy. What happened, and what is your target SpO2?Hyperoxia worsened hypercapnia (V/Q mismatch, Haldane effect, reduced respiratory drive) — CO2 narcosis. Titrate oxygen down to a target SpO2 of 88–92% and check a blood gas.
Q2. Which three symptoms make up the Anthonisen criteria for antibiotics, and which one matters most?Increased dyspnea, increased sputum volume, and increased sputum purulence. Antibiotics are indicated with ≥2 of the three (or if mechanically ventilated); sputum purulence is the strongest single predictor of bacterial benefit.
Q3. How long a steroid course does the REDUCE trial support, and is IV better than oral?Five days of systemic steroids (prednisone 40 mg/day) was non-inferior to 14 days, with no taper needed; oral is equivalent to IV for most patients who can take PO.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"Your COPD patient is working hard to breathe, sats 84%, drowsy. The nurse turns the oxygen up to 6 liters and the sats hit 100% — and twenty minutes later he's harder to rouse. Two of your orders in the next ten minutes change whether he gets intubated: the oxygen target and the decision to start NIV." Set up the bundle plus the two big levers.

Step 1 — Triage & the oxygen target 6 min

ASK: "What's the SpO2 target in a COPD patient, and why is 100% dangerous?"

Define the exacerbation (acute increase in dyspnea/cough/sputum). Decide admission (rest dyspnea, severe baseline disease, failed outpatient therapy, new gas derangement, poor support). Then the oxygen teaching point: target 88–92%. Over-oxygenation worsens hypercapnia through V/Q mismatch, the Haldane effect, and blunted drive — the cause of "CO2 narcosis." Get an early ABG/VBG to identify retainers.

Pearl: The pulse oximeter target in COPD is one of the few numbers where higher is worse.

Step 2 — The core bundle 8 min

ASK: "Steroids — how much, how long, IV or PO? And what triggers antibiotics?"

Scheduled short-acting bronchodilators (SABA ± SAMA). Systemic steroids: prednisone 40 mg PO for 5 days per the REDUCE trial — oral equals IV, no taper. Antibiotics by Anthonisen criteria: ≥2 of increased dyspnea/sputum-volume/purulence, or if ventilated; cover the usual respiratory pathogens for ~5 days and add antipseudomonal coverage only for risk factors. Always titrate oxygen to 88–92%.

Pearl: Purulence is the antibiotic trigger that carries the most weight — a green-sputum bump with dyspnea earns antibiotics; a dry cough with wheeze usually does not.

Step 3 — NIV: the mortality lever 7 min

ASK: "Of everything we do for a severe exacerbation, which single intervention most reduces mortality?"

NIV (BiPAP) in acute hypercapnic respiratory failure (pH ≤ 7.35 with hypercapnia) reduces intubation and death — the strongest evidence-based intervention in a severe exacerbation. Start it early; reassess with serial gases. Intubate for NIV failure, falling pH/mentation, inability to protect the airway, or instability. GOLD 2026 adds high-flow nasal cannula as a supportive option, but NIV remains first-line for hypercapnic acidosis.

Pearl: Hesitating on NIV is the classic missed lever — a patient with pH 7.28 and PaCO2 70 needs BiPAP now, not another neb and a recheck in an hour.

Step 4 — Mimics & discharge 2 min

Exclude pneumonia, heart failure, and especially PE in any unexplained exacerbation. Before discharge: inhaler technique, maintenance step-up (LAMA+LABA, add ICS if eosinophils up and ≥1 moderate/severe event per GOLD 2026), smoking cessation, vaccines, and a pulmonary-rehab referral within 4 weeks for its mortality benefit.

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — the oxygen trap

70M, severe COPD, brought in dyspneic, SpO2 82%. Placed on a non-rebreather; sats now 99% but he is somnolent. VBG: pH 7.26, PaCO2 78.

What happened?Hyperoxia-induced hypercapnia (CO2 narcosis) — too much oxygen worsened V/Q matching and drive.
Next steps?Titrate O2 down to 88–92%, start NIV for the hypercapnic acidosis (pH 7.26), bronchodilators, prednisone 40 mg, antibiotics if purulent. Reassess gas in 1 h.

Case 2 — antibiotics or not?

64F with moderate COPD: 3 days of more dyspnea and clearly purulent, higher-volume sputum. Afebrile, CXR clear. SpO2 90% on room air.

Antibiotics?Yes — she meets Anthonisen criteria (≥2 cardinal symptoms including purulence). Add a 5-day course covering H. flu/pneumococcus/Moraxella plus prednisone 40 mg × 5 days and bronchodilators.
If instead she had wheeze and dry cough, no purulence?Steroids + bronchodilators, but hold antibiotics — absent purulence and not ventilated, the bacterial yield is low.

Case 3 — the mimic

58M, COPD, sudden severe dyspnea and pleuritic pain, SpO2 88%, clear CXR, no sputum change, tachycardic. Minimal wheeze.

What must you exclude?Pulmonary embolism — sudden dyspnea without a sputum/purulence change, pleuritic pain, and tachycardia in a COPD patient should trigger PE work-up (Wells/D-dimer or CTPA). Don't anchor on "exacerbation."

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. A 72-year-old with COPD presents with increased dyspnea and a blood gas of pH 7.29, PaCO2 68, on controlled oxygen. He is alert. What intervention most reduces his risk of intubation and death?

  1. Increase FiO2 to achieve SpO2 of 98%
  2. Start non-invasive ventilation (BiPAP)
  3. IV aminophylline
  4. Immediate intubation
  5. A 14-day prednisone taper
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. NIV in acute hypercapnic respiratory failure (pH ≤ 7.35 with hypercapnia) is the intervention with the strongest mortality and intubation-reduction evidence. A: hyperoxia worsens hypercapnia. C: methylxanthines are not recommended (toxicity, no benefit). D: he is alert and a NIV candidate — intubation is for NIV failure. E: REDUCE supports 5 days, no taper.

2. Which patient most clearly warrants antibiotics for a COPD exacerbation?

  1. Increased dyspnea and wheeze with clear sputum
  2. Increased dyspnea, increased sputum volume, and new sputum purulence
  3. Dry cough and mild dyspnea, no sputum change
  4. Stable symptoms with an abnormal spirometry
  5. Isolated chest tightness, no sputum
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Anthonisen: antibiotics for ≥2 of increased dyspnea/sputum volume/purulence (or if ventilated); purulence is the strongest single indicator. A, C, E lack purulence and ≥2 criteria. D is not an acute exacerbation.

3. What is the evidence-based systemic corticosteroid regimen for most COPD exacerbations?

  1. IV methylprednisolone for 14 days with a taper
  2. Prednisone 40 mg orally daily for 5 days, no taper
  3. Prednisone 60 mg for 21 days
  4. Inhaled corticosteroid only
  5. No steroids unless intubated
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. The REDUCE trial showed 5 days of prednisone 40 mg/day is non-inferior to 14 days; oral equals IV for patients who can take PO; no taper needed for a short course. The others overtreat or undertreat.

4. A COPD patient on 5 L/min nasal cannula has SpO2 100% and rising drowsiness; VBG shows worsening hypercapnia. What is the best immediate action?

  1. Continue current oxygen, it is reassuring
  2. Titrate oxygen down to a target SpO2 of 88–92% and reassess, escalating to NIV if acidotic
  3. Give a fluid bolus
  4. Start a beta-blocker
  5. Order a chest CT first
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Hyperoxia precipitated CO2 retention; controlled oxygen to 88–92% and NIV for hypercapnic acidosis are the fixes. A: 100% is harmful here. C/D: irrelevant/harmful. E: stabilize gas exchange first.

5. A COPD patient with sudden severe dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia, a clear chest X-ray, and NO change in sputum is most concerning for which diagnosis?

  1. Typical bacterial exacerbation
  2. Pulmonary embolism
  3. Simple bronchospasm
  4. Anxiety
  5. Pneumonia
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. PE is a frequently missed mimic — sudden dyspnea, pleuritic pain, tachycardia, clear CXR, and no sputum change should prompt PE evaluation rather than reflexively treating an 'exacerbation.' A/C/E don't fit the abrupt pleuritic picture and absent sputum change; pneumonia would usually show an infiltrate.

Take-Home Points

  1. Target SpO2 88–92% — in COPD, over-oxygenation causes hypercapnia and narcosis.
  2. Core bundle: scheduled bronchodilators, prednisone 40 mg PO × 5 days (REDUCE, no taper), and antibiotics only for ≥2 Anthonisen criteria or ventilation.
  3. NIV is the single biggest mortality lever in acute hypercapnic respiratory failure — start it early for pH ≤ 7.35 with hypercapnia.
  4. Always exclude mimics — pneumonia, heart failure, and especially PE in an unexplained exacerbation.
  5. Discharge to prevent the next event: inhaler technique, maintenance step-up, cessation, vaccines, and pulmonary rehab within 4 weeks.
