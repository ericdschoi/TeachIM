Learning Objectives

  1. Define the diagnostic criteria for COPD (and the role of spirometry).
  2. Classify patients using the 2023 GOLD ABE groups.
  3. Choose initial and step-up inhaler therapy by GOLD group.
  4. Categorize the benefits of non-pharmacologic therapies.
  5. Apply an evidence-based approach to identifying and treating outpatient COPD exacerbations.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What single test result is required to diagnose COPD, and what's the cutoff? Post-bronchodilator spirometry with FEV1/FVC <0.70 in a symptomatic patient with risk factors. You cannot diagnose COPD without PFTs. Supportive findings: scooped flow-volume loop, ↑TLC/RV, ↓DLCO.
Q2. In the 2023 GOLD ABE scheme, which patients get a LABA+LAMA first-line, and when do you add an ICS? LABA+LAMA is first-line for Groups B and E. Add ICS (triple therapy) mainly for exacerbators with blood eosinophils ≥100; start with triple in Group E if eos ≥300. De-escalate ICS if recurrent pneumonia.
Q3. Which COPD interventions actually reduce mortality? Smoking cessation has the clearest mortality benefit; long-term oxygen in resting hypoxemia and vaccinations also help. Pulmonary rehab improves symptoms/exacerbations; lung-cancer screening reduces cancer mortality in eligible smokers.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the interactive board (COPD_Outpatient_Optimized.pptx) and click through each reveal — every cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt, so pose the question before clicking. Talk without cases: ~20–25 min; cases: +10–15 min.

Hook (2 min)

"Half the inhalers we prescribe are the wrong class for the patient in front of us — and we treat COPD exacerbations like pneumonia when most don't need it." Ask the room: name the one COPD intervention that reduces mortality. Park the answers — you'll confirm smoking cessation at Objective 4.

Objective 1 — Diagnose 5 min

ASK: "Give me three criteria for diagnosing COPD." Collect, then click through risk factors → symptoms → spirometry.

Risk factors: tobacco (and pipe/cigar/water-pipe/THC), other smoke/vapor/dust and occupational exposures, and host factors (genetics, prematurity, childhood respiratory infections). Symptoms: chronic progressive dyspnea, cough, sputum, recurrent LRTI. Diagnosis requires post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70 on spirometry — mandatory.

Pearl — Supportive PFTs: scooped (concave) flow-volume loop, ↑TLC and ↑RV (air trapping), ↓DLCO (emphysema; values <60% track with more symptoms and mortality). Post-bronchodilator FEV1 grades severity. New labels: pre-COPD (symptoms/structural lesions without obstruction) and PRISm (Preserved Ratio Impaired Spirometry) — both at risk of progressing.

Initial workup adds exposure history, ambulatory pulse-ox/ABG (ABG if SpO2 <92%), and alpha-1 antitrypsin once in every COPD patient (especially if <45 or panlobular basal emphysema). CXR excludes alternatives; CT for severe FEV1 reduction, symptoms out of proportion, or lung-cancer screening.

Objective 2 — Classify (2023 GOLD ABE) 4 min

ASK: "How would you sort COPD patients to decide therapy?" Reveal mMRC and CAT, then the groups.
GroupExacerbations (past year)Symptoms
A0–1 (no hospitalization)Low — mMRC 0–1 / CAT <10
B0–1 (no hospitalization)High — mMRC ≥2 / CAT ≥10
E≥2 moderate OR ≥1 requiring hospitalization(symptoms not used to split E)
Pearl — The 2023 ABE scheme replaced the old A/B/C/D grid. The key fork is exacerbation-prone or not, because that drives ICS decisions.

Objective 3 — Inhaler therapy by group 5 min

ASK: "First-line inhaler for a symptomatic Group B patient?" Take answers before revealing.

Group A: a single long-acting bronchodilator (LABA or LAMA), not a short-acting agent, except with very rare symptoms. Groups B and E: LABA + LAMA first-line. Add ICS (→ triple) for ongoing exacerbations with eos ≥100; start with triple in Group E if eos ≥300. Escalation options: azithromycin (esp. ex-smokers), roflumilast (FEV1 <50% + chronic bronchitis); advanced: LVRS, endobronchial valves, transplant.

AgentBest forCaveat
LABASymptom reliefLess effect on exacerbations
LAMASymptoms AND exacerbations (LAMA > LABA for exacerbations)—
ICSFewer exacerbations if eos elevated↑ pneumonia — de-escalate if recurrent PNA
Pearl — LABA-ICS combinations, though common, have no clear place in the GOLD schema. Whatever you pick, inhaler efficacy depends on technique and adherence — teach it every visit.

Objective 4 — Non-pharmacologic efficacy 4 min

ASK: "Which of these prevents death: smoking cessation, O2, pulm rehab, vaccines, lung-cancer screening?"

Smoking cessation is the standout — benefits across all COPD domains and the clearest mortality signal. Supplemental O2 for resting hypoxemia (PaO2 ≤55, or ≤59 + cor pulmonale/RHF) reduces symptoms and slows progression. Pulmonary rehab improves symptoms and exacerbation rate. Lung-cancer screening reduces mortality in eligible smokers. Vaccines (influenza, pneumococcal, COVID-19) reduce exacerbations and serious illness.

Objective 5 — Exacerbations 5 min

ASK: "Name the 3 cardinal symptoms of a COPD exacerbation." Reveal, then walk the outpatient management tree.

Cardinal symptoms: ↑ dyspnea, ↑ sputum volume, ↑ sputum purulence. First rule out mimics (PE, pneumonia, heart failure, ACS) — consider CXR, D-dimer, BNP, ECG/troponin, flu swab. Outpatient management:

InterventionWhen / how
BronchodilatorsSABA ± SAMA; MDIs as effective as nebulizers if used correctly
SteroidsPrednisone 40 mg ×5 days (REDUCE) — reduces relapse/symptoms
Antibiotics×5 days if ≥2 cardinal symptoms (incl. sputum purulence) or needs ventilation; agent per local resistance (e.g., azithromycin/doxycycline; anti-pseudomonal if high-risk)
NIPPVpH <7.35 with PaCO2 >45 (hospital)
Pearl — Hospitalize for severe exacerbation (resting dyspnea, rising O2 need, confusion), severe comorbidities, or inadequate home support.

Retrieval close (2 min)

Cover the board. Have learners rebuild: (1) the diagnostic spirometry cutoff, (2) the ABE groups, (3) the LABA+LAMA → +ICS logic, (4) the exacerbation triad and the steroid/antibiotic rules.

Cases original cases — sub-questions click-to-reveal

Case 1 — 60-year-old, 60 pack-years, progressive dyspnea

60yo, 60 pack-year smoker, progressive dyspnea and cough, breathless walking 100 ft on level ground, no prior hospitalizations.

What additional workup is needed?Spirometry (pre/post bronchodilator), CBC (eosinophils), CXR; rule out alternative diagnoses.
Results: post-BD FEV1 60%, FEV1/FVC 55%, eos 250, CXR hyperinflation. Diagnosis and group?COPD; mMRC ≥2 with 0–1 exacerbations = Group B.
Initial inhaler selection?A long-acting bronchodilator regimen — LABA+LAMA scheduled, plus PRN short-acting (SABA and/or SAMA). Plus smoking cessation, vaccines, and pulmonary rehab.

Case 2 — 60-year-old former smoker, acute exacerbation

60yo former smoker, COPD (FEV1 60%), 1 week of ↑ dyspnea and productive cough limiting strenuous (not daily) activity; 1 prior exacerbation; no Pseudomonas history. Meds: Combivent PRN + salmeterol. SpO2 90%, RR 20, diffuse wheeze.

Where should he be treated?Outpatient — he can still perform ADLs and has no severe features.
Acute management?Increase short-acting bronchodilators; prednisone 40 mg ×5 days; antibiotics ×5 days (e.g., doxycycline/azithromycin) if sputum purulence.
Long-term therapy change?His maintenance is a LABA alone — add a LAMA (LABA+LAMA). Consider ICS if eos >100 with continued exacerbations.

Case 3 — 80-year-old, severe COPD on home O2

80yo former smoker, COPD (FEV1 35%) on 2 L home O2, gradually worsening dyspnea affecting some ADLs, no exacerbations in the past year; no Pseudomonas. Meds: LABA-LAMA + PRN SABA.

Next step in chronic management?Observe inhaler technique, ensure O2 adherence, consider pulmonary rehab, and check eosinophils (consider ICS if eos >100 with frequent exacerbations); refer to pulmonology for advanced therapies (LVRS, endobronchial valves).
He is started on LABA-LAMA-ICS, then readmitted 2 months later with pneumonia + exacerbation. Now what?Rule out other causes; treat the exacerbation (steroids, antibiotics); and de-escalate the ICS given pneumonia. Consider sputum culture and anti-pseudomonal coverage given severe COPD.
Pulmonology declines advanced therapy given age; he is readmitted with another exacerbation and persistent dyspnea. Next step?Consider chronic azithromycin (pulm consult) and, importantly, a palliative care consult for symptom management and advance-care planning.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 58-year-old with a 40 pack-year history reports chronic cough and exertional dyspnea. Pre-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC is 0.66; after bronchodilator it is 0.68 with FEV1 62% predicted. What is the diagnosis?

  1. Asthma
  2. COPD (GOLD)
  3. Pre-COPD
  4. PRISm
  5. Normal spirometry
Answer & explanationB — COPD. A post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70 in a symptomatic patient with risk factors is diagnostic. A is unlikely given the largely fixed obstruction and smoking history. C (pre-COPD) requires symptoms/structural disease without obstruction (ratio ≥0.7); D (PRISm) is a preserved ratio (≥0.7) with reduced FEV1/FVC volumes — neither applies here. E is wrong: the ratio is below 0.70.

2. A patient with COPD has mMRC 3, CAT 18, and two moderate exacerbations in the past year. Which GOLD group and first-line inhaler regimen?

  1. Group B; LABA alone
  2. Group E; LABA+LAMA
  3. Group A; LAMA alone
  4. Group E; LABA+ICS
  5. Group B; triple therapy
Answer & explanationB — Group E, LABA+LAMA. ≥2 moderate exacerbations defines Group E regardless of symptom score. First-line is LABA+LAMA; add ICS only if blood eos ≥300 (initial) or for ongoing exacerbations with eos ≥100. LABA+ICS (D) has no clear place in GOLD. A and C under-treat; E starts triple without an eosinophil indication.

3. A Group E patient on LABA+LAMA continues to exacerbate. Blood eosinophils are 220 cells/µL. What is the best next step?

  1. Add an inhaled corticosteroid (triple therapy)
  2. Switch to LABA+ICS
  3. Start chronic prednisone
  4. Add dupilumab
  5. No change; eosinophils are too low to act
Answer & explanationA. For continued exacerbations with eos ≥100, escalate to triple therapy (LABA+LAMA+ICS). B abandons the LAMA (worse for exacerbations and not preferred over triple). C (chronic oral steroids) has no role in stable COPD. D (dupilumab) is reserved for ongoing exacerbations with eos ≥300 and chronic bronchitis on triple therapy. E is wrong — 220 is above the ≥100 escalation threshold.

4. A 64-year-old with COPD has 4 days of increased dyspnea, sputum volume, and purulence. He is afebrile, SpO2 91% on room air, speaking full sentences, and can do his ADLs. Best outpatient regimen?

  1. Short-acting bronchodilators only
  2. Prednisone 40 mg ×5 days + a 5-day antibiotic + increased short-acting bronchodilators
  3. Prednisone 40 mg ×14 days only
  4. Immediate hospitalization for IV steroids
  5. Antibiotics ×10 days only
Answer & explanationB. All three cardinal symptoms (including purulence) are present, so antibiotics are indicated; add prednisone 40 mg ×5 days (REDUCE) and step up short-acting bronchodilators. A omits proven therapy. C uses an unnecessarily long steroid course (5 days is sufficient). D is over-triage — he has no severe features. E omits steroids and uses an over-long antibiotic course.

5. Which COPD intervention has the best-established mortality benefit?

  1. Inhaled corticosteroids
  2. Roflumilast
  3. Smoking cessation
  4. LABA monotherapy
  5. Chronic azithromycin
Answer & explanationC — smoking cessation, the intervention with the clearest mortality benefit (and benefits across all COPD domains). Long-term oxygen in resting hypoxemia also improves survival. ICS, roflumilast, LABA, and chronic azithromycin reduce symptoms and/or exacerbations but are not established mortality-reducers in the general COPD population.

2024–2025 GOLD Practice Update NEW — projected on the appended deck slide

The deck's GOLD-2023 teaching (FEV1/FVC <0.7 diagnosis, ABE groups, LABA-LAMA first-line, eosinophil-guided ICS, 5-day steroid/antibiotic for exacerbations) was verified current in the GOLD 2024 and 2025 reports. Practice-relevant additions:

  • Triple therapy preferred: when ICS is indicated, GOLD recommends LABA+LAMA+ICS over LABA-ICS (superior exacerbations, lung function, quality of life).
  • Eosinophil thresholds (unchanged but emphasized): initial ICS in Group E if eos ≥300; escalate to triple for exacerbators with eos ≥100; ICS de-escalation more likely to trigger exacerbations if eos ≥300.
  • First biologic for COPD — dupilumab (Dupixent): add to triple therapy for ongoing exacerbations with blood eos ≥300 and chronic bronchitis. FDA-approved September 27, 2024 (BOREAS, NOTUS).
  • Novel inhaled class — ensifentrine (Ohtuvayre): dual PDE3/PDE4 inhibitor (bronchodilator + anti-inflammatory) by nebulizer; FDA-approved June 26, 2024 (ENHANCE-1/-2) — the first new inhaled COPD mechanism in about 20 years.
  • Comorbidity emphasis (GOLD 2025): greater attention to cardiovascular disease and pulmonary hypertension screening, plus refinements to spirometry/imaging guidance.

Take-Home Points

  1. In a patient with risk factors, post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.7 (scooped flow-volume loop) on spirometry is required to diagnose COPD — you can't diagnose it without PFTs.
  2. Smoking cessation is the intervention with the clearest mortality benefit; O2 for resting hypoxemia and vaccines also matter.
  3. By 2023 GOLD groups, LABA+LAMA is first-line for most symptomatic patients; add ICS mainly for exacerbators with eos ≥100 (initial ICS in Group E if eos ≥300), and de-escalate ICS for recurrent pneumonia.
  4. Treat outpatient exacerbations with short-acting bronchodilators, prednisone 40 mg ×5 days, and a 5-day antibiotic (per local resistance) when ≥2 cardinal symptoms include sputum purulence.
  5. 2024–25 add-ons: dupilumab (first biologic; eos ≥300 + chronic bronchitis) and ensifentrine (PDE3/4 inhaler).
