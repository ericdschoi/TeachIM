Common indications for a triple-lumen CVC?	Vasopressors / peripherally-incompatible infusion (TPN, vesicants), inadequate peripheral access, hemodynamic monitoring (CVP/ScvO2), transvenous pacing/PA catheter; hemodialysis via a dedicated HD catheter.
Why is a triple-lumen CVC a poor choice for massive transfusion?	Long, narrow, high-resistance lumens cause slow flow. Use a short large-bore peripheral IV or an introducer/Cordis for rapid volume/blood.
Site-specific / absolute contraindication to cannulating a vein for CVC?	Thrombus in the target vein (non-compressible on US); overlying skin infection; distorted anatomy. ESKD and a self-limited rash are NOT contraindications.
Are coagulopathy/thrombocytopenia absolute contraindications to CVC?	No, they are cautions. Correct only if clearly excessive; prefer a compressible site (IJ/femoral) over subclavian; do not delay a needed line in shock for a mild INR.
Site trade-offs: IJ vs subclavian vs femoral?	IJ = default for US-guided bedside lines. Subclavian = lowest infection/thrombosis but highest mechanical risk (PTX, non-compressible). Femoral = fastest in a code but highest infection/thrombosis; avoid for elective (3SITES, Parienti NEJM 2015).
Preferred patient position for an IJ/subclavian CVC?	Trendelenburg (head below feet) to engorge/stabilize the vein. Goal = engorge the vessel while keeping the patient comfortable/stable.
Preferred skin antiseptic for CVC, and how is it applied?	Chlorhexidine, SCRUBBED (x2, 30 s each) over an area larger than the drape aperture. (Iodine is applied and left to dry.)
Which ultrasound transducer for real-time IJ cannulation?	High-frequency LINEAR transducer.
What must the pre-procedure scan confirm?	IJV is well visualized with a predictable path relative to the carotid, and is PATENT (fully compressible mid-neck to clavicle). Non-compressible = thrombus, use another site.
"Follow-the-tip" technique, and why dynamic beats static?	Keep the probe just caudal to the advancing needle so the TIP comes into view, slide caudally, repeat. Static cannot distinguish tip from shaft cross-section, so you can pass through the vein unknowingly.
How far is the guidewire advanced, and what do you monitor?	At least 10 cm of wire. Watch telemetry, withdraw a few cm if ectopy develops (wire in RA/RV).
Most reliable confirmation of venous (vs arterial) cannulation?	Real-time ultrasound visualization of the wire IN the vein (both axes). Blood color, ease of wire passage, and non-pulsatile drip are all unreliable in shock/hypoxemia.
Maximal sterile barrier / CLABSI-prevention bundle?	Cap, mask, eye protection, sterile gown + gloves, full-body drape; chlorhexidine prep; checklist in view; CHG-impregnated dressing (Pronovost NEJM 2006).
Which port does the guidewire exit, and why?	The DISTAL port (labeled "1," typically brown), unclamp it so the wire feeds out the catheter tip; grasp the wire before advancing the catheter.
When is the guidewire removed?	AFTER the catheter is fully inserted, never before. Maintain control of the wire at every step (do not contaminate, over-advance, or retain it).
Appropriate depth for a right IJ triple-lumen catheter?	About 12-16 cm at the skin depending on patient height (~12 cm for ~5 ft, ~15 cm for ~6 ft). Tip should sit at the cavo-atrial junction.
Bedside-ultrasound confirmation of supradiaphragmatic CVC placement?	Inject 10 cc agitated saline, confirm turbulent flow in the right atrium within ~2 s (rapid atrial swirl); tip should NOT sit in the RA. Plus directly visualize the catheter in the vessel.
How do you exclude pneumothorax after a CVC, and how good is bedside US?	Anterior chest lung sliding in multiple interspaces, supine (sens ~91%, spec ~98% vs CT, Alrajhi Chest 2012), or CXR. CXR also confirms tip at cavo-atrial junction.
