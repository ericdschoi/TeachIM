Learning Objectives

  1. Identify appropriate indications and relevant contraindications to performing a central venous catheter insertion.
  2. Achieve a Minimum Passing Score (MPS) on the validated assessment checklist in a simulation setting.
  3. Perform a CVC insertion under full or partial supervision in the clinical setting until demonstrating the Unsupervised Practice Standard (UPS) on the validated assessment checklist.

Framework: Learn → See → Practice → Prove → Do → Maintain (Sawyer et al., Acad Med 2015). Simulation-Based Mastery Learning (SBML) — the learner does not graduate the sim until every checklist item is performed correctly and independently — improves skill retention and reduces clinical complications (McGaghie, Med Educ 2014; Barsuk).

Pre-work NEW — send the day before with the procedure video + quiz

Watch the procedural video, skim the instructional guide and clinical checklist, and answer these without looking anything up. (The full 10-item quiz with answer key is included as a file above.)

Q1. Which transducer and which real-time technique do you use to cannulate the internal jugular vein, and why is dynamic (not static) better? The high-frequency linear transducer. Use the "follow-the-tip" dynamic technique: keep the transducer just caudal to the advancing needle so the tip comes into view, then slide caudally and repeat. With a static probe you can't tell whether you're seeing the needle tip or a cross-section of the shaft — so you can advance past the vein without knowing it. Confirm the wire in the vein in both short- and long-axis views before dilating.
Q2. Before you dilate, what two things must the ultrasound confirm — and what one error during wire placement should you watch the monitor for? Confirm (1) the guidewire is in the IJV (not the carotid) in both short and long axis, and (2) venous (not arterial) cannulation. Watch the telemetry for ectopy while advancing the wire — ventricular ectopy means the wire is in the right atrium/ventricle; withdraw it a few centimeters. Real-time ultrasound visualization of the wire in the vein is the most reliable confirmation of venous placement (more reliable than dark blood color, ease of wire passage, or a non-pulsatile drip).
Q3. How do you confirm correct line position and rule out a pneumothorax afterward? Two acceptable approaches: chest radiograph — catheter tip at the cavo-atrial junction, no pneumothorax; or bedside ultrasound — directly visualize the catheter in the vessel, inject 10 cc agitated saline and confirm turbulent flow in the right atrium within ~2 s (rapid atrial swirl sign), and confirm pleural sliding in multiple anterior interspaces to exclude pneumothorax (sens ~91%, spec ~98% vs CT).

Simulation Teaching Script Restructured into Learn–See–Practice–Prove with timing & engagement cues

Run the simulation as a true clinical encounter with full sterile technique. Keep the clinical checklist in clear view during teaching, then remove it for the graded run. The cursor icons below mark built-in ASK prompts — pose the question before you demonstrate. Target ~60 min depending on experience.

Hook & framing (2 min)

"Your patient is in septic shock and needs norepinephrine now — but you place the line, and ten minutes later the nurse pages that the post-line CXR shows a new pneumothorax. Walk it back: which two moments in the procedure determine whether that happens?" Hold the answer; you'll return to it. Establish that the two highest-yield safety levers are real-time ultrasound (follow-the-tip + confirming the wire in the vein) and maintaining control of the guidewire — not needle dexterity.

LEARN — Indications & contraindications 8 min

ASK: "Give me three indications for a CVC — and one common scenario where a triple-lumen CVC is actually the wrong line."
IndicationsNot a good fit for a standard triple-lumen CVC
Vasopressor / peripherally-incompatible (vesicant, TPN) infusion · inadequate peripheral access · hemodynamic monitoring (CVP, ScvO₂) · hemodialysis (via a dedicated HD catheter) · transvenous pacing / pulmonary-artery catheterMassive transfusion / rapid volume resuscitation — the long, narrow lumens of a TLC have high resistance and slow flow; use a large-bore peripheral IV or an introducer/Cordis instead.
ASK: "Which of these blocks the line: INR 4, an IJ thrombus on ultrasound, a facial maculopapular rash, or end-stage kidney disease?"
Absolute / site-specific hard stopsIncreased-risk cautions (weigh & mitigate)
Thrombus in the target vein · overlying skin infection/cellulitis at the site · distorted anatomy or hardware (e.g., contralateral) — pick another siteCoagulopathy (INR ↑, thrombocytopenia) — bleeding risk; correct only if clearly excessive, prefer a compressible site (IJ/femoral) over subclavian; uncooperative patient; severe COPD/single lung (PTX consequences). ESKD and a self-limited rash are not contraindications.
Pearl — pick the site for the patient: Real-time ultrasound makes the IJ the default for most IM bedside lines. Subclavian has the lowest infection and thrombosis rate but the highest mechanical risk (pneumothorax, non-compressible bleeding); femoral is fastest in a code but has the highest infection/thrombosis risk — avoid it for elective lines (3SITES trial, Parienti NEJM 2015).

LEARN — Positioning & pre-procedure ultrasound 8 min

ASK: "What position best engorges the IJ, and what's the one thing you must check on ultrasound before you ever open the kit?"
  • Trendelenburg (head below feet) for an IJ or subclavian line engorges and stabilizes the target vein; the goal is simply to engorge the vessel for cannulation while keeping the patient comfortable and stable — drop it if not tolerated.
  • Pre-procedure scan (linear probe): confirm the IJV is well visualized and follows a predictable path relative to the internal carotid artery, then confirm patency with compression from mid-neck to clavicle. A non-compressible vein = thrombus → do not cannulate; choose another site.
  • Set up ergonomically: patient at a comfortable height, trash can and tray in reach, ultrasound screen directly in your line of sight across the patient.
Safety anchor — the IJ sits lateral/superficial to the carotid: always confirm the vein compresses and the artery pulsates. Cannulating a non-compressible structure or one that pulsates risks an arterial stick.

SEE — Sterile prep & supply preparation 10 min — instructor demonstrates each step

  1. Time-out with the patient's nurse (name, DOB, procedure, site, allergies, consent) before opening any sterile supplies.
  2. Maximal sterile barrier: cap, mask, eye protection, sterile gown and gloves; full-body drape head-to-feet. (Maximal barrier + chlorhexidine + checklist is the CLABSI-prevention bundle — Pronovost NEJM 2006.)
  3. Skin prep: chlorhexidine scrub ×2, 30 s each, over an area larger than the drape aperture. (Chlorhexidine is scrubbed — unlike iodine, which is applied and allowed to dry.) Chlorhexidine is the preferred antiseptic.
  4. Sterile probe cover on the transducer; place it securely on the chest.
  5. Prepare supplies: draw up lidocaine with the largest needle/filtered catheter; prime the guidewire (retract until the curved tip is flush with the straight end of the coil); flush all lumens with sterile saline and clamp/cap each with a Luer lock.

SEE — Cannulation, wire, dilation & catheter 16 min

  1. Anesthetize a dermal wheal with a 21–25 g needle and the track under US guidance.
  2. Advance the introducer needle at 45–60°, maintaining negative pressure, using the follow-the-tip technique with dynamic ultrasound, until dark venous blood is aspirated; then unscrew the syringe. (If the angle is too steep — e.g., 90° — you can't follow the tip.)
  3. Introduce the guidewire: pinch with a thumb at the end of the coil while advancing, release and pull back, repeat until ≥10 cm of wire is in. Watch telemetry — withdraw a few cm if ectopy develops.
  4. Withdraw the needle over the wire; confirm the wire is in the IJV on ultrasound in both short and long axis, beyond the clavicle along the posterior wall.
  5. Nick the skin with a #11 scalpel to ~½ the blade width, contiguous with the wire puncture. Advance the dilator over the wire with a clockwise–counterclockwise twist only to the depth of the vessel; remove it and hold pressure.
  6. Advance the triple-lumen catheter over the wire; near the skin, unclamp the distal port (the one the wire exits — labeled "1," typically the brown/distal port), feed the wire out the distal port, grasp the wire tip, and advance the catheter to ~12–16 cm at the skin (R IJ, by height). Remove the wire and Luer-lock the distal port.
  7. Confirm blood return in all lumens, flush each clear, then clamp/cap. Secure with a hub fastener (≥2 sutures at the insertion site, 1–2 at the lumen junction), apply a chlorhexidine-impregnated sponge and sterile dressing.
Safety anchor — never lose the wire: Maintain control of the guidewire at every step — it must not be contaminated, advanced too far (ectopy/arrhythmia), or left in the vessel after the catheter is placed. The wire is removed after the catheter is in, not before.
Pearl — venous vs arterial confirmation: The most reliable check is real-time ultrasound visualization of the wire in the vein. Dark-vs-bright blood color, ease of wire passage, and a non-pulsatile drip are all unreliable in hypoxemia, hypotension, or shock. If you're unsure, transduce the pressure or send a blood gas before dilating.

SEE — Clean-up & line verification 6 min

  • Secure ultrasound and dispose of all sharps before removing the drape; reassess vitals and update the nurse.
  • Confirm placement & exclude pneumothorax — by CXR (tip at the cavo-atrial junction) or bedside ultrasound: directly see the catheter in the vessel, inject 10 cc agitated saline and confirm turbulent flow in the right atrium within ~2 s (and that the tip does not sit in the RA), plus pleural sliding in multiple anterior interspaces (sens ~91%, spec ~98% vs CT).
  • Place the "okay to use" order only after the tip is confirmed at the cavo-atrial junction (for supradiaphragmatic lines).

PRACTICE → PROVE — Mastery learning remaining time

The learner familiarizes themselves with the kit and rehearses each step, then signals readiness for a graded run. With the checklist out of view, quiz the non-technical steps; then run a full simulation with it visible, correcting each mistake and having the learner repeat until correct. For any step the simulator can't physically reproduce, have the learner verbalize the intended step.

StageStandard
Simulation (MPS)Every item on the simulation assessment checklist performed correctly and independently (indications ×3, contraindications ×2, US patency, CHG scrub >30 s, prime/flush, follow-the-tip with negative pressure, wire >10 cm, wire confirmed both axes, wire control + removed after catheter, catheter to depth, blood return + Luer-lock, ≥2 sutures, verbalize CXR confirmation).
Clinical (UPS)Observed clinical assessment checklist + Global Skills Assessment and an Entrustment rating of "perform safely and effectively without direct supervision."

Close (2 min)

Hide the guide. Ask the learner to rebuild: three indications and the one scenario where a TLC is the wrong line; the pre-scan check that rules out a thrombus; the most reliable confirmation of venous cannulation; the two ways to confirm tip position and exclude pneumothorax; and the one rule about the guidewire.

Case Vignettes From the quiz — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Choosing the line (and the site)

A 58-year-old in septic shock needs norepinephrine and central access; they also have a GI bleed and may need rapid transfusion. INR 1.8, platelets 90K. The intern asks whether the triple-lumen CVC you're about to place will cover the transfusion too, and whether the coags should be corrected first.

How do you answer?A triple-lumen CVC is correct for the vasopressor, but it is a poor conduit for massive transfusion — the long narrow lumens have high resistance and slow flow. Place a large-bore peripheral IV or an introducer/Cordis for volume resuscitation. INR 1.8 / plt 90K are cautions, not absolute contraindications; use a compressible site (IJ) under ultrasound and don't delay a needed line in shock to chase a mild coagulopathy. Confirm IJ patency by compression before cannulating.

Case 2 — Confirming placement

You've placed a right IJ triple-lumen catheter under ultrasound. Blood returns dark and non-pulsatile from all lumens. Before calling the line "okay to use," you confirm position at the bedside.

What confirms correct supradiaphragmatic placement, and how do you exclude a pneumothorax?Inject 10 cc agitated sterile saline through a lumen and confirm turbulent flow in the right atrium within ~2 s on a subxiphoid/apical view (rapid atrial swirl) — turbulent flow confirms the tip is in the central venous system and not, e.g., arterial or extravascular. The tip should sit at the cavo-atrial junction (on CXR: lower SVC / cavo-atrial junction, not deep in the RA). Exclude pneumothorax with anterior lung sliding in multiple interspaces (or CXR). Only then place the "okay to use" order.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — 5 items, ABIM single-best-answer

1. Which of the following is the least appropriate indication for placing a standard triple-lumen central venous catheter?

  1. Norepinephrine infusion in septic shock
  2. Total parenteral nutrition
  3. Rapid massive transfusion in hemorrhagic shock
  4. Central venous pressure monitoring
Answer & explanationC. A triple-lumen CVC has long, narrow, high-resistance lumens, so flow rates are slow — it is a poor choice for rapid massive transfusion, where a short large-bore peripheral IV or an introducer/Cordis is preferred. Vasopressors (A), TPN (B), and CVP monitoring (D) are all standard indications for a TLC.

2. On the pre-procedure scan of the right internal jugular vein, the vessel does not fully collapse with probe compression. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Apply more Trendelenburg and proceed
  2. Cannulate quickly before the vein collapses
  3. Do not cannulate this vein — suspect thrombus and select another site
  4. Transfuse platelets and proceed
Answer & explanationC. A non-compressible vein suggests thrombus, an absolute contraindication to cannulating that vein; choose another site. Thrombus in the target vein is the classic site-specific contraindication. More Trendelenburg (A) or rushing (B) doesn't change the obstruction, and platelet count (D) is irrelevant here.

3. Why is dynamic "follow-the-tip" ultrasound preferred over holding the probe in a fixed position while advancing the needle?

  1. It is faster and requires less sterile setup
  2. A static image cannot distinguish the needle tip from a cross-section of the shaft, so you can advance past the vein unknowingly
  3. It avoids the need to confirm the wire in the vein
  4. It eliminates the risk of arterial puncture entirely
Answer & explanationB. With a static probe you can't tell whether the bright dot is the advancing tip or a cross-section of the shaft, so the tip can travel through the back wall of the vein and into the carotid without your knowing. Following the tip (probe just caudal to the needle, advancing in steps) keeps the tip in view. It does not remove the need to confirm the wire in the vein (C) and does not eliminate arterial puncture risk (D).

4. After cannulating the IJ you advance the guidewire and the monitor shows new frequent PVCs. What should you do?

  1. Continue advancing — ectopy is expected and harmless
  2. Withdraw the guidewire a few centimeters until the ectopy resolves
  3. Remove the wire entirely and restart
  4. Give amiodarone before continuing
Answer & explanationB. Ectopy means the wire tip is irritating the right atrium/ventricle. Withdraw the wire a few centimeters and the ectopy should resolve; this is why you monitor telemetry during wire placement. You do not need to abort the procedure (C) or give antiarrhythmics (A, D) for transient wire-induced ectopy.

5. Which finding most reliably confirms venous (rather than arterial) cannulation of the internal jugular vein before dilation?

  1. Dark (rather than bright) red blood in the syringe
  2. Ease of passing the wire to 10–15 cm
  3. Real-time ultrasound visualization of the wire within the IJ vein
  4. A slow non-pulsatile drip of blood from the needle hub
Answer & explanationC. Direct ultrasound visualization of the wire in the vein (both axes) is the most reliable confirmation. Blood color (A) and non-pulsatile flow (D) are unreliable in hypoxemia/hypotension/shock, and easy wire passage (B) can occur with arterial cannulation too. Confirming venous placement before dilating is critical — dilating an inadvertently cannulated carotid causes catastrophic injury.

Take-Home Points

  1. Match the line to the job: a triple-lumen CVC is for vasopressors, incompatible infusions, monitoring, and access — not for massive transfusion (use a large-bore peripheral or introducer).
  2. Scan before you stick: confirm the target vein is patent and fully compressible — a non-compressible vein means thrombus, so pick another site. Thrombus and overlying infection are the hard stops; coagulopathy is a caution.
  3. Real-time ultrasound is the core safety skill: use the high-frequency linear probe and the follow-the-tip technique, and confirm the wire in the vein in both axes before you dilate — the single best protection against dilating the carotid.
  4. Never lose the wire: watch telemetry for ectopy, keep control of the wire at every step, and remove it only after the catheter is in.
  5. Confirm and clear before use: tip at the cavo-atrial junction and no pneumothorax — by CXR or bedside ultrasound (agitated-saline RA swirl + anterior lung sliding).
  6. Prove competence by mastery learning: reach the MPS (every sim checklist item correct & independent), then the UPS (clinical checklist + entrustment "without direct supervision") before unsupervised practice.
