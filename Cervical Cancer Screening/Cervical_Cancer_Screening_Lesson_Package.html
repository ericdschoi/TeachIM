Learning Objectives

  1. Describe HPV virology and its role in the natural history of cervical cancer.
  2. Choose appropriate screening tests and intervals for patients at average risk.
  3. Identify high-risk populations and apply alternate screening guidelines.
  4. Apply risk-based guidelines to manage abnormal results in average- and high-risk individuals.

Pre-work (send the day before)

1. What causes nearly all cervical cancer, and where does it preferentially develop?Persistent infection with high-risk HPV (chiefly types 16 and 18). >90% of cancers arise at the transformation zone (squamocolumnar junction), so screening must sample that region. ~90% of new HPV infections clear within 2 years; the persistent minority drive precancer over years to decades.
2. For an average-risk 35-year-old, what are the acceptable screening options and intervals?Cytology alone every 3 years, OR HPV-based testing (primary hrHPV or co-test) every 5 years. USPSTF (2025) and ACS prefer primary hrHPV and now endorse self-collection.
3. What single number drives ASCCP management of an abnormal result?The immediate risk of CIN3+: if <4% → surveillance interval; if ≥4% → colposcopy and/or treatment, escalating with risk. "Equal management for equal risk."

Teaching Script (timed)

0:00–2:00 — Hook

Open with the natural-history arc: persistent high-risk HPV → atypia → precancer → cancer over ~10+ years. The whole point of screening is to catch the treatable middle of that arc. Reveal the two numbers: ~90% of HPV infections clear within 2 years; the persistent ~10% are what we screen for.

ASK: "If most HPV clears on its own, why don't we screen 22-year-olds with HPV testing?" (High prevalence of transient HPV in the young → HPV testing would generate false alarms, colposcopies, and overtreatment with obstetric harms. Cytology alone for 21–29.)
2:00–7:00 — Objective 1: HPV virology & anatomy (Pathophysiology board)
ConceptKey teaching point
CausePersistent hrHPV (chiefly 16, 18) causes nearly all cervical cancer; ~15 oncogenic types exist
Risk factors for persistenceOlder age, oncogenic subtype, immunosuppression (HIV, meds), smoking, other STIs, possibly cOCP use
AnatomyEctocervix = squamous; endocervix = columnar; they meet at the squamocolumnar junction. The transformation zone (squamous metaplasia) is where HPV preferentially infects and >90% of cancers arise
Cancer typesEctocervix → squamous cell carcinoma (~75%); endocervix → adenocarcinoma (~25%, less common)
Pearl: "Sample the transformation zone" is the anatomic reason behind every screening test — that's where the disease starts. Adenocarcinoma (endocervical/glandular) is the type cytology misses most, which is why HPV-based testing is more sensitive.
7:00–14:00 — Objectives 2 & 3: Who and how to screen (Screening boards)

Three tests: cytology (Pap), primary hrHPV (with reflex cytology if positive), and co-test (both). "HPV-based testing" means primary HPV or co-test.

Average riskUSPSTFACS
21–29 yCytology q3y(ACS starts at 25)
25/30–65 yCytology q3y OR HPV-based testing q5yPrimary hrHPV q5y (preferred) > co-test q5y > cytology q3y
Stop>65 y if no CIN2+ in 25 y AND adequate prior screening (3 neg cytology in 10 y, most recent <3 y; or 2 neg HPV-based in 10 y, most recent <5 y)
Post-hysterectomyStop after total hysterectomy (cervix removed) for benign indication with no CIN2+ in 25 y
ASK: "Does HPV-vaccination status change the screening schedule?" (No — follow age-specific recommendations regardless of vaccination status.)

High-risk (immunocompromised/immunosuppressed): HIV, solid-organ/stem-cell transplant, SLE, IBD/RA on immunosuppression, chronic steroids.

HIVRecommendation
<30 yCytology at baseline, then annually ×3; if 3 normal → cytology q3y (start at diagnosis, not before 21). Co-testing not recommended (<30)
≥30 yCytology only (baseline, annually ×3, then q3y if normal) OR co-test q3y. Primary HPV with reflex not recommended. Continue for LIFE (not stopping at 65)
Pearl: For HIV/immunosuppressed patients screening does not stop at 65 and post-hysterectomy patients still need annual screening if HIV is progressing or virally unsuppressed. Non-HIV immunosuppressed (transplant, SLE, IBD/RA on immunosuppression) follow the CDC HIV protocol; IBD/RA not on immunosuppression and type 1 DM follow the general-population guideline.
14:00–20:00 — Objective 4: Managing abnormal results (Management boards)

For average-risk patients, use the ASCCP 2019 risk-based app (app.asccp.org, endorsed by ACOG). It estimates the immediate risk of CIN3+ from current + prior results and personal factors. Positive primary HPV → reflex cytology from the same specimen.

Immediate CIN3+ riskAction
<4%Surveillance by 5-year CIN3+ risk: >0.55% → 1 y; 0.15–0.55% → 3 y; <0.15% → 5 y
4–24%Colposcopy
25–59%Expedited treatment OR colposcopy (depends on desire for future pregnancy)
60–100%Expedited treatment (excision or ablation)
Pearl — Pap vs colposcopy: Cytology grades exfoliated cells (NILM → ASC-US → LSIL → ASC-H → AGC → HSIL). Colposcopy biopsies tissue for histology (No CIN / CIN1 / CIN2-3 / AIS), assessing vertical extension + p16 staining. Excision (LEEP/cone) is generally preferred over ablation for histologic HSIL and AIS because it yields tissue for pathology.

High-risk management: immunosuppressed → colposcopy for ASC-US/HPV+, or LSIL/ASC-H/AGC/HSIL regardless of HPV. History of CIN2+ → HPV-based test at 6 mo, then 3 annual HPV-based tests, then q3y for ≥25 years (even >65).

Retrieval close: board down — rebuild the average-risk schedule (21–29 vs 30–65), the 4% CIN3+ threshold, and the one feature that flips a patient to the high-risk track (immunosuppression or prior CIN2+).

2025 Screening Update added

The deck cites the USPSTF 2018 and ACS 2020 guidelines. The risk-based framework is unchanged, but the screening recommendation has moved:

  • USPSTF finalized its update in 2025 (Grade A): for average-risk ages 30–65, primary hrHPV every 5 years is now the preferred strategy; cytology q3y remains an option. Ages 21–29: cytology q3y (unchanged).
  • Self-collection endorsed for the first time: patient-collected hrHPV samples on an FDA-approved platform are non-inferior to clinician-collected (pooled CIN2+ sensitivity ~0.86, specificity ~0.81) — a meaningful access expansion for patients who decline a speculum exam.
  • ACS (2025) recommends starting at age 25 with primary hrHPV and also endorses self-collection.
  • Unchanged: HPV as the cause, ASCCP 2019 risk-based management (4% immediate-CIN3+ threshold, app.asccp.org), distinct HIV/immunosuppressed protocols, and stopping at 65 with adequate prior screening.
Practical note: self-collection requires an FDA-approved primary-HPV assay validated for self-collected specimens — confirm your lab/platform before adopting. Societies still debate the exact self-collection interval.

Cases (from the original board — answers click-to-reveal)

Case 1 — 40-year-old with a cervix; HTN, depression; presents for screening. Co-test negative, no prior abnormal Paps

1. When should they be screened again?In 5 years — they had negative HPV-based (co-) testing.
1. Five years later: primary HPV with reflex cytology shows HPV+ (high-risk strain other than 16/18), ASC-US. Next step?Use app.asccp.org (age 30–65, routine screening within past 5 years, enter current + prior results) → repeat HPV testing at 1 year is recommended.
1. At 1-year follow-up, repeat testing still shows HPV+ (other), ASC-US. Next step?Per the ASCCP app, colposcopy is recommended at this point.
1. Colposcopy biopsy shows CIN2. Management?Per ASCCP (age 30–65, evaluation of a colposcopic biopsy): treatment (excision > ablation) followed by HPV-based testing at 6 months is recommended. For those concerned about future-pregnancy risk from treatment, observation with colposcopy + HPV-based screening at 6 and 12 months is acceptable.
1. How will you counsel them on the long-term impact of a CIN2 history?Short-term follow-up depends on treatment choice; long-term they are considered high-risk and need HPV-based testing at 3-year intervals for a minimum of 25 years (even beyond age 65).

Case 2 — 37-year-old with a cervix; SLE on hydroxychloroquine; annual wellness exam; normal Pap 3 y ago (HPV status unknown); completed HPV vaccine series

2. When and how should they be screened?Today. Given SLE on immunosuppression, screen with cytology alone or HPV co-test at least every 3 years (high-risk protocol). HPV-vaccination status does not change the schedule.
2. When can they stop screening?They don't stop at 65 — with SLE on immunosuppression, continue screening q3y throughout life (assuming reasonably good health), unlike the general population.
2. Pap returns LSIL, HPV negative. Next step?Given immunosuppression, colposcopy is indicated despite the negative HPV — high-risk patients have a lower threshold for colposcopy (LSIL or more concerning cytology regardless of HPV result).

Board-Style MCQs added

1. A healthy 24-year-old requests cervical cancer screening. Which is the most appropriate strategy?
  1. Primary hrHPV testing now
  2. Co-testing now
  3. Cytology alone every 3 years
  4. No screening until age 30
  5. Colposcopy
Answer & explanationsC. For ages 21–29, cytology alone every 3 years is recommended (USPSTF). A/B — HPV-based testing is avoided <30 due to high transient-HPV prevalence (overtreatment risk). D — screening begins at 21 (USPSTF) / 25 (ACS). E — colposcopy is for abnormal results, not primary screening.
2. A 45-year-old woman with HIV (CD4 600, virally suppressed) presents for screening. Which is correct?
  1. Primary hrHPV every 5 years; stop at 65
  2. Cytology or co-testing, continued throughout life
  3. Cytology every 3 years, stop at 65 like the general population
  4. No screening needed if HPV-vaccinated
  5. Colposcopy at baseline for all HIV patients
Answer & explanationsB. HIV patients ≥30 are screened with cytology or co-testing and continue for life (not stopping at 65). A — primary HPV with reflex is not recommended in HIV. C — they do not stop at 65. D — vaccination doesn't change the schedule. E — colposcopy is for abnormal cytology, not routine baseline.
3. The ASCCP app estimates a patient's immediate risk of CIN3+ at 30%. She desires future pregnancy. What does the guideline recommend?
  1. Surveillance with repeat testing in 1 year
  2. Return in 5 years
  3. Expedited treatment or colposcopy
  4. Immediate hysterectomy
  5. Repeat cytology in 6 weeks
Answer & explanationsC. An immediate CIN3+ risk of 25–59% warrants expedited treatment OR colposcopy — colposcopy is reasonable here given her desire for future pregnancy (avoids unnecessary excision). A/B — surveillance applies when immediate risk is <4%. D — hysterectomy is not first-line for a precancer risk. E — not a guideline option.
4. A 52-year-old average-risk woman had treatment for histologic HSIL (CIN2) 4 years ago. How long must she continue surveillance?
  1. Stop now — she was treated
  2. HPV-based testing q3y for at least 25 years, even beyond age 65
  3. Annual cytology indefinitely
  4. Co-testing q5y, stop at 65
  5. No further screening if HPV-negative once
Answer & explanationsB. A history of CIN2+ confers a persistent ~2-fold increased risk for ≥25 years — surveillance is HPV-based testing q3y for at least 25 years, continuing past 65. A/E — a single negative test does not end surveillance. C — annual testing isn't required after the initial post-treatment series. D — stopping at 65 doesn't apply to a CIN2+ history.
5. Reflecting the 2025 USPSTF update, which statement about average-risk screening for a 40-year-old is most accurate?
  1. Cytology q1y is now preferred
  2. Primary hrHPV every 5 years is preferred and may use an FDA-approved self-collected sample
  3. Co-testing every year is required
  4. Screening should now start at 18
  5. HPV testing is no longer recommended
Answer & explanationsB. The 2025 USPSTF update makes primary hrHPV every 5 years the preferred strategy for ages 30–65 and, for the first time, endorses patient self-collection on an FDA-approved platform. A/C — annual screening is not recommended for average risk. D — start remains 21 (USPSTF) / 25 (ACS). E — HPV testing is now the preferred modality, not removed.

Take-Home Points

  1. Persistent infection with oncogenic HPV subtypes causes nearly all cervical cancer; >90% arises at the transformation zone.
  2. Average-risk screening: cytology alone, HPV-based testing (primary hrHPV with reflex cytology, or co-test), per age. USPSTF (2025) and ACS prefer primary hrHPV and now endorse self-collection; both agree screening can stop at 65 with adequate prior screening.
  3. Manage abnormal results and set the surveillance interval by the immediate risk of CIN3+, calculated with the ASCCP app (4% threshold).
  4. Immunocompromised/immunosuppressed patients are higher-risk and have unique screening and management protocols — including a lower threshold for colposcopy and lifelong screening in HIV.
