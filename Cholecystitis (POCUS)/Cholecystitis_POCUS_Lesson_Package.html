Learning Objectives

  1. Identify key structures on RUQ ultrasound — liver, gallbladder, and biliary stones.
  2. Recognize which probe to use for biliary pathology and how to obtain long- and short-axis views.
  3. Differentiate gallstones from sludge and polyps, and recognize the sonographic features of cholecystitis.

Pre-Work (send the day before)

1. What single ultrasound feature tells a gallstone apart from sludge or a polyp? A posterior acoustic shadow. Stones cast one; sludge and polyps do not.
2. What is a normal gallbladder wall thickness, and name two non-cholecystitis causes of a thick wall. Normal <3 mm (TG18 dx threshold >4 mm). Non-specific causes: cirrhosis, hepatitis, pancreatitis, hypoalbuminemia.
3. Why do normal LFTs make choledocholithiasis and cholangitis unlikely here? Duct obstruction typically raises LFTs (cholestatic pattern); with normal LFTs, CBD pathology is unlikely and hard-to-image CBD views are low-yield.

Teaching Script

Run the board in presenter mode. Pose each question, ~30 s neighbor discussion, then advance the animation to reveal.

Hook (2 min)

"A 55-year-old woman with severe epigastric pain, afebrile, WBC 15, normal LFTs. You have a probe and 90 seconds. Can you tell whether this is just gallstones, or cholecystitis that buys her a surgery consult — before any formal imaging?"

Segment 1 — Anatomy & probe (Objective 1–2)

ASK: "Which probe, and what structures are you looking for?"

Low-frequency curvilinear probe. Find the liver, gallbladder, and the portal triad; sweep in long and short axis. A fasting GB distends and is easier to see; a non-fasting GB is contracted and obscured by bowel gas.

Pearl: Always image the GB in two planes — a single-plane finding can be artifact or an adjacent loop of bowel.

Segment 2 — Stone vs sludge vs polyp (Objective 3)

ASK: "Both stones and sludge are echogenic and gravity-dependent — how do you tell them apart?"
EchogenicGravity-dependentAcoustic shadow
GallstoneYes (hyperechoic rim)YesYes
SludgeYesYes (layers)No
PolypYesNo (fixed to wall)No
Pearl: The acoustic shadow is the single most useful feature. Tumefactive sludge can form a mass-like structure and mimic a stone, but it casts no shadow (and rarely obstructs).

Segment 3 — Is this cholecystitis? (Objective 3)

ASK: "The GB wall measures 7 mm. Does a thick wall mean cholecystitis?"

Not by itself. Normal wall is <3 mm (TG18 uses >4 mm), but thickening is non-specific — cirrhosis, hepatitis, pancreatitis, and hypoalbuminemia all thicken it. Put it together: stones impacted at the neck, pericholecystic fluid, a positive sonographic Murphy sign, plus systemic inflammation point to cholecystitis.

ASK: "Why is this case reassuring for the bile duct?"

Normal LFTs → choledocholithiasis and cholangitis are unlikely. The CBD is technically hard to image and low-yield when LFTs are normal.

Pearl — TG18: Definite acute cholecystitis = one local sign (A) + one systemic sign (B) + one imaging finding (C).

Retrieval close (1 min)

Have a learner rebuild it: "shadow = stone," "thick wall is non-specific," and "normal LFTs → don't chase the duct."

Board-Style MCQs

Q1

On RUQ ultrasound you see echogenic, gravity-dependent material in the gallbladder. Which single feature best confirms it is a gallstone rather than sludge?

A. Hyperechoic appearance · B. Layering in the dependent GB · C. Posterior acoustic shadow · D. Located at the fundus · E. Mobile with positioning

Answer & explanationC. A posterior acoustic shadow is the defining feature of a stone. Sludge is also echogenic, gravity-dependent, mobile, and layering — but casts no shadow. Polyps are echogenic but fixed and shadowless.
Q2

Which probe is most appropriate for biliary point-of-care ultrasound?

A. Linear high-frequency · B. Curvilinear low-frequency · C. Phased-array cardiac (for windows only) · D. Endocavitary · E. Pencil/CW Doppler

Answer & explanationB. The low-frequency curvilinear probe provides the depth and field of view needed for abdominal/biliary imaging. Linear probes are high-resolution but too shallow; phased-array is for cardiac windows.
Q3

A cirrhotic patient with ascites and a serum albumin of 2.1 g/dL has a 5 mm gallbladder wall but no pain or fever. Best interpretation?

A. Diagnostic of acute cholecystitis · B. Wall thickening is non-specific and likely from hypoalbuminemia/ascites · C. Indicates gangrenous cholecystitis · D. Requires emergent cholecystectomy · E. Confirms choledocholithiasis

Answer & explanationB. GB wall thickening is non-specific — cirrhosis, hepatitis, pancreatitis, and hypoalbuminemia all cause it. Without local/systemic inflammatory signs, this is not cholecystitis. TG18 requires A + B + C.
Q4

A patient with RUQ pain has gallstones and a positive sonographic Murphy sign, fever, and WBC 16. Per the Tokyo Guidelines (TG18), this combination establishes what?

A. Suspected cholangitis · B. Definite acute cholecystitis · C. Biliary colic only · D. Choledocholithiasis · E. Gallbladder polyp

Answer & explanationB. Local sign (sonographic Murphy / RUQ tenderness, A) + systemic sign (fever, ↑WBC, B) + imaging (stones, C) = definite acute cholecystitis by TG18. Cholangitis would require evidence of biliary obstruction/cholestasis.
Q5

A patient with RUQ pain has gallstones on POCUS, normal LFTs, and a non-dilated CBD. Which is the most appropriate next step regarding the bile duct?

A. Urgent ERCP · B. MRCP for all gallstone patients · C. No further duct workup — choledocholithiasis is unlikely with normal LFTs · D. Repeat ultrasound in 1 hour · E. HIDA scan to image the CBD

Answer & explanationC. With normal LFTs and a non-dilated CBD, choledocholithiasis and cholangitis are unlikely; routine MRCP/ERCP is not warranted. Pursue CBD imaging only if LFTs rise or the duct dilates. (HIDA assesses cystic-duct patency, not the CBD stones.)

location [body/fundus/NECK-impacted*] · sludge [none/layering*] · polyp [none*]

sonographic Murphy [neg/POS*]

Take-Home Points

  1. A gallstone has a hyperechoic rim and, most importantly, an acoustic shadow — that shadow distinguishes it from tumefactive sludge and polyps.
  2. A thickened GB wall (>3 mm, or >4 mm by TG18) is suggestive but non-specific — also seen with cirrhosis, hepatitis, pancreatitis, and hypoalbuminemia. Diagnose cholecystitis by combining local + systemic + imaging signs (TG18).
  3. Normal LFTs make choledocholithiasis and cholangitis unlikely; reserve CBD imaging for abnormal LFTs.
