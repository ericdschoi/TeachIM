Biliary POCUS — Cholelithiasis & Cholecystitis Quick Reference

RUQ ultrasound: gallbladder anatomy, stone vs sludge, wall thickening, cholecystitis. TeachIM 5-minute POCUS case · CC BY-NC 4.0 · clinician-reviewed.

One line: A gallstone is gravity-dependent echogenic material with a hyperechoic rim and a posterior acoustic shadow — the shadow is what separates a stone from sludge or a polyp. Wall thickening is suggestive but non-specific.

Technique

  • Probe: low-frequency curvilinear (abdominal).
  • Image in long AND short axis of the GB.
  • Fasting GB distends & is easier to see; non-fasting = contracted + bowel gas.
  • Landmarks: liver, GB, portal triad / main lobar fissure.

Stone vs Sludge vs Polyp

Echogenic?Gravity-dep?Shadow?
StoneYes (rim)YesYes
SludgeYesYes (layers)No
PolypYesNo (fixed)No
  • Acoustic shadow = stone. Tumefactive sludge can mimic a mass but casts NO shadow.

Wall Thickening

  • Normal GB wall <3 mm (TG18 dx threshold >4 mm).
  • Non-specific — also seen in cirrhosis, hepatitis, pancreatitis, hypoalbuminemia.
  • Other cholecystitis signs: pericholecystic fluid, GB enlargement, impacted neck stone.

Cholecystitis — TG18 Criteria

A — LocalMurphy's sign; RUQ mass/pain/tenderness
B — SystemicFever; ↑CRP; ↑WBC
C — ImagingWall >4 mm, pericholecystic fluid, stones/debris, enlarged GB (LA ≥8 / SA ≥4 cm), sonographic Murphy
  • Definite dx = one A + one B + one C.
  • Sonographic Murphy (max tenderness under the probe) = key POCUS sign.

When to Worry About the Duct

  • Normal LFTs → choledocholithiasis & cholangitis unlikely.
  • CBD imaging is technically hard & low-yield with normal LFTs.
  • Abnormal LFTs → pursue CBD / MRCP; consider cholangitis (Charcot/Reynolds).

Bedside Bottom Line

  • RUQ pain → curvilinear probe, find the GB in 2 planes.
  • Echogenic + shadow = stone; look at the GB neck.
  • Wall >3–4 mm + sonographic Murphy + systemic signs → cholecystitis.
  • Check LFTs before worrying about the duct.
