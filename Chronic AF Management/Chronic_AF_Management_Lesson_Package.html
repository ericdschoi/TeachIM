Learning Objectives

  1. Apply principles of stroke-risk assessment and prevention in atrial fibrillation (AF).
  2. Select appropriate anticoagulants in common clinical scenarios.
  3. Describe the goals of rate control in AF and select an appropriate management strategy.
  4. List the factors that favor a rhythm- over a rate-control strategy.

Scope: chronic management only. For new-onset AF and rate control of rapid ventricular response, see the separate TeachIM talk “Management of Atrial Fibrillation with Rapid Ventricular Response.”

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Q1. Name the stroke-risk and bleeding-risk tools used in AF, and state when you would start anticoagulation. Stroke risk: CHA₂DS₂-VASc. Bleeding risk: HAS-BLED. Anticoagulate when the estimated annual stroke risk is ≥2%/yr — roughly CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 in men or ≥3 in women (2023 ACC/AHA) — and in all patients around cardioversion regardless of score. A high HAS-BLED is not a reason to withhold anticoagulation.
Q2. What is the first-line anticoagulant for most AF, and which patients still need warfarin? A DOAC is first-line for most (more effective, less bleeding than warfarin). Warfarin is required only for mechanical heart valves or moderate–severe mitral stenosis (“valvular” AF). Apixaban is the go-to for ESRD/CrCl <15 or very high BMI.
Q3. What heart-rate target defines adequate rate control, and which single factor most favors a rhythm-control strategy? Target HR <110 bpm for most patients (lenient control). Symptoms attributable to AF is the strongest factor favoring rhythm control.

2024 Update — 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS AF Guideline NEW — teach with current framing

The original lesson draws on the 2019 ACC/AHA/HRS and 2020 ESC guidelines. The 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS guideline (Joglar et al., Circulation 2024) keeps the core teaching intact but reframes several points. Use this when you teach; the board's content is still excellent for the physiology and the scoring.

TopicWhat changed in 2023
AF classificationNew stages 1–4 (1 at-risk → 2 pre-AF → 3 AF [3A paroxysmal, 3B persistent, 3C long-standing, 3D post-ablation] → 4 permanent). Frames AF as a progressive disease rather than just by episode duration.
When to anticoagulateDecide by absolute risk: ≥2%/yr stroke risk → anticoagulate (Class 1) ≈ CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 men/≥3 women. Score 1 (men)/2 (women) is now “reasonable” (Class 2a). Use the score to estimate risk, not as a rigid on/off switch; aspirin is not recommended for stroke prevention.
DOAC vs warfarinDOAC over warfarin is Class 1; warfarin reserved for mechanical valve or moderate–severe MS. The label “nonvalvular AF” is de-emphasized in favor of naming the specific valve lesion.
Catheter ablationUpgraded to Class 1 first-line rhythm control in selected symptomatic paroxysmal AF (younger, few comorbidities) and in AF with HFrEF (mortality/HF-hospitalization benefit).
Lifestyle / risk factorsNow a named pillar: weight loss (target BMI <27), alcohol reduction, BP and lipid control, OSA screening — improves success of both rate and rhythm control.
LAA occlusionWATCHMAN-type devices upgraded to Class 2a for patients with a long-term contraindication to anticoagulation.

Objective 1 — Stroke risk & prevention 6 min

ASK: "Patient with AF in front of you — what's the first decision, and what tool drives it?" Elicit: stroke prevention; CHA₂DS₂-VASc. Then click each letter to build the score.
CHA₂DS₂-VAScHAS-BLED
C CHF (incl. asymptomatic LV dysfunction)1H Hypertension (uncontrolled, SBP >160)
H Hypertension (any history)1A Abnormal renal/liver function (1 each)
A₂ Age 65–74 (1) / ≥75 (2)1–2S Stroke history
D Diabetes1B Bleeding history/predisposition
S₂ Stroke/TIA/systemic embolism2L Labile INR (on warfarin)
V Vascular disease (CAD/PAD)1E Elderly (>65)
Sc Sex category (female modifier)1D Drugs (NSAID/antiplatelet) / alcohol
Pearl: The two scores overlap heavily — many stroke risk factors are also bleeding risk factors. Outside of active bleeding, do not withhold anticoagulation for a high HAS-BLED or for frequent falls; high-bleeding-risk patients often gain more absolute benefit. (An “average” elderly patient would need ≈295 falls/year for OAC harm to outweigh benefit — Man-Son-Hing 1999.) Anticoagulation can be started by any generalist — don't wait for cardiology.

Objective 2 — Choosing an anticoagulant 6 min

ASK each, then reveal: "When do you anticoagulate? Does high bleeding risk change it? First-line agent? Does paroxysmal vs persistent matter? Options if they truly can't take OAC?"
QuestionAnswer
When to anticoagulate?Estimated stroke risk ≥2%/yr → OAC (Class 1) ≈ CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 men/≥3 women; and everyone peri-cardioversion.
High bleeding risk?Generally still anticoagulate — OAC gives >60% relative stroke reduction and >25% mortality reduction; ischemic stroke is usually more disabling than the bleeds counted in risk scores.
First-line agent?A DOAC (Class 1). No DOAC is preferred over another.
Paroxysmal vs persistent?Same stroke risk — anticoagulate paroxysmal AF (and after successful cardioversion) if CHA₂DS₂-VASc qualifies.
Can't tolerate OAC?LAA occlusion (WATCHMAN; Class 2a) or surgical LAA ligation during other cardiac surgery (continue OAC).
Clinical scenarios that change the choice: Valvular AF = mechanical valve or moderate–severe MS → warfarin (everything else, incl. AS/MR/bioprosthetic, takes a DOAC). Obesity: apixaban/rivaroxaban OK to BMI ~50; shared decision above 50. Cardioversion: 3 weeks of OAC (or TEE) before if AF >48 h/unknown, then 4 weeks after regardless of score (atrial stunning). Frequent falls: not a reason to withhold. Stable CAD (>1 yr from MI/PCI): OAC alone — adding aspirin only adds bleeding. CrCl <15/ESRD: apixaban or warfarin.

Objective 3 — Rate control 4 min

ASK: "What's the heart-rate target, and what three drug classes get you there?" Reveal: <110 bpm; beta-blocker, non-DHP CCB, digoxin.

Target HR <110 bpm for most (lenient control was non-inferior to strict in RACE II). Aim lower if symptomatic, declining LV function, or tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy. All three classes slow the AV node:

ClassNotes
Beta-blockersMetoprolol, carvedilol, bisoprolol, atenolol. Prefer guideline BBs (metoprolol succinate, carvedilol, bisoprolol) in HFrEF; caution in decompensated HF and in asthma (COPD usually tolerates).
Non-DHP CCBDiltiazem most common. Contraindicated in HFrEF (strong negative inotrope).
DigoxinAdjunct, not first-line. Useful in decompensated HFrEF (positive inotrope). Renally cleared — toxicity risk in renal dysfunction.
ProceduralAV-node ablation ＋ pacemaker for patients refractory to rate and rhythm control (often tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy).

Objective 4 — Rhythm control 4 min

ASK: "What pushes you toward rhythm control rather than just rate control?"

Factors favoring rhythm control: younger age, fewer comorbidities, recent-onset AF, symptoms attributable to AF (strongest), inability to rate control, tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy, and LV dysfunction of any cause. Methods: electrical cardioversion, antiarrhythmic drugs, and ablation.

Antiarrhythmic toxicities to know: Amiodarone — lung, liver, thyroid (monitor PFTs/LFTs/TFTs). Sotalol & dofetilide — QT prolongation/Torsades, so inpatient initiation on telemetry; renally dosed. Flecainide — contraindicated in structural/coronary heart disease. 2023 change: catheter ablation is now Class 1 first-line in selected symptomatic paroxysmal AF and in AF + HFrEF, so cardiology referral happens earlier than the older rate-first reflex.

Practice Cases from the original board — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — evolving over time (1a → 1b → 1c)

1a. 59 M with HLD and OA on chronic NSAIDs presents with dyspnea on exertion; found in AF, HR 115, BMI 45. TTE: LVEF 58%, no valve disease.

Rate control? Anticoagulation?Rate: goal HR <110 — start a beta-blocker (e.g., metoprolol) or diltiazem (LVEF normal, so diltiazem is allowed). Anticoagulation: CHA₂DS₂-VASc 0, HAS-BLED 1 (NSAIDs) — not indicated.

1b. A few weeks later, home HR 95–110, ongoing dyspnea.

Next step?Up-titrate rate control toward a lower HR since symptoms persist; reassess.

1c. Two years later he is 66; BP 165/90, normal HR, ECG shows sinus rhythm, no dyspnea. Then five years on, admitted for GI bleed from a gastric ulcer with intermittent (paroxysmal) AF.

Anticoagulation now?He now has paroxysmal AF with stroke risk. CHA₂DS₂-VASc 2 (age, HTN); HAS-BLED 4 (age, bleeding history, NSAID use, uncontrolled HTN). Shared decision: consider anticoagulation with close monitoring and aggressive modifiable-risk reduction — control BP, stop NSAIDs, GI follow-up. Paroxysmal AF carries the same stroke risk as persistent.

Case 1 (cardioversion branch)

The same patient is scheduled for cardioversion.

Anticoagulation around cardioversion?Before: 3 weeks of OAC, or a TEE at the time of cardioversion, if AF >48 h/unknown. After: 4 weeks of OAC even if CHA₂DS₂-VASc is 0 (atrial stunning). Given BMI 45, use apixaban or rivaroxaban.

Case 2 — stroke prevention across patients

Choose a stroke-prevention strategy for each: (a) 75 M, HTN, moderate–severe aortic stenosis; (b) 55 F, severe asthma, normal LV; (c) 82 F, HTN, HLD, CKD IV, baseline Cr 2.4.

Answers (a) CHA₂DS₂-VASc 3 → anticoagulate with any DOAC; AS is NOT “valvular” AF (only mechanical valve or moderate–severe mitral stenosis is).
(b) For rate control here, avoid beta-blocker (severe airflow obstruction) → use diltiazem.
(c) CHA₂DS₂-VASc 4 → apixaban 2.5 mg BID (preferred at CrCl <15). Reduced apixaban dose if any 2 of: age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, or Cr ≥1.5.

Case 3 — rate control in HFrEF, and a high-risk anticoagulation call

(a) 75 M with HFrEF (EF 38%); (b) 62 M, severe obesity BMI 55, DM2, PAD; (c) 62 M with mildly decompensated HFrEF on metoprolol succinate 50 mg, HR >120, normal renal function — then later started on digoxin with up-titrated metoprolol, still HR uncontrolled, SBP 90–100; (d) 78 F with prior CVA (residual left-sided weakness) on aspirin, CAD, frequent falls, and prior NSAID-related GI bleed needing transfusion.

Answers (a) Evidence-based beta-blocker (metoprolol succinate, carvedilol, or bisoprolol); avoid diltiazem in HFrEF.
(b) CHA₂DS₂-VASc 2 → anticoagulate. BMI >50: discuss warfarin vs apixaban/rivaroxaban; limited data but BMI is not an absolute DOAC contraindication — consult a pharmacist.
(c) Cautious BB up-titration vs add digoxin as adjunct (HFrEF); when hypotension limits BB and digoxin fails → cardiology referral for rhythm control or AV-node ablation + pacemaker.
(d) CHA₂DS₂-VASc 4 (≈4.8%/yr), HAS-BLED 4 (≈8.9%/yr). Still anticoagulate — disabling-stroke risk outweighs non-major bleeding; any DOAC. Falls are not a reason to withhold.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 70-year-old woman with hypertension and diabetes has newly documented paroxysmal AF. She has never had a stroke or bleeding. Which is the most appropriate stroke-prevention strategy?

  1. Aspirin 81 mg daily
  2. A direct oral anticoagulant
  3. No therapy because her AF is only paroxysmal
  4. Warfarin with INR goal 2–3
Answer & explanationB. CHA₂DS₂-VASc = 4 (HTN 1, DM 1, age 65–74 1, female 1) → well above the ≥2%/yr threshold, so anticoagulate. A DOAC is first-line over warfarin (C is wrong — paroxysmal AF carries the same stroke risk). Aspirin is not recommended for stroke prevention in AF (A). Warfarin (D) is reserved for mechanical valves or moderate–severe mitral stenosis.

2. A 68-year-old man with AF and HFrEF (EF 30%) has a resting HR of 120 despite an attempt at rate control. Which agent is contraindicated for rate control?

  1. Metoprolol succinate
  2. Diltiazem
  3. Carvedilol
  4. Digoxin
Answer & explanationB. Non-dihydropyridine CCBs such as diltiazem are contraindicated in HFrEF (strong negative inotropes). Guideline beta-blockers (A, C) are appropriate, and digoxin (D) is a useful adjunct in HFrEF because it is a positive inotrope.

3. A 60-year-old man with persistent AF for an unknown duration is scheduled for elective cardioversion. He is not currently anticoagulated. What is required?

  1. Cardiovert now; start a DOAC only if CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2
  2. 3 weeks of anticoagulation beforehand (or a TEE), then 4 weeks after regardless of score
  3. Aspirin before and after the procedure
  4. No anticoagulation because cardioversion restores sinus rhythm
Answer & explanationB. With AF >48 h or unknown duration, give 3 weeks of OAC or perform a TEE to exclude LAA thrombus before cardioversion, then 4 weeks of OAC afterward regardless of CHA₂DS₂-VASc because of post-cardioversion atrial stunning. The other options under-treat a high-risk peri-cardioversion window.

4. A 55-year-old man with symptomatic paroxysmal AF, no structural heart disease, remains highly symptomatic despite a beta-blocker and wants to avoid lifelong antiarrhythmics. Per the 2023 ACC/AHA guideline, which is now a reasonable first-line rhythm-control option?

  1. Catheter ablation
  2. Digoxin
  3. Aspirin plus clopidogrel
  4. AV-node ablation with pacemaker
Answer & explanationA. The 2023 guideline upgraded catheter ablation to Class 1 first-line rhythm control for selected younger, symptomatic paroxysmal-AF patients with few comorbidities (and for AF + HFrEF). Digoxin (B) is a rate-control adjunct; antiplatelets (C) don't prevent AF stroke; AV-node ablation + pacemaker (D) is a last-line rate-control strategy, not first-line rhythm control.

5. An 80-year-old woman with AF (CHA₂DS₂-VASc 5) has had two falls and a prior NSAID-related GI bleed. She and her family worry that anticoagulation is too dangerous. What is the best advice?

  1. Withhold anticoagulation given her falls and bleeding history
  2. Use aspirin instead of an anticoagulant
  3. Anticoagulate — stroke risk and disability outweigh fall/bleeding risk; address modifiable bleeding factors
  4. Place a WATCHMAN device first-line before trying any anticoagulant
Answer & explanationC. Falls and an elevated HAS-BLED are not reasons to withhold anticoagulation; the disability from an AF-related stroke generally outweighs the non-major bleeding counted in risk scores, and modifiable factors (stop NSAIDs, control BP) should be optimized. Aspirin (B) isn't effective stroke prevention in AF. LAA occlusion (D) is Class 2a only when there is a genuine long-term contraindication to anticoagulation, not as a reflexive first move.

Attending Dotphrase NEW — EMR-ready A&P smart phrase

* = wildcard to edit at the bedside; brackets = pick-lists. 

#Atrial fibrillation (chronic), * [paroxysmal/persistent/long-standing/permanent; 2023 stage *]. Plan: stroke prevention + rate vs rhythm + risk-factor mod.
- STROKE RISK: CHA2DS2-VASc * [C/H/A2/D/S2/V/Sc] = * → anticoagulate if ≥2%/yr (≈≥2 men / ≥3 women; Class 1). Peri-cardioversion: anticoag x4wk regardless of score. ASA NOT for stroke prevention.
- BLEEDING: HAS-BLED * — do NOT withhold OAC for high score or falls alone; optimize modifiable factors (BP, stop NSAIDs).
- ANTICOAG: DOAC 1st-line * [apixaban / rivaroxaban / dabigatran / edoxaban]. apixaban if CrCl<15/ESRD or BMI>40-50. WARFARIN only if mechanical valve or mod-severe MS.
- CARDIOVERSION: if AF >48h/unknown → 3wk OAC or TEE first; then 4wk OAC after (atrial stunning).
- RATE CONTROL: goal HR <110 * → BB [metop succinate / carvedilol / bisoprolol] OR diltiazem (AVOID diltiazem in HFrEF); digoxin adjunct (renally cleared, watch tox). Refractory → AV-node ablation + PPM.
- RHYTHM CONTROL if *: symptoms attributable to AF / young / few comorbid / recent onset / tachy-cardiomyopathy / LV dysfunction → cardiology. Catheter ablation Class 1 1st-line in select parox AF & AF+HFrEF.
- AAD * [amiodarone (PFT/LFT/TFT q monitoring) / sotalol·dofetilide (QT, inpatient start, renal dosing) / flecainide (NO structural/CAD)].
- LAAO [WATCHMAN] 2a if long-term OAC contraindication.
- RISK-FACTOR MOD: wt loss → BMI <27, reduce/stop EtOH, BP & lipid control, OSA screen, tobacco cessation.

Take-Home Points

  1. Use CHA₂DS₂-VASc to estimate annual stroke risk; anticoagulate at ≥2%/yr (≥2 men / ≥3 women) and in all patients peri-cardioversion regardless of score. A high HAS-BLED or falls is not a reason to withhold.
  2. DOACs are first-line for most AF; apixaban for high BMI and ESRD/CrCl <15; warfarin only for a mechanical valve or moderate–severe mitral stenosis.
  3. Rate-control to HR <110 for most (beta-blocker or non-DHP CCB; digoxin adjunct). Diltiazem is contraindicated in HFrEF.
  4. Persistent symptoms most favor rhythm control; the 2023 guideline makes catheter ablation Class 1 first-line in selected patients and in AF + HFrEF.
