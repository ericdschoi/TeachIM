Learning Objectives

  1. Develop a systematic process for the evaluation and classification of a patient with chronic heart failure (HF).
  2. Describe evidence-based therapies for HF with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) and their impact on patient outcomes.
  3. Initiate and titrate guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) for HFrEF based on patient characteristics and side effects.
  4. Determine guideline-directed therapies for HFpEF, HFmrEF, and HF with improved EF.

Scope: chronic outpatient management. Evaluation of new-onset HF and inpatient management of decompensation are out of scope, as in the original.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Q1. Name the four EF-based HF categories with their cut-offs, and which one is defined by trajectory rather than a single value. HFrEF LVEF ≤40%; HFmrEF 41–49%; HFpEF ≥50%; HFimpEF — a history of LVEF ≤40% that has improved by ≥10 points to >40% (defined by trajectory, not a single value). HFimpEF patients should continue GDMT indefinitely.
Q2. List the four GDMT pillars for HFrEF and the approximate combined mortality benefit at target doses. Beta-blocker (evidence-based: metoprolol succinate, carvedilol, bisoprolol) + ARNI (preferred) or ACEi/ARB + MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone) + SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin). Combined ~73% relative mortality reduction at target doses — yet only ~1% of real-world patients reach all target doses (CHAMP-HF).
Q3. A patient on GDMT has a 20% rise in creatinine after you start an ACEi and an SGLT2i. Stop them? No. A mild GFR decline (<30%) when starting ACEi/ARB/ARNI or SGLT2i is expected and does not warrant dose reduction. Reassess volume status and diuretic dose for true AKI before touching GDMT.

Update — HF Guidance Since 2022 (2023–2025) NEW — teach with current framing

The original board teaches the 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA guideline. That guideline is still the US standard and the HFrEF quadruple-therapy teaching is unchanged and correct. Three developments since June 2023 are worth folding in, especially for the HFpEF/HFmrEF end of the spectrum, which the original (accurately, for 2022) described as therapy-poor.

TopicWhat's new since publication
SGLT2 inhibitors across the EF spectrumThe 2023 ESC focused update made empagliflozin/dapagliflozin Class 1A in HFmrEF and HFpEF (the 2022 US guideline lists 2a; the deck reflects 2a). SGLT2i now have the strongest recommendation across the whole EF range.
Finerenone in HFmrEF/HFpEFFINEARTS-HF (Solomon, NEJM 2024) showed the nonsteroidal MRA finerenone reduced CV death + worsening-HF events in LVEF ≥40%. FDA-approved July 14, 2025 (Kerendia) for HF with LVEF ≥40% — the first MRA proven to benefit HFmrEF/HFpEF, and beneficial on top of an SGLT2i. Emerging as a second pillar for preserved/mildly-reduced EF.
Obesity as a treatable HFpEF driverSTEP-HFpEF (semaglutide, NEJM 2023) and STEP-HFpEF DM (2024) improved symptoms and function in HFpEF + obesity. SUMMIT (tirzepatide, NEJM 2025) cut CV death or worsening HF by ~38% in HFpEF + obesity. Treat obesity directly rather than as a comorbidity to ignore.
HFrEF teachingUnchanged. The four pillars, target doses, NNTs, CRT/ICD criteria, and the titrate-something-every-visit philosophy in the original board all remain current.
Pearl: When you reach the HFpEF column of the EF-spectrum slide, name three things that now have outcome data: an SGLT2i (Class 1A per ESC 2023), finerenone (FDA-approved for LVEF ≥40% in 2025), and treating obesity with a GLP-1/GIP agent. HFpEF is no longer "diuretics only."

Objective 1 — The HF one-liner 6 min

ASK: "You open a chart before a clinic visit. What three descriptors should be in your HF one-liner?" Elicit: NYHA class, ejection fraction, etiology. Click each button to build it.
ComponentCategories
NYHA class (symptom burden)I asymptomatic · II symptoms with normal activity · III symptoms with less-than-ordinary activity · IV symptoms at rest/ADLs. Predicts mortality but is not a strict treatment cut-off (don't withhold GDMT in an asymptomatic patient with LV dysfunction).
Ejection fractionHFrEF ≤40% · HFmrEF 41–49% · HFpEF ≥50% · HFimpEF (was ≤40%, improved ≥10 pts to >40%). The 2022 guideline consolidated HFrEF therapy indications to LVEF ≤40%.
EtiologyIschemic (most common, ~50–60%) vs non-ischemic (HTN, toxin/alcohol, tachycardia-induced, infectious, infiltrative, genetic/idiopathic). Distinction mainly guides revascularization evaluation; GDMT is largely the same regardless of cause.
Pearl: "NYHA class III HFrEF, likely ischemic" tells you the symptom severity, the therapy class, and whether to think about revascularization — all in one line.

Objective 2 — Why GDMT matters & the four pillars 7 min

ASK each, then reveal the number: "How many Americans have HF? One-year mortality untreated? Mortality reduction with full GDMT? Percent of real patients on target doses?"

1-yr mortality untreated~25% (up to 25–50% in original ACEi placebo arms)
Mortality reduction with target-dose GDMT~73% combined
Patients actually on target doses~1% (CHAMP-HF registry)

Objective 3 — Initiate, titrate & troubleshoot 9 min

ASK: "When can you safely start each pillar?" Reveal the conditions, then work the three common roadblocks.
PillarWhen to initiate / key contraindication
Beta-blockerOnly when compensated (euvolemic, adequately perfused) — negative inotropy worsens acute decompensation. Continuation through mild decompensation is generally safe.
ARNIPreferred over ACEi/ARB; can start de novo (no need to start ACEi first). Needs SBP >100; 36-h washout off an ACEi; transition from an ARB can be immediate. Can initiate inpatient (PIONEER-HF). Watch cost.
ACEi / ARBStable creatinine and potassium. ARB often chosen to facilitate later ARNI transition.
MRAHold if K⁺ >5, GFR <30, or Cr >2.5 (men)/>2 (women). Can start early alongside loops to spare potassium supplementation.
SGLT2 inhibitorAvoid if GFR <20 (empagliflozin; <30 dapagliflozin), type 1 DM, or DKA history. Caution with recurrent UTIs. Cost can be a barrier.
Titration philosophy: there is no fixed order — start low doses of all four and up-titrate every 1–2 weeks (faster is safe: STRONG-HF). The single most important rule is to add or titrate something at every visit. Monitor HR, BP, BMP, and volume status.
RoadblockHow to keep GDMT going
AKIExpect a mild GFR dip with ACEi/ARB/ARNI + SGLT2i — <30% drop is OK. For true AKI: reassess volume and adjust diuretics first; temporarily reduce ACEi/ARB/ARNI and re-trial after resolution; hydralazine + isosorbide is a renal-sparing afterload option.
HypotensionTreat symptoms, not the number — asymptomatic SBP 90–100 is an acceptable target. Avoid non-GDMT antihypertensives (CCB, thiazide), stagger dosing AM/PM, switch carvedilol → metoprolol succinate (less alpha-blockade).
HyperkalemiaSGLT2i and ARNI mitigate K⁺; counsel low-K diet; hydralazine + isosorbide as a last resort if ACEi/ARB/ARNI truly can't be tolerated.

Other therapies — HFrEF (device & vasodilator) included above

After ≥3 months of maximally tolerated GDMT, re-image. If EF remains <35%:

TriggerTherapy · NNT (3-yr mortality)
Wide QRS (>120–150 ms, esp. LBBB)CRT (biventricular pacing) · NNT 8 (MIRACLE, COMPANION, MADIT-CRT)
Life expectancy >1 yr, EF <35%ICD (single-chamber or CRT-D) for sudden-death prevention · NNT 23 (MADIT-II, SCD-HeFT)
Self-identified African-American on background therapyHydralazine + isosorbide dinitrate (Class 1a) · NNT 8 (A-HeFT) — high pill burden; also useful when ACEi/ARB/ARNI can't be used. Note the inherent limitations of race-based recommendations.

Objective 4 — Across the EF spectrum 5 min

ASK: "What do you actually offer a patient with HFpEF? With HFmrEF? Someone whose EF recovered?"
CategoryGuideline-recommended therapy
HFimpEFContinue full GDMT indefinitely — TRED-HF showed >40% of patients relapsed after withdrawal vs none who continued.
HFmrEFSGLT2i recommended. Consider HFrEF-style therapies depending on EF and trajectory (a declining 41% behaves like HFrEF; a stable 49% may not).
HFpEFSGLT2i reduce HF hospitalizations (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER) — 2a in 2022 US guideline, 1A in 2023 ESC. MRA/ARNI 2b (TOPCAT, PARAGON-HF — negative primaries, hospitalization signal). Since publication: finerenone FDA-approved for LVEF ≥40% (2025); GLP-1/tirzepatide improve HFpEF + obesity — see the update section.

Practice Cases from the original board — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — HFrEF with a wide QRS

63 M with HTN, CAD s/p CABG, recurrent volume-overload admissions; cannot dress or walk to the kitchen without dyspnea. Meds: ASA 81, metoprolol tartrate 50 BID, lisinopril 40, furosemide 40 BID. BP 100/60, euvolemic. Cr 1.3, K 3.2. TTE: LVEF 30%, apical/anterior wall-motion abnormality. ECG: LBBB, QRS 150 ms.

HF one-liner?NYHA class IV HFrEF (EF <40%), likely ischemic cardiomyopathy (focal WMA, prior CABG).
How do you optimize?Convert metoprolol tartrate → succinate 100 mg daily (evidence-based BB). Transition lisinopril to an ARNI (≥36 h after the ACEi). Add spironolactone (K⁺ 3.2 gives room) and an SGLT2 inhibitor. With persistent EF <35% and LBBB/QRS 150 → refer for CRT-D. Consider advanced-HF / palliative input given recurrent admissions.

Case 2 — recovered EF (HFimpEF)

27 M with HF, no other history; climbs several flights without limitation. TTE 3 yr ago LVEF 25% (global hypokinesis); TTE 3 mo ago LVEF 50%. Cr 0.8, K 4.2. BP 110/60, HR 88. Meds: metoprolol succinate 100, losartan 150.

HF one-liner?NYHA class I HFimpEF, likely non-ischemic cardiomyopathy (young, global hypokinesis).
Management?Continue current GDMT indefinitely. No clear benefit to further titration in an asymptomatic patient with recovered EF, but do not withdraw therapy — TRED-HF showed high relapse rates. Counsel the patient that the medications are why his EF recovered.

Case 3 — HFpEF

67 with HTN, CKD, DM2, AF, OSA, HF; dyspnea walking to the bathroom, not at rest. BP 140/80, HR 90. Cr 1.3, K 4.1. Meds: metoprolol tartrate 50 BID, lisinopril 40, metformin 1 g BID, glipizide 5, furosemide 40 daily. TTE: LVEF 62%, LVH, LA enlargement, grade II diastolic dysfunction.

HF one-liner?NYHA class III HFpEF, likely from long-standing hypertension (LVH).
How do you optimize?Start an SGLT2 inhibitor and stop glipizide (the SGLT2i also helps glycemia without hypoglycemia). Improve BP control — spironolactone is reasonable. Refer for possible rhythm control of AF. Current additions to consider since publication: finerenone (FDA-approved for LVEF ≥40%, 2025) and, given likely obesity-driven physiology, a GLP-1/GIP agent — see update section.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 58-year-old man with newly diagnosed HFrEF (LVEF 28%), now euvolemic on a loop diuretic, has BP 118/72, HR 78, Cr 1.1, K⁺ 4.3. Which regimen best reflects guideline-directed therapy?

  1. Lisinopril alone, then add others only if symptoms persist
  2. Begin low doses of a beta-blocker, an ARNI, an MRA, and an SGLT2 inhibitor, then up-titrate
  3. Digoxin plus furosemide
  4. Wait until a follow-up echo in 6 months before starting disease-modifying therapy
Answer & explanationB. All four pillars provide additive mortality benefit, so the modern approach is to start low doses of all four in a compensated patient and titrate up — not a sequential "fail one, add the next" strategy (A). Digoxin (C) is symptomatic only, with no mortality benefit. Delaying therapy (D) forgoes early benefit; GDMT should start once the patient is compensated.

2. A 64-year-old woman with HFrEF on an ACE inhibitor is being switched to sacubitril-valsartan. What is required for a safe transition?

  1. Start the ARNI immediately after the last ACEi dose
  2. A 36-hour washout after stopping the ACEi
  3. Confirm the LVEF is ≥50% first
  4. Discontinue her beta-blocker during the switch
Answer & explanationB. A 36-hour washout off the ACEi is required before starting an ARNI to avoid overlapping angioedema risk (no washout is needed when switching from an ARB). The ARNI is indicated in HFrEF, not preserved EF (C), and the beta-blocker should be continued (D).

3. A 70-year-old man with HFrEF (EF 32%) starts dapagliflozin and lisinopril. Two weeks later his creatinine has risen from 1.2 to 1.5 mg/dL (≈25%). He is asymptomatic and euvolemic. Best next step?

  1. Stop both medications
  2. Continue both; a mild GFR decline is expected and acceptable
  3. Stop the SGLT2 inhibitor only
  4. Admit for AKI workup
Answer & explanationB. A creatinine rise of <30% after starting an ACEi/ARB/ARNI or SGLT2 inhibitor is expected hemodynamic change, not true AKI, and does not warrant stopping or dose-reducing therapy in an asymptomatic euvolemic patient. Stopping (A, C) or admitting (D) would forgo proven benefit.

4. A 60-year-old man with persistent HFrEF (EF 30%) on maximally tolerated GDMT for 4 months has a QRS of 156 ms with LBBB morphology and a life expectancy >1 year. Which intervention has the strongest indication?

  1. Cardiac resynchronization therapy (often with a defibrillator)
  2. Add a second loop diuretic
  3. Increase the SGLT2 inhibitor dose
  4. Switch the ARNI back to an ACE inhibitor
Answer & explanationA. Persistent EF <35% after an adequate GDMT trial, with a wide QRS in an LBBB pattern, is the classic indication for CRT (NNT 8), typically combined with an ICD given the EF. The other options do not address dyssynchrony and offer no comparable mortality benefit.

5. A 72-year-old woman with HFpEF (LVEF 58%), obesity (BMI 36), and class III symptoms is already on a loop diuretic. Which therapy now has guideline support to reduce heart-failure events in HFpEF?

  1. An SGLT2 inhibitor
  2. Digoxin
  3. High-dose isosorbide mononitrate
  4. Routine spironolactone for mortality benefit
Answer & explanationA. SGLT2 inhibitors reduce HF hospitalizations in HFpEF (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER) — 2a in the 2022 US guideline and Class 1A in the 2023 ESC update. Digoxin (B) and nitrates (C) have no HFpEF outcome benefit. MRAs have a 2b recommendation with a hospitalization signal but no proven mortality benefit in HFpEF, so "routine for mortality benefit" (D) overstates the evidence. (Newer options since publication: finerenone, FDA-approved for LVEF ≥40% in 2025, and GLP-1/tirzepatide for HFpEF + obesity.)

Take-Home Points

  1. Classify every HF patient with a one-liner: NYHA class + EF category (HFrEF ≤40 / HFmrEF 41–49 / HFpEF ≥50 / HFimpEF) + etiology. It drives therapy.
  2. HFrEF gets all four GDMT pillars — beta-blocker, ARNI (or ACEi/ARB), MRA, SGLT2i — for a ~73% mortality reduction at target doses, yet most patients are under-dosed.
  3. Add or titrate something every visit; manage AKI, hypotension, and hyperkalemia rather than abandoning GDMT, and expect a mild GFR dip on ACEi/ARB/ARNI + SGLT2i.
  4. Persistent EF <35% after ≥3 months of GDMT → CRT (wide QRS/LBBB) and/or ICD (life expectancy >1 yr); HFimpEF patients continue GDMT indefinitely.
  5. SGLT2i benefit HFmrEF and HFpEF. Since publication: finerenone is FDA-approved for LVEF ≥40% (2025) and treating obesity with GLP-1/tirzepatide improves HFpEF outcomes.
