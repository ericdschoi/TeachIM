Learning Objectives

  1. Identify the patients for whom starting antihypertensive therapy produces a clinically meaningful reduction in cardiovascular events and death.
  2. Set the target blood pressure and select an appropriate first-line agent based on the patient's comorbidities.
  3. Recognize the clinical features that should prompt evaluation for a secondary cause of hypertension.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Name the four first-line drug classes for chronic hypertension, with one example of each. Thiazide-like diuretic (chlorthalidone, preferred over HCTZ), dihydropyridine calcium-channel blocker (amlodipine), ACE inhibitor (lisinopril), and ARB (losartan). ACEi and ARB are never combined.
Q2. A 55-year-old with diabetes has a confirmed BP of 134/82. Do you start a medication, and to what target? Yes. Diabetes is a high-risk condition, so under the 2025 AHA/ACC guideline you treat at ≥130/80 to a target of <130/80. (Even under the older 2017 framing, the high-risk target was <130/80.)
Q3. Give three features that should make you suspect a secondary cause of hypertension. Severe or resistant HTN; acute onset or new lability; onset before puberty or age <30 without obesity/family history; unexplained electrolyte abnormalities (especially hypokalemia → hyperaldosteronism). Any of these lowers your threshold to work it up.

2025 AHA/ACC Guideline Update NEW — what changed since this talk was written

The talk was built on the 2017 ACC/AHA guideline (and JNC 8). In 2025 the AHA/ACC released a new hypertension guideline that replaces the 2017 document. The standing teaching here — the four first-line agents, the secondary-cause triggers, and the lifestyle measures — remains correct; what moved is the target, the risk tool, and the intensification strategy.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (HTN_Chronic_Optimized.pptx) and click through, or rebuild the schematic on a whiteboard. Every cursor icon on the board is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Talk without cases: ~15–20 min. Cases: +10–15 min. Print the learner summary handout and distribute it for the case pair-shares.

Hook (2 min)

"Hypertension is the single most common condition you'll manage in clinic, and the number needed to treat to prevent a cardiovascular event is lower than for statins, aspirin, or diabetes drugs. So why do we still under-treat it?" Plant the tension between how much good treatment does and how often we stop short.

Objective 1 — Why treat, and who benefits 4 min

ASK: "What fraction of US adults have hypertension, and what does treating it actually buy them?" — then click to reveal. ~45% of adults >18 by the ACC/AHA definition; lowering BP reduces stroke, MI, and heart failure.

Work the board's worked example: a 50-year-old smoker with sustained BP >150/90, treated to <140/85, gets an absolute 10-year CV-event risk reduction of ~10% (NNT 10) and an all-cause mortality reduction of ~6% (NNT 16). The teaching point is that benefit scales with baseline risk — higher-risk patients get more absolute benefit from the same BP drop.

Objective 2 — Target & first-line agent 7 min

ASK: "What's the ideal BP if you could get there with lifestyle alone — and why isn't that the medication target for everyone?" — reveal <120/80 ideal, but drug-driven over-treatment carries its own risk (syncope, electrolytes, falls), as SPRINT showed (avg 2.7 meds for tight control, more adverse events).

Set the targets, then flag the 2025 simplification (see the update section): the board shows low-risk <140/90 vs high-risk <130/80; the 2025 AHA/ACC goal is <130/80 for most. High-risk = CKD, prior stroke/MI, diabetes, heart failure, or elevated 10-year ASCVD risk.

ASK: "Four first-line agents — talk me through the best candidate and the one catch for each."
Agent (example)Best candidateKey caution / monitoring
Dihydropyridine CCB — amlodipine 5–10 mgMost patients; well tolerated; good in late CKDDose-dependent pedal edema; avoid in HFrEF (amlodipine OK if needed)
ACE inhibitor — lisinopril 5–40 mgMost patients; preferred with CVD or proteinuriaCheck BMP in 1–2 wk; a GFR drop <30% is acceptable; angioedema; never with an ARB

Pearls worth dropping in: ACE inhibitors lower BP somewhat less effectively in Black patients (but still use one if there's a compelling indication like diabetic nephropathy). Chlorthalidone is more potent and longer-acting than HCTZ — at the cost of more hypokalemia. SGLT2 inhibitors lower SBP by ~5 mmHg but are not FDA-approved for BP alone.

Objective 3 — Secondary causes 5 min

ASK: "Four features that should make you stop and think 'this isn't just essential hypertension.'"

Triggers: severe or resistant HTN; acute rise or new lability; before puberty or age <30 without obesity/family history; associated electrolyte abnormalities. Then build the differential:

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Have the group rebuild: the <130/80 target, the four first-line agents and one catch each, the two-drug rule for stage 2, and three secondary-cause triggers.

Practice Cases 10–15 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Not yet at goal on one drug

55-year-old African-American woman, no heart or kidney disease, never-smoker. Diagnosed with HTN 3 months ago (home average 155/95), started on amlodipine 10 mg; now averaging 145/90, BMI 29, normal A1c and lipids.

Next best step in management? (choose)C. Start lisinopril 10 mg daily. She is not at goal on a maximal-dose CCB, so add a second first-line agent. (B) Amlodipine BID adds little. (D) Hydralazine is not first-line. (E) A statin may be reasonable later but her 10-year ASCVD risk is <10% (~6.6%). (A) No red flags for a secondary work-up.
What lab surveillance and follow-up?If starting an ACEi/ARB or thiazide, recheck a BMP in 1–2 weeks; reassess BP, tolerance, and titrate with home monitoring in 2–4 weeks.

Case 2 — A single high reading

45-year-old man with a single office BP of 145/90.

Next steps to determine whether he has HTN?Recheck after he sits calmly 5 min, arm relaxed, correct cuff size; confirm persistent elevation with 2+ readings in or out of the office.
If confirmed, what work-up?Risk-factor assessment: diabetes screen, lipids, creatinine, urine protein:creatinine, ± ECG. No indication to evaluate for a secondary cause — no red flags.

Case 3 — High-risk comorbidity

60-year-old man with gout and peripheral arterial disease, BP 160/95 on several visits, doesn't check at home.

Goal BP?<130/80 — high risk because of established ASCVD (PAD).
What would you start?He is >20/10 above goal, so most patients like him need two agents — sequentially or together. A favored combination is an ACEi + CCB, which reduced CV risk vs ACEi + thiazide in the ACCOMPLISH trial. (Gout argues against a thiazide here.)

Case 4 — Lifestyle-first and white-coat question

60-year-old woman with obesity and pre-diabetes, 1–2 alcoholic drinks nightly, non-smoker. BP 150/90 on two consecutive visits; she prefers to try lifestyle first.

Evidence-based lifestyle changes and their BP effect?Sodium <2 g/d (↓~5/2.5); high-potassium diet if no CKD (↓~5/3); weight loss (↓~0.5–2 mmHg per kg); exercise ~40 min, 3–4×/wk (↓~4–6/3); reduce alcohol (≥2 drinks/d in women, ≥3 in men significantly raise HTN risk).
If his home BPs were normal — still start meds?White-coat HTN carries increased CV and mortality risk, but most experts still recommend against empiric drug therapy at this stage; instead aggressively manage other CV risk factors and monitor (treat it as a pre-hypertensive, higher-risk state).

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 58-year-old man with type 2 diabetes has an office BP of 136/84, confirmed by home readings averaging 134/82. His eGFR is 70 and he is on no antihypertensives. Under current AHA/ACC guidance, what is the most appropriate management?

  1. Lifestyle changes only; recheck in 6 months
  2. Start a single first-line antihypertensive, targeting <130/80
  3. Start two agents immediately
  4. No treatment; his BP is below 140/90
Answer & explanationB. Diabetes is a high-risk condition, so pharmacologic therapy is indicated at ≥130/80 with a target of <130/80 — he is at stage 1 with a compelling indication, so lifestyle alone (A) is insufficient and "below 140/90" (D) is the outdated threshold. Two agents (C) are reserved for stage 2 (≥140/90). An ACEi or ARB is especially appropriate given diabetes.

2. A 49-year-old woman is found to have a BP of 162/98, confirmed on repeat. She has no other conditions. What is the most appropriate initial pharmacologic strategy?

  1. Start a single first-line agent and titrate
  2. Start two first-line agents of different classes, ideally as a single-pill combination
  3. Start a beta-blocker
  4. Lifestyle changes for 6 months before any drug
Answer & explanationB. She has stage 2 hypertension (≥140/90), for which the guideline recommends initiating two first-line agents, ideally as a single-pill combination, because most patients this far above goal need more than one drug. A single agent (A) under-treats stage 2; a beta-blocker (C) is not first-line for uncomplicated HTN; deferring all drugs (D) is appropriate only for lower-stage, lower-risk patients.

3. A 52-year-old man with hypertension remains at 150/92 despite adherence to lisinopril, amlodipine, and chlorthalidone at adequate doses. His eGFR is 60 and potassium is 4.1. What is the best next step?

  1. Add spironolactone
  2. Add a second ACE inhibitor
  3. Switch chlorthalidone to hydrochlorothiazide
  4. Add hydralazine
Answer & explanationA. This is resistant hypertension (uncontrolled on an ACEi/ARB + CCB + thiazide-like diuretic). The guideline-preferred fourth agent is a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist — spironolactone — when the eGFR is ≥45 and potassium permits, both of which he meets. Two RAAS blockers (B) are contraindicated. Swapping to the less potent HCTZ (C) would likely worsen control. Hydralazine (D) is a later option if an MRA can't be used.

4. A 26-year-old woman with no obesity or family history of hypertension presents with a BP of 168/104 and an unprovoked serum potassium of 3.1. Which is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Start chlorthalidone and recheck in a month
  2. Reassure; this is essential hypertension
  3. Obtain a plasma aldosterone:renin ratio
  4. Order polysomnography
Answer & explanationC. Young age without typical risk factors plus unexplained hypokalemia is a classic trigger for a secondary work-up, and primary hyperaldosteronism is the most common secondary cause — screen with an aldosterone:renin ratio (off spironolactone). A thiazide (A) would worsen the hypokalemia and miss the diagnosis. Reassurance (B) ignores the red flags. Polysomnography (D) fits an obese, snoring patient, which she is not.

5. A 61-year-old man with peripheral arterial disease has a BP of 158/96 on two visits. He needs combination therapy. Which pairing has the strongest cardiovascular-outcome evidence?

  1. ACE inhibitor + thiazide
  2. ACE inhibitor + dihydropyridine CCB
  3. ACE inhibitor + ARB
  4. Beta-blocker + thiazide
Answer & explanationB. In the ACCOMPLISH trial, an ACE inhibitor + amlodipine reduced cardiovascular events more than an ACE inhibitor + hydrochlorothiazide (A) in high-risk hypertensives. ACEi + ARB (C) is contraindicated (dual RAAS blockade). Beta-blocker + thiazide (D) is not a preferred first-line combination for uncomplicated HTN and carries metabolic downsides.

NEVER ACEi+ARB.
- preferred combo: ACEi + DHP-CCB (ACCOMPLISH > ACEi+thiazide)
- resistant (uncontrolled on ACEi/ARB + CCB + thiazide-like): add MRA spironolactone* if eGFR ≥45 & K ok; then [amiloride / BB / alpha / aprocitentan / central / vasodilator]

Take-Home Points

  1. Treat to <130/80 for most adults (2025 AHA/ACC); the board's split low-risk/high-risk target reflects the older 2017 risk-based framing.
  2. Decide WHO gets a drug by absolute risk: established CVD/DM/CKD or a 10-year risk ≥7.5% (PREVENT) means treat at ≥130/80; lower-risk stage-1 patients get a lifestyle trial first.
  3. Four first-line agents — thiazide-like (chlorthalidone preferred), dihydropyridine CCB (amlodipine), ACE inhibitor (lisinopril), ARB (losartan); never ACEi + ARB; start two for stage 2.
  4. Lifestyle is foundational and effective: DASH, sodium <2 g/d, high-potassium diet, weight loss, exercise, limit alcohol.
  5. Work up a secondary cause when HTN is severe/resistant, acute/labile, very young, or paired with electrolyte abnormalities — hyperaldosteronism is the most common.
