Learning Objectives

  1. Identify the bacterial pathogens commonly responsible for CAP and the risk factors for more resistant pathogens (MRSA, Pseudomonas).
  2. Determine CAP severity using PSI, CURB-65, and the ATS criteria, and use it to choose the site of care (outpatient / floor / ICU).
  3. Manage non-severe and severe inpatient CAP, including when to culture and when to broaden coverage.
  4. Manage CAP in the ambulatory setting, stratified by comorbidities and MDR risk.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What does each letter of CURB-65 stand for, and what score sends a patient inpatient? Confusion; Urea (BUN) >19–20 mg/dL; Respiratory rate ≥30; Blood pressure (SBP <90 or DBP ≤60); age ≥65. One point each. 0–1 = outpatient candidate; 2 = consider admission/observation; 3–5 = inpatient (often ICU). PSI is the better-validated/preferred tool; CURB-65 is the easy-to-remember alternative.
Q2. Which two patient features actually justify empiric MRSA/Pseudomonas coverage now that HCAP is gone? (1) Prior respiratory isolation/colonization with MRSA or Pseudomonas, and (2) hospitalization with receipt of IV antibiotics within the prior 90 days — combined with locally validated risk and severity. Nursing-home residence, dialysis, and wound care alone are no longer indications.
Q3. A patient has CAP and a procalcitonin of 0.08 µg/L. Do you withhold antibiotics? No. Procalcitonin is not used to decide whether to start antibiotics in CAP (sensitivity for bacterial CAP only ~38–91%). Treat based on the clinical and radiographic diagnosis. Procalcitonin can help guide early discontinuation, not initiation.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the corrected interactive board (Community_Acquired_Pneumonia_Optimized.pptx) and click through Diagnosis → Classification → Management → Duration → Cases, or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. Hand learners the "Antibiotic man" worksheet (from the Antibiotics lessons) if available. Talk ~30 min; cases add ~15 min.

Hook (2 min)

"Three patients walk in with cough, fever, and an infiltrate. One goes home on a pill, one goes to the floor, one goes to the ICU on vanc and Zosyn. Same diagnosis — what are the two questions that sort them?" The whole talk answers that: how severe is it (site of care) and does this patient have MDR risk factors (how broad to cover).

Objective 1 — Microbiology & MDR risk 7 min

ASK: "Most common bacterial cause of CAP?" Reveal: S. pneumoniae (still #1 despite the pneumococcal vaccine). Then GNRs (H. flu, Moraxella), atypicals (Legionella, Chlamydia, Mycoplasma), and viruses (influenza, COVID-19) — a major cause.
Pearl — HCAP is dead (2019). The old "healthcare-associated pneumonia" label (nursing home, dialysis, wound care) over-predicted resistance and drove overtreatment. It's retired. Add MRSA/Pseudomonas coverage only for (1) prior MRSA/Pseudomonas, or (2) hospitalization + IV antibiotics within 90 days — plus local epidemiology.

Objective 2 — Severity & site of care 8 min

ASK: "PSI or CURB-65 — which decides ICU admission?" Reveal: neither directly — they triage outpatient vs inpatient. The ATS criteria (>1 major or ≥3 minor) decide ICU.
ToolWhat it decidesThresholds
PSI (preferred)Site of careClass I–II → outpatient; III → observe; IV–V → inpatient
CURB-65 (easy)Site of care0–1 → outpatient; 2 → admit/observe; 3–5 → inpatient
ATS criteriaICU admissionMajor: septic shock on pressors, mechanical ventilation. Minor (≥3): RR >30, P:F <250, multilobar, confusion, BUN >20, WBC <4k, plt <100k, T <36 °C, hypotension needing fluids
Pearl — scores assist, judgment decides. Always pair PSI/CURB-65 with clinical judgment (hypoxia, social factors, ability to take oral meds). A "low score" hypoxemic patient still gets admitted.

Objective 3 — Inpatient management 8 min

ASK: "First two questions on every admitted CAP patient?" Reveal: (1) Is it severe? (2) Are there MDR risk factors? Those two answers drive cultures and coverage.
ScenarioCultures?Empiric coverage
Severe CAPYes — blood, sputum, Legionella + pneumococcal urinary AgStandard CAP + empiric MRSA and Pseudomonas
Non-severe, prior MRSA/PseudomonasYesStandard CAP + cover MRSA ± Pseudomonas per prior data
Non-severe, hospitalization + IV abx <90 dYes (cultures)Standard CAP treatment
Non-severe, no risk factorsNoStandard CAP treatment

Standard inpatient CAP: IV beta-lactam (ampicillin-sulbactam or ceftriaxone) + azithromycin or doxycycline; or a respiratory fluoroquinolone. MRSA: IV vancomycin (or linezolid). Pseudomonas: piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, ceftazidime, aztreonam, or meropenem — continue atypical coverage. De-escalate to standard CAP if cultures are negative.

ASK: "Does procalcitonin tell you whether to start antibiotics?" Reveal: no — start based on the clinical/radiographic diagnosis. Procalcitonin only helps with early stopping.

Objective 4 — Outpatient management & duration 5 min

Outpatient groupRegimen
No comorbidities, no MDR riskAmoxicillin 1 g TID, or doxycycline (some add azithromycin for atypical coverage)
Comorbidities (chronic heart/lung/liver/renal disease, DM, alcoholism, malignancy, asplenia)Amoxicillin-clavulanate or a cephalosporin + azithromycin/doxycycline, or respiratory FQ monotherapy
Pearl — no macrolide monotherapy. US pneumococcal macrolide resistance is too high; azithromycin alone is not recommended. Amoxicillin is dosed 1 g TID (high-dose) for reliable pneumococcal coverage.
Pearl — duration is 5–7 days. Treat at least 5 days and until clinically stable (afebrile, stable vitals/oxygenation). Longer courses for extrapulmonary infection, Pseudomonas/Mycobacteria/Burkholderia, or necrotizing pneumonia / abscess / empyema.

Retrieval close (3 min)

Close the board. Have learners rebuild the algorithm from memory for an imaginary admit: name the likely bugs, score severity (which tool for site, which for ICU), decide cultures + coverage from the two questions, and state the duration. Whoever names a drug must say which organism it covers.

Cases original board cases — click to reveal

Case 1 — The floor admit

70-year-old, 4 days of productive cough, malaise, fevers. T 100.9 °F, HR 110, BP 99/53, RR 33, SpO₂ 90%. WBC 17k, lactate 2.1. CXR: focal RLL consolidation.

Where should they be managed?Inpatient. CURB-65 = 2 (age ≥65, RR >30). PSI is preferred over CURB-65 for the site-of-care decision.
What pathogens are likely?S. pneumoniae, GNRs (H. flu, Moraxella), atypicals. Viruses are also a major cause of CAP.
What antibiotics would you use?Ceftriaxone + azithromycin. Doxycycline can be substituted for atypical coverage when there are QT concerns.
How long should he be treated?5–7 days, guided by clinical stability (resolution of vital-sign abnormalities).

Case 2 — The ICU admit

54-year-old with DM2, 3 days of fever and cough, recently admitted for MRSA cellulitis. T 103 °F, HR 122, BP 80/53 after 2 L IVF, RR 33, SpO₂ 88% on 6 L NC. CXR: LUL cavitary lesion. COVID-19 and flu PCR negative.

Where should they be treated?Inpatient ICU — septic shock not responsive to fluids (an ATS major criterion).
What work-up do you want?This is severe CAP: blood and sputum cultures, Legionella and pneumococcal urinary antigens (and an MRSA swab).
What antibiotics would you use?IV vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam + azithromycin. Severe CAP plus prior MRSA warrants broad empiric coverage; a cavitary lesion raises suspicion for MRSA, anaerobes, or mycobacteria.

Case 3 — The outpatient

41-year-old, no PMH, fever and cough ×2 days. T 99 °F, HR 93, BP 110/53, RR 33, SpO₂ 94%. CXR: diffuse interstitial infiltrates. WBC 12k, BUN 12, Cr 1.1. COVID-19 and flu PCR negative.

Where should they be treated?Outpatient. CURB-65 = 0, PSI class I.
What additional work-up would you want?None. Without risk factors for resistant organisms or severe disease, blood and sputum cultures are not needed.
Procalcitonin returns at 0.2 µg/L — would you start antibiotics?Yes. The 2019 IDSA/ATS guidelines recommend antibiotics regardless of procalcitonin level (sensitivity for bacterial CAP only ~38–91%).
What antibiotics would you use?Amoxicillin 1 g TID for 5 days; doxycycline 100 mg BID is an option.
What if they had a history of COPD?Comorbidity → broaden: amoxicillin-clavulanate or cefpodoxime + azithromycin.
What if they recently received amoxicillin for a UTI?Recent antibiotic use → broaden, ideally with a different class: a respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin/moxifloxacin).

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 72-year-old nursing-home resident is admitted with non-severe CAP. He has not been hospitalized or received IV antibiotics in over a year and has no prior MRSA or Pseudomonas. The team wants to start vancomycin and cefepime "because he's from a facility." What is the most appropriate empiric therapy?

  1. Vancomycin + cefepime
  2. Ceftriaxone + azithromycin
  3. Meropenem + vancomycin + azithromycin
  4. Azithromycin monotherapy
Answer & explanationB. The 2019 IDSA/ATS guideline retired HCAP; facility residence alone no longer justifies broad coverage. Empiric MRSA/Pseudomonas coverage (A, C) is reserved for prior MRSA/Pseudomonas or hospitalization + IV antibiotics within 90 days. Azithromycin monotherapy (D) is inappropriate due to high US pneumococcal resistance. Standard CAP therapy is a beta-lactam + macrolide.

2. A 60-year-old woman with CAP has RR 34, BUN 26, confusion, multilobar infiltrates, and WBC 3.5k, but no shock and is not intubated. Which disposition is most appropriate?

  1. Outpatient with oral antibiotics
  2. General medical floor
  3. ICU admission
  4. Observation unit for 24 hours
Answer & explanationC. She meets ≥3 ATS minor criteria (RR >30, BUN >20, confusion, multilobar, WBC <4k) — ICU-level care, even without a major criterion (shock or mechanical ventilation). PSI/CURB-65 (A, B, D) decide outpatient vs inpatient, not ICU; here the ATS minor-criteria count drives the decision.

3. A 48-year-old man with CAP is being discharged. He has no comorbidities and no MDR risk factors. Which oral regimen is most appropriate?

  1. Azithromycin 500 mg daily monotherapy
  2. Amoxicillin 1 g three times daily
  3. Amoxicillin 500 mg twice daily
  4. Ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily
Answer & explanationB. For outpatient CAP without comorbidities, high-dose amoxicillin (1 g TID) or doxycycline is first-line. Macrolide monotherapy (A) is not recommended in the US due to pneumococcal resistance. Low-dose amoxicillin (C) under-doses for pneumococcus. Ciprofloxacin (D) is not a "respiratory" fluoroquinolone — it lacks reliable S. pneumoniae activity; levofloxacin or moxifloxacin would be the FQ options.

4. A 65-year-old admitted with severe CAP is started on vancomycin, piperacillin-tazobactam, and azithromycin. At 48 hours he is improving; blood and sputum cultures and the MRSA nasal PCR are all negative. What is the best next step?

  1. Continue all three agents for a full 14 days
  2. De-escalate to standard CAP therapy (e.g., ceftriaxone + azithromycin)
  3. Add an antifungal
  4. Switch to meropenem
Answer & explanationB. Negative cultures and a negative MRSA nasal PCR (high negative predictive value) support de-escalation to standard CAP coverage — a core stewardship move. Prolonged broad therapy (A, D) and empiric antifungals (C) add toxicity and resistance without benefit. Total duration is 5–7 days guided by clinical stability, not 14.

5. A 58-year-old with severe CAP in the ICU has refractory hypoxemia and high inflammatory markers. Which adjunctive therapy is now recommended by the 2025 ATS guideline?

  1. Routine procalcitonin-guided antibiotic initiation
  2. Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., hydrocortisone)
  3. Inhaled antibiotics
  4. Empiric oseltamivir regardless of testing
Answer & explanationB. The 2025 ATS update recommends systemic corticosteroids for severe CAP (hydrocortisone <400 mg/day ×5–7 days), based on CAPE COD (NEJM 2023; mortality RR 0.62) — a change from the older "against steroids" teaching, which still holds for non-severe CAP. Procalcitonin (A) is not used to start antibiotics. Inhaled antibiotics (C) have no role in CAP. Oseltamivir (D) is given for confirmed/suspected influenza, not empirically for all.

Take-Home Points

  • CAP pathogens: S. pneumoniae, GNRs (H. flu, Moraxella), atypicals, and viruses. MDR risk = prior MRSA/Pseudomonas, or hospitalization + IV antibiotics within 90 days.
  • Use PSI (preferred) or CURB-65 for site of care; use the ATS major/minor criteria for ICU admission (>1 major or ≥3 minor).
  • Severe CAP and prior MRSA/Pseudomonas: culture and empirically cover MRSA ± Pseudomonas. Non-severe CAP without risk factors: don't culture.
  • Standard inpatient CAP: IV beta-lactam + azithromycin/doxycycline, or a respiratory fluoroquinolone. De-escalate by cultures.
  • Outpatient: stratify by comorbidities and MDR risk; amoxicillin 1 g TID (no macrolide monotherapy). Treat 5–7 days, guided by stability.
  • 2025 update: add corticosteroids for severe CAP; not for non-severe CAP.
