Learning Objectives

  1. Use patient-centered, non-judgmental language in history-taking and contraceptive counseling (the PATH framework).
  2. Use evidence-based tools (US MEC, the Contraception app, Bedsider) to select a method that matches the patient's stated goals — not an efficacy-first script.
  3. Recognize how specific conditions change the safety and efficacy of each method, and apply the US MEC categories 1–4.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What are the four "PATH" domains you can use to open a contraception conversation? Pregnancy Attitudes, Timing, and How important is prevention. The PATH questions assess attitude toward pregnancy ("Do you think you might like to have (more) children at some point?"), timing ("When?"), and the importance of preventing pregnancy until then — in a way that does not assume the patient has a defined "plan."
Q2. Which four history items most often rule OUT estrogen-containing (combined) methods? Smoking at age >35, migraine with aura, personal history of VTE/CVD/CVA, and current/personal history of breast cancer. Each pushes combined hormonal contraception to US MEC category 3 or 4. (Breast cancer affects all hormonal methods.)
Q3. Rank these for emergency contraception efficacy: copper IUD, ulipristal (Ella), levonorgestrel (Plan B), combined OCPs (Yuzpe). Copper IUD is the most effective (>99% if ≤5 days). Ulipristal (Ella) outperforms levonorgestrel, especially at days 3–5 and higher BMI. Levonorgestrel (Plan B) is next and is least effective at higher BMI. Combined OCPs (Yuzpe method) are the least effective but the most widely available.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the interactive board (Contraception_Optimized.pptx) and click through each reveal. Every cursor icon on the board is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Talk without cases: ~15–20 min. Cases: +20–30 min. Print the Learner Handout so learners fill it in as you go. Have the Contraception app (Steinberg/Holms, free) and Bedsider.org open for the MEC tab.

Hook (2 min)

"Half of the patients walking into your primary-care clinic today who could get pregnant want to talk about contraception — but most won't bring it up first." Ask the room: what's the first sentence you'd actually say? Park the answers; you'll replace efficacy-first habits with the PATH approach in the next five minutes.

Objective 1 — Patient-centered language: PATH 4 min

ASK: "What phrasing do you use to bring up contraception?" Collect 2–3 openers, then click to reveal the PATH questions.

Walk the board's PATH build: Pregnancy Attitudes ("Do you think you might like to have (more) children at some point?"), Timing ("When do you think that might be?"), How important is prevention ("How important is it to you to prevent pregnancy until then?"). Add the method-preference openers ("What's the most important thing you'd like your birth control to do for you?", "What have you tried, and what worked or didn't?").

Pearl — PATH is inclusive of patients beyond cis, heterosexual couples and avoids framing pregnancy as a "plan," which can be inappropriate or triggering. Lead with open-ended questions; avoid assumptions about goals.

Objective 2a — Match the method to what matters 5 min

ASK: Before showing the comparison grid, ask: "What attributes actually drive a patient's choice?" Steer away from leading with the efficacy chart.

Click through the Methods grid by attribute, not by device: hormone-free vs hormonal, efficacy, maintenance burden (per-day vs per-week vs per-year), and impact on bleeding. Key bleeding effects:

MethodTypical bleeding effectMaintenance
Copper IUDHeavier / crampier periods — reserve for those avoiding hormones, wanting EC, or with light periodsUp to 10–12 yr
LNG IUD & implantLighter, often amenorrhea; some prolonged spotting (progestin thins endometrium — treat with oral estrogen if no contraindication)3–8 yr
Depo-Provera (DMPA)Lighter to amenorrhea, esp. >1 yr; can have ongoing spottingEvery 3 months
Pill / patch / ringLighten or maintain regular monthly menses (predictability is a feature)Daily / weekly / monthly
Pearl — Bleeding change may not settle until 3–4 cycles. Estrogen-free methods are more prone to 6–12 months of unscheduled spotting; combined methods usually settle within ~3 months. Spotting is a leading reason patients quit early — counsel up front.

Objective 2b — Choosing an OCP 4 min

Click "Estrogen" and "Progestin" to reveal mechanism. Estrogen (usually ethinyl estradiol) suppresses ovulation and stabilizes the endometrium; a common start is ~20 mcg. Lower estrogen → more breakthrough bleeding; higher estrogen → more thrombosis. Progestin thickens cervical mucus, slows tubal transport, thins the endometrium, and suppresses ovulation. Four generations: earlier = more androgenic; later (3rd/4th) = more anti-androgenic but higher VTE risk when combined with estrogen.

Pearl — Reasonable default: ~20 mcg EE + a progestin. Reserve 3rd/4th-gen progestins (e.g., drospirenone) for anti-androgenic benefit because of higher clot risk. Pick 2–3 "favorites" and titrate. Examples: Junel/Loestrin (1st-gen), Lutera (2nd-gen), Yaz (4th-gen — FDA VTE warning). Bonus: if a pill name lacks "Lo," it usually contains 35 mcg EE.

Objective 3 — Conditions that change safety/efficacy 4 min

ASK: "What are the classic contraindications to estrogen?" Take answers, then click each contraindication icon.

Avoid combined (estrogen) methods with: smoking at age >35, migraine with aura, personal VTE/CVD/CVA. Breast cancer → avoid any hormonal method. Cervical/uterine cancer, anatomic abnormality, or active PID → avoid IUD placement. When in doubt, open the US MEC tab and read the category (1–4).

US MEC categoryMeaning
1Use without restriction
2Advantages generally outweigh risks
3Risks usually outweigh advantages — not recommended unless no better option
4Unacceptable health risk — do not use

Emergency contraception 3 min

Use the STEPS lens (Safety, Tolerability, Efficacy, Price, Simplicity). Headlines: Plan B (levonorgestrel) and Ella (ulipristal) are the best efficacy/tolerability combo; copper IUD is the most effective overall (>99% if ≤5 days); combined OCPs (Yuzpe) are the most widely available backup (100 mcg EE + 0.5 mg LNG per dose, ~4–5 pills, q12h ×2). Ella and Plan B lose efficacy with higher BMI; ulipristal is preferred at days 3–5.

Pearl — The LNG IUD is non-inferior to the copper IUD for EC (Turok, NEJM 2021) though not FDA-labeled for that indication. If unprotected sex occurred within the prior 2 weeks, a negative urine pregnancy test can't fully exclude pregnancy before IUD placement.

Retrieval close (2 min)

Cover the board. Ask learners to rebuild from memory: (1) the four PATH domains, (2) the four estrogen red-flags, (3) the EC efficacy ranking, (4) the default OCP. Fill gaps from their answers, not your slides.

Cases original cases — sub-questions click-to-reveal

Print Contraception-Cases-Learner.docx for small groups; the facilitator answer key is Contraception-Cases-Facilitator.docx.

Case 1 — Ella, 23, acne, wants a COCP

23F, history of acne, establishing care, wants a combined OCP. Exam normal, BMI 35.

What US MEC category are COCPs for her?Category 2 — advantages generally outweigh theoretical/proven risks; the only flag is BMI >30.
How would you counsel on benefits/risks?Screen for the estrogen contraindications she lacks (tobacco, migraine with aura, VTE). Counsel that efficacy may be modestly reduced at higher BMI and that COCPs don't protect against STIs.
What estrogen dose / progestin generation suits her?Low-to-medium-dose estrogen; for acne, a 3rd/4th-gen (anti-androgenic) progestin can help — balanced against its higher VTE risk.
How can she skip her period for travel?Skip the placebo pills and start the next active pack immediately (continuous dosing).

Case 2 — Miranda, 35, HTN, bipolar on lamotrigine, menorrhagia

35F, well-controlled HTN, bipolar depression on lamotrigine, menorrhagia; wants to switch off her COCP.

What's the lamotrigine–combined-hormonal interaction and its category?Category 3 — estrogen increases lamotrigine clearance (lowers lamotrigine levels), risking loss of seizure/mood control. Not recommended unless no better option.
What discreet, menorrhagia-friendly options fit?LNG IUD (also treats heavy bleeding), the implant, or DMPA — all progestin-only and discreet.
How do you counsel on choosing an LNG IUD?By duration (3/5/6/7-yr devices) and her period preference (lighter vs none). Counsel: benefits = high efficacy + lighter/no periods; risks = procedural complications, no STI protection; side effect = irregular bleeding for 3–6 months.

Case 3 — David, 21, transgender man, T1DM, wants "the shot"

21yo transgender man, obesity, type 1 diabetes (A1c 9%, microalbuminuria, mild retinopathy), one male partner, requesting DMPA.

What MEC categories apply to his diabetes with complications?DM with complications: combined hormonal methods and DMPA are category 3; all others except the copper IUD are category 2.
Which methods could worsen gender dysphoria, and what do you choose instead?Avoid methods that increase menstrual bleeding (e.g., copper IUD). Favor methods that lighten or stop menses (e.g., LNG IUD).
What additional counseling does he need?Counsel on condom use for STI prevention, use PATH to assess future pregnancy goals — and (2024 update) note that testosterone is NOT reliable contraception; he still needs a method if pregnancy is undesired.

Case 4 — Kylee, 35, comorbidity vignettes

35F, contraceptive counseling. Which combined hormonal methods fit each comorbidity?

Compensated cirrhosis (hepatitis B) vs decompensated cirrhosis (alcohol)?Compensated: all methods low-risk. Decompensated: COCPs contraindicated; all other methods except the copper IUD are category 3.
Smoking and age?Combined methods: <35 yo = category 2; >35 yo and <15 cigarettes/day = category 3; >35 yo and ≥15/day = category 4. All non-combined methods remain safe.
She's on the patch, then breaks her leg needing ORIF — now what?Switch to a progestin-only method: combined hormonal methods are category 4 after major surgery with prolonged immobilization (VTE risk).

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 38-year-old woman who smokes 20 cigarettes/day requests contraception. She has regular menses and no other medical problems. Which method is US MEC category 4 (do not use)?

  1. Levonorgestrel IUD
  2. Etonogestrel implant
  3. Combined estrogen-progestin pill
  4. Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate
  5. Copper IUD
Answer & explanationC — combined estrogen-progestin pill. Age ≥35 plus smoking ≥15 cigarettes/day is US MEC category 4 for any combined (estrogen-containing) method because of arterial/VTE risk. Progestin-only methods (A, B, D) and the copper IUD (E) are estrogen-free and remain category 1–2 — they're all acceptable.

2. A 29-year-old with bipolar disorder maintained on lamotrigine wants to start a combined oral contraceptive. What is the key concern?

  1. Lamotrigine raises estrogen levels, increasing VTE risk
  2. Estrogen increases lamotrigine clearance, lowering its levels and risking breakthrough symptoms
  3. The combination is absolutely contraindicated (category 4)
  4. Lamotrigine reduces contraceptive efficacy
  5. No clinically relevant interaction exists
Answer & explanationB. Ethinyl estradiol induces glucuronidation of lamotrigine, increasing its clearance and lowering serum levels — risking loss of mood/seizure control (and levels rebound during the pill-free week). This makes combined methods US MEC category 3, not 4 (C is wrong). The interaction lowers lamotrigine, not contraceptive efficacy (D wrong); A reverses the direction.

3. A 26-year-old presents 4 days after unprotected intercourse requesting the most effective emergency contraception. BMI is 31. Which is most effective?

  1. Levonorgestrel 1.5 mg (Plan B)
  2. Ulipristal acetate (Ella)
  3. Copper intrauterine device
  4. Combined OCPs (Yuzpe method)
  5. Levonorgestrel IUD, FDA-labeled for EC
Answer & explanationC — copper IUD, the single most effective EC (>99%, up to 5 days, and unaffected by BMI). Among oral options ulipristal (B) beats levonorgestrel (A), especially at days 3–5 and higher BMI, but neither matches the copper IUD. Yuzpe (D) is least effective. E is wrong: the LNG IUD is non-inferior to copper for EC in trials but is not FDA-labeled for that indication.

4. A 22-year-old transgender man on testosterone has receptive vaginal intercourse with a male partner and does not desire pregnancy. He believes testosterone prevents pregnancy. What is the best counseling?

  1. Testosterone is adequate contraception; no method needed
  2. Testosterone is not reliable contraception; offer a method, and note estrogen-containing methods won't reverse his masculinization
  3. Only the copper IUD is safe on testosterone
  4. He must stop testosterone before starting any contraceptive
  5. Combined hormonal contraception is first-line to "balance" testosterone
Answer & explanationB. Per CDC US MEC/US SPR 2024, testosterone does not reliably suppress ovulation — a substantial fraction of users ovulate — so a person with a uterus who doesn't want pregnancy needs contraception. Progestin-only methods or the LNG IUD (which also reduces bleeding/dysphoria) are reasonable; testosterone need not be stopped (D wrong). C and E are fabricated rules.

5. A 24-year-old with no contraindications wants to start a combined OCP and asks you to pick a starting pill. Which is the most appropriate default?

  1. A 50 mcg ethinyl estradiol monophasic pill
  2. A ~20 mcg ethinyl estradiol pill with a 1st/2nd-generation progestin
  3. A multiphasic (triphasic) pill to minimize spotting
  4. A progestin-only pill, because it has fewer contraindications
  5. A 4th-generation drospirenone pill for everyone, given its anti-androgenic benefit
Answer & explanationB. A reasonable default combined pill is ~20 mcg EE with an earlier-generation progestin — effective, lowest practical estrogen dose, lowest relative VTE risk. 50 mcg EE (A) is unnecessarily high-dose. Multiphasic pills (C) confer no clinical advantage and cause more unscheduled bleeding. A POP (D) is fine but not the "default" combined choice she's asking for. Drospirenone (E) carries higher VTE risk and should be reserved for an anti-androgenic indication, not used universally.

2024 Practice Update — CDC US MEC & US SPR NEW — projected on the appended deck slide

The deck's framework (PATH, attribute-based selection, MEC categories) still holds. CDC updated both core documents in 2024 (MMWR, Aug 2024); the practice-relevant additions:

  • US MEC 2024: new recommendations for chronic kidney disease; revised guidance across DVT/PE ± anticoagulation, thrombophilia, SLE, cirrhosis/liver tumor, sickle cell disease, solid-organ transplant, obesity, surgery, and antiretroviral drug interactions; new formulations folded in (COCs, patch, ring, POPs, LNG-IUDs, vaginal pH modulator).
  • Transgender guidance (NEW): testosterone does not reliably prevent pregnancy — up to ~36% of users ovulate. Offer contraceptive counseling to testosterone users with a uterus who don't desire pregnancy. (Directly updates Case 3.)
  • US SPR 2024: lidocaine (paracervical block or topical) recommended to reduce pain at IUD placement; new support for self-administration of subcutaneous DMPA (DMPA-SC 104 mg).
  • OTC access: Opill (norgestrel 0.075 mg, progestin-only) — first FDA-approved nonprescription daily oral contraceptive (FDA July 2023; on shelves 2024, ~$20/month). No estrogen; same POP cautions (e.g., current breast cancer).
  • Newer non-hormonal method: vaginal pH modulator gel (Phexxi; lactic/citric acid + potassium bitartrate; FDA 2020) — typical-use efficacy ~86%, applied immediately before intercourse.

Take-Home Points

  1. Lead with the patient — open-ended PATH questions, not an efficacy-first script.
  2. Match the method to what matters: hormone-free vs hormonal, efficacy, maintenance, and bleeding effect.
  3. Take a focused history for estrogen red-flags (smoking at >35, migraine with aura, VTE/CVD/CVA, breast cancer); use the US MEC categories when unsure.
  4. A sensible COC default is ~20 mcg EE + a progestin; reserve 3rd/4th-gen progestins for anti-androgenic benefit given higher VTE risk.
  5. EC: copper IUD is most effective; ulipristal > levonorgestrel; combined OCPs (Yuzpe) are the available backup.
  6. Know the 2024 updates: testosterone is not contraception; lidocaine for IUD-placement pain; self-administered DMPA-SC; and OTC Opill expand access.
