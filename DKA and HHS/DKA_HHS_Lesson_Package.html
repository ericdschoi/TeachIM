Learning Objectives

  1. Diagnose and distinguish DKA and HHS using the 2024 consensus criteria, including euglycemic DKA.
  2. Sequence resuscitation correctly: balanced fluids, the potassium gate, then insulin.
  3. Treat to anion-gap/ketone resolution (not glucose alone) and add dextrose to keep insulin running.
  4. Transition safely off the insulin drip with overlapping subcutaneous basal insulin and identify the precipitant.

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient in DKA has a potassium of 3.0. Do you start the insulin drip?No — hold insulin until potassium is replaced above 3.3. Insulin shifts potassium intracellularly and, in an already-low patient, can cause life-threatening hypokalemia and arrhythmia. Give potassium first, then start insulin.
Q2. When do you add dextrose to the fluids in DKA, and why?When the glucose falls to roughly 200–250 mg/dL — you add dextrose so the insulin infusion can keep running to clear ketones and close the anion gap without causing hypoglycemia. You treat the gap, not just the glucose.
Q3. A patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor has a high anion-gap metabolic acidosis with ketones but a glucose of only 180. What is this?Euglycemic DKA — SGLT2 inhibitors (also pregnancy and starvation) can produce ketoacidosis with a near-normal glucose. Treat as DKA (fluids, insulin with dextrose, close the gap); don't be falsely reassured by the glucose.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"Two orders kill people in DKA, and they're both easy to get wrong under pressure: starting insulin before you've checked the potassium, and stopping the drip the moment the glucose looks normal. Let's make the sequence automatic." Set up diagnose → fluids/potassium → insulin/gap → transition.

Step 1 — Diagnose & distinguish 6 min

ASK: "How do DKA and HHS differ — and how can a patient be in DKA with a glucose of 180?"

DKA: ketoacidosis (the 2024 consensus lowered the bicarbonate cutoff to 15), often alert, glucose ≥200 but sometimes lower. HHS: glucose >600, effective osmolality >320, minimal ketosis, pH >7.3, often obtunded — and a higher mortality. Flag euglycemic DKA (SGLT2 inhibitors, pregnancy, starvation): a gap acidosis with ketones and a near-normal glucose.

Pearl: Check ketones (beta-hydroxybutyrate) and the anion gap, not just the glucose — euglycemic DKA hides behind a normal sugar.

Step 2 — Fluids & the potassium gate 8 min

ASK: "Potassium is 3.1 and the glucose is 600. What's your first move — insulin or potassium?"

Fluids first — and the 2024 consensus now favors balanced crystalloids over saline (faster resolution, less hyperchloremic acidosis, shorter stays). Then the potassium gate: if K <3.3, hold insulin and replace potassium first, because insulin will drive it intracellularly and can be fatal. K 3.3–5.2 means give insulin and add potassium to fluids; K >5.2 means insulin and recheck. Remember total-body potassium is depleted even when serum K looks fine.

Pearl: The potassium gate is the single most important safety check before the insulin drip.

Step 3 — Insulin & closing the gap 7 min

ASK: "Glucose is down to 210 and the gap is still open. Stop insulin?"

IV insulin infusion for moderate/severe DKA and HHS; subcutaneous rapid-acting protocols are reasonable for mild, uncomplicated DKA. Treat to gap/ketone resolution, not the glucose — when glucose hits ~200–250, add dextrose so the insulin keeps clearing ketones. Bicarbonate only if pH <7.0; phosphate only if <1.0 with weakness/cardiorespiratory compromise. Declare DKA resolved by glucose <200 plus gap closure (bicarb ≥15–18, pH >7.3).

Pearl: A normal glucose with an open gap is still DKA — keep the insulin running with dextrose.

Step 4 — Transition & trigger 2 min

Before stopping the drip, give subcutaneous long-acting insulin 1–2 hours before discontinuation — the drip has no reservoir, so an unbridged stop rebounds into ketoacidosis. Find the precipitant (the I's: infection, infarction, insulin omission, iatrogenic) and check an HbA1c.

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — the potassium gate

24F, type 1 DM, nausea/vomiting, glucose 540, pH 7.10, bicarb 8, K 3.1.

First move — insulin or potassium?Replace potassium first and hold insulin until K >3.3 — insulin would shift K intracellularly and risk fatal hypokalemia. Start balanced crystalloid concurrently.
When add dextrose?When glucose falls to ~200–250, to keep the insulin running until the gap closes.

Case 2 — euglycemic DKA

58M with type 2 DM on an SGLT2 inhibitor, post-op, nausea, glucose 175, anion gap 22, beta-hydroxybutyrate elevated, pH 7.24.

Diagnosis?Euglycemic DKA — the SGLT2 inhibitor produced ketoacidosis with a near-normal glucose. Treat as DKA.
Insulin without high glucose?Yes — run insulin WITH dextrose-containing fluids from the start to clear ketones while preventing hypoglycemia; hold the SGLT2 inhibitor.

Case 3 — premature drip-off

30M recovering from DKA: glucose now 160, but anion gap still 18; night team stops the insulin drip because 'the sugar is normal.'

Problem?The gap is still open — stopping insulin restarts ketoacidosis. Resume the drip with dextrose, resolve the gap, and only then transition with overlapping subcutaneous basal insulin given 1–2 h before stopping.

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. A patient with DKA has an initial potassium of 3.1 mmol/L. What is the most appropriate immediate action?

  1. Start the insulin infusion immediately
  2. Hold insulin and replace potassium until it exceeds 3.3
  3. Give IV bicarbonate
  4. Administer 50% dextrose
  5. Start an SGLT2 inhibitor
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Insulin shifts potassium intracellularly; with a K of 3.1, starting insulin risks fatal hypokalemia. Replace potassium (and give fluids) first, then start insulin once K >3.3. A: dangerous. C: bicarbonate is only for pH <7.0. D: no hypoglycemia. E: SGLT2 inhibitors can cause DKA and are held.

2. When should dextrose be added to fluids during treatment of DKA?

  1. When the anion gap normalizes
  2. When blood glucose falls to approximately 200–250 mg/dL
  3. Only if the patient becomes hypoglycemic
  4. At the start of therapy in all patients
  5. Never during active DKA
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Add dextrose when glucose reaches ~200–250 so the insulin infusion can continue clearing ketones and closing the gap without causing hypoglycemia. A: the gap typically closes after glucose falls. C: too late — you anticipate. D/E: incorrect timing.

3. A patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor presents with a high anion-gap metabolic acidosis, elevated beta-hydroxybutyrate, and a glucose of 185 mg/dL. The best description and management is:

  1. Lactic acidosis; give bicarbonate
  2. Euglycemic DKA; treat with fluids and insulin plus dextrose, and hold the SGLT2 inhibitor
  3. HHS; give large-volume saline only
  4. Normal anion gap; no treatment
  5. Starvation ketosis; discharge
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. SGLT2 inhibitors can cause euglycemic DKA — ketoacidosis with a near-normal glucose. Treat as DKA (fluids, insulin with dextrose to avoid hypoglycemia, close the gap) and stop the SGLT2 inhibitor. A: ketones, not lactate. C: glucose is far below HHS range. D/E: there is a clear gap acidosis with ketosis.

4. Which criterion best indicates that DKA has resolved?

  1. Blood glucose less than 200 alone
  2. Glucose <200 plus closure of the anion gap (and bicarbonate/pH recovery)
  3. A single normal potassium
  4. Resolution of thirst
  5. Glucose less than 250 within 2 hours
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Resolution requires glucose <200 AND metabolic recovery — anion gap normalization with bicarbonate ≥15–18 and venous pH >7.3. Glucose alone (A/E) is insufficient and stopping insulin then restarts ketoacidosis. C/D are not resolution criteria.

5. According to the 2024 consensus, which fluid is now preferred for initial resuscitation in hyperglycemic crises when available?

  1. 0.45% saline
  2. Balanced crystalloids (lactated Ringer's or Plasma-Lyte)
  3. Dextrose 5% in water
  4. Hydroxyethyl starch
  5. Albumin
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. The 2024 consensus suggests balanced crystalloids for initial resuscitation — faster resolution, less hyperchloremic acidosis, and shorter hospital stay than 0.9% saline. A: hypotonic fluid is not first-line for initial resuscitation. C: dextrose is added later. D/E: not resuscitation fluids here.

Take-Home Points

  1. Distinguish DKA from HHS and watch for euglycemic DKA (SGLT2 inhibitors) — check ketones and the gap, not just glucose.
  2. Resuscitate with balanced crystalloid and respect the potassium gate: hold insulin and replace K if it is below 3.3.
  3. Treat to anion-gap/ketone resolution and add dextrose at glucose ~200–250 to keep insulin running.
  4. Use bicarbonate only for pH <7.0 and phosphate only for severe hypophosphatemia with symptoms.
  5. Overlap subcutaneous basal insulin 1–2 hours before stopping the drip, and always identify the precipitant.
