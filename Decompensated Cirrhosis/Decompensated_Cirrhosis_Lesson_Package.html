Learning Objectives

  1. Differentiate compensated from decompensated cirrhosis.
  2. Recognize the significance of the MELD score (now MELD 3.0) for prognosis and transplant allocation.
  3. Evaluate a patient presenting with acute decompensation.
  4. Evaluate and manage the common complications: hepatic encephalopathy, ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatorenal syndrome, and spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.

Pre-work send the day before

Three retrieval questions to prime the session. Answers hidden — try first, then check.

1. What single procedure should essentially every patient admitted with cirrhosis and ascites receive?

A diagnostic paracentesis (SAAG, cell count with differential, culture) — to evaluate for SBP and characterize the ascites, even without classic symptoms.

2. What defines decompensation in a patient with known cirrhosis?

A rise in the MELD score or development of a new complication: ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, SBP, or hepatorenal syndrome.

3. A cirrhotic with ascitic fluid neutrophil count (ANC) of 400 — what is the diagnosis and treatment?

Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (ANC ≥250). Treat with cefotaxime or ceftriaxone PLUS IV albumin (1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3) to reduce HRS and mortality.

Teaching Script restructured into timed segments

Hook (0:00–0:02)

"Compensated cirrhosis has a median survival measured in years; the day a patient first develops ascites, a variceal bleed, or encephalopathy, that clock resets to a fraction of it. This talk is about recognizing that pivot — and reacting to each complication fast." Use the interactive board’s "cirrhosis one-liner" as the organizing spine.

ASK — "Give me the cirrhosis one-liner for any admitted patient: cause, compensated or not, MELD, and prior complications." Build it live.

Objective 1 & 2 — Compensated vs decompensated; MELD (0:02–0:10) 8 min

The "one-liner" frames every cirrhosis patient: (1) cause, (2) compensated vs decompensated, (3) baseline MELD, (4) prior complications.

Objective 3 — Evaluating acute decompensation (0:10–0:16) 6 min

ASK — "Three precipitants drive most cirrhosis complications. What are they, and what’s your reflex work-up?"

Overlapping precipitants: infection/SBP, GI bleeding, and portal vein thrombosis. Reflex work-up: diagnostic paracentesis, evaluate for bleeding, and RUQ ultrasound with Doppler. Recheck MELD components (Cr, bilirubin, INR, Na).

Pearl. "Essentially everyone admitted with cirrhosis and ascites gets a diagnostic paracentesis." Missed SBP is a preventable death.

Retrieval close (0:38–0:40) 2 min

Board off: "Reconstruct the one-liner, the three precipitants, the SBP rule (ANC ≥250 → cefotaxime + albumin), and first-line HRS-AKI therapy." Fill gaps from what they rebuild.

2021–2023 Guideline Update

The board’s framework is current; these refinements are surfaced here and on the appended deck slide:

  • MELD 3.0 replaced MELD-Na for US liver allocation (OPTN, 2023) — adds serum albumin and female sex and re-weights the other variables, modestly lowering waitlist mortality and improving access for women.
  • Terlipressin is FDA-approved (Sept 2022) and AASLD-recommended first-line for HRS-AKI (with albumin); octreotide/midodrine + albumin is the alternative where terlipressin isn’t available. Type 1 HRS is now HRS-AKI (ICA 2015 / AASLD 2021).
  • Baveno VII (2022): in compensated cirrhosis with clinically significant portal hypertension (CSPH), carvedilol (NSBB of choice) reduces first decompensation — chiefly ascites — and mortality (PREDESCI).
  • Nomenclature (2023): NASH → MASH, NAFLD → MASLD; "MetALD" for metabolic dysfunction + moderate alcohol. The board’s "NASH (10%)" cause reads as MASH.
  • Prognosis context: the board cites compensated median survival >12 y and a low figure for decompensated disease (tied to its Salpeter reference); current data place median survival after decompensation closer to ~2 years, with the most advanced sub-phenotypes far shorter.

Cases applied scenarios · click-to-reveal

The original interactive board teaches by complication rather than fixed vignettes; these consolidate the same content into board-style cases.

Case 1 — New ascites

54-year-old with alcohol-related cirrhosis presents with abdominal distension and a 6-lb weight gain. Afebrile, BP 108/64. Moderate ascites on exam.

What is the first step, and what fluid studies do you send?

Diagnostic paracentesis — SAAG (≥1.1 g/dL confirms portal hypertension), cell count with differential, and culture. Manage uncomplicated ascites with sodium restriction and diuretics (spironolactone ± furosemide); large-volume paracentesis with albumin for tense ascites.

Case 2 — Fever and abdominal pain

The same patient returns febrile with diffuse abdominal pain. Ascitic fluid ANC is 480.

Diagnosis and management?

Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (ANC ≥250). Treat with cefotaxime or ceftriaxone PLUS IV albumin (1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3) — albumin reduces HRS and mortality, especially with renal dysfunction or bilirubin >4.

Case 3 — Rising creatinine

A cirrhotic with ascites develops creatinine rising from 0.9 to 2.3 despite holding diuretics; urine is bland, no proteinuria, FeNa low, and no improvement after 2 days of albumin.

Most likely diagnosis and treatment?

Hepatorenal syndrome (HRS-AKI) — a diagnosis of exclusion after volume expansion with albumin and stopping nephrotoxins/diuretics. Treat with terlipressin + albumin (first-line) or octreotide/midodrine + albumin; evaluate for transplant.

Case 4 — Hematemesis

A cirrhotic presents with hematemesis and melena, HR 112, BP 96/58.

Initial management bundle?

Resuscitate with a restrictive transfusion strategy (Hgb ~7); start octreotide and pantoprazole; give ceftriaxone prophylaxis; arrange urgent EGD for band ligation. Antibiotics independently reduce rebleeding and mortality.

Board-style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 60-year-old with cirrhosis and ascites is admitted with worsening encephalopathy but no fever or abdominal pain. Which step is most important?

  1. Empiric lactulose alone and observe
  2. Diagnostic paracentesis
  3. CT abdomen/pelvis
  4. Check an ammonia level to confirm HE
  5. Start rifaximin and discharge
Answer & explanation

B. Essentially every admitted cirrhotic with ascites needs a diagnostic paracentesis — SBP is a common, often silent precipitant of HE. A/E wrong: treat HE but miss the precipitant. C wrong: not the first step. D wrong: ammonia neither confirms nor excludes HE and doesn’t change management.

2. A cirrhotic with ascitic ANC of 600 is diagnosed with SBP. Beyond cefotaxime, which intervention most reduces hepatorenal syndrome and mortality?

  1. IV albumin
  2. Fresh frozen plasma
  3. Octreotide
  4. Prophylactic lactulose
  5. Furosemide
Answer & explanation

A — IV albumin (1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3) reduces HRS and mortality in SBP. B wrong: FFP isn’t indicated without bleeding. C wrong: octreotide is for variceal bleeding/HRS regimens, not SBP. D/E wrong: neither addresses the SBP-related renal risk; diuretics may worsen it.

3. Which best describes the current US liver-transplant allocation score?

  1. MELD-Na, using Cr, bilirubin, INR, and sodium
  2. MELD 3.0, which adds albumin and sex
  3. Child-Pugh class
  4. The original MELD without sodium
  5. FIB-4
Answer & explanation

B. MELD 3.0 (2023) replaced MELD-Na and adds serum albumin and female sex, improving equity. A/D wrong: superseded. C wrong: Child-Pugh is prognostic but not the allocation score. E wrong: FIB-4 is a fibrosis estimate, not an allocation tool.

4. A cirrhotic with ascites develops AKI (Cr 0.9 → 2.4). Urinalysis is bland, no proteinuria; creatinine does not improve after stopping diuretics and 2 days of albumin. What is the most appropriate treatment?

  1. Aggressive normal saline boluses
  2. Terlipressin plus albumin
  3. Loop diuretics to increase urine output
  4. Empiric vancomycin and cefepime
  5. Immediate hemodialysis
Answer & explanation

B. This is HRS-AKI after excluding other causes and a failed albumin challenge — treat with terlipressin (first-line) + albumin, or octreotide/midodrine + albumin. A wrong: more crystalloid won’t help and may worsen ascites. C wrong: diuretics worsen HRS. D wrong: no infection is described. E wrong: dialysis is a bridge for refractory cases/transplant candidates, not first-line medical therapy.

5. A patient with acute variceal hemorrhage is being resuscitated. Which transfusion target and adjunct are most appropriate?

  1. Transfuse to Hgb ≥10; no antibiotics needed
  2. Restrictive strategy (Hgb ~7) plus ceftriaxone prophylaxis
  3. Transfuse to Hgb ≥9; start vancomycin
  4. Avoid all transfusion until EGD
  5. Restrictive strategy plus prophylactic FFP to normalize INR
Answer & explanation

B. A restrictive transfusion threshold (~7 g/dL) improves survival in acute UGIB (Villanueva, NEJM 2013), and antibiotic prophylaxis (ceftriaxone) reduces rebleeding, infection, and death in cirrhotic variceal bleeding. A/C wrong: liberal transfusion worsens portal pressure/outcomes. D wrong: hemodynamically significant bleeding still needs resuscitation. E wrong: routine FFP to "correct" INR is not beneficial and may volume-overload.

Take-Home Points

  1. MELD 3.0 predicts 3-month mortality and drives US transplant allocation (replaced MELD-Na in 2023).
  2. Decompensation = a rise in MELD or a new complication; precipitants overlap (infection/SBP, GI bleeding, portal vein thrombosis).
  3. Initial work-up: diagnostic paracentesis, evaluate for bleeding, RUQ US with Doppler.
  4. Essentially everyone admitted with cirrhosis and ascites gets a diagnostic paracentesis. SBP (ANC ≥250) = cefotaxime/ceftriaxone + albumin.
  5. HRS-AKI: terlipressin (first-line) or octreotide/midodrine + albumin. Variceal bleed: restrictive transfusion, octreotide, PPI, ceftriaxone, urgent EGD.
