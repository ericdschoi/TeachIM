Definition of delirium?	Acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and awareness with a medical cause.
CAM diagnostic rule?	Features 1 (acute/fluctuating) AND 2 (inattention), plus 3 (disorganized thinking) OR 4 (altered consciousness).
Most common and most missed subtype?	Hypoactive delirium (quiet, withdrawn) — worse outcomes than hyperactive.
Delirium vs dementia?	Delirium is acute, fluctuating, with inattention; dementia is chronic with relatively preserved attention (early).
Two highest-yield reversible causes on the wards?	Medications (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids) and infection.
DELIRIUM(S) mnemonic?	Drugs, Electrolytes/Endocrine, Lack of drugs/pain, Infection, Reduced sensory input, Intracranial, Urinary/fecal retention, Metabolic, Sleep/Subdural.
When is neuroimaging/LP/EEG indicated in delirium?	Only with focal neurologic signs, trauma, or no cause found — not routinely.
Evidence-based core treatment?	Multicomponent non-pharmacologic care (HELP): reorient, mobilize, sleep, sensory aids, de-prescribe.
Do antipsychotics prevent or shorten delirium (2025 APA)?	No — reserve low-dose for severe agitation threatening safety after non-drug measures fail.
When are benzodiazepines appropriate in delirium?	Only for alcohol/sedative withdrawal — otherwise they cause delirium.
Antipsychotic to avoid in which conditions?	Parkinson's disease and Lewy body dementia.
How to test attention at the bedside?	Months of the year backward or days of the week backward.
Two 'tethers' that worsen delirium?	Physical restraints and indwelling urinary catheters (plus lines).
Highest-impact strategy overall?	Prevention in at-risk patients using the same multicomponent levers.
Risk factors for delirium?	Older age, baseline cognitive impairment, sensory deficits, severe illness, polypharmacy.
