Learning Objectives

  1. Diagnose delirium with the CAM, recognizing hypoactive delirium and distinguishing it from dementia and depression.
  2. Approach delirium as a symptom and search systematically for reversible causes (medications and infection first).
  3. Lead with multicomponent non-pharmacologic care and de-prescribing, knowing antipsychotics neither prevent nor shorten delirium.
  4. Use antipsychotics and benzodiazepines only in their narrow, appropriate roles, and prevent delirium in at-risk patients.

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A quiet, withdrawn elderly patient who is hard to engage and inattentive — is this depression, dementia, or delirium?Most likely hypoactive delirium — acute, fluctuating inattention. It is the most common and most frequently missed subtype and carries worse outcomes than the agitated form. Screen with the CAM and look for a cause rather than assuming depression.
Q2. Do scheduled antipsychotics prevent delirium or shorten its duration?No — the 2025 APA guideline recommends against using antipsychotics to prevent delirium or hasten its resolution; the evidence base is multicomponent non-pharmacologic care. Antipsychotics are reserved for severe agitation threatening safety after non-drug measures fail.
Q3. Which two categories of reversible cause are most common on the wards?Medications (especially anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, and opioids) and infection. Review the medication list and screen for infection before reaching for neuroimaging.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"The night nurse calls about a 'pleasantly confused' 82-year-old — not agitated, just quiet and not quite himself. The reflex is to chart it and move on, or to order a head CT. Both miss the point: this is probably hypoactive delirium, it's a symptom of something fixable, and the head CT is usually the wrong first test." Set up diagnose → find the cause → treat non-pharmacologically.

Step 1 — Diagnose with the CAM 6 min

ASK: "What are the four CAM features, and which two are mandatory?"

Delirium is acute, fluctuating inattention with a medical cause. The CAM: (1) acute onset/fluctuating course AND (2) inattention, plus either (3) disorganized thinking OR (4) altered level of consciousness. Emphasize that hypoactive delirium — quiet, withdrawn, the patient who's easy to overlook — is the most common and most missed, with worse outcomes than the agitated form. Distinguish from dementia (chronic, attention relatively preserved) and depression.

Pearl: Test attention directly — months of the year backward, or days of the week backward. The 'pleasant' quiet patient who can't do it is delirious.

Step 2 — Find the cause 8 min

ASK: "Delirium is a symptom. Before you image the head, where do you look first?"

Work the DELIRIUM(S) mnemonic: Drugs, Electrolytes/Endocrine, Lack of drugs (withdrawal)/pain, Infection, Reduced sensory input, Intracranial, Urinary/fecal retention, Metabolic (hypoxia, hypercarbia, glucose, organ failure), Sleep/Subdural. The two highest-yield culprits are medications (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids) and infection. Targeted work-up: med review, BMP/Ca/Mg, CBC, UA, infection screen, glucose, oxygenation. Reserve neuroimaging, LP, and EEG for focal signs, trauma, or an unexplained case — not as a reflex.

Pearl: The head CT is low-yield in delirium without focal deficits or trauma — the medication list is higher-yield and free.

Step 3 — Treat: non-pharm first 7 min

ASK: "What's the evidence-based treatment — and what's the evidence on scheduled antipsychotics?"

The core is multicomponent non-pharmacologic care (HELP): reorient (clocks, calendars, family, glasses, hearing aids), mobilize and remove tethers, protect sleep, and de-prescribe deliriogenic drugs while treating pain, constipation, and retention. The 2025 APA guideline is clear that antipsychotics neither prevent delirium nor shorten it. Reserve a low dose of an antipsychotic for severe agitation that threatens safety or essential care after non-drug measures fail — a short bridge, not a treatment — and avoid it in Parkinson's/Lewy body disease. Benzodiazepines are only for alcohol/sedative withdrawal; otherwise they cause delirium.

Pearl: If you reach for haloperidol, you're managing the behavior, not the delirium — the cause and the non-drug bundle do the actual work.

Step 4 — Prevent 2 min

The biggest win is prevention in at-risk patients (older, cognitively impaired, sensory deficits, severely ill): the same HELP levers reduce incident delirium. Avoid restraints and unnecessary catheters — they worsen and prolong it.

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — the quiet one

82M, post-op day 2, 'pleasantly confused,' withdrawn, won't engage, can't recite the months backward. Vitals stable.

Diagnosis?Hypoactive delirium — acute, fluctuating inattention. The most common and most missed subtype; don't dismiss it as fatigue or depression.
First steps?Treat as a symptom: review meds (opioids? anticholinergics?), screen for infection/retention/electrolytes/hypoxia, optimize the non-pharmacologic bundle. Head CT only if focal signs or trauma.

Case 2 — the reflex sedative

78F agitated overnight, pulling at lines; intern orders scheduled lorazepam.

Problem?Benzodiazepines worsen delirium (except in alcohol/sedative withdrawal). Use non-pharmacologic de-escalation first; if severe agitation threatens safety after that fails, a low-dose antipsychotic is the bridge — not a scheduled benzodiazepine.

Case 3 — prevention

85M with baseline mild cognitive impairment and hearing loss admitted with pneumonia; no delirium yet.

What reduces his risk?Multicomponent prevention: hearing aids/glasses in, reorientation, early mobilization, sleep protection, avoid tethers and deliriogenic drugs — the HELP levers cut incident delirium in exactly this patient.

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. An 80-year-old is quiet, withdrawn, and inattentive two days after admission, with a fluctuating course. Which is the most likely diagnosis and its significance?

  1. Depression — generally benign
  2. Hypoactive delirium — common and often missed, with worse outcomes
  3. Dementia — chronic and expected
  4. Normal post-operative fatigue
  5. Primary psychiatric illness
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Acute, fluctuating inattention is delirium; the hypoactive subtype is the most common and most frequently overlooked and is associated with worse outcomes. A/E: mislabeling delays the cause hunt. C: dementia is chronic without acute inattention. D: dismisses a medical emergency.

2. Which combination of CAM features is required to diagnose delirium?

  1. Any two of the four features
  2. Acute onset/fluctuating course AND inattention, plus either disorganized thinking OR altered consciousness
  3. Inattention alone
  4. Altered consciousness alone
  5. Disorganized thinking AND altered consciousness only
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. The CAM requires features 1 (acute/fluctuating) and 2 (inattention), plus either 3 (disorganized thinking) or 4 (altered level of consciousness). The other options misstate the algorithm.

3. What does the 2025 APA guideline say about antipsychotics for delirium?

  1. They should be given prophylactically to all at-risk patients
  2. They do not prevent delirium or shorten its duration and should be reserved for severe agitation after non-drug measures fail
  3. They are first-line treatment for all delirium
  4. They are contraindicated in every circumstance
  5. They shorten hypoactive delirium specifically
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Antipsychotics neither prevent delirium nor hasten resolution; they are reserved for severe agitation threatening safety/essential care when non-pharmacologic measures have failed. A/C/E overstate benefit; D overstates the restriction (they have a narrow role).

4. A delirious patient has no focal neurologic signs, no trauma, and a medication list including oxybutynin, lorazepam, and oxycodone. What is the highest-yield next step?

  1. Immediate non-contrast head CT
  2. Review and de-prescribe deliriogenic medications and screen for infection
  3. Lumbar puncture
  4. EEG
  5. Scheduled haloperidol
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Medications (here anticholinergic, benzodiazepine, opioid) and infection are the most common reversible causes; a med review plus infection screen is higher-yield than neuroimaging without focal signs or trauma. A/C/D are low-yield without focal findings. E treats behavior, not cause.

5. Which intervention is the evidence-based core of preventing and managing delirium in hospitalized older adults?

  1. Scheduled benzodiazepines
  2. Multicomponent non-pharmacologic care (reorientation, mobilization, sleep, sensory aids, de-prescribing)
  3. Physical restraints to ensure safety
  4. Routine indwelling urinary catheters
  5. Prophylactic antipsychotics
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Multicomponent non-pharmacologic programs (HELP) are the proven core for prevention and management. A: benzodiazepines cause delirium. C/D: restraints and catheters worsen and prolong it. E: antipsychotics don't prevent delirium.

Take-Home Points

  1. Delirium is acute, fluctuating inattention — diagnose with the CAM and actively look for the hypoactive subtype, which is the most missed.
  2. It is a symptom, not a diagnosis: hunt the cause, with medications and infection the highest-yield culprits.
  3. Reserve neuroimaging, LP, and EEG for focal signs, trauma, or an unexplained case — not as a reflex.
  4. Lead with multicomponent non-pharmacologic care and de-prescribing; antipsychotics neither prevent nor shorten delirium.
  5. Use antipsychotics only as a short bridge for dangerous agitation, benzodiazepines only for withdrawal, and prevent delirium in at-risk patients.
