Learning Objectives

  1. Choose the appropriate screening and diagnostic tools for anxiety and depression (PHQ-2/PHQ-4 vs PHQ-9/GAD-7).
  2. Differentiate generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) and unipolar major depression from behavioral-health mimics that are managed differently.
  3. Construct a first-line treatment strategy, including the monitoring and follow-up timeline after starting pharmacologic and/or non-pharmacologic therapy.
  4. Select the right pharmacologic agent based on key clinical features, comorbidities, and patient preference (side-effect–guided selection).

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Which screening tools diagnose and monitor depression and anxiety, and which are only quick screens? PHQ-9 (depression) and GAD-7 (anxiety) diagnose and track response. PHQ-2 and PHQ-4 are brief screens for general populations — don't rely on them when clinical suspicion is high.
Q2. Name three features that should make you pause before starting an antidepressant because the patient may actually have bipolar disorder. Age <25, a family history of bipolar disorder, and multiple (≥3–5) recurrences of major depression. Antidepressant monotherapy can unmask mania/hypomania.
Q3. A patient on many medications needs an SSRI. Which one minimizes drug–drug interactions, and what is its main caution? Citalopram — relatively little CYP P450 inhibition. Main caution: dose-dependent QTc prolongation (max 40 mg/day; 20 mg in older adults).

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Project the interactive board (Depression_and_Anxiety_Optimized.pptx) and click through each reveal — every cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt, so pose the question before clicking. Talk without cases: ~10–15 min; with cases: +10–15 min (or split into two sessions). Print Depression-learner-handout.docx so learners have the cases for pair-shares.

Hook (2 min)

"Half of clinical depression walks out of our clinics undiagnosed — not because it's subtle, but because it hides behind fatigue, pain, and poor sleep, and we don't ask." Ask the room: how does depression actually show up in your panel? Park the somatic answers — you'll use them again at Objective 2 and in every case.

Objective 1 — Screening & diagnostic tools 3 min

ASK: "What do you actually order — PHQ-2, PHQ-9, GAD-7, PHQ-4 — and when?" Collect answers, then click to reveal the tool map.

Why screen: screening roughly doubles detection of depression (Mitchell, Lancet 2009), and treatment improves quality of life and reduces mortality and economic burden. Depression and anxiety co-occur in ~39–62% of cases. The USPSTF recommends screening all adults for depression (and, as of 2023, adults <65 for anxiety).

ToolUse
PHQ-2First 2 PHQ-9 items — quick screen for general populations
PHQ-4PHQ-2 + first 2 GAD-7 items — combined quick screen
PHQ-9Diagnoses severity AND monitors response for depression
GAD-7Diagnoses AND monitors response for anxiety
Special situationsEdinburgh (perinatal — drops somatic items); GDS-5 (geriatric)
Pearl — PHQ-9 score bands: 5–9 mild, 10–14 moderate, 15–19 moderately severe, ≥20 severe (≥10 = likely major depression). It measures and trends severity but doesn't, by itself, diagnose — diagnosis needs DSM-5 criteria plus functional impairment.

Objective 2 — Diagnose & differentiate 4 min

ASK: "Which DSM-5 symptoms are required to call it major depression?" Take answers before revealing the criteria.

Unipolar major depression: ≥5 of 9 symptoms for ≥2 weeks nearly every day, and must include depressed mood or loss of interest/pleasure (SIG-E-CAPS: Sleep, Interest, Guilt, Energy, Concentration, Appetite, Psychomotor, Suicidality). GAD: excessive, hard-to-control worry more days than not for ≥6 months plus ≥3 of (restlessness, fatigue, poor concentration, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance). Both require functional impairment and exclusion of substance/medical causes.

ASK: "Before you write for an SSRI — what would make you worry this is bipolar disorder?"
Pearl — Screen for bipolarity (age <25, family history of bipolar disorder, ≥3–5 recurrences of MDD) — antidepressant monotherapy can precipitate mania. Keep mimics on the differential: adjustment disorder, ADHD, borderline PD, complicated grief, delirium, hypothyroidism, schizophrenia/schizoaffective, substance use; for anxiety also panic, OCD, separation anxiety, PTSD.

No mandated lab panel, but for new-onset symptoms consider CBC (anemia), TSH (thyroid), HCG (pregnancy — also guides safe drug choice), and BMP (renal dosing).

Objective 3 — Treatment strategy & follow-up 4 min

ASK: "After you start a drug, when does the patient come back — and why does the timing matter?"

GAD and MDD share a first-line algorithm: medication and/or psychotherapy have comparable response rates and are additive. Set a target (patient-defined goals; remission = absence of sad mood and reduced interest for ~3 weeks), then plan close follow-up:

CheckpointPurposeAction
1–2 weeksConfirm no worsening suicidality/mania; no intolerable side effectsIf intolerable → choose another agent
2–4 weeksAssess response and resolution of early side effectsNo response → switch; partial → increase dose, recheck in 2 wk; complete → maintain (consider discontinuation at 3–9 mo)
Pearl — The first couple of weeks are the danger zone for emergent suicidality/mania and for early drop-out from transient side effects. A brief touchpoint (prescriber, RN, or therapist) prevents both. When stopping, taper slowly — withdrawal can be prolonged, and per ANTLER (NEJM 2021) relapse is common whether patients continue or stop.

Objective 4 — Pick the agent by side-effect profile 3 min

ASK: "Give me a patient feature, and I'll make you pick the drug." Run a few before revealing the medication grid.

SRIs (especially SSRIs) are first-line with the best tolerability. Choose by cost, prior response, preference, and side effects — and use side effects to your advantage:

Want…Reach for
Weight gain + sedation (poor appetite, insomnia, esp. elderly)Mirtazapine
Few drug–drug interactions (polypharmacy)Citalopram/escitalopram (watch QTc)
More activationSertraline, fluoxetine, bupropion
Comorbid chronic painSNRI (duloxetine, venlafaxine)
Avoid weight gain / shortest half-life issuesAvoid paroxetine; fluoxetine has the longest half-life (less withdrawal)
Pearl — "Start low and go slow," especially in older adults. A dramatic, "better-than-ever," high-energy response after a switch can be a good response — or unmasked bipolar disorder. Re-assess the diagnosis.

Safety assessment (every visit)

Suicide risk assessment is a critical step at each visit. Prediction is poor, but use clear, direct language ("Are you having thoughts of killing yourself right now?" — not "hurting yourself"). Asking does not increase risk. Know your site's safety-plan handout and immediately available mental-health resources; tools include the PHQ-9 item 9 and the Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale.

Retrieval close (2 min)

Cover the board. Have learners rebuild from memory: (1) which tools diagnose vs screen, (2) the two required MDD symptoms, (3) the 1–2-week and 2–4-week follow-up logic, (4) one "feature → drug" pairing. Fill gaps from their answers, not your slides.

Case 1 — 25-year-old man, new-onset low mood

25M establishing care, no PMH; over weeks since moving cities, increasing loneliness, sadness, and job stress.

Which screening test is most appropriate?PHQ-9 (high clinical suspicion warrants the full tool, not PHQ-2/PHQ-4); also consider GAD-7. Note: screening tools alone don't diagnose.
He meets criteria for moderate depression — what's the next step?A suicide risk assessment (clear, direct language). Asking about suicide does not increase risk.
He denies active SI with no prior attempts — what medication?An SRI (SSRI or SNRI) first-line; given his increased appetite, avoid paroxetine. Set expectations and arrange 1–2-week follow-up.

Case 2 — 79-year-old woman, fatigue & poor concentration

79F (HTN, RA, CAD) with 6 months of fatigue and poor concentration since an MI; cardiac workup normal.

What screening labs and questionnaires fit?Broaden the differential for vague geriatric symptoms: EKG, CBC, BMP, TSH, plus PHQ-9 or GDS-5 and a MOCA. Depression is especially common after MI and stroke.
Labs/ECG/MOCA unremarkable, GDS-5 positive — what regimen?SRIs remain first-line in the elderly; citalopram is a good choice (few interactions — watch QTc). Mirtazapine is reasonable if weight gain/insomnia are issues. Start low, go slow, and expect a delayed effect.
On citalopram 10 mg at 2 weeks: mild diarrhea (improving), GDS-5 unchanged — next?Increase citalopram to 20 mg (transient GI effects are expected; response is often delayed, especially in older adults). Recheck in ~2 weeks.

Case 3 — 28-year-old woman, somatic complaints & anxiety

28F with years of arthralgias and low back pain, longstanding "restless/on edge" feelings, intermittent untreated depression; drinks 2 glasses of wine nightly for sleep.

What should the first question prompt you to consider?Substance use (the AUDIT/CAGE) as a contributor to anxiety and depression. She has mixed GAD + unipolar depression — a common presentation.
Diagnosis: unipolar depression + GAD with chronic pain — what treatment?SRI is first-line for both; her chronic pain makes an SNRI (e.g., duloxetine) a good first choice. Add CBT and physical therapy — don't discredit the somatic symptoms.
At 8 weeks on max-dose duloxetine, GAD-7 improved but still moderate — next step?Switch to a different SRI, or augment (e.g., add buspirone or pregabalin for anxiety).
She switches to sertraline; 2 weeks later feels "better than ever," energy at an "all-time high," pain "entirely gone." Concern?Reassess the diagnosis — this could be a good response or unmasked hypomania/bipolar disorder.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 34-year-old woman has a PHQ-2 of 4 at a routine visit and reports 3 weeks of low mood and anhedonia. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Start an SSRI based on the PHQ-2
  2. Administer the PHQ-9 to characterize severity and confirm criteria
  3. Reassure and rescreen in 1 year
  4. Order a TSH and defer any mental-health assessment
  5. Refer to psychiatry before any further evaluation
Answer & explanationB. A positive PHQ-2 is a screen; it doesn't diagnose or grade severity. The next step is the PHQ-9 (plus DSM-5 criteria and functional impairment). A is premature (no severity/criteria yet); C ignores a positive screen with symptoms; D inappropriately defers; E is unnecessary for an uncomplicated presentation manageable in primary care.

2. A 22-year-old man with a first episode of major depression and a father with bipolar I disorder is about to start treatment. What most changes your plan?

  1. Nothing — start an SSRI as usual
  2. His age and family history raise bipolar risk; screen carefully before antidepressant monotherapy
  3. Start a benzodiazepine instead
  4. Antidepressants are contraindicated
  5. Begin lithium empirically
Answer & explanationB. Age <25, a family history of bipolar disorder, and recurrent MDD are red flags for bipolarity; antidepressant monotherapy can unmask mania. Screen for prior (hypo)manic episodes first. A ignores the risk; C is not a treatment for the underlying disorder and carries dependence risk; D overstates (antidepressants aren't absolutely contraindicated, but use caution); E commits to a mood stabilizer without establishing bipolar diagnosis.

3. A 70-year-old woman on warfarin, amiodarone, and metoprolol develops major depression. Which SSRI best minimizes drug–drug interactions, and what must you monitor?

  1. Fluoxetine; monitor weight
  2. Paroxetine; monitor blood pressure
  3. Citalopram; monitor the QTc
  4. Fluvoxamine; monitor liver enzymes
  5. Sertraline; monitor sodium only
Answer & explanationC. Citalopram (and escitalopram) have relatively little CYP P450 inhibition, useful in polypharmacy; the key caution is dose-dependent QTc prolongation (cap 40 mg/day; 20 mg in older adults) — especially relevant with amiodarone. Fluoxetine and fluvoxamine are strong CYP inhibitors (raising interaction risk with warfarin); paroxetine is a potent CYP2D6 inhibitor with anticholinergic effects. Hyponatremia is a class effect of all SSRIs, not sertraline-specific.

4. A patient started sertraline 10 days ago for depression and now reports markedly elevated energy, decreased need for sleep, rapid speech, and "feeling better than ever." What is the best next step?

  1. Congratulate the rapid response and continue
  2. Increase the sertraline dose
  3. Reassess for a (hypo)manic episode / bipolar disorder
  4. Add a benzodiazepine for the insomnia
  5. Switch to a different SSRI
Answer & explanationC. A rapid surge in energy with reduced sleep need shortly after an antidepressant should prompt evaluation for antidepressant-unmasked (hypo)mania. A and B could worsen mania; D treats a symptom while missing the diagnosis; E continues antidepressant monotherapy without reassessing. Re-diagnose before adjusting therapy.

5. A 29-year-old woman with GAD and comorbid chronic low back pain wants pharmacotherapy. Which first-line choice best addresses both?

  1. Paroxetine
  2. Bupropion
  3. Duloxetine
  4. Mirtazapine
  5. A benzodiazepine scheduled daily
Answer & explanationC — duloxetine, an SNRI that is first-line for GAD and also has evidence for chronic musculoskeletal/neuropathic pain. Paroxetine works for anxiety but adds weight gain/anticholinergic burden and offers no analgesic benefit. Bupropion is activating and can worsen anxiety and is not first-line for GAD. Mirtazapine helps sleep/appetite but isn't an analgesic. Scheduled daily benzodiazepines risk dependence and aren't first-line maintenance therapy.

2022–2025 Practice Update NEW — projected on the appended deck slide

The deck's framework (PHQ-9/GAD-7, SRI first-line, close early follow-up, side-effect–guided selection) was verified current. Practice-relevant additions since the lesson's 2021–22 publication:

  • USPSTF 2023 (final): screen all adults for depression (Grade B) and adults <65 for anxiety disorders (Grade B), including pregnant and postpartum adults. Insufficient evidence to screen for anxiety at ≥65 (I statement). No recommendation for routine suicide-risk screening in asymptomatic adults (I).
  • Discontinuation evidence: the ANTLER trial (Lewis, NEJM 2021) — stopping long-term antidepressants led to ~56% relapse vs ~39% with maintenance; taper slowly, and counsel that relapse is common either way.
  • Newer oral agents (context — not first-line for routine GAD/MDD): dextromethorphan–bupropion (Auvelity, FDA Aug 2022) — first rapid-acting oral NMDA-antagonist for MDD, separating from placebo by week 1; zuranolone (Zurzuvae, FDA Aug 2023) — first oral agent for postpartum depression (14-day course; not approved for general MDD); intranasal esketamine (Spravato) for treatment-resistant MDD, expanded to monotherapy in 2025.

These do not change the chalk talk's first-line algorithm; they update the screening recommendation and add newer second-/third-line and postpartum-specific options for the appended slide.

Take-Home Points

  1. PHQ-9 and GAD-7 diagnose and monitor unipolar depression and GAD; PHQ-2/PHQ-4 are screens only — don't rely on them when suspicion is high.
  2. Short-term follow-up (within ~1–2 weeks) of starting a drug is critical to catch emergent suicidality/mania, intolerable side effects, and early drop-out.
  3. SRIs are first-line for both disorders; choose by comorbidity, prior response, cost, preference, and side-effect profile.
  4. Treat somatic symptoms too (PT, headache care) and keep re-screening for bipolar disorder and substance use.
  5. USPSTF 2023: screen all adults for depression and adults <65 for anxiety. Taper antidepressants slowly when stopping (ANTLER).
