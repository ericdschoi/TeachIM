Learning Objectives

  1. Resuscitate a GI bleed and use clinical clues to localize upper versus lower sources.
  2. Apply the restrictive transfusion threshold (Hb 7) and its exceptions, and avoid over-transfusion in variceal bleeding.
  3. Risk-stratify with the Glasgow-Blatchford score and choose pre-endoscopy medications and endoscopy timing.
  4. Recognize and manage the distinct variceal-bleed pathway (octreotide, antibiotics, band ligation).

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A hemodynamically stable patient with melena has a hemoglobin of 8.5. Do you transfuse?No — the restrictive threshold for most GI bleeding is a hemoglobin of 7 g/dL (target 7–9), which improves survival versus a liberal strategy (Villanueva). A higher threshold (~8) applies only with acute coronary syndrome or significant cardiovascular disease.
Q2. What does a Glasgow-Blatchford score of 0–1 allow you to consider?Very-low-risk patients (GBS 0–1) can be considered for discharge with outpatient endoscopy rather than admission, because the risk of needing intervention or dying is very low.
Q3. A patient with cirrhosis presents with hematemesis. Beyond a PPI, which two medications reduce mortality/complications and should start early?Octreotide (lowers portal pressure) and prophylactic antibiotics such as ceftriaxone — antibiotics in particular reduce mortality in variceal bleeding. Definitive therapy is endoscopic band ligation.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"Two GI bleeders hit the floor in the same hour. One is a stable woman with melena and a hemoglobin of 8.2 — and the well-meaning intern orders two units. The other is a cirrhotic vomiting blood, and the team reaches for a PPI and nothing else. Both reflexes are wrong, and both are common." Set up resuscitate → localize → the right transfusion → the right pathway.

Step 1 — Resuscitate & localize 6 min

ASK: "The first hemoglobin is 13. Does that reassure you? And how do you tell upper from lower at the bedside?"

Stabilize first: two large-bore IVs, type & cross, fluids, judge severity by hemodynamics (the initial Hb lags — equilibration takes hours). Localize: hematemesis/coffee-grounds and melena point upper; a BUN:Cr > 30 without renal disease suggests an upper source (digested blood). Hematochezia is usually lower — unless the patient is unstable, in which case treat it as a brisk upper bleed until proven otherwise.

Pearl: Unstable hematochezia is an upper bleed until an upper scope says otherwise.

Step 2 — Transfuse restrictively 7 min

ASK: "Stable patient, hemoglobin 8.2 — transfuse or not? What's your threshold and your exception?"

The restrictive threshold is Hb 7 (target 7–9) — Villanueva showed better survival and less rebleeding than a liberal strategy. The main exception is a higher (~8) threshold with ACS or significant cardiovascular disease. In variceal bleeding, restrictive transfusion is especially important and over-transfusing plasma/platelets is harmful — it raises portal pressure and worsens bleeding.

Pearl: 'Topping up' to a normal hemoglobin causes harm in GI bleeding — resist it.

Step 3 — Risk-stratify & prep for endoscopy 7 min

ASK: "Which patients are safe to send home, and what do you give before the scope?"

Use the Glasgow-Blatchford score: GBS 0–1 is very low risk and can be considered for outpatient management. Pre-endoscopy, give IV PPI for suspected ulcer bleeding and IV erythromycin ~20–90 minutes before the scope to clear the stomach for a better view. Endoscopy timing is within 24 hours after resuscitation for most UGIB — pushing to <12 hours does not help and can harm an under-resuscitated patient.

Pearl: Resuscitation before endoscopy, not the reverse — a hemodynamically optimized patient tolerates the procedure far better.

Step 4 — The variceal pathway 3 min

With cirrhosis/portal hypertension, add octreotide and prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone) early — antibiotics reduce mortality. Definitive therapy is endoscopic band ligation; balloon tamponade and TIPS are rescue options. Keep transfusion restrictive.

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — the reflex transfusion

62F, melena, HR 88, BP 124/76, Hb 8.2, BUN 38/Cr 0.9, no cardiac history.

Transfuse?No — she is stable and above the restrictive Hb 7 threshold; transfusion would not help and could harm. Admit, IV PPI, plan endoscopy within 24 h.
Upper or lower?Upper — melena plus a BUN:Cr >30 without renal disease points to an upper source.

Case 2 — unstable hematochezia

70M, brisk bright-red rectal bleeding, HR 122, BP 86/54, pale.

First assumption?A brisk UPPER bleed until proven otherwise — unstable hematochezia warrants resuscitation and an upper endoscopy; a massive UGIB can present as hematochezia.
Management?Two large-bore IVs, crossmatch, resuscitate to vitals, IV PPI, urgent GI involvement; restrictive transfusion to Hb 7 (8 if cardiac disease).

Case 3 — the cirrhotic

54M with cirrhosis, hematemesis, HR 110, BP 96/60, Hb 7.5.

What besides a PPI?Octreotide (portal pressure) and prophylactic ceftriaxone (mortality benefit), plus restrictive transfusion. Definitive: endoscopic band ligation; TIPS/balloon tamponade as rescue.
Transfusion pitfall?Don't over-transfuse blood or plasma/platelets — raising portal pressure worsens variceal bleeding. Target Hb 7.

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. A hemodynamically stable 60-year-old with melena has a hemoglobin of 8.0 g/dL and no cardiovascular disease. What is the most appropriate transfusion strategy?

  1. Transfuse to a hemoglobin of 10
  2. Withhold transfusion and use a restrictive threshold of 7 g/dL
  3. Transfuse 2 units now regardless of stability
  4. Transfuse only fresh frozen plasma
  5. Transfuse to a hemoglobin of 9 immediately
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. The Villanueva trial established a restrictive threshold of 7 g/dL (target 7–9) as superior to liberal transfusion in acute upper GI bleeding. A/E: liberal targets increase rebleeding and mortality. C: transfusion isn't indicated in a stable patient above threshold. D: no coagulopathy indication is given.

2. A patient with a Glasgow-Blatchford score of 0 presents with a single episode of coffee-ground emesis and normal vitals. What does the score support?

  1. Immediate ICU admission
  2. Consideration of discharge with outpatient endoscopy
  3. Emergent surgery
  4. Mandatory transfusion
  5. Empiric variceal banding
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. A GBS of 0–1 identifies very-low-risk patients who can be considered for outpatient management with elective endoscopy. The other options overtreat a low-risk patient.

3. A cirrhotic patient presents with hematemesis. In addition to a PPI and restrictive transfusion, which therapy most clearly reduces mortality?

  1. Liberal platelet transfusion
  2. Prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone)
  3. High-dose vitamin K alone
  4. Beta-blocker bolus
  5. Immediate TIPS in all patients
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Prophylactic antibiotics reduce mortality and infection in variceal bleeding and are a core part of the bundle (with octreotide and band ligation). A: over-transfusing platelets/plasma raises portal pressure and worsens bleeding. C/D: not acute hemostatic priorities. E: TIPS is a rescue/secondary option, not first-line for all.

4. Which finding most strongly suggests an upper rather than lower GI source in a patient with melena?

  1. Bright red blood per rectum
  2. A BUN-to-creatinine ratio greater than 30 without renal disease
  3. Left lower quadrant pain
  4. A history of diverticulosis
  5. Painless large-volume hematochezia while stable
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. An elevated BUN:Cr (>30) without renal impairment reflects absorbed nitrogen from digested blood and points to an upper source. A/E suggest lower sources; C/D are not localizing to upper.

5. After initial resuscitation of a stable patient with upper GI bleeding, what is the recommended timing of endoscopy?

  1. Within 6 hours in all patients
  2. Within 24 hours after adequate resuscitation
  3. Only after 72 hours
  4. Immediately, before any resuscitation
  5. Endoscopy is rarely needed
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Endoscopy within 24 hours (after resuscitation) is recommended for most UGIB; very early (<12 h) endoscopy in under-resuscitated patients does not improve outcomes and may worsen them. A/D: premature. C: too delayed. E: endoscopy is central to diagnosis and hemostasis.

Take-Home Points

  1. Resuscitate to hemodynamics before scoping — the initial hemoglobin lags real blood loss.
  2. Transfuse restrictively to Hb 7 (target 7–9); use ~8 only with ACS or significant cardiovascular disease.
  3. Over-transfusion harms, especially in varices, by raising portal pressure — don't top up to 'normal.'
  4. Risk-stratify with Glasgow-Blatchford (0–1 = outpatient), give pre-endoscopy IV PPI/erythromycin, and scope within 24 hours.
  5. Treat suspected variceal bleeding differently: octreotide plus prophylactic antibiotics plus band ligation.
