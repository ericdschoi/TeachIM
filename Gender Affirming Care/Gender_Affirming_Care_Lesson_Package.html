Learning Objectives

  1. Apply appropriate, patient-centered terminology in clinical interactions with gender-diverse patients.
  2. Evaluate a patient for gender-affirming therapy using an informed-consent framework.
  3. Describe the anticipated effects, timeline, side effects, and monitoring of gender-affirming hormone therapy (GAHT).
  4. Apply additional ethical and medical considerations — fertility, perioperative/inpatient management, VTE on estrogen, cancer screening, and access.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient's chart lists "male." How would you ask, in a patient-centered way, about their name, pronouns, gender identity, and the sex assigned at their birth — using four separate questions? "What name do you go by?" (avoid deadnaming) · "What pronouns do you use?" (he/she/they/ze) · "How would you describe your current gender identity?" · "What sex was assigned to you at birth — what was marked on your birth certificate?" Add a focused organ inventory when it changes the differential, using the patient's preferred terms.
Q2. Name the two pillars of feminizing hormone therapy and the single pillar of masculinizing therapy, with target hormone levels. Feminizing = estrogen (17β-estradiol; goal estradiol 100–200 pg/mL) PLUS an anti-androgen (spironolactone, 5α-reductase inhibitor, or GnRH agonist) to suppress testosterone <50 ng/dL. Masculinizing = testosterone alone; goal trough total testosterone 400–700 ng/dL.
Q3. Which single GAHT medication carries a meaningful thrombotic risk, and what is the lowest-risk way to deliver it? Estrogen → venous thromboembolism. Transdermal (patch) estradiol carries the lowest VTE risk; oral/ethinyl-estradiol formulations carry the highest. A DVT/PE is not an absolute reason to stop — manage patient-centered (switch to transdermal + anticoagulate if continuing).

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (Gender_Affirming_Care_Optimized.pptx) and click through the Terminology → Evaluation → Therapies → FAQs → Resources → Cases tabs. Every cursor icon on the board is a built-in click-to-reveal prompt — pose the ASK before clicking. Core talk: ~20 min; cases add 10–15 min. This is adult outpatient care; keep the tone affirming and matter-of-fact.

Hook (2 min)

"Your next patient's chart says 'male,' but the person in front of you introduces herself as Maria. You have about 90 seconds to build trust. What are the first four things you ask — and what's the one thing that, if you get it wrong, ends the visit?" (Name, pronouns, gender identity, sex assigned at birth — and deadnaming/misgendering is the trust-ender.) This sets up Objective 1.

Objective 1 — Terminology & the affirming intake 5 min

ASK: "Gender identity, gender expression, sex assigned at birth, and sexual orientation — these are four independent axes. Map a patient who is a trans woman, presents femininely, was AMAB, and is attracted to women." Then click the Gender-Unicorn build.

Key distinctions: gender identity (internal sense of self) vs gender expression (outward presentation) vs sex assigned at birth (AMAB/AFAB; DSD formerly "intersex") vs sexual orientation (attraction). Gender-diverse identities include transgender, nonbinary, and genderfluid. Epidemiology: roughly 1.0% of millennials and 2% of Gen Z identify as gender-diverse; >2.6 million Americans.

Ask about…A patient-centered phrasing
Name"What name do you go by?" — then use it. Avoid deadnaming (using a former name).
Pronouns"What pronouns do you use?" (he/him, she/her, they/them, or neopronouns such as ze/zir)
Gender identity"How would you describe your current gender identity?"
Sex assigned at birth"What sex was assigned to you at birth — what was marked on your birth certificate?"
Organ inventory"What organs do you have, or have had removed, as part of your transition? Is cervix a word you're comfortable with?" — drives screening, not curiosity.
Pearl — recover gracefully from a mistake: if you misname or misgender a patient, the move is Recognize → Apologize → Commit to change → Move on. Don't over-apologize; a brief correction and continuing the visit respectfully does more than a long mea culpa.

Objective 2 — Evaluation for therapy 5 min

ASK: "Who is allowed to diagnose gender dysphoria and start hormones?" (A generalist — you don't need a specialist or a mental-health gatekeeper to initiate.)

Diagnosis of gender dysphoria uses DSM-5 criteria and can be made by a generalist. Gender incongruence itself is not a mental illness. Evaluation runs on an informed-consent model (WPATH SOC8): assess capacity, screen for contraindications, discuss expectations / anticipated changes / side effects (reversible and irreversible), and offer fertility counseling before starting — hormones can impair fertility.

Informed-consent checklistWhat it means
CapacityPatient understands risks, benefits, reversibility, and alternatives.
Contraindicationse.g., estrogen-sensitive cancer or significant unprovoked VTE for estrogen (see Objective 4).
ExpectationsRealistic changes and timeline; which effects are irreversible.
FertilityCounsel on impact and options (sperm/oocyte banking) before initiation.

Treatment options span more than hormones: GAHT, gender-affirming surgery, voice/vocal therapy, mental-health support — guided by the individual patient's goals, in an interdisciplinary medical home with appropriate monitoring.

Objective 3 — The hormones, effects & monitoring 8 min

ASK: "Feminizing therapy has two jobs. Name them." (Add estrogen; lower/block testosterone.)

Feminizing

DrugRole / key pointsSide effects & monitoring
17β-estradiolEstrogen. PO, sublingual, transdermal patch, or IM. Goal estradiol 100–200 pg/mL.VTE (patch lowest risk); estrogen-dependent cancers a contraindication. Monitor estradiol, total testosterone, LFTs, lipids.
SpironolactoneMost common anti-androgen.↑K⁺, orthostasis, ↑urination, ↓libido. Monitor K⁺ and Cr.
5α-reductase inhibitorAnti-androgen; generally used with spironolactone.Generally none significant; occasional ↓libido.
GnRH agonistPotent androgen suppression.Cost/access; bone health with prolonged suppression.

Feminizing timeline: ↓spontaneous erections/libido and ↓muscle mass + fat redistribution by ~3 months; breast growth (max 2–5 y, often Tanner 1–2, not reversible); ↓facial/body hair by ~6–12 months (slow, often incomplete — many still need electrolysis).

Masculinizing

DrugRole / key pointsSide effects & monitoring
TestosteroneSingle agent. IM, SC, or topical. Goal trough total testosterone 400–700 ng/dL.Erythrocytosis (check Hct/CBC). Monitor total testosterone (± estradiol).

Masculinizing timeline: ↑skin oil/acne and ↑libido early; voice deepening (not reversible); cessation of menses (~70%; ~30% continue to menstruate); ↑muscle mass and fat redistribution; clitoral enlargement (not reversible); ↑facial/body hair and male-pattern scalp loss over months to years.

ASK: "Both regimens get labs on the same schedule. What is it?" (Levels + exam at 3 and 6 months, then annually.)

Objective 4 — Ethics, safety & the hard FAQs 5 min

QuestionAnswer
CV risk on estradiol or testosterone?Slightly elevated CV risk; manage with USPSTF screening and risk-factor modification — not a reason to withhold therapy.
DVT/PE on estradiol?Risk-benefit and patient-centered. The patch is lowest-risk; consider anticoagulation rather than reflexively stopping.
Polycythemia on testosterone?Rule out other causes and OSA; decrease dose / increase frequency / switch injection → topical to reduce variability.
Cancer screening?Based on the organs present (not gender identity) — e.g., cervix → cervical screening; breast tissue → breast screening.
Hold hormones perioperatively / when admitted?Do not hold — with the exception of estradiol in a patient with VTE risk factors.
Cost & access?State Medicaid variable; Medicare should cover; surgery often self-pay. Know local legislation.
2025 access context: Since this talk was written (2024), the U.S. coverage and legal landscape has shifted — Executive Order 14187 (Jan 2025), proposed CMS Medicaid/Medicare rules (Dec 2025), and the ACA essential-health-benefit Final Rule (Jun 2025) primarily restrict care and coverage for patients under 19. The adult clinical standard of care is unchanged (WPATH SOC8, Endocrine Society), but coverage and access have become more variable. The deck's "know local legislation" point is now more important than ever — verify current local law and payer policy.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild from memory: the four intake questions, the two feminizing pillars + the masculinizing single agent with target levels, the monitoring schedule, and the one drug-risk pair you must never forget (estrogen → VTE). Then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases 10–15 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — The inpatient on masculinizing therapy

A 24-year-old person assigned female at birth (AFAB) on masculinizing therapy for 6 months presents with 2 months of fatigue and 2 weeks of fevers, chills, and dysuria.

Role-play an affirming initial interaction.Ask preferred name and pronouns; discuss gender, gender identity, and sex assigned at birth in a patient-centered way (Terminology).
List the expected anatomic/physiologic changes at 6 months — and do they affect the differential?↑Skin oil/acne, vocal deepening, menstrual interruption, possible ↑strength and male-pattern body hair, clitoral enlargement, vaginal atrophy. Discuss fertility (pregnancy is still possible) and take a sexual history (recent partners, protection) — relevant to the dysuria/UTI/STI workup.
Which medications, and which labs now?Medication: testosterone. Labs: CBC to assess for erythrocytosis/polycythemia. No clear role for a total-testosterone level based on these presenting symptoms.
Is an organ inventory appropriate? Perform one.Yes. Ask the patient's preferred terms, use neutral language, and assess for relevant organs — here, vagina, uterus, (constructed) penis, and urethra if there's a surgical history — to guide the GU/infectious workup.
Should testosterone be held during the hospitalization?No — do not stop it unless there are clear signs/symptoms of polycythemia.

Case 2 — Initiating feminizing therapy

A 38-year-old individual whose sex assigned at birth is male presents to clinic to discuss starting feminizing hormones.

Which medications, and what's the timeline of changes?Estradiol, typically plus one or more anti-androgens. Expect ↓strength, fat redistribution, breast-bud development, and often erectile dysfunction by ~3 months; facial/body hair decreases around 6 months.
List likely side effects and how they present.DVT/PE — unilateral leg swelling/cord, or sudden dyspnea / tachycardia / chest pain. Spironolactone — hyperkalemia (usually asymptomatic), ↑urination, sometimes orthostasis. 5α-reductase inhibitor — rarely orthostasis, sometimes ↓libido. All can cause erectile dysfunction.
What considerations precede starting GAHT or surgery?Diagnosis of gender dysphoria, capacity for consent, evaluating for contraindications, expectations/goals/side effects, and fertility counseling.
Long-term CV or bone effects of feminizing hormones?Counsel on a small CV-risk signal and the importance of bone health (especially with GnRH agonists / prolonged androgen suppression); see the STRONG study. Apply standard USPSTF screening.

Case 3 — DVT on estradiol (the patient-centered dilemma)

Rain, a 35-year-old trans female / nonbinary person (they/them) on oral estradiol for 2 years, presents with leg pain and swelling. Ultrasound shows a DVT extending through the right popliteal and femoral veins. No chest pain; hemodynamically stable.

Discuss the benefits and risks of stopping hormone therapy.Discuss in a patient-centered way. The principal risk of continuing estradiol is extension of the DVT or conversion to a PE, which can be life-threatening. But the psychological and medical risks of stopping GAHT (depression, suicidality) can also be profound and must be weighed — this is shared decision-making, not an automatic stop.
If the patient chooses to continue GAHT, what's the plan?If continuing aligns with the patient's goals: switch oral → transdermal estrogen (lower VTE risk), use therapeutic-dose anticoagulation (per UCSF estrogen-in-VTE guidance), and if there is symptomatic PE or hemodynamic instability, stop estradiol and consider restarting after PE symptoms resolve.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 29-year-old trans woman has been on oral estradiol and spironolactone for 8 months. She is satisfied with her progress. Labs: estradiol 130 pg/mL, total testosterone 35 ng/dL, K⁺ 5.2 mEq/L, Cr 1.0. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Increase estradiol to push the level higher
  2. Stop spironolactone because testosterone is already suppressed
  3. Continue current regimen; recheck potassium and counsel on dietary potassium
  4. Add a GnRH agonist
Answer & explanationC. Her estradiol (target 100–200 pg/mL) and testosterone (<50 ng/dL) are both at goal — no dose change is warranted (A, D). The actionable finding is mild hyperkalemia from spironolactone; address it (recheck, dietary counseling, consider dose adjustment) rather than abandoning effective androgen blockade (B). Spironolactone monitoring includes K⁺ and Cr.

2. A 35-year-old trans woman on oral estradiol for 2 years presents with an acute proximal DVT and is hemodynamically stable with no chest symptoms. She strongly wishes to continue feminizing therapy. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. Permanently discontinue all estrogen therapy
  2. Continue oral estradiol unchanged and start anticoagulation
  3. Switch to transdermal estradiol and start therapeutic anticoagulation
  4. Switch to high-dose ethinyl estradiol
Answer & explanationC. A DVT is not an absolute reason to stop GAHT — the psychosocial risks of abrupt cessation are real and decisions are shared. The lowest-VTE-risk delivery is transdermal, so switch from oral and add therapeutic anticoagulation (UCSF guidance). Continuing the higher-risk oral route (B) or escalating to ethinyl estradiol (D, the highest-risk estrogen) worsens thrombotic risk. Permanent discontinuation (A) ignores shared decision-making and is reserved for symptomatic PE / instability.

3. A 26-year-old trans man on intramuscular testosterone for 1 year is found to have a hematocrit of 54%. He feels well. What is the best next step?

  1. Stop testosterone permanently
  2. Evaluate for secondary causes (including OSA) and reduce dose / increase frequency or switch to topical to limit peaks
  3. Begin therapeutic phlebotomy and continue the same regimen
  4. Reassure and recheck in one year
Answer & explanationB. Erythrocytosis is the characteristic hematologic effect of testosterone. Work up other causes (notably OSA) and reduce variability — lower the dose, increase frequency, or move from injection to topical, which smooths the peaks that drive erythrocytosis. Permanent cessation (A) is excessive. Phlebotomy without changing the driver (C) is incomplete, and ignoring an Hct of 54% (D) is unsafe.

4. A generalist sees a 22-year-old who reports persistent gender incongruence and requests masculinizing therapy. Which statement about initiating care is correct?

  1. A mental-health professional must formally approve hormones before a generalist can prescribe
  2. The diagnosis of gender dysphoria can be made by a generalist, and therapy can start using an informed-consent model
  3. Gender incongruence is itself a psychiatric disorder requiring treatment
  4. Two referral letters are required before starting hormones
Answer & explanationB. Under WPATH SOC8, a generalist can diagnose gender dysphoria (DSM-5) and initiate GAHT through an informed-consent process — capacity, contraindication screening, expectations, fertility counseling. A mandatory mental-health gatekeeper (A) is not required to start hormones, and SOC8 reduced the surgical requirement to a single letter, not two (D). Gender incongruence is explicitly not a mental illness (C).

5. A 40-year-old trans man (AFAB) on testosterone for 3 years, with a retained cervix, comes in for routine care. He has had no Pap testing. What is the most appropriate screening recommendation?

  1. No cervical cancer screening is needed because he identifies as male
  2. Screen for cervical cancer per standard guidelines because a cervix is present
  3. Screen only if he reports symptoms
  4. Replace Pap testing with serum testosterone monitoring
Answer & explanationB. Cancer screening follows the organs that are present, not gender identity. A patient with a retained cervix needs cervical cancer screening on the standard schedule (testosterone can cause atrophic changes that make cytology harder to interpret, reinforcing the need for guideline-based screening). Gender identity (A), symptom-only screening (C), and substituting hormone labs (D) all miss an organ-based screening indication.

Take-Home Points

  1. Lead with patient-centered language: ask name, pronouns, gender identity, sex assigned at birth, and a focused organ inventory; if you misgender, recognize–apologize–commit–move on.
  2. A generalist can diagnose gender dysphoria and start GAHT through informed consent (capacity, contraindications, expectations, fertility counseling); gender incongruence is not a mental illness.
  3. Feminizing = estrogen (17β-estradiol; goal 100–200 pg/mL) ± anti-androgen; masculinizing = testosterone (goal trough 400–700 ng/dL). Monitor levels + exam at 3 and 6 months, then annually.
  4. Remember the drug-risk pairs — estrogen→VTE (transdermal lowest-risk), spironolactone→hyperkalemia, testosterone→erythrocytosis. Don't hold hormones when admitted (except estradiol with VTE risk); screen cancers by organs present.
