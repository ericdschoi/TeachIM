You're called about a critical K of 6.7. What are the first two steps?	Confirm the lab (expected? hemolysis? concurrent AKI?) AND obtain a STAT ECG — any K > 5.5 warrants an ECG. Confirmation must not delay treatment.
What is the ECG progression of worsening hyperkalemia?	Early: peaked T waves + QT shortening → P-wave flattening/loss with PR prolongation → QRS widening → sine wave, ventricular arrhythmia, or asystole. (Also pseudo-RBBB / pseudo-STE in V1–V2.)
Do ECG changes correlate with the serum potassium level?	Poorly. ECG changes predict poor outcomes better than the absolute K, and a rapid rise is more arrhythmogenic than a slow one — chronic CKD/ESRD patients tolerate higher K.
What triggers IV calcium in hyperkalemia?	ECG changes OR K > 6.5 (treat K > 6.5 as emergent regardless of ECG).
Compare the two IV calcium formulations.	Calcium gluconate 1–2 g IV — preferred, peripheral OK. Calcium chloride 0.5–1 g IV — more potent but caustic; give via central line.
IV calcium: onset, duration, effect on K, and re-dosing rule?	Onset 3–5 min, duration 30–60 min. No effect on serum K — it stabilizes the myocardium and should normalize the ECG; re-dose every 5–10 min if ECG changes persist.
Insulin/dextrose for hyperkalemia: dose, renal adjustment, effect?	Regular insulin 10U IV + 1 amp D50 (5U if renal impairment; skip D50 if BG > 250). Onset 15–30 min, lasts 4–6 h (longer in AKI). Lowers K ~0.5–1.0 mEq/L. Monitor glucose afterward — hypoglycemia is the most common complication.
Albuterol for hyperkalemia: dose, effect, caveat?	10–20 mg nebulized in 4 mL saline — far above the 2.5 mg bronchodilator dose. Lowers K 0.5–1.5 mEq/L, onset 15–30 min; most effective combined with insulin. Use caution in active coronary disease (tachycardia/angina).
When is sodium bicarbonate a reasonable shifting agent?	Only with metabolic acidosis and NOT in ESRD (shown ineffective on HD). 50–100 mEq IV. It is the least effective shifting strategy.
Why is intracellular shifting only a temporizing measure?	It moves K into cells without removing it from the body — serum K rebounds as the effect wears off, so always pair shifting with elimination (diuresis, binder, or dialysis).
How is potassium normally eliminated from the body?	~90% renal, ~10% GI (the basis for diuretics/dialysis vs. gut binders).
Role of loop diuretics in hyperkalemia?	First-line if hypervolemic (furosemide 40–160 mg IV); add isotonic fluids if euvolemic to avoid volume depletion worsening AKI; ineffective in anuria.
When is urgent dialysis indicated for hyperkalemia?	Severe renal dysfunction (especially anuria/ESRD without access to K elimination) or hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy — HD is the intervention most likely to achieve normal K within 4 h.
Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC/Lokelma): dosing, onset, downsides?	10 g TID for up to 48–72 h; onset ~1 h (fastest binder); works in small + large intestine. Downsides: cost/availability, sodium load → edema. UKKA 2023 now lists it as an option alongside shifting agents in acute life-threatening hyperkalemia (K ≥ 6.5).
Kayexalate (SPS): dosing and contraindications?	15–60 g PO q6h (or 30–50 g PR). Risk of bowel necrosis — avoid post-op, ileus/obstruction, IBD, infectious colitis; separate from other meds by 3 h; skip it if dialysis is planned.
Patient on an ACE inhibitor develops hyperkalemia — stop the drug?	Per KDIGO 2024: prefer lowering the K (review K-raising drugs/diet, diuretic, binder, bicarbonate if acidotic) over stopping the RASi — discontinuation is associated with higher mortality and CV events in CKD.
What monitoring follows treatment of hyperkalemia?	Recheck K in 1–4 h and repeat ECG every 30–60 min until abnormalities resolve; telemetry; repeat temporizing + elimination measures until K normalizes or dialysis occurs.
