Learning Objectives

  1. Practice a 5-step framework for managing hyperkalemia in the hospital: confirm → ECG → stabilize → shift → eliminate.
  2. Recognize the cardiac complications of hyperkalemia and the characteristic, progressive ECG changes.
  3. Distinguish temporizing measures (intracellular shift) from interventions that actually eliminate potassium — and pair them correctly.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. The nurse calls: K is 6.7. Does IV calcium lower the potassium level? No. Calcium does nothing to the serum K — it stabilizes the myocardial membrane and buys time. Its effect is judged on the ECG (changes should normalize), not on the chemistry panel.
Q2. You give insulin/D50 and albuterol and the K falls from 6.8 to 5.9. Is the problem solved? No. Shifting is temporizing — total body potassium is unchanged, and serum K rebounds in hours as the effect wears off. Definitive management requires elimination: diuresis, a GI binder, or dialysis.
Q3. Which patients should NOT receive sodium bicarbonate as a potassium-shifting agent? Patients without metabolic acidosis, and patients with ESRD (trials show non-responders were on hemodialysis). It is the least effective shifting strategy even when used correctly.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (Hyperkalemia_Optimized.pptx) and click through, or redraw the schematic on a whiteboard. Every cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Talk alone ~15 min; with the two cases ~20 min. Print the learner worksheet (blank schematic) so learners fill it in as you go.

Hook (2 min)

"You're pre-rounding and the nurse calls: 'Critical lab on Mrs. Smith — her potassium is 6.7.' You have sixty seconds before she asks what you want to do. What do you say?" Let learners commit out loud. Then frame the talk: "There are exactly five moves, in order, and two of them don't lower the potassium at all — they keep the patient alive while the others work."

Step 1 — Confirm the hyperkalemia 2 min

ASK: "Before you treat a K of 6.7 — what would make you doubt the number?" — then click to reveal.

Was the result expected (AKI, ESRD, K-raising drugs)? Is there hemolysis flagged on the specimen? Fist clenching, traumatic draw, and processing delays all spuriously raise K. Confirmation must never delay treatment when the level is high or the patient is symptomatic (myalgias, weakness, palpitations).

Step 2 — STAT ECG 3 min

ASK: "Draw the ECG progression as the potassium climbs." Have a learner sketch it before revealing.
StageECG findings
EarlyPeaked T waves (best in precordial leads), QT shortening
WorseningPR prolongation → P-wave flattening/loss; QRS widening
Pre-arrestSine wave, ventricular arrhythmia, asystole; also pseudo-RBBB and pseudo-STE patterns in V1–V2

Any K > 5.5 warrants an ECG; treat K > 6.5 as emergent regardless of ECG. Repeat the ECG every 30–60 min until abnormalities resolve; keep the patient on telemetry.

Pearl: ECG changes track outcomes better than the absolute K, but correlate poorly with the level itself. A rapid rise is far more arrhythmogenic than a chronically elevated K — CKD/ESRD patients tolerate levels that would be alarming in a previously normal patient.

Step 3 — Stabilize the myocardium 3 min

ASK: "What's the trigger for IV calcium, which salt do you reach for, and how do you know it worked?"
Calcium gluconateCalcium chloride
Dose1–2 g IV — preferred, peripheral OK0.5–1 g IV — more potent, caustic; central line
Onset / duration3–5 min / 30–60 min
EffectNo effect on serum K. Should normalize the ECG — if changes persist at 5–10 min, re-dose.

Trigger: ECG changes or K > 6.5.

Step 4 — Shift potassium intracellularly 4 min

ASK: for each agent — "What dose? How much will it drop the K? How long do you have before it wears off?" — then click each button.
AgentDoseOnset / durationΔKCaveats
Regular insulin + D5010U IV + 1 amp D50 (5U if renal impairment; no D50 if BG > 250)15–60 min / 4–6 h (longer in AKI)↓ 0.5–1.0 mEq/LHypoglycemia is the most common complication — monitor glucose (UKKA 2023: for ~6 h)
β2-agonist (albuterol)10–20 mg neb in 4 mL saline — far above the 2.5 mg bronchodilator dose15–30 min / 2–4 h↓ 0.5–1.5 mEq/LCaution in active CAD (tachycardia/angina); most effective with insulin

Pearl: Pick the shifting cocktail by comorbidity: AKI/ESRD → halve the insulin; active CAD → go easy on albuterol; acidotic non-ESRD → bicarbonate earns a slot. Shifting buys hours, not a fix.

Step 5 — Eliminate potassium 4 min

ASK: "Ninety percent of potassium leaves the body one way. What's the route, and what's the back door?" — then click the kidney and the gut.
RouteTherapyNotes
Kidney (~90%)Loop diuretic (furosemide 40–160 mg IV)1st-line if hypervolemic; onset ~30 min; ineffective in anuria; risk of volume depletion worsening AKI if used alone
Isotonic IVF (NS or isotonic bicarb)Pair with loops if euvolemic; isotonic bicarbonate also shifts K in acidosis
Hemodialysis / CRRTFor severe renal dysfunction or refractory hyperkalemia — most likely intervention to achieve normal K within 4 h
Gut (~10%)SZC (Lokelma) 10 g TID × 48 hOnset ~1 h — preferred binder; sodium load/edema, cost. UKKA 2023: an option alongside shifting agents in acute life-threatening hyperkalemia (K ≥ 6.5). (Patiromer: colon-only, onset ~7 h — chronic niche.)
Kayexalate (SPS) 15–60 g q6h PO (30–50 g PR)Bowel-necrosis risk — avoid post-op, ileus/SBO, IBD, infectious colitis; separate other meds by 3 h; skip if HD is planned (UKKA 2023: not for emergency treatment)

After interventions: recheck K in 1–4 h, repeat ECG q30–60 min, and repeat shifting + elimination until K normalizes or dialysis happens.

KDIGO 2024 update: when hyperkalemia is driven by an ACEi/ARB in CKD, prefer lowering the potassium (drug/diet review, diuretic, binder, bicarbonate if acidotic) over stopping the RASi — discontinuation is associated with higher mortality and CV events.

Close — retrieval (2 min)

Erase/hide the board and have learners rebuild the 5 steps from memory, with one drug + dose per step. Finish with: "Which two steps change the potassium not at all?" (confirm + calcium).

Cases from the original board — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1

55-year-old with ESRD on HD via an AV fistula presents with shortness of breath and pain at the fistula site after missing dialysis. Fistulogram shows stenosis of the AV fistula. They are anuric. BMP: K 6.7 (no hemolysis), HCO3 19, BUN 56, Cr 10.5.

What is the next step in management?Obtain an ECG. (Board shows diffusely peaked T waves.)
ECG shows peaked T waves — now what?IV calcium gluconate 1–2 g for cardiac stabilization. Repeat the ECG in 30–60 min; re-dose calcium if changes persist. Telemetry.
What temporizing measures would you use?IV regular insulin 5U (because of ESRD) + D50W, albuterol nebulizer 20 mg. Sodium bicarbonate is likely ineffective in ESRD.
What will you use for potassium elimination?SZC if available, Kayexalate otherwise, and urgent revision of the fistula so dialysis can resume. Loop diuretics are ineffective in anuria. (Note the board's own caution: avoid Kayexalate when HD is imminent — diarrhea complicates dialysis.)

Case 2

65-year-old with metastatic colon cancer presents for colectomy. On POD3, develops a new non-oliguric AKI. Euvolemic on exam. Labs: K 6.0, Cr 0.6 → 3.5, HCO3 13; ABG pH 7.2.

What is the next step in management?Obtain an ECG.
ECG shows no obvious changes — does this patient get calcium?The board gives IV calcium gluconate 1–2 g given the severity of hyperkalemia, with telemetry. (K 6.0 with a rising trajectory and acidosis — reasonable; mandatory once K > 6.5 or ECG changes appear.)
What temporizing measures would you use?IV regular insulin 5U (because of AKI) + D50W, albuterol nebulizer; sodium bicarbonate is appropriate here given metabolic acidosis (pH 7.2, HCO3 13) without ESRD.
What will you use for potassium elimination?IV loop diuretics +/- IVF (isotonic NaHCO3 infusion) to maintain euvolemia and avoid worsening the Cr. No Kayexalate — recent bowel surgery (necrosis risk).

Board-style MCQs NEW — 5 ABIM-format questions

1. A 58-year-old man with ESRD missed dialysis twice. He reports weakness. K is 7.1, no hemolysis. ECG shows peaked T waves and a widening QRS. Which is the best next step?

  1. IV regular insulin 10 units with D50
  2. IV calcium gluconate 1–2 g
  3. Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate 10 g
  4. Emergent hemodialysis
  5. Sodium bicarbonate 100 mEq IV
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB — IV calcium gluconate. ECG changes mandate immediate membrane stabilization (onset 3–5 min) before anything else. A: insulin is the right second move but takes 15–60 min and does not protect the myocardium now. C: SZC takes ~1 h and removes K slowly — adjunct, not first. D: dialysis is the definitive therapy and should be arranged, but it cannot start within minutes; calcium first. E: bicarbonate is the least effective shifter and is generally ineffective in ESRD. Also: insulin should be dose-reduced to 5U in renal failure.

2. A 70-year-old woman with CKD has K 6.6 treated with calcium gluconate, insulin/D50, and albuterol. One hour later K is 5.8. Which statement best describes her situation?

  1. The potassium problem is resolved; recheck in 24 h
  2. Total body potassium is unchanged and will rebound; elimination therapy is required
  3. Calcium lowered the serum potassium and can be re-dosed for further effect
  4. Albuterol provides definitive potassium removal over the next 6 h
  5. She now requires emergent dialysis regardless of further labs
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Insulin and albuterol only shift K into cells (duration 2–6 h); serum K rebounds as they wear off. A: rechecking at 24 h misses the rebound — recheck in 1–4 h. C: calcium has no effect on serum K. D: albuterol eliminates nothing. E: dialysis is for severe renal dysfunction or refractory hyperkalemia, not automatic here — diuretics/binders may suffice with residual renal function.

3. A 64-year-old man is POD2 from sigmoid colectomy with a postoperative ileus. K is 6.3 with mild AKI; ECG normal. Insulin/D50 and albuterol are given. Which elimination therapy is contraindicated?

  1. IV furosemide
  2. Isotonic sodium bicarbonate infusion
  3. Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate)
  4. Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate
  5. Hemodialysis
Answer + why each distractor is wrongC — Kayexalate. SPS carries a risk of bowel necrosis and is contraindicated post-operatively and with ileus/obstruction, IBD, or infectious colitis. A/B: loops + isotonic fluids are the first-line elimination strategy in a non-anuric patient. D: SZC acts in small and large intestine and lacks the same necrosis association (sodium load is its issue). E: dialysis is not contraindicated — just not yet indicated.

4. A 59-year-old with diabetic CKD G3b (eGFR 38) on lisinopril has a routine K of 5.7, repeatedly. He is asymptomatic; ECG unchanged. Per KDIGO 2024 guidance, the best initial approach is:

  1. Stop lisinopril permanently
  2. Review K-raising drugs/diet and add a diuretic or potassium binder, continuing the ACE inhibitor
  3. Admit for IV calcium and insulin
  4. Start Kayexalate chronically
  5. Refer for dialysis access planning
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. KDIGO 2024 recommends managing RASi-associated hyperkalemia with K-lowering measures rather than reflexively stopping the RASi, whose discontinuation is associated with higher mortality and CV events in CKD. A: last resort after other measures. C: chronic mild hyperkalemia without ECG changes is not an emergency. D: SPS is the least preferred binder for chronic use (GI toxicity); patiromer/SZC are better tolerated. E: nothing here suggests imminent ESRD.

5. Which patient is the best candidate for sodium bicarbonate as part of potassium-shifting therapy?

  1. ESRD on HD, K 6.9, pH 7.31
  2. AKI with HCO3 14 and pH 7.21
  3. CKD G3 with normal bicarbonate, K 6.0
  4. Acute hyperkalemia from rhabdomyolysis with pH 7.44
  5. Any patient already receiving insulin/D50
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Bicarbonate is reserved for metabolic acidosis in patients NOT on dialysis — responders in studies had K > 6, HCO3 < 17, pH < 7.35. A: ESRD/HD patients were the non-responders. C/D: no acidosis, no role. E: bicarbonate is not a default add-on; it is the least effective shifter and adds sodium load.

Take-Home Points

  1. Hyperkalemic crisis is a medical emergency — severe weakness and fatal arrhythmias. Any K > 5.5 → STAT ECG; treat K > 6.5 as emergent regardless of ECG.
  2. ECG changes: peaked T waves → P-wave loss → QRS widening → sine wave/VF. ECG changes or K > 6.5 → IV calcium gluconate 1–2 g; re-dose in 5–10 min if the ECG hasn't normalized. Calcium never lowers the K.
  3. Shift while you arrange elimination: insulin 10U + D50 (5U if renal impairment), albuterol 10–20 mg neb, ± bicarbonate only in non-ESRD metabolic acidosis.
  4. Eliminate: loops ± IVF by volume status; binders (SZC 10 g TID preferred over Kayexalate) when kidneys can't do the job; urgent dialysis for severe renal dysfunction or refractory hyperkalemia.
  5. Recheck K in 1–4 h and repeat the cycle — shifting buys time, it doesn't remove potassium.
