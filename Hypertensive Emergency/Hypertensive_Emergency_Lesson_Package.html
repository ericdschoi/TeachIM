Learning Objectives

  1. Define hypertensive emergency and distinguish it from hypertensive urgency (now termed severe hypertension without acute target-organ damage).
  2. Identify the signs and symptoms of end-organ failure across the CNS, cardiovascular, and renal systems.
  3. Differentiate the treatment goals — and the speed of BP lowering — for emergency vs. severe-HTN-without-damage, and name the scenarios that break the general rule.
  4. Match the common IV antihypertensives to the clinical scenario using their mechanism of action.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient has a BP of 220/125. What single additional finding turns this from "severe hypertension" into a "hypertensive emergency"? Evidence of acute target-organ damage. The absolute number does not define an emergency — a previously normotensive patient can have damage below 180/120, and a chronically hypertensive patient can tolerate 220/125 without it. The rate of rise matters more than the number.
Q2. Why is it dangerous to drop the blood pressure to normal quickly in a hypertensive emergency? Cerebral (and other vascular bed) autoregulation has reset to a higher range. A rapid drop falls below the autoregulatory floor and causes hypoperfusion/ischemia. The goal is to lower SBP by no more than ~25% in the first hour, not to normalize it.
Q3. Name one IV antihypertensive you would AVOID in (a) acute LV failure with pulmonary edema and (b) pheochromocytoma crisis, and why. (a) Avoid a non-vasodilating β-blocker / labetalol before the EF is known — losing inotropy can precipitate cardiogenic shock. (b) Avoid a β-blocker as initial therapy — unopposed α-adrenergic vasoconstriction worsens the hypertension. Treat the α first (phentolamine).

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (HTN_Emergency_Optimized.pptx) and click through, or rebuild the schematic on a whiteboard. Every cursor icon on the board is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Talk without cases: ~15–20 min. Cases: +15 min. The talk also splits cleanly into two sessions (objectives 1–3, then objective 4 + cases). Print the learner handout (first page is a blank schematic) so learners fill it in as you go.

Hook (2 min)

"Two patients, both with a BP of 230/130. One gets an ICU bed and an IV drip; the other goes home on their own pills. What's the one thing that decides which is which?" (Acute end-organ damage.) Then: "And here's the counterintuitive part — for the sick one, your job is NOT to get the pressure to normal. Fixing it too fast can stroke them out." That tension drives the whole talk.

Objective 1 — Definition 3 min

ASK: "Define hypertensive emergency" — then click to reveal. Severe HTN (SBP >180 or DBP >120) PLUS acute end-organ dysfunction. The end-organ damage — not the number — is the dividing line.

Drive home two points: (1) rate of rise > absolute number — a chronic hypertensive tolerates 200/120 without damage; a previously normotensive patient (eclampsia, acute GN) can have damage well below 180/120. (2) The contrast term has been renamed (see the 2025 update box below): what the board calls "hypertensive urgency" is now severe hypertension without acute target-organ damage.

2025 AHA/ACC terminology update: The 2025 AHA/ACC hypertension guideline formally retires "hypertensive urgency" in favor of severe hypertension without acute target-organ damage — a more precise label meant to curb unnecessary ED visits, IV drips, and aggressive lowering in this group. Management is unchanged: reinstitute/intensify oral therapy as an outpatient with close follow-up. The definition of hypertensive emergency and the >180/120 threshold are unchanged. The board still says "HTN urgency"; flag the new wording when you teach.

Objective 3 — Treatment goals 5 min

ASK: "Goal BP for a hypertensive emergency — is it normal? How fast?" — let them commit before revealing.

The goal is to prevent further irreversible organ damage, not to normalize BP. Manage in the ICU with IV titratable agents and remove exacerbating factors (pain, volume, drugs).

ScenarioBP target / pace
General principle↓ SBP by ≤25% in the first hour → ~160/100 by 2–6 h → normal over 24–48 h
Aortic dissectionHR to ~60 FIRST (β-blocker), then SBP <120 ASAP (within ~20 min)
ICH / SAHSBP <140 promptly (per the dedicated stroke/ICH protocol)
Eclampsia / pheo crisisLower SBP toward <140 in the first hour; magnesium for eclampsia
Acute ischemic strokePermissive HTN if ≤220/120; if >220/120, lower ~15% over 24 h; if tPA candidate, must be <185/110 before lytics (<180/105 for 24 h after)
Pearl — the dissection sequence: Rate before pressure. Give the β-blocker (esmolol/labetalol) to bring HR to ~60 before adding a pure vasodilator like nitroprusside. Vasodilating first triggers a reflex tachycardia that increases aortic wall shear stress (dP/dt) and can extend the dissection.

Objective 4 — Medications by mechanism 5 min

ASK: "Don't memorize a list — reason from mechanism. If I want to drop afterload without touching preload, what class? If I need to kill a reflex tachycardia, what class?"

Two big buckets: vasodilators and adrenergic antagonists.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild from memory: the one-line definition, the three organ systems, the general lowering rule + the three exceptions that break it, and the drug they'd pick for stroke, for pulmonary edema, for dissection, and for pheo.

Practice Cases 15 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Weakness + very high BP

67-year-old on chronic steroids presents with left leg > arm weakness for 18 h. BP 245/123.

Labs / imaging?CTA head: acute right ACA infarct without hemorrhagic conversion.
Diagnosis?Hypertensive emergency with acute ischemic stroke.
Treatment and BP goal?Nicardipine or labetalol. Reduce BP by ~15% over the first 24 h. With ischemic stroke and SBP ≤220/120, do NOT lower the BP (permissive hypertension); lower below 185/110 only if the patient is a tPA candidate.

Case 2 — Chest pain + pulmonary edema

59-year-old, no known PMHx, acute chest pain and shortness of breath. BP 220/130.

Labs / imaging?Troponin 1.1 → 1.2 → 1.1 ng/mL; BNP 5000; ECG: ST depressions V4–V6; CXR: marked pulmonary edema.
Diagnosis?Hypertensive emergency with acute LV failure and pulmonary edema.
Treatment and BP goal? Anything to avoid?Nitroglycerin + IV diuresis to drop preload; reduce BP ~25% in the first hour (nitroprusside may be added because nitroglycerin is a weak antihypertensive). Avoid β-blockers until the EF is known — can precipitate cardiogenic shock.

Case 3 — Tearing chest pain to the back

48-year-old, severe chest pain radiating to the back. BP 225/134. On exam BP is 201/110 in the left arm with a 1+ left radial pulse vs 2+ elsewhere; no focal neuro deficit.

Labs / imaging?ECG: no ischemic changes; troponin 0.01 ng/mL; Cr 1.0; CXR: widened mediastinum; CTA: Type B aortic dissection.
Diagnosis?Hypertensive emergency with aortic dissection. The inter-arm differential and weak left radial pulse should also raise concern for subclavian (type A) involvement — reassuringly, the normal neuro exam and cardiac markers argue against it here.
Treatment and BP goal?Labetalol or esmolol ± nitroprusside. Target HR ~60 with the β-blocker first (prevents reflex tachycardia), THEN add nitroprusside to bring SBP <120 ASAP.

Case 4 — Missed doses + nausea

67-year-old with HTN, 3 days of nausea/vomiting, no meds for 3 days. BP 190/125. Home meds: lisinopril 40, amlodipine 10, HCTZ 12.5. Reports headache; denies blurry vision, chest pain, dyspnea, focal weakness, confusion. Exam: no focal deficits.

Labs / imaging?ECG: no ischemic changes; troponin 0.01 ng/mL; Cr 1.0; CXR: no focal infiltrate. No evidence of acute organ damage.
Diagnosis and treatment?Severe hypertension without acute target-organ damage (formerly "hypertensive urgency"). Control the nausea and restart the home regimen to lower BP over days — outpatient, oral, no IV drip.

Case 5 — Episodic spells + fever + confusion

47-year-old with episodic headaches, palpitations, diaphoresis now presents with confusion and fever. T 40°C, BP 243/143, HR 140s.

Labs / imaging?ECG: sinus tachycardia; CT A/P: 1.4 cm right adrenal mass; elevated plasma and urine metanephrines.
Diagnosis, treatment, and what to avoid?Pheochromocytoma crisis. Phentolamine, nicardipine, or nitroprusside; lower SBP toward <140 in the first hour. Avoid β-blockers as initial therapy — unopposed α-adrenergic vasoconstriction worsens hypertension.

Case 6 — Oliguria + rising Cr

51-year-old with malaise and decreased urine output. BP 197/103.

Labs / imaging?BMP: K 5.6, HCO3 17, Cr 6.7; renal US: no hydronephrosis or obstruction.
Diagnosis and treatment?Hypertensive emergency with acute renal failure. Nicardipine or fenoldopam (renal vasodilation); reduce BP ~25% in the first hour.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 54-year-old man with poorly controlled hypertension presents with a BP of 224/126. He is asymptomatic. ECG, troponin, creatinine, and urinalysis are normal, and his neurologic exam is intact. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. Admit to the ICU for an IV nicardipine drip
  2. IV labetalol bolus in the ED, then reassess
  3. Restart/intensify oral antihypertensives and arrange close outpatient follow-up
  4. IV hydralazine to lower the BP below 180/120 before discharge
Answer & explanationC. This is severe hypertension without acute target-organ damage (the 2025 AHA/ACC term replacing "hypertensive urgency"). With no evidence of organ injury, there is no benefit — and real harm — from rapid parenteral lowering (A, B, D); abrupt drops risk hypoperfusion. The standard of care is to reinstitute or intensify oral therapy and follow up closely as an outpatient.

2. A 62-year-old presents with tearing chest pain radiating to the back, BP 230/120, and a CT angiogram showing a Type B aortic dissection. Which initial pharmacologic step is most appropriate?

  1. Start nitroprusside to rapidly lower SBP, then add a β-blocker
  2. Start esmolol to a target heart rate of ~60, then add nitroprusside for SBP <120
  3. Start nicardipine alone
  4. Start hydralazine for rapid afterload reduction
Answer & explanationB. Control heart rate first. A pure vasodilator given before β-blockade (A, D) causes reflex tachycardia, raising aortic wall shear stress (dP/dt) and risking extension of the dissection. Esmolol (or labetalol) to a HR of ~60 first, then nitroprusside to drive SBP <120 ASAP, is the correct sequence. Nicardipine alone (C) does not address the heart rate.

3. A 70-year-old presents with right-sided weakness for 4 hours; BP is 215/118. Non-contrast head CT shows no hemorrhage and he is not a candidate for thrombolysis. What is the most appropriate blood pressure management?

  1. Lower SBP to <140 immediately with nicardipine
  2. Lower BP to <185/110 before any further care
  3. Permissive hypertension — do not lower the BP
  4. Start nitroprusside to reduce BP by 50% over the first hour
Answer & explanationC. In acute ischemic stroke without thrombolysis, BP is permitted up to 220/120; lowering it risks extending the infarct by reducing perfusion to the ischemic penumbra (A, D). The <185/110 target (B) applies only to tPA candidates. Above 220/120, the recommendation is a modest ~15% reduction over 24 h.

4. A 45-year-old with episodic headache, palpitations, and diaphoresis presents in crisis with BP 250/140 and elevated plasma metanephrines. A resident reaches for IV metoprolol. Why is this the wrong first move?

  1. β-blockers are contraindicated in any hypertensive emergency
  2. Unopposed α-adrenergic vasoconstriction can worsen the hypertension
  3. Metoprolol cannot be given intravenously
  4. It will cause reflex bradycardia and asystole
Answer & explanationB. In catecholamine excess, blocking β2-mediated vasodilation while leaving α1-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed can precipitate a paradoxical surge in BP. Establish α-blockade first (phentolamine). β-blockers are appropriate in other emergencies such as dissection (A is false). The issue is pharmacologic, not route (C) or reflex bradycardia (D).

5. A 58-year-old with acute pulmonary edema, chest pain, and BP 210/120 is found to have an LVEF that has not yet been assessed. Which is the best initial antihypertensive strategy?

  1. IV labetalol to reduce afterload and rate
  2. Nitroglycerin (± nitroprusside) plus IV diuresis to reduce preload
  3. Oral amlodipine
  4. Hydralazine bolus
Answer & explanationB. Venodilation with nitroglycerin reduces preload and pulmonary venous hydrostatic pressure, directly relieving the edema; diuresis adds to this, and nitroprusside can be added for further BP control. A β-blocking agent like labetalol (A) is hazardous before the EF is known — loss of inotropy can precipitate cardiogenic shock. Oral amlodipine (C) is too slow for an emergency, and hydralazine (D) causes a reflex tachycardia that worsens ischemia.

Take-Home Points

  1. Hypertensive emergency = severe HTN (>180/120) WITH acute end-organ damage; the damage — not the BP number — defines it, and the rate of rise matters more than the absolute value.
  2. Screen three organ systems every time: CNS (encephalopathy, ICH/SAH, ischemic stroke), cardiac (acute LV failure, ACS, dissection), and renal (rising Cr, low UOP).
  3. Goal is to prevent further damage, not normalize BP: ≤25% SBP drop in hour 1 → 160/100 by 2–6 h → normal over 24–48 h — with faster, deeper targets only for dissection, ICH/SAH, eclampsia/pheo, and special stroke rules.
  4. Pick the drug from the mechanism: nicardipine (stroke, pregnancy), nitroglycerin (ACS/pulmonary edema), labetalol/esmolol (dissection), phentolamine (catecholamine excess); avoid β-blockers in pheo crisis and before the EF is known in acute LV failure.
  5. Markedly high BP with NO organ damage is "severe hypertension without acute target-organ damage" (2025 AHA/ACC) — oral meds and outpatient follow-up, not an ICU drip.
