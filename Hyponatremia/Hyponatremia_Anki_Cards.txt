What are the 3 labs (and 3 questions) for diagnosing hyponatremia?	Serum osm — "is it real?"; urine osm — "is ADH on or off?"; urine Na — "are the kidneys seeing enough fluid?"
Why not classify hyponatremia by volume status at the bedside?	Physician assessment of volume status is classically unreliable; the 3-lab approach interrogates the physiology (osmolality, ADH, renal perfusion) directly.
What defines TRUE hyponatremia?	Hypo-osmolar hyponatremia: serum osm < 290. Normal or high serum osm means something else is in the blood.
What is pseudohyponatremia?	Normal serum osm + low measured Na: excess lipids (hypertriglyceridemia) or protein (multiple myeloma) dilute whole plasma while water-plasma [Na] is normal. Confirm with a whole-blood Na.
What causes hyperosmolar hyponatremia, and how do you correct the Na?	An extra ACTIVE osmole (glucose, mannitol, sorbitol) pulls water out of cells, diluting Na. Corrected Na = measured Na + 1.6 × (glucose − 100)/100. (BUN and ethanol are INACTIVE osmoles — they raise measured osm but cross membranes and do not cause hyponatremia.)
What does a urine osm < 100 mean in hyponatremia?	ADH is OFF — the only causes are excess free-water intake (primary polydipsia) or inadequate solute (beer potomania, tea-and-toast diet).
What does a high urine osm (>100) mean in hyponatremia?	ADH is ON (appropriately or not) — the kidney is concentrating urine; next ask whether ADH secretion is appropriate using the urine Na.
What triggers ADH release physiologically?	Low volume/effective circulating volume (baroreceptors) and high serum osm — ADH makes the kidney reclaim free water, concentrating the urine.
ADH on + urine Na < 25: what does it mean?	RAAS is activated — the kidneys see low volume: true hypovolemia (GI losses) OR low effective circulating volume with high total body volume (heart failure, cirrhosis).
ADH on + urine Na > 40: what's the differential?	The kidneys see enough fluid → inappropriate ADH: SIADH, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency — or diuretic use (salt wasting falsely raises urine Na).
What is the exception that breaks the "low urine Na = low volume" rule?	Diuretics (loop, thiazide) — they waste salt, so urine Na stays high even in a hypovolemic patient.
Common SIADH triggers?	Medications (antidepressants, antiepileptics, antipsychotics), surgery/pain, brain injury, lung disease, malignancy. SIADH patients are euvolemic.
How does cerebral salt wasting differ from SIADH?	Both follow CNS insults (esp. SAH) with high urine Na, but cerebral salt wasting patients are HYPOVOLEMIC; SIADH patients are euvolemic.
How do hypothyroidism and adrenal insufficiency cause hyponatremia?	Both drive excess ADH secretion; the hyponatremia corrects when the hormone deficiency is replaced — always check TSH and cortisol in "SIADH-pattern" labs.
A patient with alcohol use disorder has Na 125, SOsm 270, UOsm 60, UNa 65. Diagnosis?	Beer potomania — true (hypo-osmolar) hyponatremia with ADH OFF (UOsm < 100): solute intake too low to excrete the ingested water.
A patient with DM1 has glucose 500 and Na 125 with SOsm 310. Interpretation?	Hyperosmolar hyponatremia from glucose: corrected Na = 125 + 1.6 × 4 = ~131 — a mild true hyponatremia (here from hypovolemia) plus glucose effect.
Has the 3-lab diagnostic approach changed in recent guidelines?	No — the 2024 Austrian Society for Nephrology consensus confirms serum osm, urine osm, and urine Na identify most causes of hyponatremia. (This lesson covers diagnosis only, not treatment.)
