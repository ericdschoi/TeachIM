Learning Objectives

  1. Learn the "3 lab approach" to the diagnosis of hyponatremia (this talk does not cover treatment).
  2. Understand the roles of ADH and aldosterone in hyponatremia.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient with multiple myeloma has Na 126 but a measured serum osmolality of 285. Is this true hyponatremia? No — true hyponatremia is hypo-osmolar (SOsm < 290). A normal SOsm with low measured Na is pseudohyponatremia: paraprotein (or lipid) dilutes whole plasma while the water-plasma [Na] is normal. Send a whole-blood Na.
Q2. Why does the urine osmolality tell you whether ADH is "on"? ADH's only job is to make the collecting duct reclaim free water, which concentrates the urine. Dilute urine (<100) means ADH is off; concentrated urine (>100) means ADH is acting — appropriately or not.
Q3. A hypovolemic patient on furosemide has a urine Na of 60. Does that rule out hypovolemia? No — diuretics force salt wasting, so the urine Na stays high despite RAAS activation. Diuretics are the one standard exception to "low urine Na = kidneys seeing low volume."

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected presenter board (Hyponatremia_Optimized.pptx) or redraw the schematic + timeline on a whiteboard; prepare with the learning board (Hyponatremia.pptx), which carries the extra pop-ups. Print the worksheet. ~20 min talk, +10 min with the three cases.

Hook (2 min)

"Three patients, all with a sodium of 125: a drinker in withdrawal, a diabetic with glucose of 500, and a woman with two days of vomiting and diarrhea. Same number — three completely different diseases. Three lab values will tell them apart, and none of them is your exam's guess at volume status." Acknowledge the elephant: we're all taught hyper/hypo/euvolemic — and we're all bad at assessing it.

Step 1 — Is it real? (Serum osm) 6 min

ASK: "Sodium is most of your serum osm. If the Na is 125, what SHOULD the serum osm be — and what does it mean if it isn't low?"
Serum osmInterpretationCauses
< 290True (hypo-osmolar) hyponatremia → go to Step 2—
NormalPseudohyponatremia (artifact)↑Triglycerides, ↑protein (multiple myeloma) dilute whole plasma; water-plasma [Na] normal → send whole-blood Na
> 290Hyperosmolar hyponatremia — "something extra"Active osmoles (glucose, mannitol, sorbitol, glycerin) pull water from cells and dilute Na. Inactive osmoles (BUN, ethanol) raise osm but cross membranes — no hyponatremia
Pearl: corrected Na = measured Na + 1.6 × (glucose − 100)/100. A glucose of 500 "hides" about 6.5 mEq/L of sodium.

Step 2 — Is ADH on or off? (Urine osm) 5 min

ASK: "What does ADH do to the urine, and who has hyponatremia with ADH OFF?"

ADH (pituitary; triggered by low volume via baroreceptors, or high serum osm) makes the kidney reclaim water → concentrated urine. Urine osm < 100 = ADH off: the kidney is doing its job, diluting maximally — the intake is the problem: primary polydipsia (water in > out) or low solute (beer potomania, tea-and-toast — not enough osmoles to excrete the water). Everything else is a high-ADH state → ask Step 3: is the ADH appropriate?

Step 3 — Are the kidneys seeing enough fluid? (Urine Na) 5 min

ASK: "Low volume — or low EFFECTIVE volume — does what to renin, aldosterone, and the urine sodium?"
Urine NaPhysiologyDifferential
< 25RAAS on — kidneys reclaim Na (appropriate ADH)True hypovolemia (GI losses, bleeding); low effective circulating volume with high total volume: heart failure, cirrhosis
> 40Kidneys see enough fluid — ADH is inappropriateSIADH (drugs — antidepressants/antiepileptics/antipsychotics, surgery/pain, brain injury, lung disease, malignancy); hypothyroidism; adrenal insufficiency; diuretics (the exception — salt wasting despite hypovolemia)
Pearl: cerebral salt wasting (CNS injury, esp. SAH) mimics SIADH's labs but the patient is hypovolemic — the one place a careful volume exam still earns its keep. And always check TSH + cortisol before calling SIADH: hormone replacement fixes those two impostors.

Close — retrieval (2 min)

Hide the board. Learners rebuild the three questions, the cutoffs (290 / 100 / 25–40), and one diagnosis per branch from memory. Finish: "Which lab would change first if I started this SIADH patient on furosemide?" (urine Na — the diuretic exception.)

Cases from the original board — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1

68M with alcohol use disorder presents in withdrawal. Na 125. Serum osm 270, urine osm 60, urine Na 65.

Serum osm?Low — true (hypo-osmolar) hyponatremia.
Urine osm?60 — dilute (<100): ADH is OFF. Only excess water intake or poor solute intake does this.
Urine Na?Not needed once ADH is off — the kidney is already maximally diluting.
Diagnosis?Beer potomania — alcohol calories without solute; the kidney runs out of osmoles to excrete the water load.

Case 2

21M with type 1 DM, nausea/vomiting and abdominal pain. Glucose 500, +ketones. Na 125. Serum osm 310, urine osm 800, urine Na 20.

Serum osm?310 — high: hyperosmolar hyponatremia. "Something else in the blood" — glucose is the active osmole. Corrected Na = 125 + 1.6 × (500−100)/100 ≈ 131.
Urine osm / urine Na?Urine osm 800 — ADH on. Urine Na 20 — low: kidneys see low volume (RAAS on).
Diagnosis?Hyperosmolar hyponatremia from glucose, plus a mild true hyponatremia (~131) from hypovolemia (osmotic diuresis + vomiting).

Case 3

40F with nausea/vomiting and diarrhea. Na 125. Serum osm 265, urine osm 400, urine Na < 10.

Serum osm?Low — true hyponatremia.
Urine osm?400 — ADH is ON (appropriate or not?).
Urine Na?<10 — kidneys are reclaiming sodium: low (effective) circulating volume.
Diagnosis?True hypovolemic hyponatremia from GI losses — ADH secretion is appropriate; fix the volume and the ADH switches off.

Board-style MCQs NEW — 5 ABIM-format questions

1. A 61-year-old woman with multiple myeloma has Na 124 on routine labs. She feels well. Serum osmolality is 287. What is the next best step?

  1. Fluid restriction for SIADH
  2. Send a whole-blood (direct ISE) sodium
  3. 3% saline bolus
  4. Urine osmolality and urine sodium
  5. Desmopressin clamp
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Normal serum osm + low measured Na = pseudohyponatremia from paraprotein; a direct-ISE/whole-blood Na bypasses the dilution artifact. A/D: workup of true hyponatremia starts only after confirming hypo-osmolality. C: she has no true hyponatremia and no symptoms. E: desmopressin is part of overcorrection management, irrelevant here.

2. A 45-year-old marathon runner presents with Na 122, serum osm 252, urine osm 70, urine Na 45. Which mechanism explains the hyponatremia?

  1. Inappropriate ADH secretion
  2. Water intake exceeding renal free-water excretion with ADH appropriately suppressed
  3. RAAS activation from hypovolemia
  4. Pseudohyponatremia
  5. Cerebral salt wasting
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Urine osm 70 (<100) = ADH OFF — the kidney is maximally diluting but intake outran excretion (water intoxication/polydipsia physiology). A: SIADH requires concentrated urine (ADH on). C: RAAS activation gives LOW urine Na and ADH would be on. D: serum osm is low — this is true hyponatremia. E: CSW is a high-ADH, hypovolemic state with concentrated urine.

3. A 70-year-old man with small-cell lung cancer: Na 121, serum osm 250, urine osm 600, urine Na 78, euvolemic on exam, normal TSH and cortisol. Diagnosis?

  1. SIADH
  2. True hypovolemic hyponatremia
  3. Beer potomania
  4. Heart failure
  5. Hyperosmolar hyponatremia
Answer + why each distractor is wrongA. True hyponatremia + ADH on (urine osm 600) + kidneys seeing enough fluid (urine Na 78) + euvolemia + impostors excluded (TSH/cortisol) = SIADH — classic with SCLC. B/D: both produce LOW urine Na via RAAS. C: requires dilute urine (ADH off). E: serum osm is low, not high.

4. A cirrhotic patient has Na 124, serum osm 262, urine osm 450, urine Na 8. Which best describes the physiology?

  1. ADH is inappropriately secreted; the kidneys see adequate volume
  2. Total body volume is high, but low effective circulating volume activates RAAS and (appropriately) ADH
  3. ADH is suppressed by chronic liver disease
  4. Renal salt wasting from cirrhotic nephropathy
  5. The labs indicate pseudohyponatremia from low albumin
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Cirrhosis = vasodilation + third-spacing → low EFFECTIVE volume despite total-body overload: RAAS on (urine Na 8), ADH on (urine osm 450) — the same lab pattern as true hypovolemia. A: urine Na 8 says the kidneys see LOW volume. C: urine osm 450 proves ADH is on. D: salt wasting would raise urine Na. E: hypoalbuminemia does not cause pseudohyponatremia (lipids/paraproteins do), and the serum osm is genuinely low.

5. A 58-year-old on hydrochlorothiazide presents dizzy with dry mucous membranes: Na 123, serum osm 255, urine osm 500, urine Na 52. The intern concludes "high urine sodium rules out hypovolemia — this is SIADH." What's the flaw?

  1. No flaw — this is SIADH
  2. Diuretics cause salt wasting, so urine Na stays high despite hypovolemia and RAAS activation
  3. Urine osm must be >800 to diagnose SIADH
  4. SIADH cannot occur on thiazides
  5. The serum osm excludes true hyponatremia
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Diuretics are the standard exception: they force natriuresis, making urine Na uninterpretable for volume. With an exam suggesting hypovolemia, thiazide-associated hypovolemic hyponatremia is the lead diagnosis. A: ignores the diuretic exception and the exam. C: any urine osm >100 indicates ADH activity; no 800 threshold. D: thiazides are a classic hyponatremia culprit (and can coexist with SIADH-like physiology) — but the exam points to hypovolemia. E: 255 is low — true hyponatremia.

Take-Home Points

  1. Skip the bedside volume guess — run the 3 labs in order: serum osm, urine osm, urine Na.
  2. True hyponatremia is hypo-osmolar (<290). Normal osm = pseudohyponatremia (whole-blood Na); high osm = active osmole (corrected Na = +1.6 per 100 of glucose).
  3. Urine osm < 100 = ADH off — only polydipsia, beer potomania, or tea-and-toast.
  4. ADH on + urine Na < 25 = kidneys see low volume (hypovolemia, HF, cirrhosis); urine Na > 40 = inappropriate ADH (SIADH, hypothyroid, adrenal insufficiency) — or diuretics breaking the rule.
  5. This is a diagnosis framework; treatment (rates, 3% saline, ODS risk) is a separate talk.
