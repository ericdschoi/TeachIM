Learning Objectives

  1. Build a visual model for how the immune system recognizes — and how tumors evade — destruction by T cells.
  2. Name the major classes of immune checkpoint inhibitors and the cancers in which they are most useful.
  3. Recognize the most common and the most dangerous immune-related adverse events (irAEs) and the first steps in managing them.
  4. Use the NCCN/ASCO irAE guidelines to grade severity and initiate treatment.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. In one sentence, what does an immune checkpoint normally do, and how does a tumor exploit it? An immune checkpoint (e.g., PD-L1→PD-1, ligands→CTLA-4) is a normal "brake" that prevents the immune system from attacking healthy tissue (autoimmunity). Tumors hijack this by expressing checkpoint ligands so the T cell receives the "stand down" signal and fails to kill the cancer cell.
Q2. Why are melanoma, lung, and bladder cancers especially responsive to ICIs? They carry a high mutational burden (UV light, tobacco), so they display many mutated "non-self" peptides on MHC — more targets for T cells to recognize once the brakes are off.
Q3. A patient on pembrolizumab develops diarrhea. What is the first thing you must do before blaming the drug? Rule out other causes — especially infection (stool studies including C. diff). irAE management always starts with excluding mimics, then stopping the ICI, grading severity, and starting steroids.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the interactive board (Immune_Checkpoint_Inhibitors_Optimized.pptx) and click the cursor icons in order (Immune basics → How tumors hide → Checkpoints → ICIs → When to use → Side effects → Cases), or rebuild the T-cell/tumor-cell cartoon on a whiteboard. Each cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt. Core ~10–15 min; +cases ~20–25 min; splits into two talks. Print the learner handout.

Hook (2 min) — "Why do we care?"

Open with the stakes: ~40% of cancer patients now receive an ICI, you will see their complications on the wards and in the ICU, and those complications can appear at any point — even months after the drug is stopped. "If you remember one thing today: a new autoimmune problem in a cancer patient is an ICI side effect until proven otherwise."

Objective 1 — How the immune system sees a tumor, and how tumors hide 5 min

ASK: "How does your adaptive immune system recognize an infected or cancerous cell?" Reveal: every cell displays peptide fragments on MHC; a T cell's TCR reads the MHC–peptide. Cancer cells carry mutated proteins → "non-self" peptides → the T cell sees them and kills via toxic granules.
ASK: "So how does a tumor escape an immune system that's constantly surveilling it?" Reveal the two checkpoint axes: tumor PD-L1 binds T-cell PD-1 → T-cell "exhaustion"; tumor ligands bind CTLA-4 → same effect. Both "put the brakes" on the T cell.
Pearl: Checkpoints are a normal, borrowed mechanism — PD-L1 and CTLA-4 ligands on healthy tissue prevent autoimmunity. Tumors merely hijack a system your body already uses to protect itself.

Objective 2 — The drugs, and when to use them 6 min

ASK: "What would happen if you blocked these checkpoints?" Reveal: ICIs are monoclonal antibodies that release the brakes so T cells stay active. Build the class table:
TargetClassExample agents
PD-1Anti-PD-1nivolumab, pembrolizumab, cemiplimab, dostarlimab
PD-L1Anti-PD-L1atezolizumab, durvalumab, avelumab
CTLA-4Anti-CTLA-4ipilimumab, tremelimumab
LAG-3Anti-LAG-3 2022relatlimab (with nivolumab = Opdualag)
ASK: "Which cancers respond best, and why?" Reveal the mutational-burden dot plot: high-mutation cancers (melanoma, lung, bladder) display more neoantigens → more immunogenic → better ICI response. FDA approvals now span melanoma, NSCLC/SCLC, RCC, HCC, urothelial, head & neck, Merkel cell, MSI-high/dMMR tumors, triple-negative breast, classical Hodgkin lymphoma, and more.

Objective 3 & 4 — Side effects and how to manage them 7 min

ASK: "Given the mechanism, what kind of side effects would you predict?" Reveal: autoimmune toxicity in essentially any organ (irAEs). Most common: GI/colitis, hepatitis, pneumonitis, dermatitis, thyroid, hypophysitis.
CategoryExamplesNote
Most commonColitis/diarrhea, hepatitis, pneumonitis, rash, thyroiditisUsually reversible with steroids
Often irreversibleMyocarditis, type 1 diabetes, primary adrenal insufficiencyHormone replacement / lifelong
Most deadlyMyocarditis (~40–50% fatal, rare); pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitisLow threshold to escalate
Pearl: Toxicity frequency rises with the agent and combination — severe irAEs ~16% with anti-PD-1, ~27% with anti-CTLA-4, up to ~55% with combination therapy. Most develop ~8–12 weeks in but can appear any time, including after stopping.
ASK: "Walk me through the five-step framework for any suspected irAE." Reveal:
StepAction
1Rule out other causes (infection, meds) — e.g., stool studies for diarrhea, cultures for cough/hypoxia
2Stop the checkpoint inhibitor
3Grade severity using the NCCN/ASCO guidelines (G1–G4)
4Start prednisone 1–2 mg/kg/day (G2+) to suppress the over-active immune response
5If not improving in a few days, add a second agent (e.g., infliximab for colitis/pneumonitis, vedolizumab, IVIG, mycophenolate)
Grade-based rule (ASCO/NCCN): G1 → continue ICI + monitor (exceptions: neuro/some heme). G2 → hold ICI, steroids, resume when ≤G1. G3 → hold + high-dose steroid, taper ≥4–6 wk, add 2nd agent if refractory. G4 → permanently discontinue (exception: endocrinopathies controlled on replacement). Check TSH/FT4 at baseline and q4–6 wk.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Have the group rebuild: the two checkpoint axes (PD-1/PD-L1, CTLA-4) + the newer LAG-3; why high-mutation cancers respond; the three irreversible irAEs; and the five-step management framework — then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases 10 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Diarrhea on combination therapy

46-year-old woman with metastatic melanoma on nivolumab (anti-PD-1) + ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4) presents with new diarrhea, 5–6 bowel movements per day, not interfering with her ADLs.

What initial work-up?Rule out other causes first: stool PCR (C. diff and other infectious causes), and stool lactoferrin/calprotectin. Don't assume irAE colitis until infection is excluded.
The infectious work-up is negative. How do you grade it?By NCCN/CTCAE criteria this is grade 2 colitis (increase of 4–6 stools/day over baseline, not limiting ADLs).
Treatment?Hold nivolumab and ipilimumab; start prednisone 1–2 mg/kg/day. If no response in 2–3 days, continue steroids and add infliximab or vedolizumab within ~2 weeks.

Case 2 — Cough and hypoxia on pembrolizumab

68-year-old man with stage IV NSCLC on single-agent pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1). On cycle 5 he develops worsening cough and exertional dyspnea; in clinic SpO₂ is 88% on room air with bilateral coarse crackles.

Work-up?Evaluate for infection (nasal viral swab, sputum culture) and image with CT chest with contrast; consider a pulmonary consult. The classic ICI pneumonitis pattern is multifocal peripheral and subpleural mid/lower-lung airspace consolidations.
Infectious work-up is negative. Treatment?Hold pembrolizumab; start prednisone 1–2 mg/kg/day; add empiric antibiotics if infection isn't fully excluded. If no response in 48 hours, consider infliximab, IVIG, or mycophenolate.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 60-year-old man with metastatic melanoma on ipilimumab develops 6 watery stools/day for 3 days, not limiting his activities. Stool studies including C. difficile are negative. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Continue ipilimumab and start loperamide alone
  2. Hold ipilimumab and start prednisone 1–2 mg/kg/day
  3. Permanently discontinue ipilimumab and start infliximab now
  4. Continue ipilimumab and add empiric oral vancomycin
Answer & explanationB. This is grade 2 immune-related colitis after excluding infection: hold the ICI and start prednisone 1–2 mg/kg/day. Symptomatic loperamide alone (A) undertreats an autoimmune process. Infliximab (C) is reserved for steroid-refractory disease (no improvement in 2–3 days), not first-line. Vancomycin (D) treats C. diff, which was already excluded.

2. Which immune checkpoint inhibitor mechanism was the first new target class approved after PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4?

  1. LAG-3 inhibition (relatlimab)
  2. VEGF inhibition (bevacizumab)
  3. CD20 depletion (rituximab)
  4. BTK inhibition (ibrutinib)
Answer & explanationA. Relatlimab, an anti-LAG-3 antibody, is the third checkpoint class in clinical use (after PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4); the fixed-dose nivolumab+relatlimab combination (Opdualag) was FDA-approved in March 2022 for unresectable/metastatic melanoma. Bevacizumab (B) is an anti-angiogenic, not a checkpoint inhibitor. Rituximab (C) and ibrutinib (D) are not checkpoint inhibitors.

3. A 55-year-old woman on pembrolizumab presents with fatigue, nausea, and hypotension. Labs show Na 128, K 5.4, and a low 8 AM cortisol. Which statement is correct?

  1. This toxicity is typically reversible after a short steroid course
  2. Pembrolizumab can be safely continued without intervention
  3. This is a potentially irreversible endocrinopathy requiring hormone replacement, and ICI may be continued once she is stabilized on replacement
  4. High-dose immunosuppression with infliximab is first-line
Answer & explanationC. This is ICI-induced primary adrenal insufficiency (or hypophysitis) — an endocrinopathy that is often permanent and managed with physiologic hormone replacement (stress-dose steroids in crisis). Per ASCO/NCCN, ICIs may be continued (or resumed) once the endocrinopathy is controlled on replacement — endocrinopathies are the key exception to the "permanently discontinue at grade 3–4" rule. It is not reversible with a short taper (A), should not be ignored (B), and infliximab (D) has no role here.

4. A 70-year-old man on nivolumab/ipilimumab develops chest pain, a troponin rise, and new heart block 5 weeks into therapy. Which irAE is most concerning, and what is the immediate step?

  1. Pericarditis; start NSAIDs and continue ICI
  2. Myocarditis; stop the ICI and start high-dose corticosteroids urgently
  3. Stable angina; refer to outpatient cardiology
  4. Thyroiditis; check TSH and observe
Answer & explanationB. ICI myocarditis is rare but the most lethal irAE (~40–50% mortality) and frequently presents with conduction disease and troponin elevation. Permanently discontinue the ICI and start high-dose corticosteroids immediately (with cardiology/ICU involvement); it is one of the irreversible/high-mortality toxicities. NSAIDs (A) are inadequate and risky; this is not stable angina (C) or thyroiditis (D).

5. A 64-year-old man with NSCLC on pembrolizumab has grade 1 maculopapular rash over <10% of his body surface, no systemic symptoms. What is the recommended management?

  1. Permanently discontinue pembrolizumab
  2. Hold pembrolizumab and start systemic prednisone 1–2 mg/kg
  3. Continue pembrolizumab with topical steroids/emollients and monitor
  4. Start infliximab
Answer & explanationC. For grade 1 toxicities (with the exception of some neurologic/hematologic events), the ICI can be continued with close monitoring; a limited rash is managed with topical steroids, emollients, and antihistamines. Systemic steroids (B) and discontinuation (A) are reserved for higher grades; infliximab (D) is for steroid-refractory severe irAEs, not grade 1 rash.

Take-Home Points

  1. Tumors evade T cells by engaging immune checkpoints (PD-L1→PD-1, ligands→CTLA-4) that put the brakes on the immune response — a normal anti-autoimmunity mechanism the tumor hijacks.
  2. ICIs — anti-PD-1/PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4, and now anti-LAG-3 — release those brakes; they work best in high-mutation-burden cancers (melanoma, lung, bladder).
  3. irAEs are autoimmune, can hit any organ, and may appear during or months after therapy — keep ICIs on the differential for any new autoimmune problem.
  4. Management framework: rule out other causes → stop the ICI → grade severity → prednisone 1–2 mg/kg → add a second agent if no improvement in a few days. Myocarditis, type 1 DM, and adrenal insufficiency are the irreversible ones.
  5. The NCCN/ASCO irAE guidelines are the go-to bedside resource for grading and treatment.
