Learning Objectives

  1. Use acuity (acute vs chronic) and number of involved joints (mono-, oligo-, polyarticular) to generate a focused differential for inflammatory arthritis.
  2. Determine when synovial fluid analysis, advanced microbiologic testing (Lyme PCR, GC cultures), or imaging (chondrocalcinosis, marginal erosions, SI-joint changes) is indicated to clarify the etiology.
  3. Select initial management for common arthritides and recognize red-flag presentations requiring urgent antimicrobials, rheumatology involvement, or surgical evaluation.

Pre-work send the day before

1. A patient has one hot, swollen knee that started yesterday. What diagnosis must you exclude first, and what test does it?

Septic arthritis — presume it until proven otherwise. Arthrocentesis with cell count + differential, Gram stain, culture, and crystal exam. Fluid is typically >50K WBC with PMN predominance (lower thresholds for prosthetic joints or partially treated infection).

2. Name the two crystal arthropathies and the crystal seen in each.

Gout — monosodium urate (needle-shaped, negatively birefringent). CPPD — calcium pyrophosphate (rhomboid, weakly positively birefringent); x-rays may show chondrocalcinosis.

3. Which chronic polyarthritis spares the DIP joints, and which one targets them?

RA spares the DIPs (and thoracolumbar spine); psoriatic arthritis can involve the DIPs — look for nail pitting, onycholysis, dactylitis, and enthesitis.

Teaching Script ~45 min, runs off the interactive board

Hook 2 min

"You're called about a 58-year-old with a hot, swollen knee. The night intern wants to start steroids for a 'gout flare.' What single test changes everything you do next?" Let them argue, then frame the talk: every inflammatory joint complaint can be placed on a 2-axis grid — time (acute vs chronic) and space (1 joint, 2–4 joints, 5+ joints) — and that grid, plus synovial fluid, drives the whole work-up.

Segment 1 — The time-and-space grid 8 min

ASK — "Before any labs: what two questions about the arthritis itself most narrow the differential?" (Answer: how fast, and how many joints.)

Build the board's grid: acute mono → septic, gout, CPPD; acute oligo/poly → GC, reactive, viral, sarcoid (Löfgren's); chronic mono/oligo → fungal/TB, Lyme; chronic poly → RA, SLE, psoriatic, ankylosing spondylitis, tophaceous gout.

Synovial fluid WBCThink
<2KNon-inflammatory (OA, internal derangement)
2–20KInflammatory — autoimmune, crystal, Lyme (>2K), indolent infection (fungal/TB 10–20K)

>50K, PMN-predominantSeptic until proven otherwise (lower cutoff if prosthetic joint or prior antibiotics)
Pearl: crystals in the fluid do NOT exclude infection — septic arthritis and gout coexist. If the story says infection, culture and cover even when you see crystals.

Segment 2 — Acute monoarthritis: the can't-miss three 10 min

ASK — "Gram stain shows gram-positive cocci in clusters. What's your empiric drug? What if it showed gram-negative rods?" (Vancomycin; ceftriaxone.)

Septic: hot, swollen, painful joint with limited passive ROM; fluid usually >50K PMN-predominant. Empiric therapy by Gram stain (vancomycin for GPC, ceftriaxone for GN); most need urgent surgical washout.

Acute gout: MSU crystals under polarized light; first MTP most common, lower-extremity predominant. Risk factors: obesity, HTN, CKD. Treat flares with NSAIDs, colchicine, or steroids. Start ULT (allopurinol first-line) for ≥2 flares/yr — or after one flare if CKD ≥3, urate >9, urolithiasis, erosions, or tophi — goal urate <6, and keep prophylaxis running while titrating (ACR 2020).

CPPD: CPP crystals; chondrocalcinosis on x-ray (knees, wrists, fingers). Phenotypes range from acute pseudogout to chronic RA-like arthritis to asymptomatic chondrocalcinosis. Screen for hyperparathyroidism and hemochromatosis. Flares treated like gout flares.

ASK — "Your gout patient starts allopurinol and flares two weeks later. Did the allopurinol fail?" (No — mobilization flare; this is why prophylaxis continues during titration.)

Segment 3 — Acute oligo/polyarthritis 8 min

Gonococcal: young, sexually active; migratory polyarthralgia + tenosynovitis + pustular dermatitis; fluid 10–50K; isolate N. gonorrhoeae from joint, blood, or mucosal sites. Ceftriaxone first-line; cover chlamydia if not excluded (doxycycline preferred per CDC 2021). Excellent prognosis treated.

Reactive: 1–6 weeks after GI/GU infection; asymmetric lower-extremity oligoarthritis; HLA-B27; uveitis, urethritis, keratoderma blennorrhagicum; synovial cultures negative.

Viral: parvovirus B19 (most common — RA-like small-joint polyarthritis, +IgM), then HBV/HCV; travel → dengue, chikungunya, Zika. Supportive NSAIDs. Transient autoantibodies (ANA, RF) are common — don't anchor on serologies in acute arthritis.

Sarcoid: ankles/knees/wrists; Löfgren's = erythema nodosum + bilateral hilar adenopathy + acute arthritis; NSAIDs, then sarcoid DMARDs (MTX, TNFi) if needed.

Segment 4 — Chronic arthritis 12 min

ASK — "A chronic knee monoarthritis with 15K WBC and negative cultures — what two indolent infections are still on the table, and what tests find them?" (Fungal/TB → synovial biopsy; Lyme → Borrelia PCR.)

Fungal/TB: indolent; immunosuppression or endemic travel; fluid 10–20K, may be culture-negative → synovial biopsy; surgical evaluation + ID consult.

Lyme: months after tick exposure; large joint (knee); fluid >2K; Borrelia PCR; 4-week doxycycline course (IDSA 2020); persistent post-infectious arthritis may need DMARDs.

Spondyloarthropathies (HLA-B27 family): ankylosing spondylitis (SI joints > rest of spine; marginal erosions, syndesmophytes, ankylosis; TNFi for axial disease, oral DMARDs like sulfasalazine for peripheral), psoriatic (asymmetric, DIPs, enthesitis, dactylitis, nail pitting/onycholysis — skin severity does NOT track joint severity; screen for uveitis and IBD), reactive (usually acute), and IBD-associated.

SLE: symmetric small-joint polyarthritis; ROS for photosensitive rash, oral ulcers, alopecia, Raynaud's, serositis; soft-tissue swelling > erosions; NSAIDs/low-dose prednisone acutely; HCQ, MTX, or azathioprine long-term.

RA: symmetric wrists/MCPs/PIPs/ankles/MTPs, spares DIPs and the spine; RF, CCP, ESR, CRP; periarticular osteopenia and marginal erosions; MTX is first-line DMARD; baseline and q1–2-yr hand/foot films.

Tophaceous gout: chronic urate deposition punctuated by acute flares; favors cool sites (fingertips, toes, Achilles, elbows); punched-out erosions with overhanging edges; ULT to urate <6 (<5 to shrink tophi) plus prophylaxis while titrating.

ASK — "Symmetric small-joint polyarthritis with DIP involvement and pitted nails — RA or something else?" (PsA — RA spares DIPs.)

Retrieval close 5 min

Erase the board. Have learners rebuild the time-and-space grid from memory, then state: the synovial fluid threshold for septic arthritis, the ULT start rules and urate goal, and three features that separate PsA from RA. Finish with the take-home points below.

Board-Style MCQs ABIM format

1. A 61-year-old man with diabetes presents with 24 hours of a hot, swollen right knee and fever. Arthrocentesis: 68,000 WBC/µL, 93% PMNs; Gram stain shows gram-positive cocci in clusters; intracellular needle-shaped negatively birefringent crystals are also seen. Which is the best next step?

  1. Oral colchicine and follow-up in 1 week
  2. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection
  3. IV vancomycin and urgent orthopedic consultation for washout
  4. Await synovial culture before treating
  5. Start allopurinol
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

C. Crystals do not exclude infection: with >50K PMN-predominant fluid, fever, and organisms on Gram stain, this is septic arthritis (with coexistent gout) — treat empirically by Gram stain (GPC → vancomycin) plus urgent washout. A and B treat the gout while the joint is being destroyed and can worsen infection; D delays therapy in a closed-space emergency; E — ULT is never started for the acute event alone and does nothing for infection.

2. A 24-year-old sexually active woman has 4 days of migratory wrist and ankle pain, painful tendon sheaths of the dorsal hand, and scattered pustular lesions on the extremities. Knee fluid: 32,000 WBC/µL, Gram stain negative. Which treatment is most appropriate?

  1. Ceftriaxone, plus doxycycline if chlamydia is not excluded
  2. Vancomycin alone
  3. Oral doxycycline for 4 weeks
  4. NSAIDs and observation
  5. Prednisone taper
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

A. Migratory polyarthralgia + tenosynovitis + pustular dermatitis = disseminated gonococcal infection; fluid is typically 10–50K and Gram stain is often negative — treat with ceftriaxone and cover chlamydia co-infection (doxycycline preferred, CDC 2021). B covers gram-positives, not N. gonorrhoeae; C is Lyme dosing; D and E miss a treatable bacteremic infection.

3. A 55-year-old man with CKD stage 3 has his third gout flare this year. Serum urate is 8.9 mg/dL. After the flare is treated, which long-term plan is most appropriate (ACR 2020)?

  1. No urate-lowering therapy; treat flares as they come
  2. Start febuxostat at high dose
  3. Start low-dose allopurinol, titrate to urate <6 mg/dL, with colchicine prophylaxis during titration
  4. Start allopurinol only if tophi develop
  5. Strict low-purine diet alone
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

C. ≥2 flares/year is a strong indication for ULT; allopurinol is first-line (including in CKD, started low and titrated), target urate <6, and prophylaxis continues while titrating to prevent mobilization flares. A ignores a strong ULT indication; B — febuxostat is second-line and high starting doses are not recommended; D waits for irreversible damage; E — diet alone rarely reaches target.

4. A 68-year-old woman has recurrent acute knee swelling. Radiographs show cartilage calcification in both knees and the wrist triangular fibrocartilage. Aspirate shows rhomboid, weakly positively birefringent crystals. Beyond treating the flare, which evaluation is most appropriate?

  1. Serum calcium/PTH and iron studies
  2. HLA-B27 testing
  3. Borrelia PCR of synovial fluid
  4. Anti-CCP antibodies
  5. Skin biopsy
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

A. Chondrocalcinosis + CPP crystals = CPPD; screen for the associated metabolic diseases — hyperparathyroidism (Ca/PTH) and hemochromatosis (iron studies). B is for spondyloarthropathy; C is for Lyme (chronic large-joint mono/oligoarthritis after tick exposure); D is for RA; E has no role.

5. A 39-year-old Connecticut hiker has 3 months of a swollen, mildly painful left knee. Fluid: 24,000 WBC/µL, cultures negative. He recalls a febrile illness with a spreading rash last summer, untreated. Which is the best diagnostic and therapeutic approach?

  1. Synovial fluid Borrelia PCR; if supportive, doxycycline for 4 weeks
  2. Empiric vancomycin and washout
  3. Intra-articular steroids now
  4. Start methotrexate
  5. Serum ANA panel
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

A. Chronic large-joint monoarthritis months after tick exposure in an endemic area = Lyme arthritis; fluid is inflammatory (>2K, often 10–25K), PCR supports the diagnosis, and a 4-week doxycycline course is standard (IDSA 2020). B — the indolent course and fluid count don't fit pyogenic infection; C before antibiotics can worsen outcomes; D is reserved for post-infectious arthritis after antibiotics fail; E doesn't address the leading diagnosis.

Currency Notes checked June 2026

The original is recent (Dec 2025) and its frameworks are fully current — no update slide was needed on the deck. Verified against:

  • ACR 2020 gout guideline — still the current ACR guidance (allopurinol first-line incl. CKD, start low and titrate, treat-to-target <6, prophylaxis during titration, ULT indications as taught). No newer ACR gout guideline has superseded it as of June 2026.
  • CDC 2021 STI Treatment Guidelines — ceftriaxone remains first-line for gonococcal infection; for presumptive chlamydia co-treatment, doxycycline is now preferred over azithromycin (azithromycin mainly in pregnancy). The deck's "doxycycline or azithromycin" remains acceptable wording; preference noted here.
  • IDSA/AAN/ACR 2020 Lyme guideline — 28-day oral doxycycline for Lyme arthritis, as taught.
  • IDSA/ASM 2024 microbiology utilization guide (cited by the original) — synovial fluid testing recommendations unchanged.

Take-Home Points

  1. Start with time and space — acuity and joint count are the fastest way to narrow the differential.
  2. Never miss infection — acute monoarthritis is septic until proven otherwise; synovial fluid analysis and timely antibiotics/surgical consultation are critical, and crystals don't exclude infection.
  3. Pattern recognition matters — symmetry, axial involvement, DIP disease, enthesitis, dactylitis, and extra-articular clues (skin, eye, GI, GU) separate RA, SLE, PsA, and the spondyloarthropathies.
  4. Match treatment to etiology and trajectory — flares get anti-inflammatories; chronic autoimmune and indolent infectious arthritis need early disease-modifying therapy and specialty involvement.
