Learning Objectives

  1. Develop a broad differential for acute dyspnea.
  2. Construct a framework for evaluating acute dyspnea.
  3. Identify the causes of acute hypoxemia and how to differentiate them.

Pre-work send the day before

1. List the four system categories of acute dyspnea.

Respiratory (airways / alveoli / vessels), cardiac, musculoskeletal, and the "3 A's" — anemia, anxiety, acidosis. Respiratory and cardiac are most common.

2. Write the A-a gradient and the expected-value formulas.

A-a = PAO₂ − PaO₂, where PAO₂ = FiO₂ × (Patm − PH₂O) − PaCO₂/0.8 (≈ 150 − 1.25 × PaCO₂ on room air). Expected A-a = age/4 + 4 (a 64-year-old expects ~20).

3. Which cause of hypoxemia fails to correct with 100% oxygen?

Shunt — perfusion without ventilation (anatomic shunt or a large alveolar filling/collapse process). V/Q mismatch, by contrast, improves with supplemental O₂.

Teaching Script

Two interactive boards drive the visuals: the Teaching board (less text, fewer pop-ups) for presenting and the Learning board for self-study/prep. This script adds a hook, ASK pauses, pearls, and a retrieval close. Board navigation: Causes → Evaluation → Hypoxemia → Cases.

Hook 2 min

"Your patient just desatted to 88%. Before you reflexively turn up the oxygen, you need two things: a differential built by anatomy, and one number — the A-a gradient — that splits the causes of hypoxemia in half." Project the Causes board.

Objective 1 — Differential for acute dyspnea 5 min Objective 1

ASK — "Walk the airway from mouth to alveolus — what blocks or narrows each level?"
CategoryExamples
Respiratory — airwaysAnaphylaxis/angioedema, mucus plugging, airway stenosis, bronchoconstriction (COPD, asthma)
Respiratory — alveoliFilling: DAH (blood), pneumonia (pus), edema (water). Collapse: atelectasis. Pleural process (blood/pus/water/air)
Respiratory — vesselsPulmonary embolism
CardiacMI, arrhythmia, valvular disease, pericardial disease
MusculoskeletalDiaphragm dysfunction, abdominal pressure
The "3 A's"Anemia, anxiety, acidosis (metabolic and respiratory)
Pearl. "Blockage or narrowing of any part of the respiratory system causes dyspnea." Building the list anatomically (airway → alveolus → vessel) makes it reproducible at the bedside.

Objective 2 — Evaluation toolbox 5 min Objective 2

ASK — "For each category, what first-line test confirms it?"

Map tests to pathology: physical exam everywhere; CXR for alveolar/pleural processes; CT-PE for vascular; ECG + exam for cardiac; CBC for anemia; BMP + blood gas for acidosis. The four high-yield "toolbox" tests: CXR, blood gas, CBC, ECG — now joined by bedside POCUS.

Pearl — ABG vs VBG. Get an ABG when you need the PaO₂ — to confirm/grade hypoxemia or compute the A-a gradient. Otherwise a VBG suffices: ABG pH ≈ VBG pH + 0.04; ABG PaCO₂ ≈ VBG PvCO₂ − 4.

Objective 3 — Causes of hypoxemia 6 min Objective 3

ASK — "Dyspnea vs hypoxemia — are they the same?" Reveal: no. Hypoxemia = low PaO₂ (normal 75–100); many dyspneic patients are not hypoxemic.
CauseA-a gradientNotes
Low FiO₂NormalAltitude; not an acute inpatient cause
HypoventilationNormal↑ PaCO₂ displaces O₂; CNS depression, opioids/benzos, neuromuscular weakness
V/Q mismatchElevatedMost common; obstructive disease, vascular disease, PE, small filling/collapse
ShuntElevatedPerfusion without ventilation; doesn't correct with 100% O₂
Low diffusion capacityElevatedChronic (ILD, emphysema); not acute
Pearl — the A-a split. First decide normal vs elevated A-a. Normal → hypoventilation (check the PaCO₂). Elevated → V/Q mismatch vs shunt; the 100%-O₂ challenge separates them (shunt won't budge). Expected A-a rises with age (age/4 + 4).

Retrieval close 2 min

Board off. Ask a learner to rebuild the 5 causes sorted by A-a, and to compute the expected A-a for a 60-year-old. Ask a second learner: when do you reach for an ABG instead of a VBG?

Cases click to reveal

Case 1 — 25F, type 1 diabetes

1 day of dyspnea. T 36, HR 110, BP 125/70, RR 32, SaO₂ 99% on RA. Tachypneic without increased work of breathing, clear lungs.

Work-up & diagnosis?

CXR clear; BMP Na 128, K 4.5, HCO₃ 8, BUN 32, Cr 2.2; WBC 12.5k, Hgb 10.2; VBG 7.1/29/–/9 (no hypoxemia concern, so a VBG suffices). Diagnosis: dyspnea/tachypnea from respiratory compensation of severe metabolic acidosis — DKA. (A "3 A's" cause: acidosis, normal oxygenation.)

Case 2 — 84M, HLD/CAD

1 day of dyspnea. T 38, HR 110, BP 125/70, RR 24, SaO₂ 92% on RA. Crackles in the R lower lung fields.

Work-up & diagnosis?

Focal lung findings → CXR (focal abnormality). ABG 7.48/32/65/22 (hypoxemic). Diagnosis: dyspnea and hypoxemia from RLL pneumonia.

Case 3 — 74F, non-Hodgkin lymphoma

1 day of dyspnea. T 36, HR 110, BP 98/60, RR 24, SaO₂ 94% on RA. Elevated JVP, clear lungs.

Work-up & diagnosis?

CXR shows an enlarged cardiac silhouette; ECG shows electrical alternans. With hypotension, elevated JVP, and a pericardial effusion → cardiac tamponade. (See changelog: the board's CXR line says "cardiomegaly or pleural effusion" — likely intended as "pericardial effusion"; flagged for author review.)

Case 4 — 64M, POD after R nephrectomy

Part 1 — POD1: SOB + chest/abdominal pain, BP 90/60, RR 28, SaO₂ 96%, distended/tender abdomen.

BMP/CXR/ECG largely unremarkable; ABG normal without hypoxemia; CBC shows a severe drop in hematocrit. Diagnosis: dyspnea from severe anemia (post-op bleeding) — a "3 A's" cause with normal PaO₂.

Part 2 — POD5: acute SOB + chest pain, HR 140, BP 90/60, RR 28, SaO₂ 82%, clear lungs.

BMP/CBC normal, CXR clear, ECG sinus tachycardia; ABG shows hypoxemia with an elevated A-a gradient. Worked example: PAO₂ = 150 − 1.25×32 = 110; A-a = 110 − 50 = 60; expected = 64/4 + 4 = 20 → elevated. With a normal CXR, the elevated-A-a cause is V/Q mismatch → likely pulmonary embolism.

Board-Style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 64-year-old has PaO₂ 50, PaCO₂ 32 on room air. PAO₂ ≈ 110. What is the A-a gradient and is it elevated for age?

  1. A-a 60; normal for age
  2. A-a 60; elevated (expected ≈ 20)
  3. A-a 20; normal
  4. A-a 16; elevated
  5. Cannot calculate without the FiO₂
Answer + explanation

B. A-a = PAO₂ − PaO₂ = 110 − 50 = 60. Expected = age/4 + 4 = 64/4 + 4 = 20. 60 ≫ 20 → elevated. D uses the miscalculated "16" (the original deck error — 64/4 + 4 is 20, not 16). E is wrong: room air FiO₂ 0.21 is assumed in PAO₂ ≈ 150 − 1.25×PaCO₂.

2. A patient is hypoxemic with a normal A-a gradient and PaCO₂ of 70. The most likely mechanism is:

  1. Shunt
  2. V/Q mismatch
  3. Hypoventilation
  4. Low diffusion capacity
  5. Anemia
Answer + explanation

C. A normal A-a gradient with hypercapnia = hypoventilation (the high CO₂ displaces alveolar O₂; the alveolar-capillary unit is intact). Shunt and V/Q mismatch raise the A-a gradient. Low diffusion is chronic and also raises the A-a. Anemia causes tissue hypoxia, not a low PaO₂.

3. A hypoxemic patient does not improve despite 100% oxygen by non-rebreather. This most strongly suggests:

  1. V/Q mismatch
  2. Hypoventilation
  3. Right-to-left shunt
  4. Low FiO₂
  5. Anxiety
Answer + explanation

C. Hypoxemia refractory to 100% O₂ is the hallmark of a shunt — blood bypasses ventilated alveoli, so added FiO₂ never reaches it. V/Q mismatch and hypoventilation correct with supplemental oxygen.

4. Which patient most clearly needs an ABG rather than a VBG?

  1. DKA where you only need the pH and bicarbonate
  2. A hypoxemic patient in whom you must calculate the A-a gradient
  3. Stable COPD trending CO₂
  4. Routine electrolyte check
  5. Lactate measurement
Answer + explanation

B. The ABG is required when you need the PaO₂ — to confirm/grade hypoxemia or compute the A-a gradient. For pH/CO₂ trends (DKA, COPD) a VBG suffices (ABG pH ≈ VBG + 0.04; ABG PaCO₂ ≈ VBG − 4).

5. A 74-year-old with hypotension, elevated JVP, clear lungs, and electrical alternans on ECG is dyspneic. The unifying diagnosis is:

  1. Pulmonary embolism
  2. Cardiac tamponade
  3. Pneumonia
  4. DKA
  5. Severe anemia
Answer + explanation

B. Hypotension + elevated JVP + electrical alternans + an enlarged cardiac silhouette point to a pericardial effusion causing tamponade (a cardiac cause of dyspnea, clear lungs). The others don't explain electrical alternans with elevated JVP.

Modern Framing — Context currency check

The dyspnea differential, the evaluation toolbox, and the 5-cause / A-a-gradient framework are timeless physiology, verified current as of June 2026. There are no guideline changes to this diagnostic content; the notes below reinforce it.

Lung ultrasound at the bedside. POCUS (BLUE protocol; Lichtenstein) is now a routine adjunct in acute dyspnea — it rapidly distinguishes cardiogenic pulmonary edema (B-lines), consolidation/pneumonia, pneumothorax (absent lung sliding), and pleural effusion with high sensitivity, complementing the CXR/ABG/ECG/CBC toolbox.
Don't forget non-hypoxemic hypoxia. A normal PaO₂ doesn't rule out tissue hypoxia: anemic, stagnant (low-flow), histotoxic (cyanide — sodium nitroprusside), and methemoglobinemia (nitrates, local anesthetics, sulfonamides). Pulse oximetry can be falsely reassuring in CO poisoning and methemoglobinemia — confirm with co-oximetry.

Take-Home Points

  1. Build the dyspnea differential by system: respiratory (airways/alveoli/vessels), cardiac, musculoskeletal, and the "3 A's" (anemia, anxiety, acidosis).
  2. The core work-up toolbox: CXR, blood gas, CBC, ECG — add POCUS at the bedside.
  3. Dyspnea ≠ hypoxemia. Hypoxemia = low PaO₂ (normal 75–100); confirm on an ABG.
  4. Sort hypoxemia by the A-a gradient: normal (hypoventilation, low FiO₂) vs elevated (V/Q mismatch, shunt; low diffusion is chronic). Shunt doesn't correct with 100% O₂.
  5. Get an ABG over a VBG whenever you need the PaO₂ — e.g., to calculate the A-a gradient.
