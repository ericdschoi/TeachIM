What are the two major pathophysiologic pathways driving SLE manifestations?	(1) Antibody pathway — APC presents self-antigen → T-helper activates B cells → antinuclear antibodies (ANA) cause direct cellular destruction, immune-complex/tissue deposition, and a positive ANA test. (2) Inflammatory pathway — antigen binds TLRs on macrophages → cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, IFN-alpha) → inflammation.
Why are SLE patients especially susceptible to autoantibody formation after UV light?	UV light increases apoptosis; nuclear contents spilled by dying cells are taken up by antigen-presenting cells (macrophages) that then mount immune responses against self-antigens.
How long can autoantibodies precede clinical SLE?	Years — in a VA cohort, 115/130 SLE patients had autoantibodies up to ~9.4 years before diagnosis, accumulating in a predictable order (benign → pathogenic autoimmunity → clinical illness).
Which classification criteria underlie the SLE clinical-manifestation framework?	The EULAR/ACR 2019 Classification Criteria for SLE; each manifestation counts only if not better explained by another, more probable cause.
Describe the SLE malar rash.	Sun-sensitive facial rash that SPARES the nasolabial folds; usually rough and raised (vs rosacea, which is smooth and flat). Photosensitive rashes can occur elsewhere and notably spare the knuckles.
Which mucocutaneous findings are concerning for SLE?	Malar/photosensitive rashes, alopecia (scarring or non-scarring), painless or painful oral/nasal ulcers (active disease), Raynaud's, and sicca.
Which hematologic findings prompt an SLE workup?	Cytopenias (anemia — watch for hemolysis; leukopenia WBC <4, especially lympho/neutropenia, always get a diff; thrombocytopenia, though Plt <20 is more likely ITP) and arterial/venous VTE.
What proportion of SLE patients have antiphospholipid antibodies?	30–40%; they raise the risk of vascular events and death. Revisit "provoked" VTEs; a clot is not required to diagnose antiphospholipid syndrome.
What renal findings suggest lupus nephritis, and how common is it?	Proteinuria (>0.5 g) and/or hematuria; creatinine is often normal but a rising Cr demands urgent workup. Up to 50% of SLE patients develop lupus nephritis.
Most common musculoskeletal manifestation of SLE?	Synovitis — red, hot, objectively swollen joints (often MCP/PIP, wrists, knees); joints are the first sign in ~50%. Myositis presents as quadriceps weakness (dx with CK + EMG).
Which serosal manifestations are common in SLE?	Pleural effusion (40–60%, often asymptomatic), pericardial effusion (>40%, can cause tamponade), and acute pericarditis (>50% of echos). Usually benign and atypical as an initial presentation.
Which "yellow-box" symptoms should NOT, by themselves, trigger an ANA?	Fatigue/weight change, hair loss in the shower, abdominal pain/chronic diarrhea/hematochezia (IBS/IBD), single/early miscarriage, iron-deficiency anemia, anxiety/depression/brain fog/headache, atypical chest pain, isolated dyspnea, pyuria, and generalized aches or pain without joint swelling.
For diffuse pain without objective inflammation, what is the differential instead of SLE?	Fibromyalgia, thyroid disease, osteoarthritis, or another autoimmune disease (e.g., spondyloarthritis) — absence of a red/hot/swollen joint or elevated inflammatory markers argues against lupus.
What is the recommended laboratory workup once SLE suspicion is high enough to send an ANA?	CBC w/ diff, CMP, C3/C4 (expect low), ESR (often elevated, CRP often normal — high CRP suggests RA overlap or infection), ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Smith, anti-RNP, anti-SSA/SSB, antiphospholipid antibodies, and UA.
Specificity/associations of the SLE autoantibodies?	Anti-dsDNA — specific, titer tracks activity (and nephritis); anti-Smith — specific, associated with lupus nephritis; anti-RNP — MCTD + Raynaud's; anti-SSA/SSB — Sjögren's and neonatal lupus. ANA must be positive to diagnose SLE.
Prevalence of a positive ANA in people WITHOUT SLE?	~32% at 1:40, ~13% at 1:80, ~5% at 1:160 — a low-positive ANA is common and non-specific.
In a patient with known SLE and a high CRP, what should you think of first?	Infection — infections are a leading cause of mortality in SLE; both the disease and immunosuppressants raise risk, and ESR is usually elevated out of proportion to CRP in lupus activity (a high CRP points away from pure flare).
Beyond this diagnostic talk, what is the 2024 ACR treatment shift for active class III/IV lupus nephritis?	Early triple/combination therapy: hydroxychloroquine for all, plus pulse + oral glucocorticoids and one of — MPAA + belimumab, MPAA + a calcineurin inhibitor (voclosporin), or low-dose Euro-Lupus cyclophosphamide → MPAA + belimumab; lower-dose steroid taper to ≤5 mg/day by 6 months.
