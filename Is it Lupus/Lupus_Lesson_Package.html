Learning Objectives

  1. Describe the two major pathophysiologic mechanisms underlying SLE's clinical and immunologic manifestations.
  2. Identify the most common clinical pathologies of SLE using a systems-based approach.
  3. Distinguish signs/symptoms that should prompt an SLE workup from those that are less concerning.
  4. Obtain the appropriate diagnostic workup for a presentation concerning for SLE.

Pre-work send the day before

1. A 30-year-old with fatigue and diffuse body aches but no objective joint swelling wants "a lupus test." Should you send an ANA?

Generally no. Fatigue and diffuse pain without objective inflammation are "yellow-box" symptoms; the differential is fibromyalgia, thyroid disease, OA, or another condition. An ANA in this setting is more likely to mislead than help (a low-positive ANA is common in healthy people).

2. Name two autoantibodies that are specific for SLE.

Anti-dsDNA (specific; titer tracks disease activity and nephritis) and anti-Smith (specific; associated with lupus nephritis). ANA must be positive to diagnose SLE but is not specific.

3. Why does a high CRP in a known-SLE patient point you toward infection?

In lupus activity, ESR is typically elevated out of proportion to CRP (CRP often stays normal). A markedly high CRP suggests infection (or an RA overlap) rather than a pure lupus flare — and infection is a leading cause of mortality in SLE.

Teaching Script ~20 min core, runs off the board's four tabs

Hook 2 min

"How many ANAs did your team order last month? How many were positive, and how many of those patients actually had lupus?" Name the real problem this talk solves: not 'how to treat lupus,' but 'how to decide whether to test for it' — because a poorly-chosen ANA generates anxiety, referrals, and repeat testing. The board moves Autoimmunity → Clinical Features → SLE Workup → Cases.

Segment 1 — Why SLE looks like everything (pathophysiology) 5 min

ASK — "Why can one disease hit skin, kidney, blood, joints, lung, and brain?" (One trigger — self-antigen exposure — feeding two pathways.)

UV light → apoptosis → spilled nuclear contents picked up by macrophages → two pathways: the antibody pathway (APC → T-helper → B cell → ANA → cellular destruction, immune-complex deposition, positive ANA test) and the inflammatory pathway (antigen on TLRs → IL-1, IL-6, IFN-α → inflammation anywhere). Autoantibodies can brew silently for years (up to ~9.4 yrs in the Arbuckle VA cohort) before clinical disease.

Pearl: the wide manifestation list isn't random — it's the same two pathways playing out in different organs. That's why a systems-based exam, not a reflex ANA, is the right first move.

Segment 2 — Concerning vs "yellow-box" features 8 min

ASK — Go system by system: "Constitutional — which finding is concerning, which isn't?" (Unexplained documented fever ≥38.3 vs nonspecific fatigue/weight change.)

Every feature counts only if not better explained by another cause (EULAR/ACR 2019). Walk the systems:

ASK — "A patient reports 'joint pain.' What one exam finding decides whether you're worried?" (Objective synovitis — red, hot, swollen — vs pain alone.)

Segment 3 — The workup, once you've decided to test 5 min

If suspicion is high enough for an ANA, send the full panel, not the ANA alone: CBC w/ diff (cytopenias), CMP (Cr, transaminases, bilirubin), C3/C4 (expect low), ESR/CRP (ESR ≫ CRP in lupus), ANA + anti-dsDNA/Smith/RNP/SSA/SSB, antiphospholipid antibodies, and UA (hematuria + proteinuria). Refer to rheumatology if abnormalities accompany unexplained SLE-specific features.

ANA stewardship: a positive ANA is found in ~32% of healthy people at 1:40, ~13% at 1:80, ~5% at 1:160. Order it for pre-test probability, not reassurance.

"Get an ANA?" rapid-fire (7 vignettes)

1. 37F, post-COVID, acute pericarditis (diffuse ST elevation, trop 206), started on colchicine/NSAIDs.

NO — most likely viral pericarditis; symptoms are explained by another cause.

2. Same patient, 1 year later: recurrent pericarditis plus a new low ANC on a normal WBC.

YES — now there are additional unexplained findings (recurrent serositis + cytopenia) raising SLE concern.

3. 32F with GAD/MDD/IBS: a year of fatigue, diffuse bilateral leg pain, neck pain, headaches, weight loss, body stiffness.

NO — picture fits fibromyalgia/chronic fatigue, not lupus; all "yellow-box."

4. 50F with alcohol use disorder: pancytopenia (WBC 2.7, Hgb 9.7, Plt 56), AST/ALT 360/180, T.bili 4.1, EtOH 124; next day visual hallucinations.

NO — alcoholic hepatitis + marrow suppression, then likely delirium tremens; everything is explained.

5. 27M soccer player: leg pain/weakness, raised facial and arm rash, CK 12,000, MRI thigh edema.

YES — unexplained myositis with rash; work up for dermatomyositis/polymyositis and send an ANA.

6. 19F student: "mono," weeks of fatigue, now sore arms/legs; afebrile, normal skin/gait, but tender swollen wrists.

YES — fever history + objective synovitis in a young, previously healthy patient warrants a lupus workup.

7. 32M with stimulant use disorder, found down, febrile, swollen erythematous elbow, Cr 2.3, WBC 1.7, splenic infarct.

NO — picture fits sepsis from endocarditis (septic emboli), not lupus.

Longer cases

Case 1 — 27F, new presentation (ankle synovitis, photosensitive rash, persistent arm rash, fatigue, known low WBC).

Most concerning: photosensitive symptom flares and objective joint swelling/warmth (not "aches"). Workup returns ANA 1:320 fine speckled, anti-dsDNA 1:40, low C4, lymphopenia, bland UA, negative antiphospholipid → refer to rheumatology for likely SLE. Counsel on SPF 30+, NSAIDs for joints, pregnancy prevention until a plan is set; urgent referral if end-organ damage, severe cytopenia, extensive rash, bullae/infection, or pregnancy.

Case 2 — 40F with SLE + class IV lupus nephritis on cyclophosphamide + prednisone 40 mg, now febrile with cough, dyspnea, LLL infiltrate, CRP 77, ESR 130.

Broaden beyond "lupus flare": infection (bacterial/fungal pneumonia — most likely here), lupus pneumonitis, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, PE, endocarditis, or cyclophosphamide lung injury. In SLE with a high CRP, think infection first — start empiric antimicrobials, continue home steroids, consider pulmonary/bronchoscopy. Infection is a major cause of mortality in lupus.

Case 3 — 35F with depression: 2 years of polyarthralgia, itchy winter-worse rashes on eyelids/neck, daily fatigue; ANA 1:80, rest normal.

Differential: depression, anxiety, fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue, SLE, or SLE + fibromyalgia. The pain is myofascial and the rash sounds like eczema; a low-positive ANA (1:80 occurs in ~13% of healthy people) with no other evidence does not make lupus. Reassure; at least 20% of SLE patients also have fibromyalgia, which explains many overlapping symptoms.

Board-Style MCQs ABIM format

1. A 29-year-old woman has 3 months of fatigue, "joint pain," and a positive ANA at 1:80 sent by her PCP. Exam shows no synovitis, normal skin, normal labs otherwise. Which is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Start hydroxychloroquine empirically
  2. Reassure; a low-titer ANA is common and there is no objective evidence of SLE
  3. Order anti-dsDNA, anti-Smith, C3/C4, and refer to rheumatology
  4. Repeat the ANA monthly
  5. Begin prednisone
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

B. A 1:80 ANA occurs in ~13% of healthy people; with no objective SLE features, this does not warrant treatment, cascade testing, or referral. A and E treat a disease that isn't established; C escalates a workup the pre-test probability doesn't support; D — serial ANA titers are not used to track or diagnose.

2. Which constellation most warrants ordering an ANA and a full lupus panel?

  1. Fatigue and a positive "hair loss in the shower" history
  2. Chronic widespread pain without joint swelling and a normal exam
  3. Objective wrist synovitis, an unexplained documented fever, and new lymphopenia
  4. Atypical chest pain with a normal cardiac workup
  5. Isolated chronic dyspnea without hypoxia
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

C. Objective synovitis + unexplained fever + cytopenia are SLE-specific, unexplained features that raise pre-test probability. A, B, D, and E are all "yellow-box" presentations (fibromyalgia, normal hair shedding, fibromyalgia-type chest pain, nonspecific dyspnea) that shouldn't by themselves trigger an ANA.

3. A 24-year-old woman with established SLE on mycophenolate and prednisone presents with fever, cough, a lobar infiltrate, CRP 90, and ESR 110. What is the best initial approach?

  1. Increase prednisone for a presumed flare
  2. Treat empirically for infection while evaluating; in SLE with high CRP, think infection first
  3. Order an ANA titer to assess activity
  4. Stop all immunosuppression immediately
  5. Send anti-dsDNA and wait for results before acting
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

B. In lupus, ESR is usually elevated out of proportion to CRP; a markedly high CRP and a lobar infiltrate point to infection, the leading cause of SLE mortality. A could worsen an untreated infection; C — ANA titers don't track activity; D risks adrenal crisis and isn't indicated reflexively; E dangerously delays therapy.

4. A patient with new SLE features asks which autoantibody is both specific for SLE and tends to rise with disease activity and nephritis. Which is it?

  1. Anti-RNP
  2. Anti-SSA (Ro)
  3. Anti-dsDNA
  4. Rheumatoid factor
  5. ANA
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

C. Anti-dsDNA is specific for SLE and its titer fluctuates with activity (and nephritis). Anti-RNP suggests MCTD; anti-SSA relates to Sjögren's/neonatal lupus; RF is nonspecific; ANA is sensitive but not specific (positive in many healthy people).

5. A 33-year-old woman is being worked up for possible SLE. Beyond the ANA, which panel best screens for the major organ manifestations?

  1. ANA titer alone, repeated in 3 months
  2. CBC w/ diff, CMP, C3/C4, ESR/CRP, dsDNA/Smith/RNP/SSA-SSB, antiphospholipid antibodies, and UA
  3. ESR and CRP only
  4. ANA and rheumatoid factor
  5. Skin biopsy
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

B. Once you've decided to test, send the broad panel that screens cytopenias, renal/hepatic involvement, complement consumption, the SLE-specific antibodies, antiphospholipid antibodies, and urinary findings. A and C are insufficient and ANA titers aren't tracked serially; D misses organ screening; E isn't a screening test.

Currency Notes checked June 2026

This is a diagnosis / ANA-stewardship talk; its content was verified current and not changed. Scope and currency:

  • EULAR/ACR 2019 Classification Criteria for SLE (Aringer, Arthritis Rheumatol 2019) — the basis for the deck's clinical-manifestation framework; still the current classification standard as of June 2026.
  • ANA prevalence figures (Tan, Arthritis Rheum 1997: ~32%/13%/5% at 1:40/1:80/1:160) remain the canonical reference and are accurately quoted.
  • Out of scope — treatment update: the 2024 ACR Lupus Nephritis guideline (Sammaritano, Arthritis Care Res 2025) marks a conceptual shift to early COMBINATION therapy — hydroxychloroquine for all, plus for active class III/IV(±V) LN, triple therapy = pulse + oral glucocorticoids and one of (MPAA + belimumab), (MPAA + a calcineurin inhibitor, e.g. voclosporin), or low-dose Euro-Lupus cyclophosphamide → MPAA + belimumab; lower-dose steroid taper to ≤5 mg/day by 6 months, add a RAAS inhibitor for proteinuria; KDIGO 2024 aligns. This is surfaced as context only — the diagnostic teaching is unchanged.

Take-Home Points

  1. SLE's many complications trace to a few immune pathways — autoantibody production and TLR-driven inflammatory cytokines.
  2. Before ordering an ANA, use a systems-based exam to look for SLE-specific manifestations and gauge pre-test probability.
  3. If suspicion is high enough to order an ANA, add the broader metabolic and immunologic workup to screen the major manifestations.
  4. A low-positive ANA is common in healthy people, and fibromyalgia frequently coexists with (and mimics) lupus — test for probability, not reassurance.
