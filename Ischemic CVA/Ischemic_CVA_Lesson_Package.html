Learning Objectives

  1. Apply a 5-step approach to evaluating and managing acute ischemic CVA: exclude hemorrhage → acute reperfusion → determine etiology → secondary prophylaxis → risk modification.
  2. Decide which strokes are eligible for IV thrombolysis, endovascular therapy (EVT), or neither — based on timing, clinical assessment, and initial imaging.
  3. Determine CVA etiology (atherosclerotic vs cardioembolic vs other) using targeted diagnostics.
  4. Match secondary prophylaxis (antiplatelet vs anticoagulation, single vs dual) to the etiology and timing.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What is the single first branch point in every acute stroke algorithm, and what test answers it? Hemorrhagic vs ischemic — answered by an emergent noncontrast head CT. Everything downstream (thrombolysis, antithrombotics) depends on first excluding intracranial hemorrhage.
Q2. A patient is 3 hours from last-known-well with a disabling deficit and no hemorrhage on CT. What two reperfusion questions are you now asking? (1) Is the patient eligible for IV thrombolysis (within 4.5 h, no contraindications)? (2) Is there a large-vessel occlusion amenable to thrombectomy? The two are complementary, not either/or — give IV lytic if eligible and pursue EVT for LVO.
Q3. Why does stroke etiology change your antithrombotic choice? Atherosclerotic strokes are prevented mainly with antiplatelet therapy (± short-course DAPT); cardioembolic strokes (e.g., AF, LV thrombus) need anticoagulation. Picking the wrong class either under-treats the embolic source or needlessly raises bleeding risk.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected 5-step interactive board (Ischemic_CVA_Optimized.pptx) and click through the animated builds, or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. The board is organized as a numbered 1→5 spine with sub-boards for acute management, etiology, and prophylaxis — use the "Main Board" button to return. Print the learner handout. Suggested timing is on the board (10–15 / 15–20 / 10–15 min per block).

Hook (2 min)

"68-year-old, last seen normal 2 hours ago, now with dense left hemiparesis and gaze deviation. The clock is running — what happens in the next 30 minutes decides whether she walks out of the hospital." Set the door-to-needle/door-to-groin frame, then build the 5 steps.

Step 1 — Exclude hemorrhage 3 min

ASK: "First test, before anything else?" Reveal: noncontrast head CT. ~85% of strokes are ischemic, ~15% hemorrhagic; you cannot give an antithrombotic or lytic until hemorrhage is off the table.

Step 2 — Acute reperfusion without causing ICH 12 min

The acute-management sub-board runs two parallel tracks off "acute focal deficit, no hemorrhage on CT":

TrackTriggerAction
IV thrombolysisLKW < 4.5 h, no contraindication, BP < 185/110tPA — or tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg (2024 Class 1 alternative). Door-to-needle < 30 min.
Endovascular therapy (EVT)CTA shows LVO; salvageable brain (ASPECTS ≥6, or CT perfusion mismatch); LKW < 6 h, selected to 24 hMechanical thrombectomy ± stent. Door-to-groin < 90 min.
ASK: "Name three absolute contraindications to IV thrombolysis." Reveal the board's checklist: prior ICH, surgery < 3 mo, active bleeding, coagulopathy, BP > 185/110 (and more on the linked full list).
Pearl — 2023–2024 expansion: Thrombectomy now benefits large-core infarcts too — anterior LVO, 6–24 h, NIHSS ≥6, ASPECTS 3–5, age < 80 (SELECT2, ANGEL-ASPECT, NEJM 2023; 2024 AHA/ASA Class 1). Don't write off a big established infarct as "too late" without imaging-based selection. Basilar-occlusion EVT is also now supported (ATTENTION/BAOCHE 2022).
Pearl — acute BP: Permissive hypertension < 220/120 ×48 h if not reperfused; < 185/110 if thrombolysed/EVT. Avoid tubes and lines; DVT prophylaxis with SCDs (pharmacologic held < 24 h post-tPA); dysphagia screen before anything PO; PT/OT/SLP; intensive statin before discharge if atherosclerotic.

Step 3 — Determine etiology 10 min

Etiology guides secondary prevention. The sub-board sorts into atherosclerotic (large- vs small-artery), cardioembolic (atrial/LV thrombus, valvular, VTE-with-shunt), or other (dissection, vasculitis, thrombophilia). Standard diagnostics on everyone: brain MRI, H&N CTA/MRA, carotid/vertebral/transcranial Doppler, telemetry, TTE with bubble study.

Pattern on imagingSuggested etiology
Large and/or multiple-vessel territoryCardioembolic
Lacunar (small, deep)HTN or amyloid vasculopathy
Small/single vessel + vascular risk factorsAtherosclerosis
ASK: "When does a patient need a TEE instead of a TTE?" Reveal: only with moderate-high suspicion of a cardioembolic source, no identified intracardiac source on TTE, or a confirmed right-to-left shunt — TEE better visualizes the LA appendage, mitral valve, and ascending aorta.
Pearl: Thrombophilia testing is low-yield in the acute phase — defer to hematology/neurology after the acute thrombotic state resolves and only with moderate-high pretest probability.

Steps 4 & 5 — Secondary prophylaxis + risk modification 10 min

The prophylaxis sub-board contrasts embolic (antiplatelet bridge 1–2 weeks → anticoagulation after the hemorrhagic-conversion window) vs atherosclerotic (antiplatelet, with short-course DAPT for high-risk patients then step-up monotherapy).

ASK: "Minor stroke, NIHSS 2 — single or dual antiplatelet, and for how long?" Reveal: short-course DAPT (clopidogrel + aspirin) then narrow to one.
Pearl — 2021 AHA/ASA secondary prevention: DAPT (clopidogrel 300–600 mg load → 75 mg daily + aspirin) for minor stroke NIHSS ≤3 or high-risk TIA ABCD2 ≥4, started < 24 h and continued 21 days (then single agent) — the CHANCE/POINT window that maximizes benefit while limiting bleeding. Ticagrelor + aspirin ×30 d is an alternative (THALES). Long-term: high-intensity statin to LDL < 70, BP < 130/80, A1c < 7, tobacco cessation; acute inpatient glucose < 180 mg/dL. PFO closure is reasonable for cryptogenic embolic stroke in selected patients < 60 (RESPECT/CLOSE/REDUCE).

Retrieval close (3 min)

Close the laptop. Have learners reconstruct the 5 steps in order and, for a given stroke, state the reperfusion decision and the etiology-matched antithrombotic. Then have them recite the take-home points before you reveal them.

Cases Worked examples, click-to-reveal

The original board is an interactive algorithm rather than a fixed case set; these vignettes apply it. Pose each question, take answers, then reveal.

Case 1 — Hyperacute LVO

72-year-old with AF (not anticoagulated), last-known-well 90 minutes ago, NIHSS 16 with right gaze preference and left hemiplegia. CT: no hemorrhage. CTA: right M1 occlusion. BP 178/96.

Step 1–2: reperfusion plan?Within 4.5 h and no contraindication → IV thrombolysis (alteplase 0.9 mg/kg or tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg). LVO with salvageable brain → activate thrombectomy. Give the lytic and go to EVT — they are complementary. Keep BP < 185/110 around lysis.
Step 3–4: etiology and long-term antithrombotic?AF → cardioembolic. After the hemorrhagic-conversion window (antiplatelet bridge ~1–2 weeks given infarct size), start anticoagulation for secondary prevention; do not combine chronic anticoagulant + antiplatelet without a separate indication.

Case 2 — Minor stroke / high-risk TIA

59-year-old with HTN, DM, smoking; 40 minutes of right-hand weakness and dysarthria, now resolved. NIHSS 0, ABCD2 = 5. CT without hemorrhage; MRI shows a small left-corona-radiata infarct.

Reperfusion?No — deficit resolved/minor and non-disabling; no lytic/EVT. Focus shifts to rapid secondary prevention.
Antiplatelet strategy?High-risk (NIHSS ≤3 / ABCD2 ≥4): start DAPT (clopidogrel load + aspirin) within 24 h, continue 21 days, then single-agent. High-intensity statin to LDL < 70, BP and glucose control, smoking cessation. Work up etiology (carotid imaging, telemetry, echo).

Case 3 — Young stroke, cryptogenic

41-year-old, no vascular risk factors, wakes with aphasia and right facial droop. MRI: left MCA cortical infarct. CTA/carotids normal; telemetry without AF; TTE-bubble shows a right-to-left shunt.

Next diagnostics?Pursue cardioembolic/shunt work-up: TEE if moderate-high suspicion with no identified source; prolonged rhythm monitoring for occult AF; consider hypercoagulable evaluation (deferred, hematology-guided). LE duplex to assess for paradoxical-embolism source.
Management of the PFO?For cryptogenic embolic stroke with PFO in a patient < 60 and an otherwise negative work-up, PFO closure is reasonable (RESPECT/CLOSE/REDUCE). If a DVT/VTE source is found with a confirmed shunt, anticoagulation is generally preferred over closure to address the venous source.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 64-year-old presents 2 hours after sudden right-sided weakness; NIHSS 12. Noncontrast CT shows no hemorrhage and an ASPECTS of 9. BP 170/92. CTA shows a left M1 occlusion. What is the best immediate management?

  1. Aspirin 325 mg and admit
  2. IV thrombolysis (alteplase or tenecteplase) AND activate thrombectomy
  3. Thrombectomy only; withhold thrombolysis
  4. Lower BP to < 140/90 before any therapy
  5. Heparin drip
Answer + explanationB. Within 4.5 h, no hemorrhage, no contraindication → give IV lytic (alteplase 0.9 mg/kg or TNK 0.25 mg/kg, 2024 Class 1) and, for the M1 LVO with good ASPECTS, pursue thrombectomy — they are complementary. A under-treats a disabling LVO. C needlessly withholds an eligible lytic. D — target is < 185/110 for lysis, not < 140/90 (over-lowering risks hypoperfusion). E acute full-dose anticoagulation is not indicated and raises ICH risk.

2. An 71-year-old presents 10 hours after last-known-well with an anterior LVO, NIHSS 14, and ASPECTS 4 (large core). Which statement is correct?

  1. The infarct is too large; thrombectomy is contraindicated
  2. Thrombectomy is reasonable — large-core EVT now has Class 1 support in this window
  3. Only IV thrombolysis should be offered
  4. Defer all therapy and treat with aspirin
  5. EVT requires ASPECTS ≥ 8
Answer + explanationB. SELECT2 and ANGEL-ASPECT (NEJM 2023) extended thrombectomy benefit to large-core infarcts (ASPECTS 3–5) for anterior LVO, 6–24 h, NIHSS ≥6, age < 80 — now a 2024 AHA/ASA Class 1 recommendation. A/E reflect the older ASPECTS ≥6 threshold. C/D withhold a beneficial therapy. He is past the 4.5-h lytic window, so EVT is the reperfusion option.

3. A 58-year-old has a minor ischemic stroke (NIHSS 2) from small-vessel atherosclerosis, presenting at 6 hours. What antithrombotic regimen best reduces 90-day recurrence?

  1. Aspirin monotherapy indefinitely
  2. Clopidogrel + aspirin (DAPT) for 21 days, then single-agent
  3. Warfarin to INR 2–3
  4. Apixaban 5 mg twice daily
  5. DAPT continued indefinitely
Answer + explanationB. For minor noncardioembolic stroke (NIHSS ≤3) or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 ≥4), short-course DAPT started < 24 h and continued ~21 days then narrowed to monotherapy reduces early recurrence (CHANCE/POINT; 2021 AHA/ASA). A misses the early high-risk DAPT window. C/D anticoagulants are for cardioembolic sources, not atherosclerotic. E prolonged DAPT increases bleeding without added benefit.

4. A patient with a large cardioembolic MCA infarct from newly diagnosed AF is ready for secondary prevention. What is the most appropriate timing of anticoagulation?

  1. Start full-dose anticoagulation within the first 24 hours
  2. Bridge with an antiplatelet, then start anticoagulation after the hemorrhagic-conversion window (often ~1–2 weeks for large infarcts)
  3. Anticoagulation is not indicated for AF-related stroke
  4. Lifelong DAPT instead of anticoagulation
  5. Start anticoagulation only if a repeat CT at 6 hours is clear
Answer + explanationB. AF stroke needs anticoagulation for prevention, but starting too early after a large infarct risks hemorrhagic conversion — bridge with an antiplatelet and delay full anticoagulation (timing scaled to infarct size, often ~1–2 weeks for large strokes). A is too early for a large infarct. C/D under-treat the embolic source. E 6 hours is too short to exclude conversion.

5. Which acute blood-pressure approach is correct for an ischemic stroke patient who did NOT receive thrombolysis or thrombectomy?

  1. Lower to < 140/90 immediately
  2. Permissive hypertension, treating only if > 220/120, for ~48 h
  3. Lower to < 120/80 to protect the brain
  4. Start a nitroprusside drip for any BP > 160/90
  5. Keep BP < 185/110
Answer + explanationB. In the non-reperfused acute ischemic stroke, permissive hypertension preserves penumbral perfusion — treat only if > 220/120 (or for another indication) for about 48 h. E < 185/110 applies specifically to patients who received thrombolysis/EVT. A/C over-lowering worsens ischemia. D is overly aggressive and not threshold-based.

Take-Home Points

  1. Run every ischemic stroke through the same 5 steps: exclude hemorrhage → reperfuse → etiology → secondary prophylaxis → risk modification.
  2. Reperfusion is time-critical and complementary: IV lytic (alteplase or tenecteplase) if LKW < 4.5 h, plus thrombectomy for LVO with salvageable brain — now including large-core infarcts in extended windows.
  3. Etiology picks the antithrombotic: atherosclerotic → antiplatelet (DAPT 21 d for minor stroke/high-risk TIA, then mono); cardioembolic → anticoagulation after the hemorrhagic-conversion window.
  4. Acute BP is permissive (< 220/120 ×48 h; < 185/110 if reperfused); long-term targets are LDL < 70, BP < 130/80, A1c < 7.
  5. Don't dismiss a big or "late" stroke without imaging-based selection — and don't start full anticoagulation too early in a large infarct.
