Most accurate approach for intra-articular knee injection?	Superolateral (~90% accuracy vs ~70% for anteromedial/anterolateral; Hermans 2011). Also most reliable for aspiration.
Patient position for the superolateral knee injection?	Supine with the leg fully extended (or slightly hyperextended), relaxes quad/patellar tendons and opens the superolateral space.
Exam finding with the highest +LR for knee OA?	Palpable bony enlargement (+LR ~12). Varus deformity +LR ~3.4; morning stiffness <30 min ~3; crepitus ~2.
+LR of McMurray's and inability to fully extend for meniscal tear?	McMurray's +LR ~4; inability to fully extend (locking) +LR ~3.2.
Where is pes anserine bursitis tenderness?	Anteromedial tibia ~2-5 cm BELOW the joint line (insertion of sartorius/gracilis/semitendinosus), anterior & inferior to the medial joint line (distinguishes from medial meniscus tear).
Best-evidence injectable for knee OA?	Corticosteroids, small-to-moderate pain relief ~6 weeks (Cochrane 2015). Hyaluronic acid, PRP, stem cells: limited/uncertain efficacy (ACR conditionally recommends against).
What beats the steroid injection for long-term knee OA?	Physical therapy / exercise and weight loss (core therapy); topical NSAIDs (diclofenac) preferred first-line drug. Set expectations: steroid = modest, short-term.
Risk of REPEATED intra-articular knee steroids?	May accelerate cartilage/structural loss (McAlindon JAMA 2017 RCT; OAI cohort 2019). Use for flares/short-term; don't over-inject.
Absolute contraindication to intra-articular knee steroid?	Hardware/prosthetic joint; overlying skin infection; suspected septic joint (aspirate & treat, do not inject steroid).
Are anticoagulation or diabetes contraindications to knee steroid injection?	No, generally safe on anticoagulants and in well-controlled diabetes. Defer if joint surgery is imminent (<3 months).
Effect of intra-articular steroid on blood glucose in T2DM?	Mean rise >100 mg/dL for ~24-48 h (up to several days), greatest with poorer baseline control (Waterbrook 2017). Counsel monitoring.
Degenerative meniscal tear (older adult + OA), surgery or PT?	PT is as effective as arthroscopic surgery for pain/function (Khan CMAJ 2014). MRI usually won't change management.
Which meniscal-tear features favor surgery?	Younger patient, acute tear, true mechanical symptoms (locking / inability to fully extend). Concomitant OA, gradual swelling, or pain only on deep flexion favor conservative management.
Supplies for a knee steroid injection?	Marker, chlorhexidine, 1% lidocaine (5 cc), triamcinolone 40 mg/mL (1 cc), 18 g needle to draw up, 21-25 g 1.5" needle to inject, 5 cc syringe, gauze, band-aid (+ 18 g needle & 20-30 cc syringe if aspirating).
"No-touch" draw-up tips?	Clean vial tops with alcohol; separate needles for drawing up vs injecting; inject air when withdrawing >3 cc; draw lidocaine first and add a little to the steroid vial to ease aspiration.
Needle trajectory for the superolateral approach?	Insert superolateral to the patella, advance parallel to the floor, perpendicular to the leg axis or slightly toward the toes, ~3 cm; displace patella laterally with the non-dominant hand.
What does resistance on injection mean?	The tip is NOT in the joint space (soft tissue/cartilage/bone). Stop, withdraw slightly, redirect. Free flow = intra-articular.
First-line pharmacotherapy for knee OA per ACR 2019?	Topical NSAIDs (e.g., diclofenac) and oral NSAIDs, alongside exercise/PT and weight loss as core nonpharmacologic therapy.
