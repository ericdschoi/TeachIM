Learning Objectives

  1. Determine the appropriate indications and contraindications for an intra-articular knee corticosteroid injection (and for diagnostic aspiration).
  2. Perform all critical steps of a knee injection (except needle insertion) using high-fidelity simulation under direct preceptor observation, to a Minimum Passing Score.
  3. Perform all critical steps of a steroid injection of the knee in the clinical environment under direct preceptor observation, to the Unsupervised Practice Standard.

Framework: Learn → See → Practice → Prove → Do → Maintain (Sawyer et al., Acad Med 2015). This lesson pairs a brief knee exam (likelihood-ratio–based) with the procedure — correctly identifying the indication is the most-often-missed step.

Pre-work NEW — send the day before with the procedure video + quiz

Watch the procedural video, skim the instructional guide, and answer these without looking anything up. (The full 10-item quiz, with and without answers, is included as files above.)

Q1. Which injection approach is most accurate, and how should the patient be positioned for it? The superolateral approach — ~90% accuracy for intra-articular placement vs ~70% for anteromedial/anterolateral (Hermans, Semin Arthritis Rheum 2011), and the most reliable site for aspiration. Position the patient supine with the leg fully extended (or slightly hyperextended) to relax the quadriceps/patellar tendons and open the space superolateral to the patella.
Q2. Which knee-exam finding most strongly suggests osteoarthritis? Palpable bony enlargement (+LR ≈ 12). Crepitus on passive motion (+LR ≈ 2) and varus deformity (+LR ≈ 3.4) are also associated with OA but are far less predictive (McGee, Evidence-Based Physical Diagnosis).
Q3. What's the single absolute contraindication to an intra-articular knee steroid — and what's the most important thing to tell a diabetic patient? Hardware/prosthetic joint in the knee is the only absolute contraindication on the list. Anticoagulation, well-controlled diabetes, and surgery >3 months away are not contraindications. Counsel diabetic patients that blood glucose may rise on average >100 mg/dL for ~24–48 h (up to several days), greatest in those with poorer baseline control.

Simulation Teaching Script Restructured into Learn–See–Practice–Prove with timing & engagement cues

Run the simulation as a true clinical encounter (no-touch technique). Pair it with the knee-exam cases. Keep the clinical checklist in view during teaching, then remove it for the graded run. The cursor icons mark built-in ASK prompts. Target 45–70 min including the exam.

Hook & framing (2 min)

"A 65-year-old with chronic knee pain asks for 'the shot that fixed my neighbor.' Before you reach for the triamcinolone — what's the one intervention that beats the injection for long-term pain and function, and what one exam finding would most change your diagnosis?" Hold the answers. Establish that the two highest-yield skills here are making the diagnosis on exam and setting expectations (steroids = modest, short-term relief; PT/exercise = durable benefit).

LEARN — Indications, contraindications & expectations 8 min

ASK: "Which of these blocks the injection: anticoagulation, T2DM on insulin, a knee prosthesis, or surgery in 6 months?"

Symptomatic knee OA; aseptic inflammatory arthritis flare; (diagnostic aspiration to evaluate an effusion / r/o septic joint or crystals)Hardware / prosthetic joint in the knee; overlying skin infection; suspected septic joint (aspirate & treat — do not inject steroid)Anticoagulation; well-controlled diabetes; surgery >3 months out. (Defer steroid if surgery is imminent, e.g. <3 months.)
Pearl — efficacy & expectation setting (ACR 2019 + Cochrane 2015): Of injectables, corticosteroids have the best evidence — but only a small-to-moderate pain benefit lasting ~6 weeks. Physical therapy/exercise and weight loss are more effective long-term and are core therapy; topical NSAIDs (diclofenac) are preferred first-line pharmacotherapy. ACR conditionally recommends against intra-articular hyaluronic acid, PRP, and stem cells (limited/uncertain efficacy, high cost).
Safety — repeated steroids & joint structure: Repeated intra-articular steroids may accelerate cartilage/structural loss (McAlindon RCT, JAMA 2017; OAI cohort, Osteoarthritis Cartilage 2019). Use steroids for flares/short-term relief, don't over-inject, and lean on PT/weight loss for durable benefit.

SEE — Supply prep (no-touch technique) 10 min — instructor demonstrates

  1. Identify supplies with non-sterile gloves: marker, chlorhexidine swab, 1% lidocaine (5 cc), triamcinolone 40 mg/mL (1 cc), an 18 g needle to draw up, a 21–25 g 1.5" needle to inject, 5 cc syringe, gauze, band-aid. (Add a 2nd 18 g needle + 20–30 cc syringe if aspirating an effusion.)
  2. Draw-up tips: clean vial tops with alcohol; use a separate needle for drawing up vs injecting; inject air into a vial when withdrawing >3 cc; draw up lidocaine first so a little can be injected into the small steroid vial to ease aspiration.

SEE — Marking, prep & injection 12 min

  1. Mark the superior and lateral edges of the patella; draw two straight intersecting lines to mark the insertion point superolateral to the patella.
  2. Sterilize with a chlorhexidine scrub ×30 s.
  3. Position: supine, leg fully extended; use the non-dominant hand to gently displace the patella laterally.
  4. Insert the needle at the marked point, advancing parallel to the floor, directed slightly under the patella (perpendicular to the leg axis or just slightly toward the toes), to ~3 cm.
  5. Aspirate first (no blood return), then inject the lidocaine/triamcinolone mix without resistance (resistance = not in the joint space — reposition). Remove needle, apply band-aid.
ASK: "You meet firm resistance when you push the plunger. What does that tell you, and what do you do?"

Resistance means the needle tip is not in the joint space (it's in soft tissue, cartilage, or against bone). Stop, withdraw slightly and redirect; injecting against resistance risks intra-substance/tendon injection. Free flow = intra-articular.

PRACTICE → PROVE — Mastery learning remaining time

Each learner takes a turn as proceduralist with a peer/standardized patient as model. In the simulation, the learner lists indications/contraindications and verbalizes needle insertion (insertion is not physically performed in sim). In the clinical setting, they must identify the specific indication for this patient and demonstrate the supporting history/exam finding. Repeat until the learner meets the passing standard on the clinical assessment tool (error-counting checklist + Global Skills + Entrustment).

Close (2 min)

Hide the guide. Ask the learner to rebuild: the most accurate approach and position, the exam finding with the highest +LR for OA, the one absolute contraindication, what to tell a diabetic patient, and why PT beats the injection long-term.

Case Vignettes From the practice cases & quiz — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Chronic knee OA, stepwise management

A 65-year-old man has 6 months of worsening left knee pain, <15 min of morning stiffness, worse with activity; no inciting event, swelling, or locking. Exam: bony enlargement (+LR 12), crepitus (+LR 2), varus deformity (+LR 3.4); negative McMurray's/Lachman's. He's tried acetaminophen and ibuprofen with partial relief but no PT.

Diagnosis and next steps?Knee osteoarthritis. First-line: topical diclofenac (preferred), PT referral, and weight management if appropriate — these give durable benefit. X-ray is optional (poor correlation with symptoms); MRI is not indicated. If he returns without adequate relief, a corticosteroid injection may give small-to-moderate short-term relief (less effective than PT). If he returns months later with only ~1 month of relief from the injection, refer to orthopedics for consideration of total knee replacement rather than repeating injections indefinitely.

Case 2 — Degenerative meniscal tear vs surgery

A 70-year-old man has 2 months of worsening right medial knee pain, no trauma, no swelling/locking/instability. Exam: medial joint-line tenderness (+LR 2) and pain/clicking on McMurray's (+LR 4).

Best next step?Physical therapy referral. For degenerative meniscal tears (older adults, often with concomitant OA), PT is as effective as arthroscopic surgery for pain and function (Khan, CMAJ 2014). MRI isn't recommended when conservative management is appropriate and wouldn't change management. Surgery is favored for younger patients with acute tears and mechanical symptoms (true locking / inability to fully extend).

Board-Style MCQs NEW — 5 items, ABIM single-best-answer

1. Which approach is most accurate for intra-articular knee needle placement?

  1. Lateral midpatellar
  2. Superolateral
  3. Anteromedial
  4. Anterolateral
Answer & explanationB. The superolateral approach has the highest accuracy (~90%) for intra-articular placement vs ~70% for anteromedial/anterolateral, and is the most reliable for aspiration (Hermans 2011; Wind 2004). Position supine with the leg fully extended.

2. Which exam finding has the highest positive likelihood ratio for knee osteoarthritis?

  1. Crepitus on passive motion
  2. Palpable bony enlargement
  3. Genu varum deformity
  4. Normal joint temperature
Answer & explanationB. Palpable bony enlargement carries a +LR ≈ 12 for knee OA. Crepitus (+LR ≈ 2) and varus deformity (+LR ≈ 3.4) are associated but far less predictive; normal temperature simply argues against an inflammatory/septic process.

3. Which injectable has the best evidence for symptomatic knee OA?

  1. Hyaluronic acid
  2. Corticosteroids
  3. Platelet-rich plasma
  4. Multipotent stem cells
Answer & explanationB. Of the options, corticosteroids are best studied — a small-to-moderate pain benefit for ~6 weeks (Cochrane 2015). Physical therapy is more effective long-term. ACR 2019 conditionally recommends against hyaluronic acid, PRP, and stem cells (limited/uncertain efficacy, high cost).

4. A patient with T2DM asks what an intra-articular steroid will do to their blood sugar. The most accurate counseling is:

  1. No change in blood glucose
  2. A rise <100 mg/dL for 6–12 hours
  3. A mean rise >100 mg/dL lasting ~24–48 hours (up to several days), greatest with poorer baseline control
  4. A drop in blood glucose for 1–2 days
Answer & explanationC. Studies show a mean glucose elevation >100 mg/dL for roughly 24–48 hours (sometimes several days), most pronounced in patients with worse baseline control (Waterbrook 2017; Shin 2020). Counsel monitoring; it is not a contraindication in well-controlled diabetes.

5. Which is an absolute contraindication to an intra-articular knee corticosteroid injection?

  1. Use of an anticoagulant
  2. Type 2 diabetes on insulin
  3. Presence of hardware/prosthesis in the joint
  4. Knee surgery scheduled in 6 months
Answer & explanationC. Injecting into a prosthetic joint risks seeding hardware and is an absolute contraindication (as is overlying infection or a suspected septic joint — aspirate and treat, don't inject steroid). Anticoagulation (A), well-controlled diabetes (B), and surgery >3 months away (D) are not contraindications.

Take-Home Points

  1. Make the diagnosis on exam first: palpable bony enlargement (+LR ≈ 12) for OA; McMurray's (+LR ≈ 4) / locking for meniscal tear; anteromedial tibial tenderness below the joint line for pes anserine bursitis.
  2. Superolateral approach, supine with the leg fully extended — ~90% accuracy and the most reliable site for aspiration.
  3. Set expectations: intra-articular steroids give modest relief for ~6 weeks; PT/exercise and weight loss are more effective long-term and are core therapy; topical NSAIDs are preferred first-line. ACR recommends against HA/PRP/stem cells.
  4. One absolute contraindication — hardware/prosthesis (plus overlying infection / suspected septic joint, where you aspirate and treat rather than inject steroid). Anticoagulation and controlled diabetes are not contraindications.
  5. Counsel diabetics that glucose may rise >100 mg/dL for ~24–48 h; and don't over-inject — repeated steroids may accelerate structural progression.
  6. Inject only without resistance — resistance means you're not in the joint space.
