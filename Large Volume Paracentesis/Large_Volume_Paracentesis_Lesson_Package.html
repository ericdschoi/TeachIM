Learning Objectives

  1. Determine the appropriate indications and contraindications to performing a large volume paracentesis.
  2. Achieve a Minimum Passing Score (MPS) on the validated competency assessment tool in a simulation setting.
  3. Perform a large volume paracentesis under full or partial supervision in the clinical setting until demonstrating the Unsupervised Practice Standard (UPS) on the validated assessment tool.

Framework: Learn → See → Practice → Prove → Do → Maintain (Sawyer et al., Acad Med 2015). The error-counting checklist cutoffs (MPS <30/41 mistakes, UPS <8/41) were set by an Angoff process among 25 procedural experts; SBML improves paracentesis skills (Barsuk, J Grad Med Educ 2012).

Pre-work NEW — send the day before with the procedure video + quiz

Watch the procedural video, skim the instructional guide, and answer these without looking anything up. (The full 10-item quiz, with and without answers, is included as files above.)

Q1. What's the minimum "safe pocket" on ultrasound — and what additional depth makes a therapeutic tap worthwhile? A safe pocket = >2–3 cm of hypoechoic (black) fluid from peritoneum to viscera in all three dimensions; that's enough for a diagnostic tap. For a therapeutic (large-volume) tap, you generally want a maximal fluid depth >5 cm in three dimensions — less than that won't yield enough fluid to relieve symptoms and the catheter will occlude early. Scan with the low-frequency curvilinear/phased-array probe; note the probe-to-skin angle to guide your needle angle.
Q2. Where do you insert, and which two structures must you screen for with the linear probe + Doppler before sticking? Insert in the LLQ (or RLQ) ~2 cm superior and ~2 cm medial to the anterior superior iliac spine — this avoids the flank musculature and stays lateral to the rectus sheath. Switch to the high-frequency linear probe with color/power Doppler to screen for (1) superficial abdominal-wall veins at the site (use minimal pressure — they compress easily) and (2) the inferior epigastric vessels (slide medially until the rectus abdominis comes into view). The inferior epigastrics run just lateral to the rectus — stay lateral to them.
Q3. How much albumin do you give, and after what volume? Post-paracentesis albumin is generally not needed for taps <4–5 L. For large-volume taps, give 6–8 g of 25% albumin per liter removed — this reduces post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction and improves survival (AASLD 2021). For a 10 L tap that's ≈60–80 g (e.g., 75 g of 25% albumin).

Simulation Teaching Script Restructured into Learn–See–Practice–Prove with timing & engagement cues

Run the simulation as a true clinical encounter with sterile technique. Keep the clinical checklist in clear view during teaching, then remove it for the graded run. The cursor icons below mark built-in ASK prompts — pose the question before you demonstrate. Target a 60–90 min session depending on experience.

Hook & framing (2 min)

"Your cirrhotic patient with a tense, painful belly needs a tap. The intern wants to drain 'until it's dry' and skip the albumin to save time. What two decisions in that sentence could hurt the patient?" Hold the answer. Establish that the two highest-yield safety levers are ultrasound site selection (avoiding the inferior epigastric and abdominal-wall vessels) and replacing albumin for large-volume removal — not needle technique.

LEARN — Indications & contraindications 8 min

ASK: "A patient has large ascites but feels fine — do you need another indication to do a therapeutic tap? And which of these counts: early satiety, leg edema, AKI?"
Indications for therapeutic (large-volume) tapDiagnostic tap (separate)
Symptomatic ascites: abdominal distention/pain, early satiety, dyspnea from increased intra-abdominal pressure. Large ascites alone, without symptoms, is not by itself an indication for a large-volume tap.New-onset ascites; every cirrhotic admitted to hospital with ascites (rule out SBP) — even if asymptomatic.
ASK: "Which is the real contraindication to a large-volume tap: sepsis/hemodynamic instability, intra-abdominal hypertension, INR >2, or platelets <50K?"
Weigh before large-volume removalNOT contraindications
Hemodynamic instability / sepsis — large-volume removal can precipitate or worsen circulatory dysfunction and hypotension (a diagnostic tap is still indicated to evaluate for SBP); also a clear surgical abdomen, or DIC / clinically evident fibrinolysis.INR >2, platelets <50K — coagulopathy alone does not increase clinically significant bleeding and routine correction/transfusion is not recommended. Intra-abdominal hypertension is relieved by the tap. Pregnancy and prior abdominal surgery are cautions (use ultrasound, avoid scars).
Pearl — coags don't block the tap: Paracentesis is safe across a wide range of INR/platelets; AASLD does not recommend routine FFP or platelet transfusion before the procedure. Hemodynamic instability is what makes you defer large-volume drainage, not the coags.

LEARN — Positioning & pre-procedure ultrasound 12 min

ASK: "How do you position the patient to pool the most fluid in a dependent quadrant — and why does it matter more at the end of the drain than the start?"
  • Position: head of bed 15–45°, rotate the patient ~15° toward the side of the tap so air-filled bowel floats up and fluid pools in the dependent lower quadrant. Often unnecessary to find a pocket at the start, but as ascites drains the space shrinks and the catheter occludes early if the patient isn't optimally positioned.
  • Pre-procedure scan (low-freq curvilinear/phased-array): start in the RLQ/LLQ ~2 cm superior and ~2 cm medial to the ASIS. Find hypoechoic fluid >2–3 cm from peritoneum to viscera in all dimensions (≥5 cm for a worthwhile therapeutic tap). Note the probe angle — it guides needle angle.
  • Vascular screen (high-freq linear + color/power Doppler): look for superficial abdominal-wall veins at the site (compress easily — use light pressure; fan tangential to flow), then slide medially to identify the inferior epigastric vessels just lateral to the rectus abdominis. Stay lateral to them.
Safety anchor — the inferior epigastric arteries: they run just lateral to the rectus sheath; a tap placed too medially can lacerate one and cause a rectus-sheath hematoma. Go lateral (≥2 cm medial to ASIS, lateral to rectus), and Doppler the site first.

SEE — Sterile prep & anesthesia 10 min — instructor demonstrates each step

  1. Time-out with the nurse (and patient) before opening sterile supplies; confirm consent (or the emergent indication).
  2. Sterile gear: mask, eye protection, sterile gloves (cap and gown optional for paracentesis — less than the maximal barrier required for a CVC).
  3. Skin prep: chlorhexidine scrub ×2, 30 s each, over an area larger than the drape aperture (chlorhexidine is scrubbed; iodine is applied and left to dry); apply the fenestrated drape.
  4. Anesthetize: dermal wheal with a 25 g needle, then advance a 21 g needle on the same syringe under negative pressure, anesthetizing the tract until straw-colored fluid is aspirated; withdraw slightly and deposit ~2 cc at the peritoneum.
  5. Prepare supplies: draw up lidocaine with the largest needle; identify the simple IV tubing (NO one-way valves) for draining ascites (different from the thoracentesis valved set).

SEE — Catheter insertion, drainage & closure 14 min

  1. #11 scalpel skin nick at the marked site.
  2. Advance the catheter-over-needle slowly under negative pressure (a "Z-track" — pull skin taut/offset before entry to reduce post-procedure leak) until straw-colored fluid returns; advance the unit another 0.5 cm, then thread the flexible catheter over the needle to the hub and remove the needle.
  3. Drain: connect simple IV tubing to the stopcock and to a vacuum bottle/vacutainer; exchange bottles until flow stops and symptoms improve.
  4. Closure: withdraw the catheter, clean, and dress with gauze. Document volume, color, lidocaine, and albumin given.
ASK: "Flow slows to a trickle after 4 L but the patient still feels tense — run me through your troubleshooting before you give up."

In order: (1) turn off suction and reposition — raise the HOB or roll the patient toward the side; (2) flush the catheter with 5–10 cc sterile saline (bowel may be plugging it); (3) rotate the catheter; (4) turn off suction and withdraw the catheter to ~2 cm + skin-to-peritoneum depth (often ~4 cm total). If there's too little ascites (<2 cm), switch to dynamic ultrasound guidance or refer to IR.

Pearl — give the albumin: for >5 L removed, infuse 6–8 g of 25% albumin per liter (≈75 g for 10 L). This prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction (a fall in effective volume → renin/AKI) and improves survival (AASLD 2021).

PRACTICE → PROVE — Mastery learning remaining time

The learner rehearses each step, then signals readiness. With the checklist out of view, quiz the non-technical steps; then run the full simulation with it visible, marking each mistake or required prompt, and assign Global Skills + Entrustment scores. Repeat until the learner reaches the MPS. For steps the simulator can't reproduce, have the learner verbalize the intended step.

Case Vignettes From the quiz — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — High-SAAG, high-protein ascites

A patient presents with subacute dyspnea and painful abdominal distention. Ultrasound shows ascites; 2 L of clear straw-colored fluid is removed. Fluid: WBC 420 (40% PMN), protein 3.3 g/dL, albumin 2.1 g/dL. Serum: creatinine 1.9, total bilirubin 1.5, AST 70, ALT 100, albumin 3.3 g/dL.

Next step in management?Obtain a transthoracic echocardiogram. The PMN count is 168 (40% of 420), well below 250 — so this is not SBP, and ceftriaxone is not indicated. The SAAG = 3.3 − 2.1 = 1.2 (≥1.1 → portal hypertension), but the high ascitic protein (>2.5 g/dL) points to a post-hepatic/cardiac cause rather than cirrhosis (cirrhotic ascites is high-SAAG but low-protein). High SAAG + high protein → evaluate the heart with an echocardiogram.

Case 2 — Which fluid studies on a routine therapeutic tap?

A patient with known alcohol-related cirrhosis, whose ascites is usually controlled with diuretics, presents with a tense, painful abdomen and no other new symptoms. Large ascites is confirmed and a therapeutic tap is performed.

Most appropriate peritoneal fluid studies?Cell count + differential, Gram stain and culture (and, on a first/diagnostic tap, albumin and total protein for the SAAG). Even when the indication is symptom relief, send a cell count and culture every admission to exclude SBP — it is frequently asymptomatic in cirrhotics. Cytology (A) is for suspected malignancy, not routine; "just remove fluid" (D) misses an occult SBP.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — 5 items, ABIM single-best-answer

1. Which finding is an appropriate indication for a therapeutic large-volume paracentesis in a patient with large ascites?

  1. Lower-extremity edema
  2. Early satiety from abdominal distention
  3. Acute kidney injury
  4. No additional indication is needed — large ascites alone justifies it
Answer & explanationB. Therapeutic taps are for symptoms of increased intra-abdominal pressure — distention/pain, early satiety, or dyspnea. Lower-extremity edema (A) and AKI (C) are not relieved by (and may be worsened by) large-volume removal, and asymptomatic large ascites (D) does not by itself warrant a therapeutic tap (though a diagnostic tap is indicated on admission to exclude SBP).

2. A cirrhotic patient needs a tap. INR is 2.4 and platelets are 45K. What is the most appropriate approach?

  1. Transfuse FFP and platelets to correct the coagulopathy first
  2. Proceed with ultrasound guidance — coagulopathy alone is not a contraindication
  3. Cancel the procedure; INR >2 is an absolute contraindication
  4. Place a central line for access first
Answer & explanationB. Paracentesis is safe across a wide range of INR and platelet counts; AASLD does not recommend routine FFP or platelet transfusion beforehand, and abnormal coags alone don't increase clinically significant bleeding (A, C wrong). The real reason to defer a large-volume tap is hemodynamic instability/sepsis, not the coags.

3. When mapping a site with ultrasound, which structure must you identify and stay lateral to, to avoid a rectus-sheath hematoma?

  1. The inferior epigastric vessels (just lateral to the rectus abdominis)
  2. The portal vein
  3. The gallbladder
  4. The internal iliac artery
Answer & explanationA. The inferior epigastric arteries run just lateral to the rectus sheath; a too-medial puncture can lacerate one and cause a rectus-sheath hematoma. Insert in the LLQ/RLQ ~2 cm medial to the ASIS and lateral to the rectus, after a Doppler screen for these and superficial abdominal-wall veins.

4. After removing 10 L of ascites, what albumin replacement is most appropriate?

  1. 50 g of 5% IV albumin
  2. No albumin is needed
  3. ~75 g of 25% IV albumin (6–8 g per liter removed)
  4. 2 units of fresh frozen plasma
Answer & explanationC. For large-volume taps (>5 L), give 6–8 g of 25% albumin per liter removed — ≈60–80 g for 10 L — to prevent post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction and improve survival (AASLD 2021). 5% albumin (A) is too dilute (large volume needed), no replacement (B) risks circulatory dysfunction after a large tap, and FFP (D) is not volume-expansion therapy here.

5. During a therapeutic tap, fluid flow slows and stops after 3 L though the abdomen is still tense. What is the best first troubleshooting step?

  1. Remove the catheter and abort the procedure
  2. Turn off suction and reposition the patient (raise HOB / roll toward the side), then flush the catheter with saline
  3. Apply higher wall suction
  4. Advance the catheter deeper toward the bowel
Answer & explanationB. When flow slows, turn off negative pressure and reposition to move fluid toward the catheter, then flush with 5–10 cc saline (bowel may be occluding the port); next rotate or withdraw the catheter slightly. Higher suction (C) and advancing toward bowel (D) increase the risk of perforation/occlusion, and aborting (A) is premature.

Take-Home Points

  1. Indication is symptoms, not just fluid: a therapeutic tap is for distention/pain, early satiety, or dyspnea — but every cirrhotic admitted with ascites still gets a diagnostic tap to exclude SBP.
  2. Coagulopathy doesn't block the tap: INR >2 and platelets <50K are not contraindications and don't warrant routine FFP/platelets. Hemodynamic instability/sepsis is what makes you defer large-volume removal.
  3. Ultrasound site selection is the core safety skill: LLQ/RLQ ~2 cm medial to the ASIS, lateral to the rectus; Doppler to avoid the inferior epigastric vessels and abdominal-wall veins; safe pocket >2–3 cm (≥5 cm to be worth a therapeutic tap).
  4. Replace albumin for large volumes: 6–8 g of 25% albumin per liter removed for taps >5 L prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction and improves survival.
  5. Know the troubleshooting ladder when flow stops: reposition → flush → rotate → withdraw the catheter.
  6. Prove competence by mastery learning: reach the MPS (<30/41 mistakes) in simulation, then the UPS (<8/41) under graded observation before unsupervised practice.
