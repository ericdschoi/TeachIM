Learning Objectives

  1. Describe the appropriate indications and contraindications to performing a bedside lumbar puncture.
  2. Achieve a minimum passing score (MPS) on the clinical assessment checklist in a simulation setting.
  3. Perform a lumbar puncture under full or partial supervision in the clinical setting until demonstrating the Unsupervised Practice Standard (UPS).

Framework: Learn → See → Practice → Prove → Do → Maintain (Sawyer et al., Acad Med 2015). The TeachIM curriculum emphasizes Proving competence for unsupervised practice, not just minimum simulation competence.

Pre-work NEW — send the day before with the procedure video + pre-quiz

Watch the procedural video, skim the instructional guide and clinical checklist, and answer these without looking anything up. (The full 7-item MCQ pre-quiz is included as a file above.)

Q1. Name the one absolute contraindication to LP, and three relative ones. Absolute: overlying skin infection or suspected epidural abscess at the puncture site. Relative: space-occupying lesion / Arnold-Chiari malformation (get CT ± neurosurgery first), platelets <50K, INR ≥1.5, therapeutic anticoagulation, and prior lumbar surgery at the target level. A space-occupying lesion is relative, not absolute — but get a head CT first when there are red flags (see Q2).
Q2. Which clinical findings should prompt a head CT before LP in suspected meningitis? Altered mentation, focal neurologic signs, papilledema, seizure within the past week, or impaired cellular immunity (Hasbun, NEJM 2001). Absent all of these, imaging is not required before LP — and giving antibiotics must never wait for the CT or the LP in suspected bacterial meningitis.
Q3. Why must opening pressure be measured in the lateral decubitus position, and what one maneuver lowers a falsely elevated reading? In the upright (sitting) position the hydrostatic column falsely elevates the measured pressure, so a true opening pressure can only be obtained lying laterally with legs and neck relaxed. Normal OP is 10–25 cm H₂O. If OP is elevated, straightening the patient's flexed legs can drop it by ~5 cm H₂O (tense flexion and Valsalva artificially raise it).

Simulation Teaching Script Restructured into Learn–See–Practice–Prove with timing & engagement cues

Run the simulation as a true clinical encounter with full sterile technique. Keep the Clinical Assessment Tool visible during teaching (reinforces correct sequence), then remove it for the graded run. The cursor icons below mark built-in ASK prompts — pose the question before you demonstrate. Target a 60–90 min session depending on experience.

Hook & framing (2 min)

"Your intern is about to do their first LP on a febrile, confused 59-year-old. Before they touch a needle, what are the two questions that decide whether the procedure happens at all — and in what order?" (Indication confirmed? Any contraindication / need for CT first? Then consent.) Establish that most procedural failures are cognitive, not manual — landmarks and patient selection drive success more than needle skill.

LEARN — Indications & contraindications 8 min

ASK: "Sort these into urgent-diagnostic, non-urgent-diagnostic, and therapeutic: suspected meningitis, suspected SAH with negative CT, NPH, MS, idiopathic intracranial hypertension, intrathecal chemo."

ASK: "Which contraindication is absolute, and which are relative?"
AbsoluteRelative (weigh urgency & mitigate)
Overlying skin infection or suspected epidural abscess at the siteSpace-occupying lesion / Arnold-Chiari (CT ± neurosurgery first) · platelets <50K · INR ≥1.5 · therapeutic anticoagulation (hold heparin gtt <4 h, LMWH <12 h, DOAC <2.5 half-lives, or per local guideline) · prior lumbar surgery
Pearl — herniation fear vs reality: Not every space-occupying lesion raises ICP, and not every raised ICP herniates after LP. Get a head CT first only with altered mentation, focal signs, papilledema, recent seizure, or immunocompromise (Hasbun, NEJM 2001). In suspected bacterial meningitis, never delay empiric antibiotics for the CT or the LP.

LEARN — Consent, positioning & landmarks 10 min

Consent (confirm understanding of indication, benefits, risks & mitigation, and the alternative of not doing it). Position to maximize spinal flexion and minimize R/L rotation:

Upright (sitting)Lateral decubitus
Edge of bed, leaning over a side table, feet on a stool so knees are above hips. Easier landmarking in larger patients — but no opening pressure possible.Provider-handed side down (left lateral if right-handed). Stack ankles–knees–shoulders (pillow between knees), pillow under head, chin tucked, knees toward abdomen. Required for opening pressure.
ASK: "Show me how you find the interspace — by palpation and by ultrasound. Which interspaces are safe?"

Palpation: palpate the iliac crest → corresponds to ~L4; palpate spinous processes to find midline; mark L3–L4 and L4–L5 (or L2–L3). Ultrasound (recommended): low-frequency transducer from the sacrum cephalad — transverse view marks midline across 3 spinous processes; longitudinal view marks the interspinous spaces. Stay at or below L2–L5 to remain below the conus medullaris.

Pearl — ultrasound earns its place: Pre-procedure US marking improves first-pass success and reduces needle passes, especially in obesity and difficult anatomy (SHM position statement, Soni, J Hosp Med 2019; Millington, Chest 2018). It does not need to be used in real time — mark, then proceed sterile.

SEE — Sterile prep, anesthesia & needle insertion 12 min — instructor demonstrates each step

  1. Supplies & time-out: gather kit; confirm name, DOB, procedure, site, allergies with the nurse before opening sterile supplies; include the patient.
  2. Sterile prep: iodine-povidone ×3, let dry (preferred over chlorhexidine given theoretical meningeal-irritation risk); non-fenestrated drape on the bed, then the fenestrated drape in landscape orientation.
  3. Draw-up lidocaine: aspirate 5–10 cc of 1% lidocaine without epinephrine through the filter/large needle, then switch to a 25 g needle.
  4. Anesthetize the tract: raise a superficial dermal wheal using only ~0.5 cc so you don't obscure your skin marks, then anesthetize deeper along the planned path.
  5. Insert the spinal needle: stylet in, at the midline, aimed toward the umbilicus, along the anesthetized tract. Advance slowly, removing the stylet frequently to check for CSF return. If you strike bone, withdraw to the subcutaneous tissue and redirect — each pass should go deeper if you're staying midline.
Safety stop: Always advance with the stylet in place and never inject through the spinal needle. Use an atraumatic (pencil-point) needle — strong recommendation for all patients (see Pearl). If you cannot stay midline, stop, re-mark (ultrasound), and reposition rather than fanning blindly.
Pearl — needle choice is the single biggest modifiable risk: Atraumatic pencil-point needles cut PDPH roughly in half (≈11% → ≈4%) with equal success, a strong recommendation for all ages and indications (Rochwerg/Nath, BMJ 2018; Lancet 2018). A 22 g atraumatic needle balances PDPH reduction, flow, and OP accuracy. Replacing the stylet before withdrawal also reduces PDPH.

SEE — Opening pressure, collection & closure 8 min

  • Opening pressure (lateral decubitus only): prepare the manometer/column and extension tubing beforehand; once CSF returns, attach with tubing horizontal, open the stopcock to the column, read when the meniscus stalls. Normal 10–25 cm H₂O. If elevated, straighten the legs (can drop OP ~5 cm H₂O).
  • Collection: close the stopcock to the patient, then collect into tubes 1–4. If OP was not obtained, remove the stylet and collect directly. Slow flow → rotate the needle in 30° increments. Send the minimum necessary volume per study (see ASK below).
  • Closure: replace the stylet, then withdraw the needle (stylet in); ensure hemostasis; apply dressing.
  • Post-procedure: dispose of sharps; reassess vitals/clinical status; label and send all tubes; update nursing; document the procedure note.
ASK: "Standard studies need ~8–12 mL across four tubes. Which studies need much more — and how much?"

Routine chemistries, cell counts, viral studies, and typical bacterial cultures: ~8–12 mL total over 4 tubes. Cytology and slow-growing cultures (fungal, AFB): a minimum of ~30 mL distributed over the tubes — under-collecting is a common reason these come back non-diagnostic.

PRACTICE → PROVE — Mastery learning remaining time

The learner familiarizes with the kit and rehearses each step, then signals readiness for a graded run. With the checklist out of view, quiz the non-technical steps, then return the checklist to view and run the full simulation. Mark each mistake or required prompt. Repeat until the learner reaches the minimum passing standard.

Close (2 min)

Hide the guide. Ask the learner to rebuild from memory: the one absolute contraindication, the five clinical triggers for pre-LP CT, the safe interspace levels, why OP needs lateral decubitus, and the single most effective way to prevent PDPH.

Case Vignettes From the pre-quiz — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Morning headache + pulsatile tinnitus

35-year-old woman with headache worse in the morning, faint blurred vision, and a buzzing in her ears when lying down. No fever, confusion, or URI symptoms. PMH: obesity, dysfunctional uterine bleeding on an OCP. Neuro exam non-focal. MRI with gadolinium shows faint optic-nerve enhancement, otherwise normal. An LP is planned.

What single test result most confirms the suspected diagnosis?Opening pressure. The syndrome is highly consistent with idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH); a clearly elevated OP (>25 cm H₂O) is the classic confirmatory finding. Send routine studies too (cell counts, protein, glucose, bacteriology). Cytology, fungal culture, and oligoclonal bands are not indicated here.
Position?Lateral decubitus — you need an accurate opening pressure, which cannot be obtained upright.

Case 2 — Headache, confusion, subjective fever

59-year-old man with 2 days of mild headache, then morning confusion and feeling warm. PMH: OA, HTN, HLD, remote L4/5 discectomy. Exam: T 100.4 °F, disoriented, mildly agitated; can sit up and extend his arms on command. An LP is planned for possible meningoencephalitis.

Beyond routine studies (cell count, culture, protein, glucose), what should be added?HSV 1 & 2 PCR — send for all patients with possible meningoencephalitis. Also obtain an opening pressure. Fungal cultures and oligoclonal bands are not indicated without immunosuppression or another specific concern.
Does he need a head CT before the LP?No — he does not meet criteria (no focal deficit, papilledema, recent seizure, or immunocompromise; able to follow commands). The remote discectomy at L4/5 is a relative consideration for site selection (target a different interspace) — not a reason for CT. And don't delay empiric antibiotics ± acyclovir for the LP.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — 5 items, ABIM single-best-answer

1. A 48-year-old woman needs a diagnostic LP for suspected MS. Which choice most reduces her risk of post-dural puncture headache?

  1. Strict 4-hour supine bed rest afterward
  2. A 20-gauge cutting (Quincke) needle
  3. An atraumatic (pencil-point) needle
  4. 1 L of IV fluid before the procedure
Answer & explanationC. Atraumatic pencil-point needles roughly halve PDPH (≈11% → ≈4%) with equal success and are a strong recommendation for all patients (BMJ/Lancet 2018). Post-procedure bed rest (A) and IV fluids (D) do not prevent PDPH (Cochrane). A larger cutting needle (B) increases PDPH. Replacing the stylet before withdrawal also helps.

2. A 24-year-old with suspected bacterial meningitis is febrile and confused with new papilledema. What is the correct sequence?

  1. LP immediately, then antibiotics
  2. Blood cultures + empiric antibiotics now, head CT, then LP if safe
  3. Head CT, then LP, then antibiotics after CSF results
  4. MRI brain before any antibiotics
Answer & explanationB. Papilledema (plus altered mentation/focal signs/recent seizure/immunocompromise) mandates a head CT before LP (Hasbun, NEJM 2001). But antibiotics must never wait for imaging or LP in suspected bacterial meningitis — draw cultures and treat empirically first (A and C dangerously delay therapy; D delays both imaging interpretation and treatment).

3. During an LP you obtain CSF and want an accurate opening pressure. Which condition must be met?

  1. Patient seated upright leaning forward
  2. Patient in lateral decubitus with legs relaxed
  3. Manometer held above the level of the heart
  4. Patient performing sustained Valsalva
Answer & explanationB. Opening pressure is valid only in lateral decubitus; the upright column (A) falsely elevates it. Sustained Valsalva (D) and tense leg flexion artificially raise OP — straightening the legs can lower a high reading ~5 cm H₂O. Normal OP is 10–25 cm H₂O.

4. A 62-year-old needs CSF cytology and fungal cultures to evaluate possible leptomeningeal disease. What volume should you collect?

  1. 1 mL in each of 4 tubes
  2. 2 mL in each of 4 tubes
  3. A minimum of ~30 mL distributed over the tubes
  4. 100 mL distributed over the tubes
Answer & explanationC. Cytology and slow-growing cultures (fungal, AFB) need substantially more CSF (~30 mL) than routine chemistries, cell counts, viral studies, or typical bacterial cultures (~8–12 mL total). Under-collection (A, B) is a frequent cause of non-diagnostic cytology. 100 mL (D) far exceeds what's needed or safe.

5. A 70-year-old on apixaban for AF needs a non-urgent diagnostic LP. Which is the most appropriate approach?

  1. Proceed now; DOACs are not a contraindication
  2. Hold apixaban for ~2.5 half-lives (or per local protocol) and confirm timing/renal function before proceeding
  3. Give andexanet alfa, then proceed immediately
  4. Cancel the LP permanently
Answer & explanationB. Therapeutic anticoagulation is a relative contraindication. For an elective LP, hold the DOAC for ≥2.5 half-lives (longer with renal impairment), or follow local/ABN periprocedural guidance, and confirm timing before the needle (A is unsafe for an elective procedure). Reversal agents (C) are reserved for emergent, not elective, situations. Permanent cancellation (D) is unnecessary once anticoagulation is safely managed.

Take-Home Points

  1. The only absolute contraindication is overlying skin infection / epidural abscess; the rest are relative — weigh urgency and mitigate (platelets, INR, anticoagulation, prior surgery).
  2. Get a head CT before LP only for altered mentation, focal signs, papilledema, recent seizure, or immunocompromise — and never delay antibiotics for it in suspected bacterial meningitis.
  3. Stay at L2–L5; use ultrasound to mark midline and interspace — it improves success and cuts passes.
  4. Use an atraumatic (pencil-point) needle — the single most effective way to prevent PDPH; bed rest and IV fluids do not help.
  5. Opening pressure is valid only in lateral decubitus (normal 10–25 cm H₂O); collect ~30 mL when cytology or fungal/AFB cultures are needed.
  6. Competence is proven by mastery learning: reach the MPS in simulation, then the UPS under graded clinical observation before unsupervised practice.
