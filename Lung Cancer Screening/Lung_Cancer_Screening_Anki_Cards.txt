Where does lung cancer rank as a cause of cancer death?	#1 cause of cancer-related death worldwide; #1 cancer in men, #2 in women. Most internists will diagnose or co-manage it.
USPSTF 2021 lung cancer screening criteria?	Annual low-dose CT for adults 50-80 yo with a >=20 pack-year history who currently smoke OR quit within the past 15 years (mnemonic A-50-80-20-15). Stop after 15 years smoke-free or once life expectancy/surgical candidacy is limited.
How did the ACS 2023 guideline change screening eligibility?	Age 50-80, >=20 pack-year, current OR former smoker -- it REMOVED the "quit within 15 years" requirement entirely (years since quitting no longer limits starting or stopping). Broadens eligibility to ~5 million more US adults.
Other (non-pack-year) risk factors for lung cancer?	Radon, occupational/chemical exposures, secondhand smoke; family history of lung cancer (1st-degree relative); personal history of COPD or pulmonary fibrosis; prior cancer/cancer treatment.
First imaging step for a SYMPTOMATIC patient with possible lung cancer?	A chest X-ray. If concerning -> CT chest +/- abd/pelvis (contrast preferred for nodes). If not concerning -> risk assessment; persistent symptoms or high risk (e.g., hemoptysis) still warrant CT despite a clear CXR.
CT features concerning for malignancy?	Size >15 mm, irregular/spiculated margins, thick-walled cavitation, solid component within ground glass, extrapulmonary/satellite lesions, and growth over time.
Which lesion should you biopsy, and why?	The most advanced (farthest) lesion that is safe to sample -- it gives BOTH diagnosis and staging at once (a distant metastasis automatically = stage IV).
Why prefer core/excisional biopsy over FNA?	Preserves tissue architecture and yields enough tissue for molecular/genetic testing (especially important for lymphoma; also enables EGFR/ALK/PD-L1 testing in lung cancer).
Staging workup after a lung cancer diagnosis?	CT chest/abdomen/pelvis with contrast OR PET-CT; MRI brain with and without contrast (PET is poor for brain -- it always lights up); bronchoscopy +/- mediastinoscopy for nodes; PFTs if a surgical candidate; CBC, BMP+Ca, LFTs; PD-L1 and molecular testing.
Why are PFTs part of lung cancer staging?	To assess baseline lung function and predict tolerance of lobectomy/pneumonectomy in potential surgical candidates. Metastatic patients are usually not surgical candidates, so PFTs are often unnecessary.
SCLC -- key features?	10-15% of lung cancers; strongly smoking-associated; rapidly progressive/aggressive; high rate of brain metastases; paraneoplastic syndromes; initially chemo/radiation-sensitive but frequently recurs.
SCLC staging and treatment?	Limited stage = confined to ipsilateral chest, treatable in a single radiation field (limited stage can occasionally be resected). Extensive stage = beyond ipsilateral chest/distant mets. Mainstays: chemotherapy + radiation; prophylactic cranial irradiation in limited stage without brain mets.
SCLC prognosis (5-yr survival, median survival)?	Limited stage ~10-13% 5-yr survival (median 15-20 mo); extensive stage ~1-2% (median 8-13 mo).
NSCLC -- key features?	Most common lung cancer (80-85%); histologic subtypes (adenocarcinoma, squamous, other) direct treatment; molecular testing changes therapy. Staged I-IV by tumor size, nodes, metastases.
NSCLC treatment by stage?	Early stage: resection or definitive radiation (curative intent). Advancing stages: systemic therapy becomes standard. Incurable disease -> "palliative" intent (distinct from palliative care service).
NSCLC prognosis by stage (5-yr survival)?	Stage I ~73-90% vs Stage IV ~0-10% -- survival drops sharply with each stage, which is the rationale for screening.
Neoadjuvant vs adjuvant vs palliative therapy?	Neoadjuvant = before surgery to shrink the tumor/ease resection. Adjuvant = after resection to kill residual/micrometastatic disease. Palliative = control/shrink disease without curative intent.
Three major systemic therapy classes for lung cancer + a toxicity hallmark of each?	Chemotherapy (cisplatin/carboplatin, etoposide, pemetrexed, taxanes, lurbinectedin) -- N/V, cytopenias, neuropathy, nephropathy. Immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab+/-ipilimumab, atezolizumab, durvalumab) -- the "-itises" (any organ, can be delayed). Targeted/TKIs (-tinib: osimertinib, alectinib, lorlatinib, entrectinib) -- rash (EGFR), diarrhea, rare pneumonitis/QTc prolongation.
Common targetable molecular alterations in NSCLC?	EGFR (osimertinib, erlotinib), ALK (alectinib, crizotinib), ROS1 (crizotinib, entrectinib, lorlatinib). ~50% of never-smokers with lung cancer harbor a targetable mutation -- it dramatically changes treatment and prognosis.
What changed in resectable NSCLC treatment since 2022-2023?	Immunotherapy moved around surgery: neoadjuvant nivolumab+chemo (CheckMate 816), perioperative pembrolizumab (KEYNOTE-671), adjuvant atezolizumab (IMpower010), perioperative durvalumab (AEGEAN). Adjuvant osimertinib is SOC for EGFR-mutated resected stage IB-IIIA (ADAURA).
