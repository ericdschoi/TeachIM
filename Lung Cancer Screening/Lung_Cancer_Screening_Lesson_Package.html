Learning Objectives

  1. Select the appropriate screening or diagnostic imaging based on age, tobacco use, and symptoms.
  2. Identify the preferred biopsy site and procedural service that establish accurate staging while minimizing risk.
  3. Once cancer is confirmed, obtain the studies needed to fully stage disease and determine eligibility for surgery and targeted therapy.
  4. Compare the indications and side effects of the major oncologic treatment categories: chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and targeted therapy.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Who qualifies for annual low-dose CT lung cancer screening? USPSTF 2021 (the basis for most US coverage): adults 50–80 years old with a ≥20 pack-year history who currently smoke or quit within the past 15 years ("A-50-80-20-15"). The ACS updated its guideline in 2023 to drop the years-since-quitting limit entirely — age 50–80, ≥20 pack-year, current or former smoker.
Q2. A patient has several lesions on imaging. Which one do you biopsy, and why? The most advanced (farthest) lesion that can be sampled safely. A single distant lesion gives you both the diagnosis and the stage at once — a distant metastasis automatically makes it stage IV, which changes everything downstream.
Q3. Why does molecular testing matter so much in NSCLC? EGFR, ALK, and ROS1 alterations (and PD-L1 expression) determine eligibility for targeted therapy and immunotherapy, which dramatically change both treatment and prognosis. About 50% of never-smokers with lung cancer have a targetable mutation — and adjuvant osimertinib is now standard for EGFR-mutated resected disease.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the interactive board (Lung_Cancer_Screening_Optimized.pptx) and click through the flowchart and click-to-reveal builds, or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. Every cursor icon on the board is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Modular: the SCLC/NSCLC/Summary pages can be delivered focused or in full. Core talk ~25–30 min; with cases ~30–45 min.

Hook (2 min)

"Where does lung cancer fall in the spectrum of cancer deaths?" Let them guess, then reveal: #1 cause of cancer death worldwide, #1 cancer in men, #2 in women. Follow with the stat that does the teaching work — stage I 5-year survival is ~73–90%, stage IV is ~0–10%. Everything in this talk exists to move patients leftward on that curve.

Objective 1 — Screening & the diagnostic pathway 8 min

ASK: "What signs and symptoms make you worry about lung cancer?" — click "Symptoms?" to reveal. Early disease is usually asymptomatic; advanced disease brings weight loss, malaise, hemoptysis, and metastatic symptoms (bone pain, headache/seizure).

The board splits on a single question: symptomatic or not?

Asymptomatic → screening. Run the risk assessment. High risk = annual low-dose CT; low risk = reassess annually.

GuidelineWho gets annual LDCT
USPSTF 2021 (coverage basis)Age 50–80, ≥20 pack-year, current smoker OR quit ≤15 years ago
ACS 2023 (expanded)Age 50–80, ≥20 pack-year, current OR former smoker — no years-since-quitting limit

Other risk modifiers: radon, occupational/chemical exposures, secondhand smoke, family history (1st-degree relative), COPD/pulmonary fibrosis, prior cancer/treatment.

Symptomatic → CXR first. Not concerning → risk-assess (persistent symptoms or red flags like hemoptysis still warrant CT). Concerning → CT chest ± abd/pelvis (contrast preferred for nodal/structural detail).

ASK: "What on the CT makes you think malignancy?" Reveal: size >15 mm, irregular/spiculated margins, thick-walled cavitation, a solid component within ground glass, extrapulmonary/satellite lesions, and interval growth.
Pearl — tissue is the issue: imaging raises suspicion; only biopsy diagnoses. Prefer core or excisional biopsy over FNA when feasible — it preserves architecture and yields enough tissue for the molecular testing that now drives treatment.

Objective 2 — Where & how to biopsy 5 min

ASK: "You see a lung mass and a distant lesion. Which do you biopsy?" — click "Which lesion would you biopsy?"

Biopsy the most advanced lesion that is safe to reach: one sample yields diagnosis and staging (a distant met = stage IV automatically). The procedural service follows the location:

Objective 4 — Systemic therapy 6 min

ASK: "Neoadjuvant, adjuvant, palliative — what's the difference?" Reveal: neoadjuvant = before surgery (shrink/ease resection); adjuvant = after surgery (clear residual/micrometastatic disease); palliative = disease control without curative intent (≠ palliative care service).
ClassExamplesHallmark toxicity
ChemotherapyCarboplatin/cisplatin, pemetrexed, etoposide, taxanes, lurbinectedinN/V/diarrhea, cytopenias, neuropathy, nephropathy, mucositis
ImmunotherapyPembrolizumab, nivolumab ± ipilimumab, atezolizumab, durvalumabThe "-itises" — any organ, may appear late or after stopping
Targeted (TKIs)Osimertinib, erlotinib (EGFR); alectinib, crizotinib (ALK); crizotinib, entrectinib, lorlatinib (ROS1)Rash (EGFR), diarrhea, anemia/fatigue; rarely pneumonitis, QTc prolongation
2023–25 update: Immunotherapy has moved around surgery for resectable NSCLC — neoadjuvant nivolumab+chemo (CheckMate 816), perioperative pembrolizumab (KEYNOTE-671), adjuvant atezolizumab (IMpower010), perioperative durvalumab (AEGEAN). And adjuvant osimertinib (ADAURA) is standard for EGFR-mutated resected stage IB–IIIA disease, with an overall-survival benefit.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild: who to screen, the symptomatic vs asymptomatic split, the biopsy rule, the staging set, and the three treatment classes — then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases 10 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Screening the asymptomatic patient

47-year-old with HLD, HTN, CAD presents to establish care, no complaints. 20 pack-year history; strong family history of lung cancer (mother, father, paternal grandparent, all smokers).

What are their risk factors for lung cancer?Smoking history / pack-years and a strong family history of lung cancer.
Would you screen for lung cancer now?No. Despite elevated risk, screening for asymptomatic patients begins at age 50. At 47 with 20 pack-years they don't yet meet the age criterion.
How would you counsel them?Emphasize smoking cessation (the single highest-yield intervention), and plan to start annual low-dose CT at age 50 — note the deck frames this as "low-dose CT in 3 years at 50."

Case 2 — Newly metastatic adenocarcinoma

The same patient returns 5 years later (age 52) with 3 months of cough, 2 weeks of hemoptysis, fatigue, 20-lb weight loss over 6 months, and persistent RUQ pain. Still smoking 1 ppd.

Next step in evaluation?For symptomatic patients, a CXR is the first step; add labs (CBC, BMP, LFTs) to evaluate for other causes. (CXR shows a 2.5 cm ill-defined nodule.)
Next step after the abnormal CXR?CT chest + abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast for staging. (CT shows a 2.5 cm spiculated nodule plus multiple liver hypodensities.)
Where do you biopsy, and why?Consult IR for liver biopsy — sampling the most advanced (distant) lesion gives both diagnosis and staging (liver mets = stage IV). Send tissue for molecular and PD-L1 testing.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 58-year-old man quit smoking 12 years ago after a 30 pack-year history. He is asymptomatic. Which is the most appropriate recommendation?

  1. No screening — he quit more than 10 years ago
  2. Annual low-dose CT
  3. Annual chest radiograph
  4. Low-dose CT only if he develops symptoms
Answer & explanationB. He meets USPSTF 2021 criteria: age 50–80, ≥20 pack-years, and quit within the past 15 years (12 < 15). Annual low-dose CT is indicated (A is wrong — the cutoff is 15, not 10 years; the ACS 2023 guideline removes the quit-time limit altogether). Chest radiograph (C) is not a screening tool — the NLST showed LDCT, not CXR, reduces lung-cancer mortality. Waiting for symptoms (D) defeats the purpose of screening, which is to catch disease while it's curable.

2. A 64-year-old woman with biopsy-proven lung adenocarcinoma has a 3 cm lung mass and a solitary adrenal lesion that is the only possible site of distant disease. Which approach best establishes diagnosis and stage with one procedure?

  1. Bronchoscopic biopsy of the lung mass
  2. Biopsy of the adrenal lesion
  3. Sputum cytology
  4. Repeat the lung biopsy for more tissue
Answer & explanationB. Biopsy the most advanced lesion that is safe to sample. If the adrenal lesion is malignant, the patient is stage IV — diagnosis and stage in a single procedure. Sampling the lung mass (A, D) confirms the diagnosis but leaves the critical staging question (is the adrenal a met?) unanswered. Sputum cytology (C) is insensitive and yields no staging or tissue for molecular testing.

3. After diagnosing NSCLC, which staging study is most important specifically for detecting central nervous system metastases?

  1. PET-CT
  2. CT chest/abdomen/pelvis with contrast
  3. MRI brain with and without contrast
  4. Bronchoscopy with EBUS
Answer & explanationC. MRI brain with and without contrast is the test for CNS staging. PET-CT (A) is excellent for body staging but unreliable intracranially — the brain's high glucose uptake always lights up, masking metastases. CT C/A/P (B) assesses thoracic and extrathoracic non-CNS disease. EBUS/bronchoscopy (D) clarifies nodal (N) status, not brain involvement.

4. A never-smoker is diagnosed with metastatic lung adenocarcinoma. Which test most directly determines whether she is a candidate for targeted therapy?

  1. PD-L1 immunohistochemistry only
  2. Molecular testing for EGFR, ALK, and ROS1
  3. Serum tumor markers
  4. PFTs
Answer & explanationB. Targeted therapy is driven by molecular alterations — EGFR, ALK, ROS1 (and others). Roughly 50% of never-smokers harbor a targetable driver, so molecular testing is mandatory. PD-L1 (A) guides immunotherapy, a different class. Serum tumor markers (C) have no role in selecting lung-cancer therapy. PFTs (D) assess surgical candidacy, not drug selection.

5. A 60-year-old man has a fully resected stage II EGFR exon 19–deleted NSCLC. Which adjuvant therapy is now standard of care?

  1. Osimertinib
  2. Pembrolizumab monotherapy regardless of PD-L1
  3. No adjuvant therapy — surgery is sufficient
  4. Erlotinib indefinitely
Answer & explanationA. The ADAURA trial established adjuvant osimertinib for resected stage IB–IIIA EGFR-mutated NSCLC (FDA-approved Dec 2020; overall-survival benefit reported 2023). Pembrolizumab (B) has perioperative roles but isn't the answer for a driver-mutated tumor, where targeted therapy takes precedence. "Surgery alone" (C) is no longer standard for this group. Erlotinib (D) is an older EGFR TKI not used in this adjuvant setting; osimertinib is preferred (better CNS penetration and efficacy).

Take-Home Points

  1. Screen the right people: annual low-dose CT for 50–80 yo with ≥20 pack-years (USPSTF requires current or quit ≤15 yr; ACS 2023 drops that limit). Asymptomatic → risk assessment; symptomatic → CXR first.
  2. Tissue is the issue, and biopsy the most advanced lesion when safe — one sample gives diagnosis and staging (distant met = stage IV).
  3. Stage fully before treating: CT C/A/P or PET-CT, MRI brain, node sampling, PFTs if a surgical candidate, plus PD-L1 and molecular testing.
  4. Molecular markers and PD-L1 steer targeted therapy and immunotherapy — and now change early-stage care (adjuvant osimertinib). Early stage can be cured; survival falls sharply with each stage, which is why screening matters.
