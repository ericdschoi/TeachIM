What is the classic presentation of an acute gout flare?	Abrupt onset of a warm, red, excruciatingly painful, swollen peripheral joint, sometimes with fever; up to 90% of gout patients involve the first MTP at some point (~73% of flares are at the MTP).
Why must arthrocentesis be done when gout is suspected with signs of infection?	Septic arthritis is a surgical emergency (rapid joint destruction) and can mimic — or coexist with — gout; crystals do NOT exclude infection.
Synovial fluid cell count ranges for non-inflammatory, inflammatory, and septic?	<2,000 = non-inflammatory; 2,000–75,000 = inflammatory; >75,000 = high risk for septic (overlaps exist — gout can exceed 100K; septic can be lower after antibiotics).
Crystal appearance of gout vs pseudogout?	Gout = needle-shaped, negatively birefringent monosodium urate (yellow when parallel to the red compensator). Pseudogout (CPPD) = rhomboid, weakly positively birefringent calcium pyrophosphate.
Three first-line options for an acute gout flare?	NSAIDs, corticosteroids (systemic or intra-articular), or colchicine; choose by comorbidities.
When are NSAIDs a poor choice for a gout flare?	History of GI bleeding, renal disease, or ischemic cardiac disease.
When should colchicine be used for a flare, and key cautions?	Only effective within ~48 h of onset; renally dose in CKD; main side effect is diarrhea; caution with statins (myotoxicity) and CYP3A4 inhibitors.
What agent treats a gout flare when NSAIDs, steroids, and colchicine are all contraindicated?	Anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist), off-label but far cheaper than canakinumab; with rheumatology referral; avoid in active infection.
Common medication precipitants of gout?	Loop and thiazide diuretics, aspirin, ACE inhibitors, ARBs (except losartan), ethambutol, pyrazinamide, niacin.
Which antihypertensives lower gout risk?	Losartan (uricosuric via URAT1 inhibition) and amlodipine — reasonable preferential BP agents in gout patients.
Dietary contributors to gout vs protective?	Increase risk: red meat, shellfish, alcohol (beer & spirits), fructose-rich drinks. Protective: higher dairy intake.
Indications to start urate-lowering therapy (ACR 2020)?	>=2 flares/year, tophi, or 1 flare + radiographic erosions; also consider after 1 flare if CKD stage >=3, serum urate >9 mg/dL, or urolithiasis.
First-line urate-lowering therapy and the serum urate target?	Allopurinol (a xanthine oxidase inhibitor), treat-to-target serum urate <6 mg/dL (often <5 to dissolve tophi).
How is allopurinol started and titrated?	Start 100 mg daily (≤50 mg/day if CKD), up-titrate q2–4 weeks to target urate <6; max ~800 mg/day.
Why is prophylaxis given when starting ULT, and for how long?	Starting ULT without prophylaxis precipitates flares as urate falls; give low-dose colchicine, NSAID, or prednisone for 3–6 months until the target ULT dose is reached.
What is allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome and who is at risk?	Cutaneous reaction, fever, eosinophilia, abnormal LFTs, and renal failure; risk markedly higher with HLA-B*5801 (highest in Han Chinese, Korean, Thai ~7.4%, and African American ~3.8%) — screen these groups and avoid allopurinol if positive.
Why is febuxostat second-line?	FDA boxed warning for increased cardiovascular and all-cause death (CARES, NEJM 2018); reserve for allopurinol intolerance or failure.
Does starting ULT during an acute flare worsen it, and should chronic ULT be stopped during a flare?	No — starting ULT during a flare does not worsen or prolong it; patients already on ULT should continue it through flares.
