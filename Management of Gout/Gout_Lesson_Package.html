Learning Objectives

  1. Describe the presentation of acute gout and contrast it with acute septic arthritis.
  2. Identify first-line treatments for an acute gout flare and pick the preferred agent by comorbidity.
  3. Counsel patients on modifiable risk factors that reduce future flares.
  4. State the indications to start urate-lowering therapy (ULT) plus prophylaxis in a patient presenting with acute gout.

Pre-work send the day before

1. A patient with a hot, swollen first toe has crystals on aspiration. Does that rule out septic arthritis?

No. Crystals confirm crystal arthritis but do not exclude infection — gout and septic arthritis can coexist. If infection is suspected, send Gram stain/culture and treat empirically while awaiting results.

2. Name the four indications to start urate-lowering therapy (ACR 2020).

≥2 flares/year, tophi, or 1 flare + radiographic erosions; also consider after a single flare if CKD stage ≥3, serum urate >9 mg/dL, or urolithiasis.

3. Your patient has diabetes and a flare. Which flare drug do you avoid, and why?

Systemic corticosteroids worsen glycemic control; prefer an NSAID or colchicine (if <48 h, renally dosed) — or an intra-articular steroid for monoarticular disease.

Teaching Script ~30 min core, runs off the board's six tabs

Hook 2 min

"Your 72-year-old diabetic comes in with a hot, swollen ankle overnight. The resident says 'classic gout, start prednisone.' Name one thing that could go very wrong if you skip a step — and one thing that could go wrong with the prednisone itself." Surface septic arthritis and hyperglycemia, then frame: the board walks Diagnosis → Acute Rx → Modifiable RFs → Urate-Lowering Rx → Takeaways → Cases.

Segment 1 — Diagnosis & mimics 8 min

ASK — "What single bedside procedure separates gout from septic arthritis, and what do you send?" (Arthrocentesis: cell count, Gram stain/culture, crystals.)

Acute gout: abrupt warm, red, exquisitely painful joint, often the first MTP (~73% of flares); ±fever, ↑inflammatory markers, leukocytosis. Mimics: pseudogout (rhomboid CPP crystals, larger joints, knee >50%), hemarthrosis (sanguineous fluid), and septic arthritis (the surgical emergency).

Synovial WBCInterpretation
<2,000Non-inflammatory
2,000–75,000Inflammatory (gout typical, with MSU crystals)
>75,000High risk for septic arthritis
Pearl: the curves overlap — septic arthritis can be <75K after antibiotics, and gout can exceed 100K. Cover infection when the story fits even if crystals are present.

Segment 2 — Acute flare therapy by comorbidity 8 min

ASK — "Same flare in three patients: a GI bleeder, a brittle diabetic, and someone on a statin with CKD. One drug each?" (Avoid NSAID in the bleeder, steroids in the diabetic, full-dose colchicine in the statin/CKD patient → pick the one that fits.)
AgentTypical doseAvoid / caution
NSAIDNaproxen 500 BID / indomethacin 50 TID ×5–7 d (all equivalent)GI bleed, renal disease, ischemic cardiac disease
SteroidPrednisone 40 mg ×5 d (extend 7–10 d if severe/tophaceous); triamcinolone IA 40/20/10 mg by joint sizeDiabetes (hyperglycemia), concurrent infection
Colchicine1.2 mg → 0.6 mg in 1 h → 0.6 mg QD/BID until resolvedOnly if <48 h; renally dose in CKD; statin myotoxicity, CYP3A4 inhibitors; diarrhea
IL-1 (anakinra)100 mg SQ daily ×3–5 d (off-label, cheaper than canakinumab)Active infection; use for polyarticular flare when others contraindicated; with rheumatology

Segment 3 — Modifiable risk factors 5 min

Diet ↑risk: red meat, shellfish, beer & spirits, fructose drinks; dairy is protective. Meds ↑risk: loop/thiazide diuretics, aspirin, ACE-I, ARBs (except losartan), some TB drugs (ethambutol, pyrazinamide), niacin. Metabolic: HTN, diabetes, hyperlipidemia — treating them lowers risk; losartan and amlodipine are preferred BP agents (mild uricosuric effect).

Pearl: do NOT stop indicated low-dose aspirin to manage gout (ACR 2020) — the cardiovascular benefit outweighs the small flare risk.

Segment 4 — Urate-lowering therapy 7 min

ASK — "Who actually needs a urate-lowering drug, and what's the urate number you're chasing?" (≥2 flares/yr, tophi, or 1 flare + erosions; target <6.)

Start ULT for ≥2 flares/yr, tophi, or 1 flare + erosions — also consider after one flare if CKD ≥3, urate >9, or urolithiasis. Allopurinol first-line (start 100 mg/day, ≤50 if CKD; titrate q2–4 wks; max ~800 mg). Treat-to-target urate <6 (<5 for tophi). Always pair with prophylaxis (low-dose colchicine, NSAID, or prednisone) for 3–6 months until the target dose is reached. Starting ULT during a flare does not worsen it; continue ULT through flares.

HLA-B*5801: allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome (rash, fever, eosinophilia, ↑LFTs, renal failure) is far more likely with this allele — screen patients of Han Chinese, Korean, Thai, or African-American ancestry and avoid allopurinol if positive.
ASK — "Why not just start febuxostat for everyone?" (CARES boxed warning — increased CV and all-cause death; it's second-line.)

Retrieval close 5 min

Erase the board. Have learners rebuild from memory: the synovial WBC cutoff that worries you for septic arthritis, the flare drug to avoid in a diabetic, the four ULT indications, and the urate target. Finish with the take-home points.

Cases from the original board — click to reveal

Case 1. 72M with insulin-dependent DM2, HTN (on an ACE-I), HLD (on a statin); 1 day of a hot, swollen, painful right ankle, no trauma, no fever.

Next diagnostic step — and why?

X-ray the joint (fracture, erosions) and arthrocentesis to rule out septic arthritis — the can't-miss emergency. Aspirate: negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals (gout); Gram stain/culture negative.

Best way to manage his flare?

Avoid steroids (diabetes) → short course of NSAID or colchicine. Review meds: switch the ACE-I to losartan (uricosuric, also treats his HTN).

His gout risk factors?

Non-modifiable: age, male sex. Modifiable: diabetes, HTN, hyperlipidemia, ACE-I use.

Case 2. 49M, Han Chinese, three gouty flares this year, CKD and HFpEF; stone-like nodule on the right elbow; hot, tender right great toe and 3rd MCP.

Classify the presentation.

Tophaceous gout with an acute polyarticular flare.

Management approach?

Flare: prednisone preferred (renal disease + HF). Start allopurinol (≥3 flares; if GFR <60, start 50 mg/day). Prophylaxis: renally-dosed colchicine or low-dose prednisone, continued through up-titration.

Lab to check before allopurinol?

HLA-B*5801 — elevated allopurinol-hypersensitivity risk in Han Chinese ancestry; if positive, don't use allopurinol.

Goal urate?

<6 mg/dL (consider <5 to dissolve tophi).

Case 3. 60M with known tophaceous gout on allopurinol, recently treated for LLE cellulitis; 3 days of severe left-ankle pain and swelling; T 38.5°C, warm, erythematous, exquisitely tender ankle.

Initial labs?

CBC (WBC 20), CMP, ESR/CRP, ankle X-ray, blood cultures; serum urate (may be falsely low/normal/high in a flare — don't anchor on it).

Next step?

Arthrocentesis: synovial WBC 100,000, culture pending; polarized microscopy shows MSU crystals. The high count + fever + recent skin infection means you must treat for possible septic arthritis even with crystals present.

Management?

Consider ortho consult for washout/source control, start antibiotics, and avoid steroids during active infection — treat gout with NSAIDs/colchicine (or after washout) once infection is controlled.

Board-Style MCQs ABIM format

1. A 66-year-old man with CKD stage 3 and a prior GI bleed presents with an acutely hot, swollen first MTP that began 12 hours ago. Aspirate: MSU crystals, Gram stain negative, WBC 22,000. Which is the best treatment for this flare?

  1. Naproxen 500 mg BID
  2. Colchicine 1.2 mg then 0.6 mg, renally dosed
  3. Allopurinol 300 mg daily
  4. High-dose aspirin
  5. Febuxostat 80 mg daily
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

B. Within 48 h, colchicine (renally dosed) is appropriate and avoids both the NSAID (GI bleed, CKD) and steroids. A is contraindicated by the bleed and CKD; C and E are urate-lowering drugs that do nothing for the acute flare and can trigger flares if started without prophylaxis; D — aspirin raises urate and isn't a flare treatment. (An intra-articular steroid would be another reasonable choice.)

2. A 58-year-old woman has had three gout flares this year. Serum urate is 8.2 mg/dL; renal function normal. After treating the current flare, which long-term plan best follows ACR 2020?

  1. No ULT; manage flares as they occur
  2. Start allopurinol 100 mg daily, titrate q2–4 weeks to urate <6, with colchicine prophylaxis
  3. Start febuxostat as first-line ULT
  4. Start allopurinol 600 mg daily immediately
  5. Low-purine diet only
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

B. ≥2 flares/year is a strong ULT indication; allopurinol is first-line, started low and titrated to target <6, with prophylaxis during titration. A ignores the indication; C — febuxostat is second-line (CARES boxed warning); D — high starting doses raise hypersensitivity risk and are not recommended; E rarely reaches target alone.

3. Before prescribing allopurinol to a 45-year-old man of Han Chinese ancestry, which test most reduces the risk of a life-threatening reaction?

  1. HLA-B*5801 genotyping
  2. G6PD activity
  3. TPMT activity
  4. Baseline serum urate only
  5. Anti-CCP antibody
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

A. HLA-B*5801 strongly predicts allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome and is recommended before allopurinol in higher-prevalence ancestries (Han Chinese, Korean, Thai, African-American); avoid the drug if positive. G6PD relates to rasburicase/oxidant drugs; TPMT to thiopurines; D and E don't address the reaction risk.

4. A 70-year-old man with gout, hypertension, and known coronary disease needs urate-lowering therapy. Which choice is most appropriate?

  1. Allopurinol, titrated to target, as first-line
  2. Febuxostat as first-line because it is more potent
  3. No ULT because of his cardiac disease
  4. Pegloticase as first-line
  5. Probenecid regardless of renal function
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

A. Allopurinol is first-line for all patients including those with cardiac disease. B — febuxostat carries an FDA boxed warning for CV/all-cause death (CARES) and is second-line; C — cardiac disease is not a reason to withhold needed ULT; D — pegloticase is reserved for refractory tophaceous gout; E — probenecid is uricosuric and ineffective/contraindicated with reduced renal function and stones.

5. A 62-year-old man on chronic allopurinol presents with a single acutely inflamed knee, fever to 38.6°C, and a recent overlying skin infection. Aspirate: WBC 90,000 with MSU crystals; culture pending. What is the best next step?

  1. Treat empirically for septic arthritis (antibiotics ± washout) and avoid systemic steroids
  2. Diagnose a gout flare based on the crystals and give prednisone
  3. Stop his allopurinol
  4. Start febuxostat
  5. Reassure and discharge on colchicine
Answer + why each distractor is wrong

A. Crystals don't exclude infection; with WBC 90K, fever, and adjacent cellulitis, treat as septic arthritis (cover and consider washout) and avoid steroids during active infection. B anchors on crystals and risks steroids in infection; C — ULT should be continued through flares, not stopped; D — a urate-lowering drug doesn't treat the acute problem; E misses a joint-space emergency.

Currency Notes checked June 2026

The original is recent (Oct 2024) and built directly on the 2020 ACR gout guideline, which remains the current standard — no update slide was needed for standing content. Verified:

  • ACR 2020 gout guideline (FitzGerald, Arthritis Care Res 2020) — allopurinol first-line for all including CKD, start-low-and-titrate, treat-to-target urate <6, prophylaxis during titration, the four ULT indications, HLA-B*5801 screening, and "don't stop indicated low-dose aspirin." No newer ACR gout guideline supersedes it as of June 2026.
  • Febuxostat CV safety — the deck's boxed-warning teaching reflects CARES (White, NEJM 2018): similar overall MACE but higher CV and all-cause death vs allopurinol; febuxostat stays second-line.
  • Newer agents — dotinurad, a selective URAT1 reabsorption inhibitor, is approved in Japan and remains investigational in the US (Phase 1 US data 2024–25); not yet a US prescribing option. Pegloticase remains the choice for refractory tophaceous gout.

Take-Home Points

  1. Tap the joint — arthrocentesis rules out septic arthritis whenever infection is possible or the picture is ambiguous; crystals don't exclude infection.
  2. Treat flares with NSAIDs, colchicine (if <48 h), or steroids — matched to comorbidities; anakinra (with rheumatology) if all are contraindicated.
  3. Start ULT (allopurinol first-line) for ≥2 flares/yr, 1 flare + erosions, or tophi — also consider after 1 flare with CKD ≥3, urate >9, or urolithiasis.
  4. Treat-to-target urate <6, up-titrating q2–4 weeks, and keep flare prophylaxis running until the target dose is reached. Screen HLA-B*5801 before allopurinol in high-risk ancestry.
