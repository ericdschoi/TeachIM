Learning Objectives

  1. Identify relative and absolute contraindications to medication abortion.
  2. Counsel patients on options for medication abortion and correct administration of the medications.
  3. Identify adverse effects of medication abortion and when patients should be referred for emergency care or procedural abortion.
  4. Locate your local laws and institutional guidelines regarding prescription of medication abortion.
Facilitator prep (30–60 min): verify the deck's QR codes/links still work, review your state law (Guttmacher Institute tracker) and institutional policy, and consider adding one local-resources slide (where medication abortion can be prescribed locally, who manages complications, local restrictions). Print the pre/post-test. Allow learners who are uncomfortable with the topic to excuse themselves.

Pre-work (send the day before)

Three retrieval questions. Answers hidden — try first, then click.

1. What are the two medications used for first-trimester medication abortion, and what does each one do?Mifepristone — a progesterone-receptor modulator that promotes placental separation, cervical dilation, and sensitizes the myometrium to prostaglandins. Misoprostol — a prostaglandin E1 analogue that softens the cervix and causes uterine contractions. The combination is 95–98% effective up to 11 weeks.
2. Up to what gestational age is medication abortion used, and how is gestational age usually established?Up to 11 weeks (77 days); use beyond 10 weeks is off-label. Gestational age is estimated from the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP). Ultrasound is only needed if the LMP is unknown, the patient conceived on hormonal contraception, or ectopic pregnancy is suspected.
3. Name two absolute contraindications to any medication abortion.Ectopic pregnancy, IUD in place, known coagulopathy, or anticoagulant therapy (any two). Medication-specific: chronic corticosteroids/adrenal failure (mifepristone), porphyria (misoprostol).

Teaching Script (timed)

0:00–2:00 — Hook (Impact board)

Open on the Impact slide. Since Dobbs (June 2022), hundreds of thousands of patients per year lost local access to abortion care; lack of access disproportionately affects people of color and lower-income patients. Adults with uteruses are half of an internist's panel — and competent prescribing here requires exactly two medications. Reveal the three numbers on the board: 250k patients/year without access, 27 states with mandatory waiting periods, 11 gestational weeks of safety/efficacy.

ASK: "Before we start — who do you think can legally prescribe medication abortion where we practice, and where would you look that up?" (Answer: depends on state; Guttmacher Institute keeps the current map. Surface this up front because every later decision sits inside it.)

15:00–20:00 — Objective 3: Follow-up, side effects, complications (Follow-up & Side Effects boards)
ASK: "What follow-up is needed at 1–2 weeks? At 4 weeks?" (click to reveal)

1–2 wk: all patients must be offered phone or in-person follow-up within 14 days (NAF 2020) — they may opt for self-assessment. Screen for ongoing pregnancy symptoms (breast tenderness, fatigue, morning sickness), no passage of clot/POC, ongoing bleeding. Optional pelvic US — an empty gestational sac confirms success; there is no endometrial-thickness cutoff, so don't let an "RPOC" read trigger reflex re-intervention — route concerns to family planning. 4 wk: urine pregnancy test (home or clinic) — not before 4 weeks (false positives from residual hCG). Still symptomatic and <11 wk EGA? One more vaginal misoprostol dose is an option before aspiration.

Access & Regulatory Update (2023–2026) added

The clinical teaching above is unchanged. The access landscape has moved since the June 2023 original:

  • Jan 2023 — FDA REMS modification: the in-person dispensing requirement was removed; certified retail and mail-order pharmacies may dispense mifepristone with a prescription from a certified prescriber. CVS and Walgreens completed certification and began dispensing in select states in March 2024.
  • Jun 13, 2024 — FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine: the U.S. Supreme Court unanimously held the challengers lacked standing — FDA approval and the REMS changes (2016, 2021–23) remain in effect.
  • May 2026 — Louisiana v. FDA: a Fifth Circuit panel (May 1, 2026) ordered a return to in-person-only dispensing; on May 14, 2026 the Supreme Court stayed that order (Danco Laboratories v. Louisiana), preserving telehealth prescribing and mail/pharmacy dispensing while the litigation continues. A merits ruling is still pending — check the current status and your state law before prescribing.
Teaching tie-in: this is exactly why Objective 4 ("locate your local laws") is a standing objective, not a footnote. The Guttmacher Institute tracker and your institution's policy are the two sources to check the week you give this talk.

Cases (from the original board — answers click-to-reveal)

Case 1 — 30-year-old transgender man (they/them) with a uterus, missed period, LMP 6 weeks ago, no hormonal contraception

1a. What is your next step in evaluation?Confirm pregnancy with urine hCG. (It returns positive.)
1a. What is the gestational age?EGA is 6 weeks by LMP — no ultrasound needed given certain dates and no hormonal contraception.
1a. What history might lead you to suspect ectopic pregnancy?Prior ectopic pregnancy, tubal surgery or pathology, IUD in place, IVF conception — these warrant ultrasound.
1a. How do you counsel on the patient's choice regarding their pregnancy?Review all options without directiveness: continuing the pregnancy, adoption after birth, and termination (medication or procedural). Provide written resources.
1b. They choose medication abortion (no medical problems, no meds). Any contraindications? What do you check in your state?No contraindications (none of: ectopic, IUD, coagulopathy/anticoagulation; no steroids/adrenal failure; no porphyria). Check Guttmacher for waiting periods/restrictions — 27 states have mandatory waiting periods between counseling and abortion.
1c. You prescribe mifepristone + misoprostol ×1. How do you counsel on side effects?Severe cramping and bleeding heavier than menses, typically 2.5–4 h after misoprostol, usually ~1 h of heavy flow (can be up to 24 h); nausea/vomiting/diarrhea; fever <24 h, hot flushes, chills.
1c. They call back: heavy bleeding × 5 h, soaking 4 pads/hour for the past 2 hours, no lightheadedness or dyspnea. Next step?This exceeds the threshold (>2 pads/h × 2 h) — evaluate in the ED. Uterine aspiration is needed in ~1% for bleeding; transfusion <0.1%.

Case 2 — 21-year-old woman with SLE on prednisone 5 mg daily; urine hCG positive; wants medication abortion; unclear LMP (9–11 wk ago, irregular periods)

2a. Per the CDC, which medical conditions raise the risk of adverse health events from pregnancy?SLE, insulin-dependent diabetes with complications, uncontrolled hypertension, heart disease (valvular/ischemic/peripartum), bariatric surgery within 2 years, solid-organ transplant within 2 years (see CDC US-MEC for the full list). Offer MFM referral if she chooses to continue a pregnancy.
2b. What is the next step in evaluation?Ultrasound — she is unclear on her LMP, so imaging is needed to confirm EGA <11 weeks. (Transabdominal US shows EGA 9 weeks.)
2b. What medication(s) do you prescribe?Misoprostol 800 mcg q3h × 3 doses. Mifepristone is contraindicated because of her chronic corticosteroid use (antiglucocorticoid effect → risk of adrenal insufficiency). At EGA 9 wk the multi-dose regimen also covers the 9–11-wk window.
2b. How do you counsel on the risk of incomplete abortion?Higher than average: misoprostol-alone success is 78–93% vs 93–95%+ for combination (2019 meta-analysis), and incomplete-abortion risk rises as EGA approaches 10 weeks. Review the bleeding/infection precautions and the aspiration referral pathway up front.
2c. She anticipates future pregnancy. Recommend Rh testing before the abortion?No. Rh testing is no longer recommended for abortion under 12 weeks, even for known Rh-negative patients — maternal-fetal blood mixing at this gestational age is below the alloimmunization threshold (Society of Family Planning 2022).
2c. Telephone visit 1 week later: ongoing morning sickness and breast tenderness. Next steps?If still <11 wk EGA, she may take one additional dose of vaginal misoprostol, or be referred for uterine aspiration. A urine pregnancy test now would be uninterpretable (<4 wk). Pelvic US showing an empty gestational sac would confirm completion.
2c. She wants a copper IUD afterward. When can contraception start?Per ACOG, every method except an IUD and sterilization can start on day 1 of the abortion. IUD placement requires confirmed completion of the abortion first (an IUD in place is a contraindication to medication abortion, and placement into a continuing pregnancy must be avoided).

Board-Style MCQs added

1. A 27-year-old healthy woman requests medication abortion. She is certain her LMP was 7 weeks ago and conceived off contraception. Urine hCG is positive. Which management is most appropriate?
  1. Pelvic ultrasound before any prescription
  2. Mifepristone 200 mg PO, then misoprostol 800 mcg buccally 24–48 h later
  3. Misoprostol 800 mcg q3h × 3 doses
  4. Mifepristone 200 mg PO and misoprostol 800 mcg taken simultaneously
  5. Referral for procedural abortion only
Answer & explanationsB. Combination mifepristone → misoprostol 24–48 h later is the recommended regimen (95–98% effective ≤11 wk). A — ultrasound is unnecessary with a certain LMP, no hormonal contraception, and no ectopic risk factors. C — misoprostol-alone is reserved for mifepristone contraindication/unavailability (less effective). D — efficacy requires ≥24 h between the drugs. E — medication abortion is appropriate at 7 wk; procedural abortion is an alternative, not the only option.
2. A 24-year-old woman with adrenal insufficiency on chronic hydrocortisone requests medication abortion at 8 weeks EGA. Why is the standard combination regimen contraindicated?
  1. Misoprostol induces acute porphyria attacks in adrenal insufficiency
  2. Mifepristone blocks glucocorticoid receptors and can precipitate adrenal crisis
  3. Misoprostol is ineffective beyond 6 weeks gestation
  4. Mifepristone requires intact adrenal function for hepatic activation
  5. The combination causes refractory hypertension with steroid use
Answer & explanationsB. Mifepristone is a synthetic steroid that at higher doses antagonizes cortisol at the glucocorticoid receptor — it can negate exogenous steroids and precipitate adrenal insufficiency (monitor for hypokalemia and hypotension if ever used). She should receive misoprostol-only or procedural abortion. A — porphyria is a separate misoprostol-specific caution, unrelated to adrenal disease. C — misoprostol works through 11 weeks. D — invented mechanism. E — hypotension, not hypertension, is the adrenal-crisis concern.
3. Ten days after mifepristone/misoprostol at 6 weeks EGA, a patient reports persistent breast tenderness and morning sickness. Which next step is most appropriate?
  1. Urine pregnancy test today
  2. Serum quantitative hCG today, repeat in 48 h
  3. One additional dose of vaginal misoprostol
  4. Immediate uterine aspiration without further evaluation
  5. Reassurance; symptoms always persist 6 weeks
Answer & explanationsC. With ongoing pregnancy symptoms and EGA still <11 wk, an additional vaginal misoprostol dose is appropriate (alternatively pelvic US — an empty sac confirms success — or referral for aspiration). A — urine hCG is uninterpretable before 4 weeks post-abortion (residual hCG). B — serial quantitative hCG is not the recommended follow-up pathway here. D — aspiration is an option but not obligatory before a repeat misoprostol dose at <11 wk. E — persistent pregnancy symptoms at 1–2 wk suggest possible ongoing pregnancy and need action.
4. Six hours after misoprostol, a patient reports heavy bleeding soaking 3 pads per hour for the past 2 hours and feels lightheaded. What do you advise?
  1. Expected effect; recheck tomorrow
  2. Take a second dose of misoprostol now
  3. Oral iron and outpatient follow-up in 1 week
  4. Present to the emergency department now
  5. Urine pregnancy test to assess completion
Answer & explanationsD. Bleeding >2 pads/h for 2 consecutive hours defines prolonged/heavy bleeding (~1% of patients) — ED evaluation, possible aspiration; <0.1% need transfusion. Lightheadedness raises concern for hemodynamically significant loss. A — expected bleeding is heavier than menses but below this threshold. B — more misoprostol doesn't treat hemorrhage. C — too slow for active heavy bleeding. E — irrelevant in an emergency and uninterpretable this early.
5. A 31-year-old Rh-negative woman undergoes medication abortion at 8 weeks EGA. Regarding Rh testing and anti-D immune globulin, which is correct?
  1. Rh testing and anti-D are required for all abortions
  2. Anti-D is needed only if bleeding lasts >24 hours
  3. Neither Rh testing nor anti-D is recommended for abortion under 12 weeks
  4. Rh testing is needed, but anti-D can be deferred to 4-week follow-up
  5. Anti-D should be given only with misoprostol-alone regimens
Answer & explanationsC. Per the Society of Family Planning (2022) consensus, Rh testing is no longer recommended before abortion <12 weeks — even for known Rh-negative patients — because maternal-fetal blood mixing at this gestational age is insufficient to cause alloimmunization. A — outdated practice. B/D/E — invented conditions; the recommendation is independent of bleeding duration, follow-up timing, and regimen.

Take-Home Points

  1. Before prescribing: confirm pregnancy, estimate gestational age from LMP (± US), screen contraindications, and check whether your state has a mandatory waiting period.
  2. State and federal rules around abortion remain in active flux (see the 2023–26 update above) — always confirm your local law and the current federal status before prescribing.
  3. Medication abortion can be considered up to 11 weeks. Mifepristone + misoprostol combination therapy is more effective than misoprostol alone; misoprostol-alone (800 mcg q3h ×3) is the fallback when mifepristone is contraindicated or unavailable.
