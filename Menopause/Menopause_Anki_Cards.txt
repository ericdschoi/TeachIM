How is the menopausal transition (perimenopause) defined?	By irregular menses (a change in intermenstrual interval) plus hormonal symptoms (hot flashes, mood changes). Begins on average ~4 years before the last menstrual period.
How is menopause defined?	Retrospectively, after 12 consecutive months of amenorrhea. Mean age ~51.
Is menopause a clinical or laboratory diagnosis, and when is testing indicated?	Clinical diagnosis. In women >45 with irregular menses and classic symptoms, no testing is needed. Test (hCG, TSH, prolactin ± FSH) in women <45, and a full amenorrhea work-up if <40 (primary ovarian insufficiency).
Why is a single FSH a poor test for menopause?	FSH varies widely during the transition; irregular menses predict menopause better than FSH (SWAN study). FSH + estradiol can confirm completed menopause but is rarely needed.
What is the most effective treatment for moderate-to-severe hot flashes?	Menopausal hormone therapy (MHT).
Who is a candidate for systemic MHT?	Women with moderate-to-severe vasomotor symptoms who are <60 years old OR within 10 years of menopause; absolute risks are low in this group.
What are the contraindications to systemic MHT?	History of breast cancer, prior VTE or stroke/CHD, active liver disease, unexplained vaginal bleeding, and high-risk endometrial cancer.
When must progesterone be added to estrogen MHT?	In all patients with an intact uterus, to prevent endometrial hyperplasia/cancer.
Why is transdermal estrogen preferred over oral for some patients?	It confers lower risk of VTE and stroke; preferred for moderate ASCVD risk (5–10%) and migraine. Main downside is cost.
What did the Women's Health Initiative (2002) show, and what was its key limitation?	Excess CHD, stroke, VTE, and breast cancer with MHT — but the mean age was 63, far older than typical symptomatic patients, so results overstated risk for younger women.
How does the risk-benefit profile differ between estrogen-only and estrogen+progesterone MHT (ages 50–59, 5 yr)?	Estrogen alone is more favorable (e.g., fewer CHD and breast cancer cases); E+P carries more additional cases of CHD and breast cancer. Average absolute risks are small (~3 extra cases/1000 over 5 yr).
What is the recommended duration of MHT?	Generally 5–10 years, re-evaluating risk/benefit yearly and ideally stopping by age 60; longer use can be considered for severe symptoms after weighing risks. Rebound hot flashes are common on stopping.
What nonhormonal medications are effective for hot flashes?	SSRIs/SNRIs (venlafaxine and escitalopram best studied; paroxetine is the only FDA-approved one but is anticholinergic), and gabapentin/pregabalin. Avoid fluoxetine and sertraline for this indication.
What is the preferred treatment for isolated genitourinary syndrome of menopause (vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, recurrent UTIs)?	Vaginal moisturizers/lubricants for mild symptoms; vaginal (topical) estrogen is most effective with limited systemic absorption.
What is fezolinetant and its key safety monitoring?	Veozah, an NK3-receptor antagonist (FDA May 2023) for moderate-to-severe vasomotor symptoms; monitor LFTs (rare serious liver injury warning).
What is elinzanetant?	Lynkuet, a dual NK1/NK3-receptor antagonist (FDA Oct 2025) for moderate-to-severe vasomotor symptoms; ~73% VMS reduction and improves sleep.
What did the 2022 Menopause Society (NAMS) hormone therapy position statement conclude?	Benefits of hormone therapy outweigh risks for most healthy symptomatic women <60 years or within 10 years of menopause; HT remains most effective for VMS and genitourinary syndrome and prevents bone loss/fracture.
What are the menopause-related preventive priorities besides symptom control?	Estrogen deficiency raises atherosclerosis and osteoporosis risk: lifestyle ± statin by ASCVD risk; calcium, vitamin D, exercise, smoking cessation, and reduced alcohol for bone health.
A perimenopausal patient has heavy menstrual bleeding — what work-up is required?	Rule out malignancy: pregnancy test, pelvic exam, pelvic/transvaginal ultrasound ± endometrial biopsy (based on ultrasound findings and endometrial-cancer risk factors); consider TSH/prolactin.
