Learning Objectives

  1. Diagnose menopause and the menopausal transition from characteristic clinical features, and recognize when additional evaluation is warranted.
  2. Identify evidence-based hormonal and nonhormonal treatments for menopausal symptoms.
  3. Counsel patients on the risks and benefits of hormone therapy and on appropriate treatment duration.

Pre-Work send the day before

1. A 52-year-old whose last period was 14 months ago has hot flashes. What lab work-up is needed to "confirm" menopause? None — menopause is a clinical diagnosis (≥12 months of amenorrhea at a typical age). FSH is a poor single marker. Testing is reserved for women <45 or with atypical features.
2. Who is an appropriate candidate for systemic menopausal hormone therapy? A woman with moderate-to-severe hot flashes who is <60 years old OR within 10 years of menopause, without contraindications (breast cancer, prior VTE/stroke/CHD, active liver disease, unexplained bleeding, high-risk endometrial cancer). Add progesterone whenever the uterus is intact.
3. Name two nonhormonal medication options for hot flashes. SSRIs/SNRIs (venlafaxine and escitalopram best studied; paroxetine is the only FDA-approved one), gabapentin/pregabalin, and the newer NK-receptor antagonists fezolinetant and elinzanetant. (Avoid fluoxetine and sertraline for this indication.)

Teaching Script

Project the interactive board and reveal each click-to-reveal element only after the group answers. The board moves through Definitions/Diagnoses → Symptom management (peri- vs. menopause) → MHT candidacy and formulations → the risk/benefit chart → 6 cases.

Hook (2 min)

"A 50-year-old asks for 'the menopause blood test' and wants to know if hormones will give her breast cancer. What do you tell her about the test — and where does the breast-cancer fear come from?" (Surfaces by the end: usually no test needed, and the fear traces to the WHI's older population.)

Objective 1 — Definitions & Diagnosis (5 min)
ASK: "Define perimenopause vs. menopause." Reveal each definition.
TermDefinitionTiming
Menopausal transition (perimenopause)Irregular menses (changed interval) + hormonal symptoms (hot flashes, mood)~4 yr before LMP
Menopause12 consecutive months of amenorrhea (retrospective)Mean age ~51
ASK: "When is testing indicated?" Reveal the clinical-diagnosis button.

No testing in women >45 with classic symptoms. For symptoms <45, work up (hCG, TSH, prolactin ± FSH); <40 = full amenorrhea/primary-ovarian-insufficiency work-up (see the AUB talk).

Pearl: A single FSH is unreliable — it swings during the transition. In the SWAN study, irregular menses predicted menopause better than FSH.
Objective 2 — Symptom Management (8 min)
ASK: "A perimenopausal patient has heavy bleeding — what must you do first?" (Rule out malignancy: hCG, pelvic exam, pelvic ultrasound ± endometrial biopsy.)
SymptomFirst-line management
Hot flashes (mild)Lifestyle: avoid heat/EtOH/caffeine/cigarettes; fan; layers; weight loss; exercise
Hot flashes (mod–severe)MHT (most effective); nonhormonal SSRI/SNRI, gabapentin; NK antagonists (fezolinetant, elinzanetant)
Mood / sleepAs for hot flashes; SSRI preferred if mood predominates
GSM (dryness, dyspareunia, recurrent UTIs)Moisturizers/lubricants; vaginal estrogen (most effective, minimal systemic absorption)
Menstrual irregularityMHT; IUD or combined OCP until menopause
Pearl: Avoid fluoxetine and sertraline for hot flashes (no demonstrated benefit). Don't forget prevention — estrogen loss raises atherosclerosis and osteoporosis risk (lifestyle ± statin by ASCVD risk; calcium/vitamin D/exercise).
Objective 3 — Hormone Therapy & Counseling (8 min)
ASK: "Who is a candidate for systemic MHT, and for how long?" Reveal the candidate and duration buttons.

Candidate: moderate-to-severe hot flashes, <60 yr OR <10 yr post-menopause. Duration: ~5–10 yr, re-evaluate yearly, stop by ~60. Add progesterone whenever the uterus is intact.

ASK: "What formulation would you pick for a patient with migraine or moderate ASCVD risk?" (Transdermal estrogen — lower VTE/stroke risk than oral.)

The WHI (2002) showed excess CHD, stroke, VTE, and breast cancer — but the mean age was 63, so it overstated risk for the younger, symptomatic women we typically treat. Use of the risk/benefit chart (per 1000 women, ages 50–59, 5 years): estrogen-alone is more favorable than estrogen+progesterone; average absolute risks are small (~3 additional cases/1000 over 5 years).

Pearl (newer context): The 2022 Menopause Society (NAMS) statement reaffirms that HT benefits outweigh risks for healthy symptomatic women <60 / <10 yr post-menopause. New nonhormonal NK-receptor antagonists — fezolinetant (Veozah, FDA 2023; monitor LFTs) and elinzanetant (Lynkuet, FDA 2025) — expand options for those who can't or won't take hormones.
Retrieval Close (2 min)

Close the board and have learners rebuild the three take-home points and the MHT candidacy rule from memory.

Cases (from the original board)

Case 1 — 45-year-old, 3 months of amenorrhea, fatigue, hot flashes. First steps: pregnancy test (women in their 40s are a high-risk group for unintended pregnancy); consider TSH and prolactin (see AUB talk). If perimenopause is suspected, the most effective hot-flash medications are SSRI/SNRI (venlafaxine, escitalopram) and gabapentin/pregabalin.
Case 2 — 50-year-old, heavy prolonged bleeding (10–18 days, soaking pads q1–2h). Evaluate menorrhagia: pelvic exam, transvaginal ultrasound ± endometrial biopsy to rule out endometrial cancer; consider FSH/prolactin/TSH. If other causes are negative, manage with a progesterone IUD or OCPs. If sexually active, counsel on contraception (IUD ok to age 55, OCPs to age 51).
Case 3 — 49-year-old with DM, HTN, PAD, tobacco use, asking about MHT for hot flashes. MHT is contraindicated (PAD/established vascular disease; HTN + DM + tobacco together are high-risk). Combined OCP contraindications in perimenopause include CVD risk factors, prior VTE, migraine with aura, and breast cancer. Use nonhormonal options.
Case 4 — 48-year-old with migraine with aura, severe hot flashes. If MHT is used, choose transdermal estrogen (lower stroke risk than oral) + progesterone (uterus intact). Counsel: slight ↑ breast cancer risk; slight ↓ fracture and all-cause mortality.
Case 5 — 50-year-old post-hysterectomy (TAH), refractory hot flashes, ASCVD risk 7.5%. Transdermal estrogen (moderate ASCVD risk); no progesterone needed (no uterus). Estrogen-alone has a favorable profile: slight ↓ CAD, CVA, breast cancer, fracture, and mortality.
Case 6 — 52-year-old with breast cancer history, recurrent UTIs and dyspareunia unresponsive to nonhormonal therapy. Systemic MHT is contraindicated. Discuss low-dose vaginal estrogen with her oncologist: limited data do not show increased recurrence, but it may interfere with aromatase-inhibitor efficacy, so shared decision-making with oncology is essential.

Board-Style MCQs

1. A 53-year-old woman whose last menstrual period was 13 months ago reports hot flashes and disturbed sleep. Exam is normal. What is the most appropriate next step to confirm the diagnosis of menopause?
  1. Serum FSH and estradiol
  2. No testing — the diagnosis is clinical
  3. Transvaginal ultrasound
  4. Anti-Müllerian hormone level
  5. Endometrial biopsy
Answer & explanationB. Menopause is a clinical diagnosis in a woman >45 with ≥12 months of amenorrhea and typical symptoms; no labs are needed. FSH (A) is unreliable as a single test. C/E are indicated only for abnormal bleeding, not for confirming menopause. AMH (D) has no role here.
2. A 51-year-old with severe hot flashes, an intact uterus, and no contraindications wants the most effective therapy. Which regimen is appropriate?
  1. Estrogen alone
  2. Estrogen plus progesterone
  3. Progesterone alone
  4. Vaginal estrogen alone
  5. Gabapentin alone
Answer & explanationB. MHT is most effective for moderate-to-severe hot flashes, and progesterone must be added whenever the uterus is intact to prevent endometrial hyperplasia/cancer. Estrogen alone (A) is reserved for women without a uterus. Vaginal estrogen (D) treats GSM, not systemic VMS. Gabapentin (E) is nonhormonal second-line.
3. A 49-year-old with migraine with aura and an intact uterus has severe hot flashes and elects hormone therapy. Which formulation best reduces her thrombotic risk?
  1. Oral conjugated estrogen + progesterone
  2. Transdermal estradiol + progesterone
  3. Oral estradiol alone
  4. High-dose vaginal estradiol ring (Femring)
  5. Combined oral contraceptive
Answer & explanationB. Transdermal estrogen has lower VTE and stroke risk than oral and is preferred in migraine and moderate ASCVD risk; progesterone is added for the intact uterus. Oral routes (A, C) carry higher thrombotic risk. Femring (D) delivers systemic-level doses. Combined OCPs (E) are contraindicated in migraine with aura.
4. Which statement best explains why the 2002 WHI results overstated the risk of hormone therapy for typical symptomatic patients?
  1. It used only transdermal estrogen
  2. The mean participant age was 63, older than most symptomatic women starting therapy
  3. It enrolled only women with prior breast cancer
  4. It studied vaginal estrogen
  5. It lasted only 6 months
Answer & explanationB. WHI's mean age was ~63 — well beyond the typical <60 / <10-yr-post-menopause window — so absolute risks were higher than in younger symptomatic women, for whom benefits generally outweigh risks (2022 Menopause Society statement). A, C, D, E are factually wrong.
5. A 55-year-old with a history of breast cancer on an aromatase inhibitor has bothersome vasomotor symptoms and wants a nonhormonal prescription option. Which newer agent is an NK3-receptor antagonist FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe hot flashes, requiring LFT monitoring?
  1. Paroxetine
  2. Fezolinetant
  3. Clonidine
  4. Oral estradiol
  5. Raloxifene
Answer & explanationB. Fezolinetant (Veozah), an NK3-receptor antagonist (FDA May 2023), treats moderate-to-severe VMS and carries a warning for rare serious liver injury → monitor LFTs. Paroxetine (A) is an SSRI option but not an NK antagonist. Clonidine (C) is weakly effective. Estradiol (D) is hormonal and contraindicated here. Raloxifene (E) does not treat hot flashes (may worsen them).

Take-Home Points

  1. Menopause is primarily a clinical diagnosis from age and symptoms; lab testing is only for younger women or atypical features, and FSH is a poor single marker.
  2. Clinical features: menstrual irregularity, vasomotor symptoms, mood/sleep changes, and genitourinary symptoms (dryness, dyspareunia, recurrent UTIs).
  3. MHT is the most effective treatment for moderate-to-severe hot flashes; risk is low with good selection (<60 yr or <10 yr post-menopause). Add progesterone whenever the uterus is intact.
  4. Transdermal estrogen lowers VTE/stroke risk vs. oral; vaginal estrogen is preferred for isolated GSM.
  5. Nonhormonal options include SSRI/SNRI, gabapentin, and the NK-receptor antagonists fezolinetant and elinzanetant.
