What is a myeloproliferative neoplasm (MPN)?	A clonal disease of the hematopoietic stem cell causing overproduction of MATURE myeloid cells. (Contrast with MDS = abnormal/ineffective maturation.)
The four most common MPNs?	Essential thrombocythemia (ET), polycythemia vera (PV), primary myelofibrosis (PMF), and chronic myeloid leukemia (CML).
Which MPNs share driver mutations, and which?	ET, PV, and PMF are grouped by three common driver mutations: JAK2, CALR, MPL. CML is defined separately by BCR-ABL1.
Essential thrombocythemia — definition and mutations?	Clonal overproduction of platelets; associated with JAK2, CALR, or MPL. 10–20% are "triple negative" (no identifiable mutation) and may need a marrow biopsy.
ET — symptoms?	Often asymptomatic; vasomotor symptoms, TIAs, ocular migraines, 1st-trimester pregnancy loss; thrombosis or bleeding less common.
Differential for thrombocytosis?	Reactive (iron-deficiency anemia — commonly missed; infection/inflammation; post-splenectomy) vs primary (the 4 MPNs).
ET/PV treatment principle and risk stratification?	Goal is to prevent thrombosis; platelet/Hgb magnitude does NOT drive thrombosis risk (don't treat reactive thrombocytosis). Low risk (<60 yo, no prior thrombosis): observation + low-dose aspirin (PV: phlebotomy to Hct <45%). High risk (>60 yo OR prior thrombosis): add cytoreduction; anticoagulate if prior venous clot, aspirin if arterial.
Polycythemia vera — hallmark and key mutation?	Erythrocytosis (often with leukocytosis/thrombocytosis too); >95% carry JAK2 V617F. Suspect with Hgb >16.5 (M) / >16.0 (F), a 25% rise in RBC mass, or arterial/venous thrombosis.
WHO 2022 PV diagnostic thresholds?	Hgb >16.5 (M) / >16.0 (F) OR Hct >49% (M) / >48% (F), plus marrow panmyelosis and a JAK2 mutation; subnormal EPO is the minor criterion.
Classic thromboses that should raise suspicion for PV?	Budd-Chiari, portal/splenic/mesenteric vein thrombosis (splanchnic) — portal hypertension can mask the lab signs of PV.
EPO level across causes of erythrocytosis?	Relative (hemoconcentration/dehydration): normal EPO. Secondary (hypoxia — COPD/OSA/altitude/RAS; or EPO-secreting tumor — HCC/RCC/pheo): elevated EPO. PV: suppressed EPO (but normal in ~15%).
PV treatment?	Phlebotomy (goal Hct <45%) + low-dose aspirin; add a cytoreductive agent (hydroxyurea or interferon) if high risk. Ropeginterferon alfa-2b (Besremi) is now an NCCN-preferred first-line cytoreductive.
Primary myelofibrosis — pathology and findings?	Marrow replaced by fibrosis (→ extramedullary hematopoiesis); JAK2/CALR/MPL; can evolve from PV or ET. Classic: teardrop cells (dacrocytes) on smear and a "dry tap" with megakaryocyte hyperplasia.
PMF — symptoms and treatment?	Hepatosplenomegaly, bone pain, cytopenias (anemia, bleeding, infections). Only cure = allogeneic HSCT (for poor-prognosis transplant candidates); otherwise active surveillance/supportive care or JAK inhibitors (ruxolitinib).
Newer JAK inhibitors for myelofibrosis?	Four total: ruxolitinib, fedratinib, pacritinib (Vonjo — for severe thrombocytopenia, plt <50k), and momelotinib (Ojjaara — for MF with anemia). Chosen by blood counts.
Chronic myeloid leukemia — definition and genetics?	Malignancy of mature granulocytes; BCR-ABL1 fusion (Philadelphia chromosome, t(9;22)) → ↑tyrosine kinase activity. Granulocytes are morphologically normal but cytochemically abnormal with a LOW leukocyte alkaline phosphatase (LAP) score.
Differential for leukocytosis?	Leukemoid reaction (infection, stress, medications, tobacco) vs malignancy. Suspicious for CML: neutrophilia with left shift, basophilia, eosinophilia, constitutional symptoms, abdominal fullness; WBC can exceed 100k.
Natural history and treatment of CML?	Chronic phase (85% at diagnosis) → accelerated phase → blast crisis. First-line = a tyrosine kinase inhibitor (imatinib was the first); switch TKIs for resistance (BCR-ABL1 testing); blast phase may need induction chemo + TKI. Asciminib (Scemblix, a STAMP inhibitor) is approved for ≥2 prior TKIs or T315I.
AML vs CML vs ALL vs CLL — smear/marrow snapshot?	AML: myeloblasts/Auer rods, >20% marrow blasts (DIC esp. APL). CML: eosinophilia/basophilia, median WBC 100k, granulocytic hyperplasia <20% blasts, t(9;22). ALL: lymphoblasts, >20% marrow lymphoblasts, children, CNS infiltration (LP before tx). CLL: lymphocytosis + smudge cells, median age 70, Richter transformation, autoimmune cytopenias.
Why is leukostasis rare in CML despite high WBC?	CML (and CLL) tolerate higher WBC counts than acute leukemias; leukostasis/hyperleukocytosis is a hematologic emergency far more typical of AML/ALL.
