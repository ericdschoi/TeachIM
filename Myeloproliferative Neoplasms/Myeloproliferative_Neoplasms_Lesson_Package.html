Learning Objectives

  1. Diagnose the four most common myeloproliferative neoplasms.
  2. Build a differential for erythrocytosis, leukocytosis, and thrombocytosis.
  3. Describe the basic principles of treating MPNs.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What distinguishes an MPN from a myelodysplastic syndrome? Both are clonal stem-cell diseases, but MPNs overproduce mature myeloid cells, while MDS produces cells with abnormal/ineffective maturation (cytopenias with dysplasia). The four classic MPNs are ET, PV, PMF, and CML.
Q2. How does the EPO level help you work up erythrocytosis? Low/suppressed EPO points to polycythemia vera (the marrow is autonomously making RBCs, suppressing EPO). Elevated EPO points to a secondary cause (hypoxia or an EPO-secreting tumor). Normal EPO fits relative (hemoconcentration) erythrocytosis. EPO isn't perfectly sensitive/specific (normal in ~15% of PV), but it orients you.
Q3. Does a very high platelet count mean you should start aspirin? No. The magnitude of thrombocytosis does not predict thrombosis (or bleeding) risk, and reactive thrombocytosis (e.g., iron-deficiency anemia) does not warrant aspirin. Treatment decisions in ET/PV are driven by thrombosis risk stratification (age >60, prior thrombosis), not the count.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the interactive board (Myeloproliferative_Neoplasms_Optimized.pptx) and click through the tabs (Overview → ET → PV → PMF → CML → Cases), or rebuild the hematopoiesis tree on a whiteboard. Each cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt. Modular — any single MPN works as a short talk. Core ~30 min, +cases ~45 min. Print the learner worksheet.

Hook (2 min)

Put a CBC on the board: Hgb 18, platelets 600k, WBC 14k. "Three lines up — what's going on in this marrow?" Use it to introduce the MPN concept: a clonal stem-cell disease that overproduces mature cells (unlike MDS, where maturation is abnormal).

Overview 4 min

ASK: "What is an MPN, and what are the four most common?" Reveal: overproduction of mature myeloid cells → ET (platelets), PV (RBCs), CML (granulocytes), PMF (marrow fibrosis). ET/PV/PMF share the JAK2/CALR/MPL drivers; CML is BCR-ABL1. Any MPN can transform to acute leukemia or marrow failure.

Objective 1 & 2 — Diagnose each MPN with its differential 18 min

Essential thrombocythemia

Clonal platelet overproduction; JAK2/CALR/MPL (10–20% triple-negative → marrow biopsy). Symptoms: often asymptomatic; vasomotor symptoms, TIAs, ocular migraines, 1st-trimester loss.

ASK: "Differential for thrombocytosis?" Reactive (iron-deficiency anemia — commonly missed; infection/inflammation; post-splenectomy) vs primary (the 4 MPNs).
Pearl: Platelet count magnitude does NOT predict thrombosis — don't reflexively start aspirin for reactive thrombocytosis.

Polycythemia vera

Most common MPN; erythrocytosis (± leukocytosis/thrombocytosis); >95% JAK2 V617F. Suspect with Hgb >16.5 (M)/>16.0 (F), 25% rise in RBC mass, or thrombosis. Symptoms: aquagenic pruritus, headache, thrombosis, bleeding (acquired vWD), TIA/ocular migraine.

Pearl: Splanchnic-vein thrombosis (Budd-Chiari, portal/splenic/mesenteric) should trigger a PV hunt — portal hypertension can mask the lab signs.
ASK: "Walk the erythrocytosis differential by EPO level." Build the EPO physiology (hypoxia → renal EPO → marrow RBCs), then:

Primary myelofibrosis

Marrow replaced by fibrosis → extramedullary hematopoiesis; JAK2/CALR/MPL; can evolve from PV or ET. Hepatosplenomegaly, bone pain, cytopenias. Classic findings: teardrop cells (dacrocytes) on smear, "dry tap" with megakaryocyte hyperplasia on biopsy.

Chronic myeloid leukemia

Mature granulocyte excess; BCR-ABL1 (Philadelphia chromosome, t(9;22)) → ↑tyrosine kinase activity; low LAP score. Chronic phase (85% at dx) → accelerated → blast crisis.

ASK: "Differential for leukocytosis?" Leukemoid reaction (infection, stress, meds, tobacco) vs malignancy. Suspicious for CML: neutrophilia with left shift, basophilia, eosinophilia, constitutional symptoms, splenomegaly; WBC can exceed 100k but leukostasis is rare (vs acute leukemia).

Objective 3 — Treatment principles 8 min

ETPrevent thrombosis; risk-stratify (age >60, prior clot)Low risk: observe + low-dose ASA. High risk: cytoreduction (hydroxyurea/interferon); anticoagulate if prior venous, ASA if arterial
PVPrevent thrombosis; risk-stratifyPhlebotomy (goal Hct <45%) + low-dose ASA; add cytoreduction if high risk

2022–25 update: PV — ropeginterferon alfa-2b (Besremi) is now an NCCN-preferred first-line cytoreductive. Myelofibrosis — four JAK inhibitors chosen by counts: ruxolitinib, fedratinib, pacritinib (Vonjo; severe thrombocytopenia, plt <50k), momelotinib (Ojjaara; MF with anemia). CML — asciminib (Scemblix), a STAMP inhibitor binding the ABL myristoyl pocket, for ≥2 prior TKIs or T315I.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Have the group rebuild the four MPNs with their defining cell line and mutation, the EPO-based erythrocytosis differential, and the one treatment principle for each — then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases 10 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Erythrocytosis

53-year-old woman with fatigue. T 37.6°C, HR 76, BP 118/77, SaO₂ 99%. CBC shows erythrocytosis (Hgb 17.4, Hct 51.6).

What additional history?Evaluate for PV symptoms (headache, aquagenic pruritus, thrombosis) and secondary risk factors (smoking, cancer). She reports headache and itching after showering; denies smoking/cancer.
What test next?EPO level — a low level raises suspicion for PV (a normal level doesn't exclude it). Hers is low.
Next step?JAK2 mutation testing. She is diagnosed with PV → start low-dose aspirin for thrombosis prophylaxis (and phlebotomy to Hct <45%), refer to heme/onc.

Case 2 — Leukocytosis

45-year-old establishing care; routine CBC shows leukocytosis (WBC 29.3, with basophilia/eosinophilia on differential).

Differential for the leukocytosis?Infection, chronic inflammation, medication-induced (steroids, drug reaction), leukemoid reaction (surgery, GI bleed), tobacco, hematologic malignancy.
Next step in evaluation?Peripheral smear/differential — shows basophilia, eosinophilia, and promyelocytes. Then bone marrow biopsy: granulocyte hyperplasia with 10% myeloblasts; cytogenetics reveal t(9;22).
Diagnosis and treatment?Chronic myeloid leukemia → a tyrosine kinase inhibitor.

Case 3 — Cytopenias and a dry tap

67-year-old with 1 month of malaise and weakness. Labs: anemia and thrombocytopenia; smear shows teardrop cells.

What does the smear suggest, and next step?Teardrop cells (dacrocytes) suggest myelofibrosis → bone marrow biopsy. Initial attempt is a "dry tap"; pathology shows reticular fibrosis and megakaryocyte hyperplasia.
Diagnosis and management?Primary myelofibrosis. Determine prognosis for HSCT (the only cure); manage symptoms/cytopenias with transfusions, and consider a JAK inhibitor if transplant-ineligible with poor prognosis.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 58-year-old man has Hgb 18.9, Hct 56%, platelets 520k, and WBC 13k. He reports itching after hot showers. SaO₂ is 99%. Which test best distinguishes polycythemia vera from a secondary cause?

  1. Serum ferritin
  2. Serum erythropoietin level
  3. Arterial blood gas
  4. Peripheral smear for teardrop cells
Answer & explanationB. A suppressed EPO points to PV (autonomous marrow production suppresses EPO), whereas an elevated EPO indicates a secondary cause (hypoxia or EPO-secreting tumor). Ferritin (A) doesn't separate these. ABG (C) helps only if hypoxia is suspected and his SaO₂ is normal. Teardrop cells (D) suggest myelofibrosis, not PV. Confirm with JAK2 testing (>95% V617F-positive in PV).

2. A 40-year-old woman has an isolated platelet count of 850k. She is asymptomatic. Iron studies show iron-deficiency anemia. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Start low-dose aspirin for thrombosis prophylaxis
  2. Start hydroxyurea
  3. Treat the iron deficiency and recheck the platelet count
  4. Bone marrow biopsy now
Answer & explanationC. This is reactive thrombocytosis from iron-deficiency anemia (a commonly missed cause) — treat the iron deficiency and the count should normalize. Aspirin (A) and cytoreduction (B) are not indicated for reactive thrombocytosis; platelet magnitude does not drive thrombosis risk. Marrow biopsy (D) is premature; it's reserved for suspected primary thrombocytosis, e.g., a triple-negative ET workup.

3. A 50-year-old man has WBC 145k with neutrophilia, basophilia, and eosinophilia, plus splenomegaly. A low leukocyte alkaline phosphatase score is noted. Which finding confirms the diagnosis?

  1. JAK2 V617F mutation
  2. BCR-ABL1 fusion / t(9;22)
  3. >20% myeloblasts in the marrow
  4. Smudge cells on the smear
Answer & explanationB. The picture is chronic myeloid leukemia, defined by the BCR-ABL1 fusion (Philadelphia chromosome, t(9;22)); a low LAP score is a classic supportive clue. JAK2 (A) drives the BCR-ABL1–negative MPNs (PV/ET/PMF), not CML. >20% marrow blasts (C) would indicate acute leukemia / blast crisis, not chronic-phase CML. Smudge cells (D) are seen in CLL.

4. A 72-year-old woman with primary myelofibrosis has symptomatic splenomegaly and platelets of 38k. Which JAK inhibitor is best suited given her thrombocytopenia?

  1. Ruxolitinib at full dose
  2. Pacritinib
  3. Hydroxyurea
  4. Imatinib
Answer & explanationB. Pacritinib (Vonjo) is specifically approved for myelofibrosis with severe thrombocytopenia (platelets <50k), where full-dose ruxolitinib (A) is limited by dose-dependent cytopenias. Hydroxyurea (C) helps proliferative symptoms but isn't the targeted answer here and can worsen cytopenias. Imatinib (D) targets BCR-ABL1 (CML), not myelofibrosis. (Momelotinib is the analogous choice when anemia dominates.)

5. A patient newly diagnosed with polycythemia vera is low-risk (age 45, no prior thrombosis). Which is the cornerstone of initial management?

  1. Phlebotomy to a hematocrit goal <45% plus low-dose aspirin
  2. Therapeutic anticoagulation
  3. Immediate allogeneic stem cell transplant
  4. Hydroxyurea for all patients
Answer & explanationA. For PV, phlebotomy to a hematocrit <45% (CYTO-PV target) plus low-dose aspirin reduces thrombotic events and is the foundation for low-risk patients. Anticoagulation (B) is reserved for an established thrombosis. Transplant (C) has no role in PV. Cytoreduction such as hydroxyurea or ropeginterferon (D) is added for high-risk patients (age >60 or prior thrombosis), not universally.

- leukocytosis w/u: leukemoid [infection, steroids, stress, tobacco] vs malignant; smear (left shift, basophilia/eosinophilia, blasts); if CML susp -> BCR-ABL1/t(9;22), low LAP; WBC >100k but leukostasis rare in CML

- dx confirm: heme/onc referral, marrow bx + cytogenetics + NGS [JAK2, CALR, MPL, BCR-ABL1]
- treatment by dx:
  - ET/PV: prevent thrombosis, risk-stratify [age >60 OR prior clot]. low risk -> ASA (+ phlebotomy Hct <45% for PV); high risk -> + cytoreduction [hydroxyurea / ropeginterferon]; anticoag if prior venous, ASA if arterial

- counsel: thrombosis/bleeding precautions, smoking cessation, hydration; *

Take-Home Points

  1. Four MPNs — ET, PV, PMF, CML — are clonal overproductions of MATURE myeloid cells (vs MDS = abnormal maturation). ET/PV/PMF share JAK2/CALR/MPL; CML is defined by BCR-ABL1.
  2. A low EPO helps separate polycythemia vera from secondary (high EPO) and relative (normal EPO) erythrocytosis.
  3. ET/PV treatment is risk-stratified by age >60 and prior thrombosis — aspirin/phlebotomy for low risk, add cytoreduction for high risk — and platelet magnitude does not drive that decision.
  4. PMF is prognosis-driven (HSCT the only cure; JAK inhibitors for symptoms); CML is treated with TKIs and is now highly effective.
