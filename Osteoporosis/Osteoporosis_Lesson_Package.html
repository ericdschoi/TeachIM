Learning Objectives

  1. Apply osteoporosis screening guidelines based on age and risk factors.
  2. Diagnose osteoporosis by any of three criteria (T-score, fragility fracture, or FRAX).
  3. Initiate treatment based on severity and comorbidities, and know when a secondary work-up is needed.
  4. Determine appropriate DXA monitoring intervals and drug-holiday timing.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What are the three ways to diagnose osteoporosis? (1) T-score ≤ −2.5 on DXA; (2) a fragility fracture (low-trauma fracture of hip, vertebra, wrist, pelvis, ribs, or proximal humerus) regardless of T-score; (3) a high FRAX 10-year risk (≥20% major osteoporotic or ≥3% hip fracture).
Q2. Who should be screened with DXA, and why does T-score alone miss patients? All women ≥65; postmenopausal women <65 with ≥1 risk factor; men with major risk factors. Most fragility fractures actually occur in people with T-scores better than −2.5, so screening on BMD alone misses many at-risk patients — clinical risk factors and FRAX matter.
Q3. A patient on long-term denosumab wants to stop. What is the one thing you must not do? Do not simply discontinue it. Stopping denosumab causes a rapid rebound in bone turnover and a surge of vertebral fractures; you must transition to a bisphosphonate. (And do not follow denosumab with a PTH analog.)

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (Osteoporosis_Optimized.pptx) and click through, or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. Each cursor icon is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Core talk: ~20–25 min. With cases: +10–15 min. Print the learner handout for reference.

Hook (2 min)

"A hip fracture in an older adult carries a one-year mortality around 25% — comparable to many cancers — and only half of survivors return to independent living. Yet osteoporosis is asymptomatic until it breaks a bone, and we have treatments that cut fracture risk by ~40%." Frame the talk as finding the silent disease before the fracture.

Objective 1 — Burden & screening 5 min

ASK: "Describe osteoporosis at the bone level." — low bone mass + microarchitectural deterioration → skeletal fragility. ~20% of women and ~5% of men over 50 have it.

Who to screen with DXA: all women ≥65; postmenopausal women <65 with ≥1 risk factor (use FRAX); men with major risk factors.

ASK: "Name risk factors by category." — then click each.
CategoryExamples
SubstancesTobacco; alcohol >2 drinks/day
MedicationsGlucocorticoids (≥20 mg/day ×3 mo), aromatase inhibitors, androgen-deprivation therapy, antiepileptics
EndocrinopathiesPremature menopause, hypogonadism, hyperparathyroidism, T1DM, Cushing's
Chronic disease / malignancyMalabsorption/celiac/IBD, RA, chronic liver disease, CKD, HIV, multiple myeloma
OtherLow BMI/anorexia, family history of hip fracture, prior fracture

Objective 2 — Diagnosis 5 min

ASK: "What are the three ways to diagnose osteoporosis?" — then reveal.
CriterionDetail
T-score ≤ −2.5 on DXANormal > −1.0; osteopenia −1.0 to −2.5; osteoporosis < −2.5. (T-score, not Z-score, in postmenopausal women/older men.)
Fragility fractureLow-trauma fracture (ground-level fall) of hip, vertebra, wrist, pelvis, ribs, proximal humerus — diagnostic regardless of T-score. Start treatment without waiting for DXA (can begin ~2 wk post-fracture).
High FRAX10-year risk ≥20% major osteoporotic OR ≥3% hip fracture.
Pearl — BMD underestimates risk: Most fragility fractures occur in patients with T-scores better than −2.5. Vertebral fractures are the most common manifestation and two-thirds are asymptomatic/incidental. Treat by clinical risk, not the DXA number alone.

Who needs a secondary work-up? All men; premenopausal women; and anyone with osteoporosis unexplained by comorbidities or under 50. Initial labs: CBC, CMP, Mg, Phos, 25-OH vitamin D, testosterone (men). Subsequent (directed): TSH, intact PTH, Cushing's screen, celiac panel, ESR/ferritin, SPEP/UPEP, FSH/LH/estradiol.

Objective 3 — Treatment 7 min

Primary interventions for all: calcium ~1200 mg/day (ideally dietary), vitamin D 800 IU/day (50,000 IU weekly if level <40), weight-bearing high-impact exercise (brisk walking, jogging, dancing, stairs — not cycling/swimming), smoking cessation, limit alcohol.

Pearl — the VITAL trial: VITAL (NEJM 2022) found no fracture reduction from vitamin D in community-dwelling adults with a normal baseline level. But still replete to "normal" when starting a bisphosphonate (efficacy concern at low levels) — and never delay the bisphosphonate while repleting; start them together.
AgentRoleKey points
Bisphosphonate (alendronate PO weekly; zoledronic acid IV yearly)First-line for most (eGFR ≥35)Take oral on empty stomach with 8 oz water, sit upright ≥30 min, nothing by mouth ≥30 min. Harms: esophagitis, hypocalcemia, rare ONJ and atypical femur fracture. Drug holiday after 5 yr PO / 3 yr IV.
Denosumab (SQ q6 months)Second-line (intolerance/contraindication, eGFR <35)Anti-RANKL. Effectively lifelong — stopping causes rebound vertebral fractures, so always transition to a bisphosphonate.
Anabolics — teriparatide (SQ daily), romosozumab (SQ monthly)Very-high-risk (T ≤ −3.5, or T ≤ −2.5 + fragility fracture) or failure/intolerance of antiresorptivesMost effective; endocrinology-led. Avoid romosozumab with recent MI/stroke. Follow the anabolic course with an antiresorptive.
SERM — raloxifene (PO daily)Rarely; selected postmenopausal womenUseful if also high breast-cancer risk; harms: ↑VTE, hot flashes.
ASK: "How should a patient take an oral bisphosphonate, and who needs IV instead?" — empty stomach, full glass of water, stay upright 30 min; IV for esophageal disease, GI intolerance, Roux-en-Y, or inability to sit upright.

Objective 4 — Monitoring & drug holidays 4 min

SituationRepeat DXA
Low risk (normal DXA)~15 years
Moderate risk (osteopenia, e.g., T −2.2)1–2 years
On IV bisphosphonate~3 years
On oral bisphosphonate~5 years
During a drug holidayEvery 2–4 years

After a treatment course, if BMD is stable/improved and risk has fallen → consider a drug holiday (lasting bisphosphonate benefit; cumulative atypical-fracture risk rises with duration). If BMD worsens or risk stays high → continue or refer to endocrine for denosumab/anabolic. (Intervals are largely expert opinion, not firm data.)

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild: who to screen, the three diagnostic criteria, first-line therapy and its counseling points, and when to consider a drug holiday.

Guideline Update ACP 2023 living guideline & ASBMR/BHOF 2024

The talk (2022, rev. 2023) is built on the 2019 Endocrine Society guideline. The 2023 ACP living guideline (Ann Intern Med) confirms and sharpens the same framework:

TopicCurrent guidance
First-lineBisphosphonates — strong recommendation in postmenopausal women, conditional in men with primary osteoporosis.
Second-lineDenosumab when bisphosphonates are contraindicated or not tolerated.
Very-high-risk womenAn anabolic first (romosozumab or teriparatide), then a bisphosphonate. Anabolic-first-then-antiresorptive yields greater BMD and earlier fracture reduction than the reverse sequence — important for imminent risk (recent hip/vertebral fracture).
Denosumab discontinuationNever stop without transitioning to a bisphosphonate (rebound vertebral fractures). Do not follow denosumab with a PTH analog.
Estrogen / raloxifeneNot recommended as first-line for most; reserve SERMs for selected patients.
Treat-to-targetASBMR/BHOF 2024 goal-directed position: aim for a target BMD/T-score, not a fixed duration.

Sources: Qaseem A, et al. ACP living clinical guideline, Ann Intern Med 2023 (M22-1034); ASBMR/BHOF goal-directed therapy position statement, JBMR 2024. Clinician-reviewed before teaching.

Practice Cases 10–15 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Compression fracture in an older man

72 yo man, 2 wk of low back pain; 50-pack-year tobacco + alcohol use, CKD stage 3. 70 kg, 160 cm. Lumbar XR: L4 compression fracture. DXA T-score −2.2 (LS spine), −1.7 (left hip).

What is the FRAX, and the diagnosis?FRAX 10-yr: major fracture ~15%, hip ~6.5%. He has osteoporosis by fragility fracture and high FRAX (hip >3%) — note the DXA (−1.7 to −2.2) is only osteopenic, illustrating that BMD alone misses fractures.
Is a secondary work-up indicated?No — the osteoporosis is well explained by age and identified risk factors (tobacco, alcohol).
Treatment, and bisphosphonate counseling?Calcium, vitamin D, weight-bearing exercise, smoking/alcohol reduction, and an oral bisphosphonate (eGFR permitting). Counsel: take with a full glass of water, stay upright >30 min; ONJ/atypical fractures are very rare. Reassess BMD at ~5 yr; if stable, consider a drug holiday.

Case 2 — Hip fracture after a fall

64 yo woman with HTN, rheumatoid arthritis, and T2DM admitted after slipping on ice with a left femoral-neck fracture. Femoral-neck T-score −2.7.

Diagnosis and next steps?Osteoporosis (fragility fracture + T ≤ −2.5). Start calcium, vitamin D, and an antiresorptive; check baseline labs (vitamin D, BMP, calcium). This is a high-risk patient — a recent hip fracture marks imminent risk, so consider whether an anabolic-first strategy (endocrine input) is warranted.

Case 3 — Worsening on treatment

74 yo woman with severe COPD and osteoporosis after an atraumatic vertebral compression fracture from a coughing spell. Femoral-neck T was −2.8 five years ago; on alendronate since; repeat DXA now −3.1.

Next step in management?She is failing an antiresorptive and is now very-high-risk (worsening BMD + new fragility fracture on therapy). Consider an anabolic agent — teriparatide or romosozumab — when T < −3.0, or T ≤ −2.5 with a vertebral fracture. (The original board said "anabolic steroids" — corrected to "anabolic agents"; teriparatide and romosozumab are not steroids.)
When would you repeat the DXA, and what if it stabilizes?Repeat in ~2 years. If the repeat is stable, resume a bisphosphonate (or denosumab) — anabolic gains are lost without a follow-on antiresorptive.

Case 4 — The unifying secondary cause

65 yo man, never-smoker, no chronic conditions, acute atraumatic low back pain + fatigue. Lumbar XR: L1 compression fracture. Work-up: Hgb 13.1, Cr 2.4, Ca 10, albumin 2.7, total protein 10.8, ALP 200; TSH/PTH/vitamin D/testosterone normal; femoral-neck T −2.7.

What is the likely diagnosis, and the next test?The combination of age, renal insufficiency, and a high total-protein/low-albumin gap (with a fragility fracture) points to multiple myeloma. Next step: SPEP/UPEP (± serum free light chains).
Once myeloma is treated, what bone therapy?Vitamin D plus a bisphosphonate (first-line in myeloma bone disease). Avoid calcium supplementation while myeloma is active (hypercalcemia risk).

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 68-year-old woman with no prior fractures has a DXA showing a femoral-neck T-score of −2.6 and normal labs. eGFR is 70. Which is the most appropriate initial pharmacologic therapy?

  1. Denosumab
  2. Oral alendronate
  3. Romosozumab
  4. Raloxifene
Answer & explanationB. Bisphosphonates are first-line for most patients with osteoporosis and a preserved eGFR (ACP 2023, strong recommendation in postmenopausal women). Denosumab (A) is second-line for bisphosphonate intolerance/contraindication. Romosozumab (C) is reserved for very-high-risk patients. Raloxifene (D) is rarely first-line and adds VTE risk.

2. A 70-year-old woman on denosumab for 4 years wants to stop because of the injections. What is the most important next step?

  1. Simply discontinue denosumab and monitor
  2. Stop denosumab and start teriparatide
  3. Transition to a bisphosphonate after the last denosumab dose
  4. Reduce the dosing interval to once yearly
Answer & explanationC. Stopping denosumab abruptly triggers a rebound in bone turnover with a surge of multiple vertebral fractures; transition to a bisphosphonate to prevent it. Simple discontinuation (A) is the classic error. Following denosumab with a PTH analog (B) can amplify the rebound and is not recommended. Stretching the interval (D) precipitates the same rebound.

3. A 67-year-old woman sustains a vertebral compression fracture; her DXA T-score is −3.6 and she has had a prior wrist fracture. Which strategy gives the greatest fracture-risk reduction?

  1. Oral alendronate alone
  2. Raloxifene
  3. An anabolic agent (e.g., romosozumab or teriparatide) followed by a bisphosphonate
  4. Calcium and vitamin D alone
Answer & explanationC. She is very-high-risk (very low T-score plus fragility fractures, imminent risk). An anabolic first, then an antiresorptive, produces greater BMD gain and earlier fracture reduction than starting with a bisphosphonate (ACP 2023; sequential-therapy data). Bisphosphonate alone (A) or a SERM (B) is less effective here; supplements alone (D) are inadequate.

4. A 66-year-old man presents with an atraumatic vertebral fracture, fatigue, Cr 2.3, calcium 10.6, total protein 10.5, and albumin 2.6. DXA shows osteoporosis. What is the best next diagnostic test?

  1. Repeat DXA in 2 years
  2. SPEP/UPEP
  3. Start a bisphosphonate and reassess
  4. 24-hour urine cortisol
Answer & explanationB. Renal insufficiency, hypercalcemia, and a wide protein–albumin gap with a fragility fracture suggest multiple myeloma; SPEP/UPEP (± free light chains) is the next step. Osteoporosis in a younger man warrants a secondary work-up. Deferring evaluation (A, C) risks missing the diagnosis; a Cushing's screen (D) does not fit this picture.

5. A 72-year-old woman completes 5 years of oral alendronate. Her femoral-neck T-score has improved to −2.0, she has had no fractures, and FRAX risk is now modest. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. Switch to denosumab indefinitely
  2. Add an anabolic agent
  3. Consider a bisphosphonate drug holiday, with DXA every 2–4 years
  4. Continue alendronate lifelong without monitoring
Answer & explanationC. After 5 years of oral bisphosphonate with improved BMD, no fractures, and lower risk, a drug holiday is appropriate — benefit persists after stopping, and cumulative use raises atypical-femur-fracture risk. Re-screen every 2–4 years during the holiday. Escalation (A, B) is not indicated for a patient whose risk has fallen; indefinite therapy without monitoring (D) ignores the holiday rationale.

Take-Home Points

  1. Screen all women ≥65 (and younger postmenopausal women or men with major risk factors) with DXA. Diagnose by T-score ≤ −2.5, a fragility fracture, or a high FRAX — and remember most fractures occur above a −2.5 T-score.
  2. Bisphosphonates are first-line for most (eGFR ≥35); counsel on rare harms (esophagitis, ONJ, atypical femur fracture). Denosumab is second-line — never stop it without bridging to a bisphosphonate. Anabolics (anabolic-first, then antiresorptive) for very-high-risk.
  3. Consider a drug holiday after 5 years oral / 3 years IV bisphosphonate, re-screening every 2–4 years. Work up secondary causes in men, premenopausal women, and unexplained disease.
