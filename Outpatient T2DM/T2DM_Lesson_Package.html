Learning Objectives

  1. Identify which patients should be screened for type 2 diabetes (T2DM), and how to make the diagnosis.
  2. Recognize and screen for the microvascular and macrovascular complications of T2DM.
  3. Choose pharmacologic therapy driven first by comorbidities (ASCVD, HF, CKD) and only then by the A1c.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. List the four ways to diagnose T2DM and the threshold for each. (1) Random glucose >200 mg/dL with symptoms of hyperglycemia or hyperglycemic crisis; (2) A1c ≥6.5%; (3) fasting glucose ≥126 mg/dL (≥8 h fast); (4) 2-h glucose ≥200 mg/dL on a 75-g OGTT. In an asymptomatic patient, confirm with a repeat or a second test type.
Q2. Name the three microvascular and three macrovascular complications of diabetes, and how each microvascular one is screened. Microvascular: retinopathy (annual dilated retinal exam), nephropathy (annual spot urine albumin:creatinine ratio, abnormal >30 mg/g), neuropathy (annual foot exam + monofilament). Macrovascular: coronary (CAD/MI), cerebrovascular (stroke), peripheral arterial disease — no routine screening test; evaluate by symptoms and treat risk factors.
Q3. A patient has an A1c of 6.6% (at goal) plus HFrEF and diabetic CKD with albuminuria. Should you change their diabetes meds? Yes. Comorbidity drives therapy independent of A1c: this patient warrants an SGLT2 inhibitor (HF + CKD benefit) regardless of being at glycemic goal — the modern central teaching point of the talk.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (T2DM_Optimized.pptx) and click through, or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. Each cursor icon on the board is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Core talk: ~20 min. With cases: +15–20 min. Print the learner handout so residents take notes. Scope: type 2 only (type 1 covered separately).

Hook (2 min)

"Your patient's A1c is 6.7% — at goal. They also have heart failure, diabetic kidney disease, and obesity. Do you change their diabetes regimen?" Most learners say no. The whole talk argues yes — and reframes diabetes as a cardiovascular-renal disease, not just a glucose number.

Objective 1 — Diagnosis & screening 5 min

ASK: "What are the classic symptoms of diabetes?" — then click. Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, blurry vision; acanthosis nigricans on exam.

Diagnostic criteria:

TestDiagnostic threshold
Random glucose + symptoms / crisis>200 mg/dL
Fasting glucose (≥8 h)≥126 mg/dL
HbA1c≥6.5%
2-h glucose, 75-g OGTT≥200 mg/dL (mainly pregnancy)

In asymptomatic patients, confirm with a repeat value or a second method (e.g., fasting glucose ×2, or A1c + fasting glucose).

Pearl — when the A1c lies: A1c tracks RBC age. Low RBC turnover (iron/B12/folate deficiency, asplenia) → too many old glycated cells → A1c falsely high. High RBC turnover (hemolysis, recent EPO or B12/folate repletion, pregnancy, CKD) → A1c falsely low. Use fasting glucose or a CGM/GMI when A1c is unreliable.
ASK: "Who gets screened, and how often?" — then click.

Screen at age ≥35 for everyone; screen earlier if BMI ≥25 (≥23 in Asian Americans, who develop metabolic disease at lower BMI) plus a risk factor (high-risk ethnicity, first-degree relative, HTN, dyslipidemia, ASCVD, PCOS, physical inactivity); also screen patients on diabetogenic drugs (steroids, atypical antipsychotics, some HIV meds) and those with prior gestational DM. Frequency: every 3 years, or yearly with prediabetes (A1c 5.7–6.4%), or in 3–6 months for marginal results.

Objective 2 — Complications 6 min

ASK: "Name the microvascular and macrovascular complications." — then click each.
Microvascular (small vessel)ScreeningPrevention
Retinopathy → blindnessAnnual dilated retinal examTight glycemic control (A1c ~7%) reduces microvascular disease. Benefit takes ~10 yr — relax the target in limited life expectancy.
Nephropathy → CKD, albuminuriaAnnual spot urine albumin:Cr (abnl >30 mg/g)
Neuropathy (periph + autonomic)Annual foot exam + monofilament
Macrovascular (large vessel)ScreeningPrevention
CAD/MI, stroke, PADNone routine — evaluate by symptomsTight A1c does NOT reduce events in longstanding diabetes. Aggressively treat BP, lipids, tobacco: moderate-intensity statin; BP <130/80 if higher CV risk (10-yr risk >15% or ASCVD), else <140/90; smoking cessation.
Pearl — the legacy effect: Early intensive control in newly diagnosed diabetes may reduce later macrovascular events (UKPDS legacy effect), but starting tight control late does not. Glucose is a weak lever on the heart; the statin and the BP med do the heavy lifting.

Objective 3 — Comorbidity-driven management 6 min

ASK: "Before you look at the A1c, what four patient factors should steer therapy?" — comorbidities (ASCVD/HF/CKD), desire for weight loss, cost, and hypoglycemia risk.

Step 1 — lifestyle for all (≥5% weight loss, ≥150 min/wk moderate-vigorous activity). Metformin (eGFR ≥30) remains a reasonable, cheap, safe anchor for most — though the original ADA algorithm no longer mandates it first for everyone.

Step 2 — comorbidity first. If the patient has ASCVD (or high risk), HF, or CKD, start an SGLT2 inhibitor and/or GLP-1 RA with proven benefit regardless of the A1c — these reduce mortality and renal events; insulin has no such benefit.

Step 3 — only now consider the A1c. If no high-risk comorbidity and A1c is above goal, prioritize by: weight loss (GLP-1 RA, then SGLT2i), cost (formulary), and avoiding hypoglycemia (liberalize to 8–8.5% in frail/elderly). If still above goal → add sulfonylurea or insulin. Severe hyperglycemia (glucose >300, A1c >10%, or symptoms) often warrants starting insulin up front, then simplifying.

ASK: "Pair the side effect to the drug class." — metformin (GI upset, avoid eGFR<30); GLP-1 RA (GI upset, pancreatitis, medullary thyroid ca contraindication); SGLT2i (genital/UTI infection, euglycemic DKA, volume depletion, ↑amputation with canagliflozin); sulfonylurea/insulin (hypoglycemia, weight gain).

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild from memory: the four diagnostic criteria, the micro- vs macrovascular complications and how each is screened, and the one rule that changed practice — comorbidity before A1c.

Guideline Update ADA Standards of Care 2025–2026

The original talk (2022, rev. 2023) is built on the 2022–2023 ADA Standards. The core framework — comorbidity-driven therapy — has only been reinforced since. Key changes to surface:

TopicCurrent ADA guidance (2025–2026)
Organ-protection drugsSGLT2i and/or GLP-1 RA with proven benefit are a core, foundational strategy for ASCVD/HF/CKD — used for cardiorenal protection independent of A1c and independent of metformin.
First-line therapyMetformin is no longer mandated first for all; therapy is chosen by comorbidity. Metformin remains an appropriate, low-cost option (eGFR ≥30).
Initial combinationConsider starting two agents at once when A1c is 1.5–2.0% above goal, or with established/high-risk CVD.
Weight + glycemiaPrefer a GLP-1 RA or dual GIP/GLP-1 RA (semaglutide, tirzepatide) for greater weight loss; relevant in obesity, MASLD/MASH, HFpEF.
Heart failureSGLT2 inhibitor recommended in both HFrEF and HFpEF for glycemic control and fewer HF hospitalizations.
CKD with residual albuminuriaAdd finerenone (nonsteroidal MRA) when albuminuria persists despite a maximally tolerated ACEi/ARB plus SGLT2i — reduces CKD progression and CV events (FIDELIO-/FIGARO-DKD).

Sources: ADA, Standards of Care in Diabetes — 2025 and 2026, Diabetes Care Suppl. 1 (Sections 2, 9, 10, 11). Medical content should be clinician-reviewed before teaching.

Practice Cases 10–15 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Would you screen? (asymptomatic)

(a) 42 yo with SLE, ESRD on HD, HTN, hyperlipidemia, on prednisone, EPO, amlodipine, atorvastatin. BMI 22, BP 115/74. Fasting glucose 160, A1c 5.6%.  (b) 33 yo Asian woman, BMI 23, PCOS.  (c) 28 yo on olanzapine, BMI 22.

Does patient (a) have diabetes?Very likely — confirm with a second fasting glucose ≥126. The A1c (5.6%) is discordant and underestimates average glucose here because of high RBC turnover (EPO therapy). Trust the glucose, not the A1c.
Should (b) and (c) be screened?Yes to both. (b) Asian Americans are screened at BMI ≥23 (vs ≥25) when a risk factor (PCOS) is present. (c) Atypical antipsychotics — like steroids, thiazides, and some HIV meds — are diabetogenic and warrant screening even at normal BMI.

Case 2 — New diagnosis, severe

55 yo establishing care, 2–3 months of polyuria/polydipsia, BMI 42.

What additional tests?BMP, urine albumin:creatinine, lipid panel, plus a baseline retinal exam and foot exam to screen for complications already present at diagnosis.
What medications, and any referrals?If A1c >10% / symptomatic: metformin + insulin to control acute hyperglycemia, then simplify. Given obesity, add a GLP-1 RA at follow-up (weight + glycemia). Refer for nutrition/weight-management counseling; consider metabolic surgery if BMI >40.

Case 3 — Comorbidity drives therapy

(a) 45 yo, T2DM + tobacco, on metformin 2000 mg ER + simvastatin. BP 152/83, normal foot exam, BMI 24. A1c 7.4%, Cr 1.2, urine albumin:Cr 303 mg/g.  (b) 45 yo on metformin, PAD s/p amputation, A1c 6.5%.  (c) 72 yo on metformin, prior DKA, HFrEF, A1c 8.6%.

(a) How to manage HTN and the albuminuria?ACE inhibitor or ARB (albuminuria + HTN) plus lifestyle. Add an SGLT2 inhibitor for diabetic kidney disease with albuminuria to slow CKD progression — independent of the near-goal A1c. Tobacco cessation is the single biggest ASCVD lever.
(b) Which agent despite an A1c of 6.5%?A GLP-1 RA for ASCVD (PAD), regardless of A1c. Avoid SGLT2i here given the amputation history (canagliflozin signal).
(c) HFrEF with prior DKA — what to use, what to avoid?A GLP-1 RA given HFrEF and the need for weight/CV benefit. Avoid SGLT2i because of prior DKA. (In most HFrEF patients without a DKA history, an SGLT2i would be first-line.)

Case 4 — Glycemic targets & the frail patient

82 yo with T2DM, COPD, HTN, CAD, diabetic CKD stage 2, BPH, cognitive impairment, presenting with recurrent falls. A1c 7.5% on metformin + glargine 10 U qHS, lisinopril, tamsulosin, atorvastatin.

What is the A1c goal, and how do you adjust meds?Liberalize to <8% (even up to 8.5%) given age, comorbidity, cognitive impairment, and fall risk. The glargine is a hypoglycemia and falls hazard — consider reducing or stopping it. The priority in this patient is avoiding hypoglycemia, not chasing a lower number.

Case 5 — Cost & access

53 yo, homeless and uninsured, T2DM on metformin, CKD stage 3, A1c 8.5%; later loses job and A1c rises.

What meds, and what else must you address?Glipizide (a sulfonylurea — not glyburide, which accumulates in CKD and causes hypoglycemia). Insulin is hard to manage without stable housing/refrigeration. Equally important: assess social determinants of health and mental health — access and adherence, not drug choice, are the rate-limiting step.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 58-year-old woman with T2DM (A1c 6.8% on metformin), HFrEF (EF 30%), and stage 3 CKD with a urine albumin:creatinine ratio of 250 mg/g returns for follow-up. Her glucose logs are at goal. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Make no changes — she is at her A1c goal
  2. Add an SGLT2 inhibitor
  3. Add basal insulin
  4. Add a sulfonylurea
Answer & explanationB. Therapy is driven by comorbidity, not the A1c. An SGLT2 inhibitor reduces HF hospitalizations and slows CKD progression and is indicated here despite a glucose already at goal — the central teaching point. Doing nothing (A) misses a mortality/renal benefit. Insulin (C) and sulfonylureas (D) add hypoglycemia risk with no cardiorenal benefit.

2. A 47-year-old man with iron-deficiency anemia has a fasting glucose of 138 mg/dL and an A1c of 5.9%. How should you interpret the discordance?

  1. The A1c is most reliable; he does not have diabetes
  2. Iron deficiency falsely lowers the A1c
  3. Iron deficiency falsely raises the A1c; the fasting glucose is the more reliable diagnostic test here
  4. Repeat the A1c in one year
Answer & explanationC. Low RBC turnover states (iron, B12, or folate deficiency; asplenia) leave a disproportionate share of old, heavily glycated red cells, falsely elevating the A1c — yet here the A1c is paradoxically the lower number, so it is unreliable and the diabetic-range fasting glucose should drive diagnosis (confirm with a repeat). Conversely, hemolysis or recent EPO/iron repletion falsely lowers A1c (B describes the wrong direction for iron deficiency). When A1c and glucose disagree, trust glucose (or a CGM).

3. A 60-year-old man with T2DM and established peripheral arterial disease (prior toe amputation), A1c 7.1% on metformin, asks to intensify therapy for cardiovascular benefit. Which agent is the best choice?

  1. Canagliflozin
  2. A GLP-1 receptor agonist
  3. Glipizide
  4. NPH insulin
Answer & explanationB. A GLP-1 RA provides cardiovascular benefit in established ASCVD and is preferred here. Canagliflozin (A) — and SGLT2 inhibitors broadly — carry an amputation signal and are best avoided after amputation/severe PAD. Sulfonylureas (C) and insulin (D) offer no cardiovascular benefit and add hypoglycemia.

4. An 84-year-old woman with T2DM, moderate dementia, and recurrent falls has an A1c of 6.7% on metformin and glargine 14 U nightly. She has had two episodes of morning shakiness. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. Continue current regimen; her A1c is excellent
  2. Increase glargine to lower the A1c further
  3. Liberalize the A1c target and reduce or stop the insulin
  4. Add a sulfonylurea
Answer & explanationC. An A1c of 6.7% on insulin in a frail elder with dementia and falls is too tight — the morning shakiness signals hypoglycemia. Liberalize to <8% (up to 8.5%) and de-intensify, starting with the insulin. Tightening (A, B) or adding a hypoglycemic agent (D) increases harm; in this population hypoglycemia carries more risk than modest hyperglycemia.

5. A 52-year-old man with T2DM and CKD (eGFR 40, urine albumin:creatinine 400 mg/g) is on a maximally tolerated ACE inhibitor, an SGLT2 inhibitor, metformin, and a statin. Albuminuria persists and potassium is 4.6. Per current ADA guidance, what should be added to further reduce kidney and cardiovascular risk?

  1. Finerenone
  2. Spironolactone
  3. A second ACE inhibitor
  4. Basal insulin
Answer & explanationA. Finerenone, a nonsteroidal MRA, reduces CKD progression and CV events in diabetic kidney disease with residual albuminuria despite ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i (FIDELIO-/FIGARO-DKD), and is the 2025–2026 ADA recommendation; normokalemia makes it appropriate to start. Steroidal MRAs (B) carry more hyperkalemia/gynecomastia and lack the same DKD outcome data. Dual RAAS blockade (C) is harmful. Insulin (D) does not slow CKD.

Take-Home Points

  1. Screen at age 35, or earlier with BMI ≥25 (≥23 in Asian Americans) plus a risk factor; diagnose with A1c ≥6.5%, fasting glucose ≥126, random >200 + symptoms, or OGTT ≥200 (confirm asymptomatic cases).
  2. Diabetes causes microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy — screen yearly) and macrovascular (ASCVD) disease. Tight A1c prevents microvascular complications; controlling BP, lipids, and tobacco prevents the macrovascular ones.
  3. Choose therapy by comorbidity FIRST: ASCVD/HF/CKD → SGLT2 inhibitor and/or GLP-1 RA regardless of the A1c (mortality and renal benefit). Add finerenone for residual albuminuria. Liberalize the A1c and avoid hypoglycemia in frail elders.
