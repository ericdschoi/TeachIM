Learning Objectives

  1. Name the factors that affect oxygenation and ventilation.
  2. Identify common oxygen delivery devices and differentiate them.
  3. Differentiate CPAP vs BiPAP and their respiratory and cardiac physiology.
  4. Name the indications (and contraindications) for non-invasive positive-pressure ventilation.

Pre-work send the day before

1. Which two settings improve oxygenation, and which two determine ventilation?

Oxygenation: FiO₂ and PEEP. Ventilation: tidal volume and respiratory rate (minute ventilation = Vt × RR).

2. Why does a patient in respiratory distress on 6 L nasal cannula get a lower effective FiO₂ than the same flow in a comfortable patient?

Distress raises inspiratory flow (30–120 L/min), which far exceeds the cannula's flow, so each breath entrains more ambient air (21% O₂), diluting the delivered oxygen.

3. A COPD patient is hypoxemic AND hypercapnic. Which non-invasive mode, and why?

BiPAP. The EPAP (= PEEP) treats hypoxemia; the IPAP−EPAP difference (ΔPAP) augments tidal volume to treat hypercapnia. CPAP only adds PEEP and would not fix ventilation.

Teaching Script

The original interactive board drives the visuals; this script adds a hook, ASK pauses, pearls, and a retrieval close. Board navigation: The Basics → O₂ Devices → NIPPV (Effects, Indications) → Cases. Tip from the author: for the devices section, pick the 4 modalities most used at your institution.

Hook 2 min

"Your patient is satting 86%. You can turn two knobs — FiO₂ and PEEP — and you have a menu of devices that each deliver them differently. Today we'll match the right device to the right physiology, and know when to jump to non-invasive ventilation." Project The Basics slide.

Objective 1 — Oxygenation vs ventilation 3 min Objective 1

ASK — "What raises oxygenation? What changes ventilation?" Reveal: oxygenation = ↑FiO₂ and ↑PEEP (recruits alveoli); ventilation = Vt and RR.
Pearl. Most ward devices only adjust FiO₂. PEEP requires HFNC (a little), CPAP/BiPAP, or the ventilator. Keep "FiO₂ vs PEEP" in mind as the organizing axis for the whole talk.

Objective 2 — Oxygen delivery devices 8 min Objective 2

ASK — "If a patient's inspiratory flow exceeds the device's flow, what happens to delivered FiO₂?" Reveal: ambient air is entrained → effective FiO₂ falls.
DeviceTypical flowMax effective FiO₂Notes
Nasal cannula2–6 L/min~44% at 6 L~3–4% FiO₂ per L; FiO₂ falls with mouth-breathing / high work of breathing

Venturi maskset by adapter~60%Delivers a precise, fixed FiO₂ — best for variable breathing patterns
Oxymizerup to ~12–15 L/min>80% (reported)~20 mL pendant/mustache reservoir → higher FiO₂ per flow than NC
Non-rebreather12–15 L/min~75–90%Rescue only — inflate the bag; de-escalate once stable
High-flow nasal cannulaup to ~60 L/min~100%Matched flow, titratable FiO₂, small PEEP ~3–5 cm H₂O; improves WOB & minute ventilation
Pearl — the reservoir concept. The bigger the 100%-O₂ reservoir, the less ambient air is entrained: nasopharynx (~50 mL, plain NC) < oxymizer pendant (~20 mL added) < face-mask cup (100–250 mL) < NRB bag (600–1500 mL). HFNC instead floods the upper airway with high flow so the airway itself becomes the reservoir.
Pearl — HFNC does more than oxygen. Beyond high titratable FiO₂, HFNC washes out anatomic dead space (lowers CO₂ rebreathing), reduces work of breathing, and adds a small PEEP. It's the bridge between simple devices and non-invasive ventilation.

Objective 3 — CPAP vs BiPAP 5 min Objective 3

CPAPBiPAP
PressureContinuous (= PEEP)IPAP (inspiration) + EPAP (= PEEP)
AffectsOxygenation onlyOxygenation (EPAP) + ventilation (ΔPAP raises Vt)
Use forHypoxemic failureHypoxemic and hypercapnic failure
ASK — "Name the three hemodynamic effects of positive intrathoracic pressure and why they help in flash pulmonary edema." Reveal: ↓ RV preload, ↓ LV preload, ↓ LV afterload — unloads the failing heart.
Pearl — titrating BiPAP. To improve oxygenation, raise FiO₂ or raise both pressures together (↑EPAP keeping ΔPAP). To improve ventilation (lower CO₂), widen the gap — raise IPAP while holding EPAP, increasing ΔPAP and thus Vt.

Objective 4 — Indications & contraindications for NIPPV 4 min Objective 4

ASK — "Where is the evidence for NIPPV strongest, and when is it contraindicated?"
Strong indications (acute)Contraindications
Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema (CPAP or BiPAP); COPD exacerbation with hypercapnia (BiPAP); immunocompromised AHRF; post-op/post-extubation; palliationCan't protect/remove the mask (AMS, paralysis); active vomiting / copious secretions; facial trauma; recent esophageal or tracheal surgery; hemodynamic instability
Pearl. NIPPV is not recommended for ARDS hypoxemia and is weak in pneumonia unless there's concurrent COPD. For most non-cardiogenic, non-COPD hypoxemic failure, HFNC is now the preferred first step (see Modern Framing).

Retrieval close 2 min

Board off. Ask a learner to rank devices by max FiO₂ and to state which add PEEP. Ask a second learner: CPAP vs BiPAP — what each affects and one indication for each.

Cases click to reveal

Case 1 — 56-year-old, HFrEF exacerbation

Diffuse bilateral alveolar infiltrates. T 36.5, HR 110, BP 160/80, RR 32, SaO₂ 75% on RA.

How would you improve SaO₂ now?

Given severe hypoxemia, a non-rebreather quickly improves oxygenation as a rescue. On NRB, SaO₂ improves to 89%, RR 28, ABG 7.46/29/59/24.

Next step?

Start CPAP. There's persistent hypoxemia despite near-max FiO₂, and acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema is a strong NIPPV indication (CPAP unloads the heart and adds PEEP).

Case 2 — 74-year-old, COPD exacerbation

Sleepy but arousable, diffuse wheeze, clear CXR. T 37.1, HR 120, BP 160/60, RR 32, SaO₂ 82% on RA. ABG 7.21/72/45/28.

How would you support them?

BiPAP — this is hypoxemic and hypercapnic failure; BiPAP treats both. Started on 10/5, 35% FiO₂; repeat ABG 7.30/60/79/28.

How would you adjust the BiPAP?

Increase the IPAP (widen ΔPAP) — persistent hypercapnia means ventilation needs more support; raising IPAP raises tidal volume.

Case 3 — 64-year-old, POD5 after R nephrectomy

Acute SOB. T 37.1, HR 140, BP 90/60, RR 28, SaO₂ 82% on RA.

First step?

Trial nasal cannula for mild–moderate hypoxemia; escalate as needed. They desaturate to 89% on 6 L NC → step up to oxymizer, Venturi, or HFNC.

POD7: HFNC 100% FiO₂, 60 L/min, RR 24 and shallow, SaO₂ 86%, obtunded, ABG 7.21/72/45/20. Next step?

Intubation and mechanical ventilation. Hypoxemic + hypercapnic failure despite max FiO₂; PEEP is needed and BiPAP is contraindicated by the altered mental status. Other NIPPV contraindications: facial trauma, copious secretions, vomiting, hemodynamic instability.

Board-Style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A patient in respiratory distress (RR 35, accessory muscle use) on a 6 L/min simple face mask has SaO₂ 86%. The team is puzzled the mask "isn't delivering enough oxygen." Best explanation?

  1. The mask is broken
  2. Their high inspiratory flow entrains ambient air, lowering effective FiO₂
  3. They have a shunt unresponsive to any oxygen
  4. The wall oxygen is set to 21%
  5. CO₂ narcosis
Answer + explanation

B. When inspiratory flow (30–120 L/min in distress) exceeds device flow, each breath pulls in 21%-O₂ ambient air and dilutes the delivered oxygen — a device-flow/inspiratory-flow mismatch. The fix is a device that matches flow and titrates FiO₂ (HFNC) or a Venturi for a fixed FiO₂. C is unlikely to be absolute. A/D/E are not the physiology described.

2. Which device most reliably delivers a precise, set FiO₂ regardless of a patient's variable breathing pattern?

  1. Nasal cannula
  2. Simple face mask
  3. Venturi mask
  4. Oxymizer
  5. Non-rebreather
Answer + explanation

C. The Venturi mask entrains a fixed ratio of ambient air per jet adapter, delivering a precise FiO₂ independent of breathing pattern. The others deliver an FiO₂ that varies with flow and inspiratory effort.

3. A COPD patient with pH 7.22, PaCO₂ 70 and SaO₂ 84% is alert. Best initial support?

  1. CPAP
  2. BiPAP
  3. Non-rebreather mask
  4. Immediate intubation
  5. Nasal cannula at 6 L
Answer + explanation

B. Hypercapnic respiratory failure in a COPD exacerbation is the strongest evidence-based indication for BiPAP — IPAP augments tidal volume to clear CO₂ while EPAP supports oxygenation. A only adds PEEP (no ventilation). C/E add FiO₂ but not ventilation. D is premature in an alert, protectable patient.

4. A non-intubated patient with non-cardiogenic acute hypoxemic respiratory failure (P/F 150) is on a non-rebreather. According to current guidance, the best next step to reduce intubation risk is:

  1. High-flow nasal cannula
  2. Continue non-rebreather
  3. CPAP for COPD
  4. Routine immediate intubation
  5. Simple face mask
Answer + explanation

A. For non-cardiogenic, non-COPD AHRF, HFNC is preferred over conventional oxygen and reduces intubation (FLORALI; ESICM ARDS 2023), and is at least as good as NIV. Track the ROX index and add awake proning. B/E are inferior. C misapplies a COPD indication. D is not routine if non-invasive support can be trialed safely.

5. A patient on HFNC (FiO₂ 0.8, flow 60) has SpO₂ 90%, RR 34. The ROX index is trending down over 6 hours. Best action?

  1. Reassure — HFNC always succeeds
  2. Recognize impending HFNC failure and escalate / prepare for intubation
  3. Switch to simple face mask
  4. Stop monitoring the ROX index
  5. Add a non-rebreather over the HFNC
Answer + explanation

B. The ROX index = (SpO₂/FiO₂) ÷ RR; values ≥4.88 predict success, and a low or falling ROX predicts HFNC failure — escalate support and don't delay intubation (Roca, AJRCCM 2019). The other options ignore or worsen a deteriorating trajectory.

  NC 2-6L [~44%] -> oxymizer/simple mask [~60-70%] -> Venturi [set FiO2, ~60%]
  -> NRB 12-15L [RESCUE only, ~75-90%] -> HFNC <=60L [~100%, PEEP ~3-5 cm H2O]

NIPPV contraindic: AMS/can't protect airway, vomiting/secretions, facial trauma, recent esoph/tracheal sx, instability

Modern Framing — 2022–25 Context currency check

Device physiology, NIPPV mechanics, and indications are timeless and verified current as of June 2026. The items below add modern evidence; none change the lesson's standing teaching.

HFNC is first-line for AHRF. ESICM ARDS 2023 recommends HFNC over conventional oxygen in non-intubated acute hypoxemic respiratory failure to reduce intubation — except cardiogenic pulmonary edema and COPD exacerbation, where NIPPV is preferred. Consistent with FLORALI (Frat, NEJM 2015).
Track the ROX index. ROX = (SpO₂/FiO₂) ÷ RR; ≥4.88 at 2/6/12 h predicts HFNC success, and a low or falling ROX predicts failure — escalate and don't delay intubation (Roca, AJRCCM 2019).
Awake prone positioning. A recommended adjunct for non-intubated patients with COVID-related hypoxemic AHRF on HFNC/NIPPV — reduces intubation (strong recommendation, 2023 rapid practice guideline). Trial ~15 min and continue if tolerated.
CPAP in COVID AHRF (RECOVERY-RS, JAMA 2022). CPAP reduced the intubation/death composite vs conventional oxygen; HFNC did not differ in that trial. Across AHRF generally, HFNC and NIV are similarly effective at avoiding intubation (2023 meta-analysis) — choose based on the failure type and local resources.

Take-Home Points

  1. Hypoxemia is corrected by raising FiO₂ or adding PEEP; most ward devices adjust only FiO₂, and effective FiO₂ falls as work of breathing rises.
  2. HFNC delivers high matched flow, titratable FiO₂ to ~100%, and a small PEEP (~3–5 cm H₂O); the non-rebreather is a rescue device only; the Venturi gives a precise set FiO₂.
  3. CPAP supports oxygenation (PEEP); BiPAP adds ventilation via ΔPAP — use it for hypercapnic failure. Both unload the heart in acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema.
  4. Strongest NIPPV indications: acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema and COPD hypercapnic failure. Mind the contraindications.
  5. For non-cardiogenic/non-COPD hypoxemic failure, HFNC is first-line; add awake proning, track the ROX index, and escalate early.
