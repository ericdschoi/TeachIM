Learning Objectives

  1. Identify symptoms and clinical signs concerning for PCOS.
  2. Develop a differential and initial evaluation for patients with symptoms concerning for PCOS.
  3. Identify gynecologic and non-gynecologic comorbidities commonly associated with PCOS.

Pre-work (send the day before)

1. What are the three Rotterdam criteria, and how many are needed to diagnose PCOS?(1) Hyperandrogenism — clinical (acne, hirsutism, androgenic alopecia) or biochemical (↑ androgens); (2) menstrual irregularity / ovulatory dysfunction (oligomenorrhea, amenorrhea); (3) polycystic ovaries on ultrasound (>12 follicles and/or volume >10 cc). Two of three — AND exclusion of mimics.
2. Why is PCOS called a "diagnosis of exclusion," and what is the single best test for hyperandrogenism?Many conditions (CAH, thyroid disease, hyperprolactinemia, Cushing's, androgen-secreting tumors, pregnancy) mimic its features, so secondary causes must be excluded first. The single best test for hyperandrogenism is total testosterone (free testosterone is unreliable; can calculate the free androgen index from total T and SHBG).
3. Name three comorbidities every PCOS patient should be screened for.Insulin resistance/T2DM (A1c, fasting glucose, or 2-h OGTT — 7.5–10× higher undiagnosed T2DM); cardiovascular risk (BP, fasting lipids); mood/eating disorders (PHQ-9, GAD-7, binge eating). Also endometrial hyperplasia (3× cancer risk), infertility, OSA, NAFLD.

Teaching Script (timed)

0:00–2:00 — Hook

PCOS affects 8–13% of people with ovaries of reproductive age — one of the most common endocrine disorders you'll see. Its two core features are hyperandrogenism and menstrual dysfunction, but the teaching trap is that it's a diagnosis of exclusion wrapped in a positive-criteria set.

ASK: "A 24-year-old has irregular periods and acne. Can you diagnose PCOS today?" (Not yet — you need 2 of 3 Rotterdam criteria AND exclusion of mimics. Acne alone is an unreliable sign of hyperandrogenism.)
2:00–9:00 — Objective 1: Diagnosis (Rotterdam) (Diagnosis board)
Rotterdam criterion (need 2 of 3)Definition / teaching point
HyperandrogenismClinical (hirsutism by Ferriman-Gallwey, acne, androgenic alopecia) OR biochemical (↑ androgens). Serum testing misses 20–40% of biochemical hyperandrogenism. Voice deepening or clitoromegaly → think androgen-secreting tumor, not PCOS
Menstrual irregularityChronic anovulation: oligomenorrhea (<8 cycles/yr or cycle >35 d) or amenorrhea (no menses ×3 mo). Regular cycles don't fully exclude anovulation (unopposed estrogen → DUB)
Polycystic ovaries>12 follicles and/or ovarian volume >10 cc on ultrasound. Non-specific in isolation — common in normal young cycling patients
Pearl: Hirsutism is androgen-pattern terminal hair (upper lip, chin, chest, linea alba, inner thighs, lower back). Acne and alopecia in isolation are unreliable signs of hyperandrogenism — anchor on hirsutism and labs.
9:00–15:00 — Objective 2: Differential & evaluation (DDx and Eval board)

Because PCOS is a diagnosis of exclusion, drive the workup off each criterion:

FeatureMimics to excludeInitial evaluation
HyperandrogenismNon-classic CAH (21-hydroxylase deficiency), acromegaly, androgen-secreting tumor, Cushing'sTotal testosterone; ± 17-OH progesterone (CAH); ± SHBG, DHEAS (tumor if very high T / rapid virilization); midnight salivary cortisol if Cushingoid
Menstrual irregularityPregnancy, thyroid disease, hyperprolactinemia/prolactinoma, hypothalamic amenorrhea, POI/perimenopause, structural (polyp, fibroid, hyperplasia/malignancy)TSH ± free T4, β-hCG, prolactin; ± pelvic US (structural); ± LH/FSH (hypothalamic dysfunction, POI)
Polycystic ovariesNormal ovulatory cycles, exogenous testosterone useInterpret in context — non-specific alone
Pearl: Total testosterone is the single best androgen test (free T assays are unreliable). An increased LH/FSH ratio supports PCOS but its absence doesn't rule it out (LH secretion is pulsatile). Reserve DHEAS / midnight salivary cortisol for clinical red flags (very high T, rapid virilization, Cushingoid features).
15:00–20:00 — Objective 3: Comorbidities (Co-Morbidities boards)
Non-gynecologicScreen / manage
Mood disorders (depression, anxiety, binge eating) — independent of weight/hirsutism/infertilityPHQ-9, GAD-7; ask about disordered eating; refer
Hyperandrogenism (hirsutism)Combined OCPs (less-androgenic 3rd/4th-gen progestin) ± spironolactone (50 mg → up to 100 mg BID; teratogenic — need contraception); cosmetic (laser)
Cardiovascular risk (↑ LDL, ↓ HDL)Screen BP + fasting lipids; statin by calculated CV risk
Insulin resistance / T2DM (7.5–10× higher undiagnosed T2DM)2-h OGTT (or fasting glucose / A1c) q1–2y; lifestyle ± metformin (also helps cycles, androgens, fertility)
Also: OSA, NAFLDScreen per symptoms
GynecologicScreen / manage
Endometrial hyperplasia (3× endometrial cancer risk from chronic unopposed estrogen)Ensure a withdrawal bleed ≥ every 90 days: OCPs, progestin IUD, or cyclic progesterone. Routine US screening NOT recommended
Abnormal uterine bleedingCombined OCPs (less-androgenic progestin) — also helps hirsutism
Infertility (most common cause of anovulatory infertility; ~5–10%/cycle vs 10–15%)Discuss fertility goals early; 5–10% weight loss; refer OB/Gyn or REI; clomiphene/letrozole for ovulation induction
Pregnancy complications (early loss, GDM, PIH/pre-eclampsia, preterm labor — GDM risk independent of BMI)Close monitoring; treat as they arise
Pearl: The 90-day rule is the single most actionable gynecologic point — chronic anovulation means no progesterone to oppose estrogen, so the endometrium needs a scheduled withdrawal bleed at least quarterly to prevent hyperplasia.

Retrieval close: board down — have learners rebuild the 3 Rotterdam criteria, the "all-patients" labs (testosterone, TSH, prolactin, β-hCG), and the comorbidity screening panel (OGTT/A1c, lipids, PHQ-9/GAD-7, 90-day endometrial protection).

2023 Guideline Update added

The deck builds on the 2018 international guideline. The 2023 International Evidence-Based Guideline for PCOS (Teede et al.) keeps the Rotterdam framework and the "diagnosis of exclusion" approach, with these refinements:

  • AMH as an alternative to ultrasound: in adults, anti-Müllerian hormone may establish polycystic ovarian morphology in place of pelvic ultrasound. For patients with irregular cycles + hyperandrogenism (~70% of cases), neither US nor AMH is required — those two criteria already make the diagnosis.
  • Adolescents: require BOTH hyperandrogenism AND ovulatory dysfunction; ultrasound and AMH are NOT recommended (<8 years post-menarche) due to poor specificity.
  • Broader burden emphasized: metabolic and cardiovascular risk, obstructive sleep apnea, and very high prevalence of psychological features — screen for depression, anxiety, and disordered/binge eating.
  • Therapy: lifestyle first; metformin for metabolic/weight/cycle benefit; combined OCPs ± anti-androgen for hyperandrogenism and cycle control. Anti-obesity pharmacotherapy, including GLP-1 receptor agonists, may be considered for weight in adults — evidence in PCOS is still limited and flagged as a research priority.

Cases (from the original board — answers click-to-reveal)

Case 1 — Olga, 24-year-old woman, irregular periods since menarche (every 45–60 days); FHx type 2 DM

1a. What additional history or exam findings help build your differential?Screen for the causes/associated signs of oligomenorrhea: hirsutism (PCOS/hyperandrogenism), breast discharge (prolactinoma), hot flashes (premature ovarian failure), weight gain (Cushing's, hypothyroidism), weight loss (stress, malnutrition, hyperthyroidism), sexual activity (pregnancy, STIs), abnormal pelvic exam (structural cause — polyp, fibroid). She has acne, some chin hair growth, slow weight gain, and is not sexually active.
1a. What are your next steps in evaluation?β-hCG (rule out pregnancy in all reproductive-age women); transvaginal US (structural causes); TSH, prolactin, FSH; total testosterone (± 17-OH progesterone, ± SHBG, ± DHEAS for hyperandrogenism workup).
1b. Labs: normal TSH and prolactin, elevated total testosterone; TVUS shows 15–20 small follicles in both ovaries. Diagnosis?PCOS. She meets all 3 Rotterdam criteria (oligomenorrhea, biochemical hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries) and other causes of oligomenorrhea were excluded — remember, PCOS is a diagnosis of exclusion.
1b. What additional non-gynecologic comorbidities should you screen for?Psychiatric → PHQ-9, GAD-7 (and disordered eating); insulin resistance → A1c, fasting glucose (FHx of T2DM raises concern); cardiovascular risk → fasting lipids.
1b. What gynecologic comorbidities is she at risk for, and what are her options for endometrial protection?Infertility, pregnancy complications, and endometrial hyperplasia with higher endometrial cancer risk. Endometrial protection: combined OCPs, progestin-containing IUDs, or cyclic progesterone — ensure a withdrawal bleed at least every 90 days.

Board-Style MCQs added

1. A 26-year-old woman has hirsutism and cycles every 50 days. Total testosterone is mildly elevated; TSH, prolactin, and β-hCG are normal. Pelvic ultrasound is not yet done. What is the most accurate statement about her diagnosis?
  1. She cannot be diagnosed without ultrasound
  2. She already meets criteria for PCOS (hyperandrogenism + ovulatory dysfunction), with mimics excluded
  3. She needs an LH/FSH ratio >2 to confirm PCOS
  4. A normal DHEAS rules out PCOS
  5. Acne alone would have been sufficient
Answer & explanationsB. With hyperandrogenism plus ovulatory dysfunction and mimics excluded, she meets 2 of 3 Rotterdam criteria — ultrasound (or AMH) is not required (this is ~70% of cases per the 2023 guideline). A — US is unnecessary when 2 criteria are already met. C — an elevated LH/FSH supports but isn't required; its absence doesn't exclude PCOS. D — DHEAS is for tumor workup, not a rule-out. E — acne in isolation is an unreliable sign of hyperandrogenism.
2. A 28-year-old woman is being evaluated for hyperandrogenism. Which single test is the best initial measure of androgen excess?
  1. Free testosterone immunoassay
  2. Total testosterone
  3. DHEAS
  4. 17-hydroxyprogesterone
  5. LH/FSH ratio
Answer & explanationsB. Total testosterone is the single best test; free testosterone immunoassays are unreliable (the free androgen index can be calculated from total T and SHBG). C — DHEAS is reserved for suspected adrenal/androgen-secreting tumor (very high T, rapid virilization). D — 17-OHP screens for non-classic CAH, not the primary androgen measure. E — LH/FSH supports but doesn't quantify androgen excess.
3. A 30-year-old woman with PCOS has chronic anovulation and does not desire pregnancy. Which intervention most directly reduces her risk of endometrial hyperplasia?
  1. Annual transvaginal ultrasound surveillance
  2. Spironolactone monotherapy
  3. Ensuring a withdrawal bleed at least every 90 days (OCP, progestin IUD, or cyclic progesterone)
  4. Metformin alone
  5. Statin therapy
Answer & explanationsC. Chronic unopposed estrogen drives a 3× endometrial cancer risk; a progestin-based regimen producing a withdrawal bleed ≥ every 90 days promotes endometrial turnover. A — routine US screening for endometrial cancer is not recommended. B — spironolactone treats hirsutism, not the endometrium (and is teratogenic). D/E — metformin and statins address metabolic/CV risk, not endometrial protection.
4. Which comorbidity screening is recommended for ALL patients with PCOS?
  1. Coronary CT angiography
  2. Glucose testing (A1c, fasting glucose, or 2-h OGTT), fasting lipids, and mood/eating-disorder screening
  3. Routine endometrial biopsy
  4. Sleep study for everyone
  5. 24-hour urinary free cortisol
Answer & explanationsB. All PCOS patients should be screened for impaired glucose tolerance/T2DM (q1–2y), cardiovascular risk (BP + fasting lipids), and psychological comorbidity (PHQ-9, GAD-7, disordered eating). A/C/D/E — these are directed tests for specific clinical concerns (e.g., OSA symptoms, Cushingoid features), not universal screens.
5. A 17-year-old, 2 years post-menarche, has hirsutism and irregular cycles. Per the 2023 PCOS guideline, which is correct for adolescents?
  1. Pelvic ultrasound is required to diagnose PCOS
  2. AMH should be used in place of ultrasound
  3. Both hyperandrogenism and ovulatory dysfunction are required; ultrasound and AMH are not recommended
  4. One criterion is sufficient at this age
  5. Irregular cycles are always pathologic at this age
Answer & explanationsC. In adolescents the 2023 guideline requires BOTH hyperandrogenism AND ovulatory dysfunction, and does not recommend ultrasound or AMH (poor specificity <8 years post-menarche). A/B — neither US nor AMH is used in adolescents. D — one criterion is insufficient. E — irregular cycles can be normal for 2–3 years after menarche.

Take-Home Points

  1. PCOS is defined by the Rotterdam criteria — hyperandrogenism, menstrual irregularity, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound (or AMH in adults per the 2023 guideline). You need two of three.
  2. It is a diagnosis of exclusion — rule out other causes of menstrual irregularity and/or hyperandrogenism (pregnancy, thyroid, prolactin, CAH, Cushing's, androgen-secreting tumor) before assigning the diagnosis.
  3. Screen for the many associated comorbidities: abnormal uterine bleeding, endometrial hyperplasia/cancer, insulin resistance/T2DM, cardiovascular risk, mood/eating disorders, infertility, OSA/NAFLD, and pregnancy complications.
