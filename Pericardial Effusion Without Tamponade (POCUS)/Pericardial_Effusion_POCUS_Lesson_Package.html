Learning Objectives

  1. Identify a pericardial effusion in the parasternal long-axis (PLAX) view and distinguish it from a pleural effusion.
  2. Build a focused differential for pericardial effusion based on the clinical setting.
  3. Describe the key clinical features that suggest tamponade physiology.

Pre-Work (send the day before)

Three retrieval questions — answer from memory, then check.

1. On the PLAX view, how do you tell a pericardial from a pleural effusion? A pericardial effusion lies anterior to the descending thoracic aorta; a pleural effusion lies posterior to it. Confirm in a second view.
2. Name the three key clinical features of cardiac tamponade. Elevated JVP, tachycardia, and pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg. (Beck's triad is rarely present in medical tamponade.)
3. What is the single most common cause of pericardial effusion in the developed world? In the developing world? Developed world: viral. Developing world: tuberculosis (>70%).

Teaching Script

Run the board in presenter mode so the diagnosis isn't revealed. Pose each question, give ~30 s of neighbor discussion, then advance the animation to reveal the answer.

Hook (2 min)

"Your 78-year-old patient with ESKD comes in with a viral-sounding illness and fatigue. HR 110, BP 100/60, SpO2 82% on 3 L. You grab the probe and put it on the chest. In 90 seconds, POCUS will change what you're worried about — and what you do next. Let's read this clip together."

Segment 1 — Identify the view & finding (Objective 1)

ASK: "What cardiac view is this, and what's your one-line interpretation?"

Reveal: Parasternal long axis (PLAX). Grossly normal chamber sizes and LV function with a small circumferential pericardial effusion.

ASK: "How do you know that dark stripe is pericardial and not pleural fluid?"
FeaturePericardial effusionPleural effusion
Relation to descending thoracic aorta (PLAX)Anterior to the aortaPosterior to the aorta
Most sensitive windowSubxiphoid (subcostal) view
Pearl: Always confirm any cardiac POCUS finding in a second view before you act on it. The aorta is your landmark: fluid in front of it is in the pericardium, behind it is in the pleura.

Segment 2 — Differential by setting (Objective 2)

ASK: "Given an ESKD patient with a viral prodrome — what's on your differential for the effusion?"
Infectious (more common)Non-infectious (less common)
Viral — most common in developed world (Coxsackie, echovirus, HHV6, parvovirus B19, COVID-19)

Pearl: Match the differential to the patient. Here, both a viral pericarditis and uremic effusion (ESKD) are in play — the clinical course and labs sort it out.

Segment 3 — Assess for tamponade (Objective 3)

ASK: "Does this patient need a pericardiocentesis right now? What would tell you the effusion is hemodynamically significant?"

Reveal: No — likely viral, no signs of hemodynamic compromise. Tamponade is a clinical diagnosis supported by imaging, not a number on the screen.

Physical findingFrequency in tamponade (%)
Elevated JVP100
Tachycardia81–100
Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg98
Beck's triad (↑JVP, hypotension, muffled tones)0–19
ASK: "How is pulsus paradoxus measured, and how do higher cutoffs change the test?"

Inspiratory fall in systolic BP >10 mmHg. Raising the cutoff increases specificity and decreases sensitivity: >10 mmHg LR+ 3.3 (LR− 0.03); >12 mmHg LR+ 5.9 (LR− 0.05).

Pearl — 2025 ESC framing: Tamponade is a dynamic process driven by the rate of fluid accumulation, not absolute size. A small, rapidly accumulating effusion can tamponade; a large chronic one may not.

Retrieval close (1 min)

Have a learner rebuild the take-home points from memory: the aorta rule, the three tamponade signs, and "treat the cause when there's no compromise."

The Case (click to reveal)

78-year-old with ESKD, URI symptoms and fatigue. HR 110, RR 18, BP 100/60, SpO2 82% on 3 L NC. PLAX clip shows a small circumferential pericardial effusion with normal chamber sizes and LV function.

What view is this and what is the finding?PLAX; small circumferential pericardial effusion, normal LV function.
Pericardial or pleural — how do you prove it?The fluid lies anterior to the descending aorta → pericardial. Confirm in a subxiphoid view.
Most likely causes here?Viral pericarditis and/or uremic effusion (ESKD). Less likely: autoimmune, malignancy, hypothyroidism.
Does the patient need pericardiocentesis?No — no tamponade signs (no elevated JVP/pulsus/hemodynamic compromise). Supportive care; treat the underlying cause. He improves clinically.

Board-Style MCQs

Q1

A 64-year-old man has a PLAX clip showing an anechoic stripe. To distinguish a pericardial from a pleural effusion in this view, you should note the fluid's relationship to which structure?

A. Mitral valve · B. Left atrium · C. Descending thoracic aorta · D. Right ventricular outflow tract · E. Aortic valve

Answer & explanationC. In PLAX, a pericardial effusion tracks anterior to the descending thoracic aorta, whereas a pleural effusion lies posterior to it. The aorta is the dividing landmark. The MV, LA, RVOT, and AV do not reliably separate the two fluid spaces.
Q2

You see a moderate pericardial effusion on PLAX. Which single view is MOST sensitive for confirming and sizing a pericardial effusion?

A. Apical 4-chamber · B. Subxiphoid (subcostal) · C. Parasternal short axis · D. Suprasternal notch · E. PLAX only

Answer & explanationB. The subxiphoid view is the most sensitive cardiac window for detecting a pericardial effusion and is the standard confirmatory/second view. Always corroborate a POCUS finding in a second window.
Q3

A 40-year-old woman with a large pericardial effusion has BP 96/70, HR 118, JVP elevated, and an inspiratory systolic BP drop of 14 mmHg. Which finding most strongly raises the likelihood of tamponade?

A. Effusion size · B. Beck's triad · C. Pulsus paradoxus >12 mmHg · D. Friction rub · E. Low QRS voltage on ECG

Answer & explanationC. Pulsus paradoxus >12 mmHg carries an LR+ of 5.9 for tamponade (>10 mmHg LR+ 3.3); higher cutoffs increase specificity. Beck's triad is present in only 0–19% of medical tamponade and is unreliable. Effusion size alone does not establish tamponade — physiology does.
Q4

A patient from a TB-endemic region has a large pericardial effusion. Which is the single most likely cause in that setting?

A. Viral · B. Malignancy · C. Tuberculosis · D. Uremia · E. Hypothyroidism

Answer & explanationC. Tuberculosis causes >70% of pericardial effusions in the developing world. Viral is the most common cause in the developed world. The differential should always be weighted by the clinical setting.
Q5

A 55-year-old develops hypotension 30 minutes after a cardiac procedure. Echo shows only a SMALL pericardial effusion, yet there is RA/RV collapse and pulsus paradoxus. Best explanation per current (2025 ESC) understanding?

A. Small effusions never cause tamponade · B. Tamponade depends on the rate of accumulation, not absolute size · C. Beck's triad rules in tamponade · D. Pericardiocentesis is contraindicated for small effusions · E. The finding must be a pleural effusion

Answer & explanationB. Tamponade is a dynamic process governed by the rate of fluid accumulation and pericardial compliance, not absolute volume — a rapidly accumulating small effusion (e.g., post-procedure) can tamponade. Management is urgent pericardiocentesis for hemodynamically significant effusion (2025 ESC). Beck's triad is unreliable; the RA/RV collapse and pulsus confirm physiology.

Take-Home Points

  1. On PLAX, a pericardial effusion lies anterior to the descending aorta; a pleural effusion lies posterior. Confirm in a second view (subxiphoid is most sensitive).
  2. Most pericardial effusions do not cause tamponade. The key clinical features are elevated JVP, tachycardia, and pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg — and tamponade is driven by the rate of accumulation, not size.
  3. Weight the differential by setting (viral vs TB vs uremic, etc.), and treat the cause when there is no hemodynamic compromise.
