Learning Objectives

  1. Review the pathogenesis and etiology of the most common complications of multiple myeloma (MM) — the CRABI features.
  2. Describe the basics of testing for plasma cell dyscrasias: SPEP with immunofixation, serum free light chains (FLC), and the role (and limits) of UPEP.
  3. Differentiate monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS), smoldering myeloma (SMM), and MM by M-protein size, marrow plasma-cell burden, and end-organ damage / myeloma-defining biomarkers.
  4. Bonus: Understand prognosis and natural course of MM (R-ISS / R2-ISS; induction → autoSCT → maintenance).
  5. Bonus: Recognize complications of MM and the side effects of commonly used therapies.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What does the mnemonic CRABI stand for, and what is the underlying mechanism for each? Calcium (hyperCalcemia, from osteoclast activation / RANK-L), Renal dysfunction (paraprotein/light-chain cast nephropathy), Anemia (marrow infiltration), Boney lytic lesions (osteoclast activation), Immunodeficiency (suppression of normal plasma cells → hypogammaglobulinemia, infection). These are the end-organ-damage criteria that define MM.
Q2. A urine dipstick reads "negative protein," yet you suspect myeloma. Why can the dipstick be falsely reassuring? Urine dipstick detects albumin only. It misses monoclonal free light chains ("Bence Jones proteins"), which are what spill into urine in many plasma cell dyscrasias. You need UPEP with immunofixation or — far more conveniently — a serum free light chain (FLC) assay to detect them.
Q3. Name the three screening tests you would order together to work up a suspected plasma cell dyscrasia, and why FLC matters. SPEP + serum immunofixation + serum FLC. SPEP+IFX alone is ~80% sensitive; adding FLC raises sensitivity to ~99% and captures the ~16% of patients with light-chain-only myeloma that SPEP misses — and it replaces the cumbersome 24-hour UPEP.

Hook (2 min)

"A 55-year-old is admitted for AKI and gets a routine UA — dipstick protein is negative. The hospitalist almost stops there. Two weeks later their SPEP shows a 3.5 g/dL IgA-lambda M-spike." Ask: how did a 'negative' urine protein nearly miss myeloma? (Dipstick reads albumin, not Bence Jones light chains.) You're planting Case 1.

Objective 1 — Pathogenesis & the CRABI complications 8 min

Briefly name the family of plasma cell disorders (MGUS, SMM, MM, solitary plasmacytoma, Waldenström, AL amyloid, POEMS, cryoglobulinemia), then narrow to the three this talk covers: MGUS, SMM, MM. Click the lineage/structure buttons: lymphoid progenitor → B cell → plasma cell; each immunoglobulin = heavy chain + light chain (κ or λ). A clone overproduces one → an M protein (paraprotein) above the polyclonal background.

ASK: "What clinical findings come from (a) marrow infiltration, (b) RANK-L / osteoclast activation, (c) paraprotein deposition?" Reveal each: marrow infiltration → anemia + immunodeficiency; RANK-L → hypercalcemia + lytic bone lesions; paraprotein → renal disease (cast nephropathy in MM; nephrotic-range in AL amyloid).
PEARL: The complications aren't a random list — they map 1:1 to three mechanisms. CRABI = the marrow (A, I), the bone axis (C, B), and the paraprotein (R). Teach the mechanism and the mnemonic writes itself.

Objective 2 — Testing: SPEP, UPEP, FLC 8 min

Build the SPEP: electrophoresis separates serum protein by charge into albumin / α1 / α2 / β / γ. A monoclonal M-spike is usually in the γ region (sometimes β or α2). SPEP tells you the size (concentration) of the spike. Immunofixation (IFX) is more sensitive and types the heavy + light chain (e.g., IgG-κ) but gives no size — always pair it with SPEP. SPEP+IFX ≈ 80% sensitive.

TestWhat it addsSensitivity (cumulative)
SPEP + immunofixationDetects & sizes M-protein; types heavy/light chain~80%
+ UPEP (24-h urine)Catches Bence Jones light chains in urine~95%
+ serum FLC (κ/λ)Catches light-chain-only disease; no 24-h collection~99%
ASK: "Urine dipstick protein is negative — can you stop?" No. Dipstick = albumin only; it misses Bence Jones free light chains. UPEP or (better) serum FLC is needed.

FLC ratio (κ/λ): normal 0.26–1.65. Ratio >1.65 → κ clone; <0.26 → λ clone. Renal failure raises the ratio (reduced clearance, up to ~3) — so use stricter thresholds (>4 or <0.25) to flag a true clone in ESRD. A markedly skewed ratio (>100 or <0.01) with a high involved FLC can itself be myeloma-defining.

PEARL: Up to 16% of myeloma is light-chain-only and can be invisible on SPEP. Order SPEP+IFX and serum FLC together — the FLC both rescues those cases and lets you skip the 24-h UPEP.

Objective 3 — MGUS vs SMM vs MM 10 min

Three axes separate them: M-protein size, % clonal marrow plasma cells, and end-organ damage (CRABI) or a myeloma-defining biomarker.

MGUSSmoldering MMMultiple Myeloma
Serum M-protein<3 g/dL≥3 g/dL≥3 g/dL*
Urine M-protein<500 mg/24h≥500 mg/24h≥500 mg/24h*
Clonal marrow plasma cells<10%10–60%≥10% (≥60% is diagnostic)
End-organ damageNoneNoneCRABI or a myeloma-defining event
Progression risk~1%/yr~10%/yr (first 5 yr)—

*Oligo-secretory (M-protein <1 g/dL) and non-secretory MM will not meet the M-protein criterion — diagnose on marrow / biomarkers / FLC.

Myeloma-defining events (IMWG "SLiM" biomarkers), any one of which = MM even without CRABI: clonal marrow plasma cells ≥60%; involved:uninvolved serum FLC ratio ≥100 (involved FLC ≥100 mg/L); >1 focal lesion on MRI.

ASK: "Why bother separating MGUS from smoldering myeloma if neither is treated?" Because the slopes differ a lot — MGUS ~1%/yr vs SMM ~10%/yr for the first 5 years — which drives how closely you monitor (and, increasingly, whether to intervene early in high-risk SMM).
PEARL: SMM staging needs sensitive imaging to be sure you're not missing a lytic lesion (which would reclassify as MM). Whole-body X-ray is easy but insensitive; whole-body low-dose CT or MRI is preferred, and PET/CT is the most sensitive.

Bonus — Prognosis (R-ISS / R2-ISS) 5 min

At diagnosis, send β2-microglobulin (mg/L), LDH, albumin, and FISH — these drive staging. R-ISS combines ISS (β2M, albumin) with LDH and high-risk cytogenetics [del(17p), t(4;14), t(14;16)].

R-ISSDefinition (abbreviated)5-yr OSMedian OS
Iβ2M <3.5 mg/L + normal LDH + standard-risk FISH + albumin ≥3.5 g/dL82%Not reached
IINeither I nor III62%83 months
IIIβ2M ≥5.5 mg/L AND (elevated LDH OR high-risk FISH)40%43 months

Note the units: β2-microglobulin is reported in mg/L (a frequent slide/chart error fixed in the optimized deck), albumin in g/dL. See the 2023–26 update for R2-ISS, which adds gain/amp 1q21.

Bonus — Treatment overview & toxicities 7 min

Natural history: induction → (autoSCT if eligible) → maintenance, with serial M-protein monitoring; relapse/remission cycles recur. Induction is now a quadruplet — an anti-CD38 monoclonal (daratumumab) added to a proteasome inhibitor + immunomodulatory agent + steroid (e.g., D-VRd; see update). autoSCT eligibility: roughly age <75, ECOG <3, no cirrhosis/severe HF — renal disease is not a contraindication.

ASK: "A myeloma patient on bortezomib + lenalidomide + dex — what three prophylactic meds should already be on the chart?" Acyclovir (VZV), aspirin/anticoagulation (VTE), and — if steroids continue — PJP prophylaxis ± PPI and bone support.

Cases original board cases — sub-questions click-to-reveal

Case 1 — "negative" urine protein

55-year-old recently admitted for AKI and proteinuria, now in clinic for follow-up (AKI resolved at discharge). SPEP + FLC ordered for the proteinuria: M-protein 2 g/dL; IFX "IgG-κ monoclonal protein"; κ 6.3, λ 2.1, κ/λ ratio 3.0.

How would you interpret these results, and what plasma cell dyscrasia is this? There is an M-spike <3 g/dL and an elevated κ/λ ratio (κ clone). Labs: Hgb 13, Ca 9, Cr stable at 1. PET-CT: no lytic lesions. Marrow: 5% clonal plasma cells. M-protein <3 g/dL, <10% marrow involvement, no CRABI → MGUS.
What lab monitoring should this patient have? Serial SPEP, immunofixation, serum FLC (± UPEP), CBC, calcium, creatinine — repeat at intervals (~every 3 months initially), spacing out over time if the disease stays stable. MGUS progresses ~1%/yr.

Case 2a → 2b → 2c — the SMM→MM trajectory

64-year-old, follow-up of abnormal labs. SPEP/IFX/FLC: M-protein 3.5 g/dL; IFX "IgA-λ monoclonal protein"; κ 1, λ 5, κ/λ ratio 0.2. Labs: Hgb 14, Cr 1.0, Ca 9.5. PET/CT: no lytic lesions. Marrow: 12% involvement.

2a. What is the diagnosis and the yearly progression risk? Smoldering myeloma — M-spike ≥3 g/dL and ≥10% marrow involvement, but no CRABI/end-organ damage. Progression risk ~10%/yr for the first 5 years (then ~5%/yr).
2b. One year later: acute back pain, lumbar X-ray shows two vertebral fractures + several lucencies; Cr 2.1 (baseline 1.1), Ca 10.5. Now what — and how do you treat the AKI? Now IgA-λ multiple myeloma — end-organ damage has appeared (lytic/pathologic fractures, rising Cr, Ca). The renal failure is likely cast nephropathy ("myeloma kidney"): aggressive IVF (if no contraindication) targeting ~3 L urine output/day to limit further cast deposition, correct hypercalcemia, and start urgent systemic therapy. Workup: repeat SPEP/UPEP/FLC, marrow biopsy, and β2-microglobulin/LDH/albumin for staging. Marrow shows no high-risk features, R-ISS stage I → started on RVd (lenalidomide, bortezomib, dex; quadruplet D-RVd now standard — see update).
2c. After remission on maintenance lenalidomide, the patient returns with fever, cough, hypoxia; CT shows bilateral ground-glass opacities. Differential + what supportive/prophylactic meds were needed? Immunocompromised differential: common CAP (S. pneumoniae, COVID-19, viruses), opportunistic (PJP, Aspergillus, endemic fungi), nosocomial (Staph, Pseudomonas) — and rarely, advanced myeloma infiltrate. Prophylaxis this patient should have had: bisphosphonate; acyclovir while on bortezomib; aspirin/anticoagulation while on lenalidomide; PJP prophylaxis + PPI + Ca/vit-D while on steroids. For recurrent infections with low IgG, IVIG is indicated.

Board-style MCQs NEW — ABIM single-best-answer, click to reveal

1. A 68-year-old man is found to have total protein elevation. SPEP shows a 1.8 g/dL IgG-κ M-spike; marrow biopsy 6% clonal plasma cells; Hgb 13.4, Ca 9.4, Cr 0.9, normal skeletal survey. Which is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Begin lenalidomide–dexamethasone
  2. Autologous stem cell transplant referral
  3. Periodic monitoring with SPEP, FLC, CBC, Ca, Cr
  4. Start bortezomib-based induction
  5. Empiric bisphosphonate therapy
Answer & explanation C. This is MGUS (M-protein <3 g/dL, <10% marrow plasma cells, no CRABI) — managed with observation, not treatment. A/D (induction) and B (transplant) are for MM, not MGUS/SMM. E bisphosphonates are indicated for myeloma bone disease or hypercalcemia, neither present here. Monitor: progression risk ~1%/yr.

2. A 72-year-old woman has fatigue and back pain. Hgb 8.9, Ca 11.6, Cr 2.4. SPEP M-spike 4.2 g/dL IgG-κ; marrow 40% clonal plasma cells; spine MRI shows multiple focal lesions. Which feature alone is sufficient to diagnose multiple myeloma here?

  1. Marrow plasma cells 40%
  2. M-spike 4.2 g/dL
  3. Hypercalcemia (Ca 11.6) with anemia and renal failure
  4. Age >70
  5. IgG-κ isotype
Answer & explanation C. CRABI end-organ damage (hyperCalcemia ≥11, Anemia, Renal dysfunction) defines MM. A 40% marrow involvement meets the ≥10% threshold but isn't diagnostic on its own (≥60% would be a myeloma-defining biomarker). B M-spike size doesn't establish MM without CRABI or a biomarker. D/E are not diagnostic criteria. (Here she also has >1 focal MRI lesion — independently myeloma-defining.)

3. A 60-year-old with suspected myeloma has a normal SPEP and normal immunofixation, but anemia and renal insufficiency. Which test is most likely to reveal the diagnosis?

  1. Repeat SPEP in 3 months
  2. Serum free light chain (κ/λ) assay
  3. Serum viscosity
  4. Peripheral blood flow cytometry
  5. Bone scan (technetium-99m)
Answer & explanation B. Up to ~16% of myeloma is light-chain-only and can be missed by SPEP/IFX; the serum FLC assay detects the involved light chain and an abnormal ratio. A delays diagnosis. C viscosity is for hyperviscosity (more Waldenström/IgM). D flow of blood rarely shows marrow plasma cells. E technetium bone scans miss myeloma's purely lytic lesions — use skeletal survey/CT/MRI/PET.

4. A newly diagnosed MM patient is about to start bortezomib, lenalidomide, and dexamethasone. Which prophylaxis set best matches the regimen?

  1. Acyclovir + aspirin (or anticoagulation) + consider PJP prophylaxis
  2. Fluconazole + warfarin + allopurinol
  3. Levofloxacin + IVIG + calcitonin
  4. TMP-SMX only
  5. No prophylaxis is required
Answer & explanation A. Bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor) → VZV reactivation risk → acyclovir. Lenalidomide (IMiD) → VTE/arterial thrombosis → aspirin or anticoagulation. High-dose steroids → consider PJP prophylaxis (± PPI, bone support). The other combinations mix unrelated or contraindicated agents.

5. At diagnosis of MM, which set of labs is required to assign the R-ISS (and feeds the newer R2-ISS) stage?

  1. Ferritin, reticulocyte count, haptoglobin
  2. β2-microglobulin, albumin, LDH, and FISH cytogenetics
  3. ESR, CRP, serum viscosity
  4. 24-hour urine creatinine clearance only
  5. Vitamin B12, folate, copper
Answer & explanation B. R-ISS = ISS (β2-microglobulin + albumin) plus LDH and high-risk FISH [del(17p), t(4;14), t(14;16)]. R2-ISS adds gain/amp 1q21 to these. β2-microglobulin is reported in mg/L. The other panels are unrelated to myeloma staging.

2023–2026 Guideline & Practice Update NEW — surfaced here & on appended deck slide

The original lesson's diagnostic framework is unchanged and remains correct (SPEP/IFX + serum FLC; CRABI; MGUS/SMM/MM thresholds; IMWG SLiM biomarkers). The standing teaching slides were left untouched; movement since Jan 2023 is in therapy and prognosis:

  • Quadruplet induction is now standard. Adding an anti-CD38 monoclonal (daratumumab) to VRd (D-VRd) reduced progression/death ~60% vs VRd in transplant-eligible newly diagnosed MM (PERSEUS, NEJM 2024); FDA-approved July 2024. Isatuximab-VRd was approved for transplant-ineligible patients (IMROZ, 2024). The deck's "3 or 4 drugs ± monoclonal antibody" is now "quadruplet with the mAb routinely included."
  • R2-ISS refines prognosis. The Second Revision of the ISS (D'Agostino, JCO 2022) adds gain/amplification of 1q21 to ISS, LDH, and del(17p)/t(4;14) as an additive 4-tier score — better separating the large, heterogeneous R-ISS stage II group.
  • Earlier intervention in high-risk smoldering myeloma. Daratumumab monotherapy delayed progression to active MM (5-yr PFS 63% vs 41% with active monitoring) in high-risk SMM (AQUILA, NEJM 2024) — shifting practice toward treating select high-risk SMM rather than observing.
  • New relapsed/refractory classes. BCMA-directed therapies are now mainstream: CAR-T cilta-cel (CARTITUDE-4; FDA April 2024 for ≥1 prior line, lenalidomide-refractory) and ide-cel; bispecific T-cell engagers teclistamab (BCMA, FDA Oct 2022), elranatamab (BCMA), and talquetamab (GPRC5D).
  • Monitoring. Mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF) is emerging as a more sensitive alternative to immunofixation for detecting/monitoring M-protein (not yet routine).

Oncology therapy evolves quickly — confirm regimens with current NCCN/heme-onc guidance and your treating oncologist before teaching specifics.

Take-Home Points

  1. MGUS, smoldering myeloma, and MM are a spectrum of clonal plasma-cell disorders; complications follow CRABI — hyperCalcemia, Renal dysfunction, Anemia, Boney lytic lesions, Immunodeficiency.
  2. Screen with SPEP + serum immunofixation AND serum FLC. FLC catches the ~16% of light-chain-only myeloma that SPEP misses, brings sensitivity to ~99%, and replaces the 24-h UPEP. A "negative" urine dipstick does not rule out myeloma.
  3. Distinguish the three diagnoses by M-protein size, % clonal marrow plasma cells, and end-organ damage (CRABI) or a myeloma-defining biomarker (≥60% marrow, FLC ratio ≥100, >1 focal MRI lesion).
  4. Progression risk: MGUS ~1%/yr; SMM ~10%/yr for the first 5 years. At MM diagnosis, send β2-microglobulin (mg/L), LDH, albumin, and FISH for R-ISS / R2-ISS staging.
  5. Front-line therapy is now quadruplet (anti-CD38 mAb + VRd) ± autoSCT then maintenance; manage hypercalcemia, cast nephropathy (aggressive IVF + urgent systemic therapy), and class-specific infection/VTE prophylaxis.
