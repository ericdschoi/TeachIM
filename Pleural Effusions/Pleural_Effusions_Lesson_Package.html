Learning Objectives

  1. Apply the criteria that separate pleural effusions into transudative and exudative.
  2. Generate a differential for each, and tie each diagnosis to its characteristic pleural-fluid studies.
  3. Build a framework for the management of parapneumonic effusions (uncomplicated vs complicated vs empyema).

Pre-work send the day before

1. State Light's criteria and how many must be met to call an effusion exudative.

Exudate if any one of: pleural/serum protein >0.5; pleural/serum LDH >0.6; pleural LDH >2/3 the serum upper limit of normal. Sensitivity ~98%, specificity ~83%.

2. A patient on diuretics has fluid that looks exudative by Light's — what's the catch and the fix?

Diuretics concentrate protein/LDH and misclassify ~25% of transudates as exudates. Use the serum − pleural albumin gradient >1.2 g/dL to reclassify it as a transudate.

3. What three findings flip a parapneumonic effusion from "antibiotics alone" to "must drain"?

pH <7.2, low glucose, or loculations/septations (complicated); frank pus or a positive Gram stain/culture defines empyema. All need drainage in addition to antibiotics.

Teaching Script

The original interactive board drives the visuals; this script adds a hook, ASK pauses, pearls, and a retrieval close. Board navigation: Light's Criteria → Differential → Management → Cases (with a "Bonus information" deep-dive on each test).

Hook 2 min

"You see a blunted costophrenic angle and a meniscus on the CXR. Before you order a single send-out test, you need to answer two questions in order: is this fluid transudate or exudate, and if it's a parapneumonic exudate, does it need a drain? Get those two right and the rest of the work-up falls into place." Project the title slide.

ASK — "Which effusions warrant a tap?" Reveal: nearly all effusions >1 cm on ultrasound or lateral decubitus film should get an ultrasound-guided diagnostic thoracentesis.

Objective 1 — Transudate vs exudate (Light's) 4 min Objective 1

ASK — "Recite Light's criteria. How many must be met for exudate?" Reveal: any one.

Protein ratioPleural/serum protein > 0.5

Pearl. Light's is sensitive (~98%) but only ~83% specific and over-calls exudate in diuretic-treated patients. When the clinical picture screams transudate (HF, cirrhosis) but Light's says exudate, check the serum − pleural albumin gradient: >1.2 g/dL = transudate. High pleural RBCs (>10k) also falsely raise LDH.

Objective 3 — Parapneumonic management 4 min Objective 3

ASK — "Sort a parapneumonic effusion into uncomplicated, complicated, or empyema — what features and what management for each?"
CategoryDefining featuresManagement
UncomplicatedNo loculations, pH >7.2, normal glucoseAntibiotics alone
ComplicatedLoculations/septations, pH <7.2, or low glucoseAntibiotics + drainage
EmpyemaFrank pus or positive Gram stain/cultureAntibiotics + drainage; often surgical (VATS/decortication)
Pearl — drain choice and adjuncts. A small-bore (≤14F) pigtail is first-line for most pleural infection (BTS 2023; less pain, equal efficacy); reserve larger bore (24–32F) for heavy loculation or pus, and VATS/decortication for empyema. Intrapleural tPA + DNase (MIST-2) improves drainage and cuts surgical referral but does not lower mortality.

Retrieval close 2 min

Board off. Have one learner rebuild the algorithm: tap any effusion >1 cm → Light's → if exudate, targeted studies → if parapneumonic, pH/glucose/loculation decide drain. Have a second learner state the three Light's criteria and the albumin-gradient rescue.

Cases click to reveal

Case 1 — 46-year-old, fevers/cough/SOB

L-sided consolidation + moderate effusion. Pleural: protein 3.5, LDH 76, pH 7.26, Gram stain +PMNs, cell count 350 (88% PMN). Serum: protein 4.0, LDH 278.

Transudate or exudate?

Exudate — pleural/serum protein 3.5/4.0 = 0.88 (>0.5). (LDH ratio 0.27 and absolute LDH are not met, so the protein criterion alone makes it an exudate.)

Specialized testing?

Not needed — clinical picture + fluid are diagnostic.

Diagnosis & management?

Uncomplicated parapneumonic effusion (pH >7.2, no loculation). Treat the pneumonia with antibiotics alone.

Case 2 — 72-year-old, prior CVA + chronic aspiration

CT: loculated R effusion with septations + RLL consolidation. Pleural: protein 3.5, LDH 356, pH 7.16, Gram stain +PMNs, cell count 980 (90% PMN). Serum: protein 3.5, LDH 278.

Transudate or exudate?

Exudate — meets all three (protein ratio 1.0, LDH ratio 1.28, pleural LDH 356 > 2/3 of serum ULN).

Diagnosis & management?

Complicated parapneumonic effusion (pH 7.16 <7.2 + loculations). Antibiotics PLUS drainage of the loculated space (pigtail vs chest tube); intrapleural tPA can augment drainage.

Case 3 — 82-year-old, metastatic breast cancer

Near-opacified L hemithorax; therapeutic tap. Pleural: protein 2.1, LDH 156, pH 7.46, Gram stain none, cell count 580 (80% lymphocytes). Serum: protein 2.5, LDH 278.

Transudate or exudate?

Exudate — pleural/serum protein 2.1/2.5 = 0.84 (>0.5). (Note: the LDH ratio here is 156/278 = 0.56, which is below the 0.6 threshold — see the changelog "human review" item.)

Specialized testing & result?

Send cytology for any effusion of uncertain etiology (BTS). Here cytology is negative on the first tap.

Diagnosis & management?

Likely malignant effusion. Repeat a large-volume thoracentesis — serial taps raise cytologic sensitivity. For recurrent symptomatic disease, indwelling pleural catheter or talc pleurodesis (both first-line).

Board-Style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A patient with decompensated heart failure on high-dose furosemide has a pleural tap: pleural/serum protein 0.55, pleural/serum LDH 0.62. Serum albumin 3.6, pleural albumin 1.9. What best explains the discordance, and how should you classify the effusion?

  1. True exudate — pursue malignancy work-up
  2. Diuretic-induced pseudo-exudate; albumin gradient 1.7 >1.2 = transudate
  3. Chylothorax
  4. Empyema
  5. Hemothorax
Answer + explanation

B. Diuretics concentrate pleural protein and LDH, pushing the ratios just over Light's thresholds (~25% of transudates misclassified). The serum − pleural albumin gradient = 3.6 − 1.9 = 1.7 (>1.2) reclassifies this as a transudate consistent with HF. A over-tests. C/D/E have specific findings absent here.

2. A parapneumonic effusion shows pH 7.10, glucose 35, and loculations on ultrasound; no frank pus, Gram stain negative. Best management?

  1. Antibiotics alone
  2. Observation with repeat imaging in 48 h
  3. Antibiotics plus chest-tube drainage
  4. Pleurodesis
  5. Diuresis
Answer + explanation

C. pH <7.2, low glucose, and loculations define a complicated parapneumonic effusion → antibiotics PLUS drainage. A is for uncomplicated effusions. B delays needed drainage. D treats malignant effusions, not infection. E treats transudates.

3. A lymphocyte-predominant exudative effusion with negative cytology and an ADA of 78 U/L in a patient from a TB-endemic region. Most likely diagnosis and next best test?

  1. Malignancy — repeat cytology only
  2. TB pleurisy — pleural biopsy for tissue stain/culture
  3. Chylothorax — triglycerides
  4. Rheumatoid pleurisy — RF
  5. Hemothorax — pleural hematocrit
Answer + explanation

B. A markedly elevated ADA (>40) is highly sensitive/specific (>90%) for TB pleurisy; because pleural-fluid AFB stain/culture are insensitive, pleural biopsy for tissue stain and culture is the diagnostic confirmatory test. The other options don't fit a lymphocytic, high-ADA effusion.

4. Which statement about intrapleural tPA/DNase for pleural infection is correct?

  1. It reduces mortality compared with chest tube alone
  2. It improves fluid drainage and reduces surgical referral but has no proven mortality benefit
  3. It is contraindicated in all loculated effusions
  4. It replaces antibiotics
  5. It is first-line for transudative effusions
Answer + explanation

B. MIST-2 (Rahman, NEJM 2011) showed combined tPA + DNase improved radiographic drainage and reduced surgical referral/length of stay, without a mortality benefit. It supplements, not replaces, antibiotics and drainage; it has no role in transudates.

5. An 82-year-old with metastatic cancer has a large recurrent symptomatic effusion; first cytology is negative. Best next step?

  1. Stop the work-up — malignancy excluded
  2. Repeat a large-volume thoracentesis (and consider cytology again)
  3. Start empiric antibiotics
  4. Immediate decortication
  5. Pleural pH to decide drainage
Answer + explanation

B. A single negative cytology does not exclude malignancy (initial sensitivity ~60–65%); a second large-volume tap raises yield substantially (~75–92%). A is wrong. C treats infection. D is over-aggressive. E applies to parapneumonic, not malignant, effusions.

Modern Framing — 2023 Context currency check

Light's criteria, the differential, and the parapneumonic framework are timeless and verified current as of June 2026. The items below add modern context; none change the lesson's standing teaching.

BTS 2023 pleural disease guideline. Pleural fluid pH ≤7.2 (or pus / positive Gram stain or culture) defines a complicated effusion/empyema → drain. Small-bore (≤14F) chest tubes are first-line for pleural infection (equal efficacy, less pain). RAPID score risk-stratifies pleural-infection prognosis.
tPA + DNase (MIST-2, Rahman NEJM 2011). Improves radiographic drainage and reduces surgical referral and length of stay — but no mortality benefit. The deck's caveat remains accurate.
Malignant pleural effusion (ATS/STS/STR 2018). For recurrent symptomatic MPE, indwelling pleural catheter and talc pleurodesis are both first-line; IPC + talc (IPC-PLUS) increases pleurodesis success. Send cytology on initial work-up; serial large-volume taps raise yield.

Take-Home Points

  1. Tap any effusion >1 cm on US/lateral decubitus with an ultrasound-guided diagnostic thoracentesis.
  2. Light's criteria separate transudate (HF, renal/liver failure, hypoalbuminemia) from exudate (infection, malignancy, autoimmune, PE, chylothorax). Any one criterion = exudate; use the albumin gradient when diuretics confound.
  3. Targeted fluid studies localize the cause — cell count/differential, pH/glucose, Gram stain/culture, cytology, ADA, amylase, triglycerides, hematocrit.
  4. Parapneumonic: uncomplicated → antibiotics; complicated (pH <7.2, low glucose, loculations) and empyema (pus/+culture) → antibiotics + drainage, often multidisciplinary.
  5. tPA/DNase augments drainage without a mortality benefit; small-bore tubes are first-line (BTS 2023).
