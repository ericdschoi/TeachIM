Is S. aureus in a blood culture ever a contaminant?	No — always significant; treat as true bacteremia.
The five core interventions for SAB?	ID consult, echocardiography, repeat blood cultures (clearance), source control, appropriate drug & duration.
Empiric therapy for suspected SAB?	Vancomycin (covers MRSA) while awaiting susceptibilities.
Preferred therapy for MSSA bacteremia?	Cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin (nafcillin/oxacillin) — both beat vancomycin.
Why not keep MSSA on vancomycin?	Vancomycin is inferior for MSSA — higher failure and mortality; de-escalate.
Therapy for MRSA bacteremia?	Vancomycin (AUC-guided) or daptomycin.
Criteria for UNCOMPLICATED SAB (all required)?	Negative follow-up cultures, defervescence within 72 h, no endocarditis/prosthetic material, no metastatic foci.
Duration for uncomplicated SAB?	≥14 days IV from the first negative blood culture.
Duration for complicated SAB?	4–6 weeks or longer (endocarditis, osteomyelitis, hardware).
When does the treatment clock start?	From the first negative (clearance) blood culture, not the first positive.
Common metastatic foci of S. aureus?	Endocarditis, vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis, epidural abscess, septic arthritis, psoas/splenic abscess, septic emboli.
TTE vs TEE in SAB?	TEE is more sensitive for endocarditis; pursue with prosthetic valve/device, persistent bacteremia, community-onset, or unclear source.
Why repeat blood cultures in SAB?	To document clearance — persistent positives indicate complicated infection and reset duration.
Does ID consultation affect outcomes in SAB?	Yes — independently improves survival; recommended for every case.
New back pain + persistent bacteremia in SAB suggests?	Vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis ± epidural abscess — image with MRI; complicated SAB.
