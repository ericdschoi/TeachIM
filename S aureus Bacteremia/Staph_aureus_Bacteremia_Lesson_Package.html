Learning Objectives

  1. Treat every positive S. aureus blood culture as significant and apply the five core interventions.
  2. Choose pathogen-directed therapy — empiric vancomycin, then de-escalate MSSA to cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin.
  3. Classify SAB as complicated vs uncomplicated and set the correct duration from the first negative culture.
  4. Search systematically for metastatic foci and use echocardiography (TTE vs TEE) appropriately.

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A blood culture grows methicillin-susceptible S. aureus and the patient is on vancomycin. What should you do?De-escalate to cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin (nafcillin/oxacillin) — both are superior to vancomycin for MSSA, which is associated with higher failure and mortality if continued. Leaving an MSSA patient on vancomycin is a classic error.
Q2. Name the five core interventions for S. aureus bacteremia.Infectious diseases consultation, echocardiography to evaluate for endocarditis, repeat blood cultures to document clearance, source control (remove lines/drain foci), and appropriate antibiotic choice and duration based on complicated vs uncomplicated status.
Q3. What four criteria must all be met to call SAB uncomplicated, and how long do you treat?Negative follow-up cultures (documented clearance), defervescence within 72 hours, no endocarditis and no prosthetic material/hardware, and no metastatic foci. Uncomplicated SAB is treated with at least 14 days of IV therapy counted from the first negative culture; complicated SAB requires 4–6 weeks or longer.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"The lab calls: one of four bottles is growing Gram-positive cocci in clusters, and the cross-cover resident says 'probably a contaminant, recheck in the morning.' For S. aureus, that sentence can kill the patient. There is no such thing as a contaminant Staphylococcus aureus in the blood — and there are five things you owe every one of these patients." Set up: take it seriously → right drug → complicated vs uncomplicated → find the foci.

Step 1 — The five core interventions 7 min

ASK: "Name everything you owe a patient with S. aureus in the blood — before you even pick the antibiotic."

Five evidence-based interventions, every time: ID consultation (independently lowers mortality), echocardiography for endocarditis, repeat blood cultures every 48–72 hours to document clearance, source control (remove infected lines, drain abscesses), and appropriate antibiotic choice and duration. Emphasize that S. aureus in the blood is never a contaminant.

Pearl: An ID consult in SAB is itself a mortality intervention — call them on every case, complicated or not.

Step 2 — The right drug 7 min

ASK: "The culture is back as MSSA and the patient's on vancomycin. What now?"

Start empiric vancomycin to cover MRSA while you wait. Once you know it's MSSA, de-escalate to cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin (nafcillin/oxacillin) — both are superior to vancomycin, which performs worse for MSSA. Cefazolin is convenient and well-tolerated; antistaph penicillins avoid the theoretical inoculum effect in deep-seated infection. For MRSA, use vancomycin (AUC-guided) or daptomycin.

Pearl: 'Vanc covers everything' is the trap — for MSSA, vancomycin is the inferior drug. Switch.

Step 3 — Complicated vs uncomplicated 7 min

ASK: "What lets you give only two weeks — and what forces four to six?"

Uncomplicated requires all of: documented culture clearance, defervescence within 72 hours, no endocarditis and no prosthetic material, and no metastatic foci. That earns ≥14 days IV from the first negative culture. Any of persistent bacteremia, endocarditis, hardware, or metastatic infection makes it complicated — 4–6 weeks or longer (e.g., osteomyelitis, prosthetic valve endocarditis). The clock starts at clearance, not at the first positive.

Pearl: If you haven't documented a negative culture, you can't start counting — and you can't call it uncomplicated.

Step 4 — Hunt the foci 3 min

S. aureus seeds widely: endocarditis, vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis, epidural abscess, septic arthritis, psoas/splenic abscess, septic emboli. Ask about back and joint pain, listen for a new murmur, and image (MRI for spine). TEE beats TTE for endocarditis — pursue it in prosthetic valves/devices, persistent bacteremia, community-onset disease, or when no source is found.

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — 'contaminant'

Cross-cover is told one bottle grew Gram-positive cocci in clusters; species pending. Plan offered: 'recheck in AM, likely contaminant.'

Right response?Treat as significant until speciated; if S. aureus, it is NEVER a contaminant. Start empiric vancomycin, draw repeat cultures, consult ID, arrange an echo, and look for a source.

Case 2 — the MSSA on vanc

Day 2: cultures speciate as MSSA. Patient stable on vancomycin.

Action?De-escalate to cefazolin or nafcillin/oxacillin — both outperform vancomycin for MSSA. Continue the five core interventions; document clearance with repeat cultures.
Duration if uncomplicated?At least 14 days IV counted from the first negative blood culture, provided all uncomplicated criteria are met.

Case 3 — the missed focus

MSSA bacteremia, now afebrile on appropriate therapy, but new severe low back pain on day 5; repeat cultures still positive.

Concern?Persistent bacteremia + back pain = metastatic focus — vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis ± epidural abscess. Get spine MRI and a TEE; this is complicated SAB requiring 4–6 weeks (or longer) and possible source control.

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. A blood culture grows methicillin-susceptible Staphylococcus aureus in a patient currently receiving vancomycin. What is the best antibiotic management?

  1. Continue vancomycin for the full course
  2. De-escalate to cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin (nafcillin/oxacillin)
  3. Switch to oral linezolid immediately
  4. Add gentamicin to vancomycin
  5. Stop antibiotics if afebrile
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. For MSSA, cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin is superior to vancomycin, which is associated with higher failure/mortality — de-escalate. A: keeping MSSA on vancomycin is a classic error. C: early oral switch is not standard for SAB. D: aminoglycoside addition is not routinely recommended. E: SAB requires a full course regardless of fever.

2. Which set best represents the five core interventions for S. aureus bacteremia?

  1. Oral step-down, outpatient follow-up, CRP, repeat CBC, discharge planning
  2. ID consultation, echocardiography, repeat blood cultures, source control, appropriate antibiotic/duration
  3. Vancomycin, gentamicin, rifampin, CT chest, restraint
  4. Blood cultures once, empiric meropenem, no consult, 7-day course, TTE only
  5. Procalcitonin, steroids, antipyretics, fluids, telemetry
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. The evidence-based five are ID consultation, echocardiography, repeat blood cultures to document clearance, source control, and appropriate drug/duration. The other options omit core elements or add non-recommended ones.

3. Which feature would make a case of S. aureus bacteremia 'complicated,' mandating 4–6 weeks of therapy?

  1. Defervescence within 48 hours
  2. Vertebral osteomyelitis on MRI
  3. A single negative follow-up culture
  4. No prosthetic material
  5. Rapid clinical improvement
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Metastatic infection such as vertebral osteomyelitis defines complicated SAB requiring 4–6 weeks (or longer). A, C, D, and E are features consistent with an uncomplicated course.

4. From what point should the duration of therapy for uncomplicated SAB be counted?

  1. From the first positive blood culture
  2. From the first negative (clearance) blood culture
  3. From hospital admission
  4. From defervescence only
  5. From the day of the echocardiogram
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Duration is counted from the first negative blood culture documenting clearance — at least 14 days IV for uncomplicated SAB. The other anchors would under- or over-estimate the true treatment window.

5. Why is infectious diseases consultation recommended for every case of S. aureus bacteremia?

  1. It is required only for MRSA
  2. It independently improves survival and adherence to evidence-based care
  3. It is only useful for antibiotic dosing
  4. It replaces the need for echocardiography
  5. It shortens every course to 7 days
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. ID consultation is independently associated with improved survival and better adherence to the core interventions in SAB. A: it benefits both MSSA and MRSA. C/D: it complements, not replaces, the other interventions. E: duration is set by complicated vs uncomplicated status, not the consult.

Take-Home Points

  1. S. aureus in the blood is never a contaminant — apply all five core interventions every time.
  2. ID consultation, echocardiography, repeat cultures, source control, and the right drug/duration each independently improve care.
  3. Start empiric vancomycin, but de-escalate MSSA to cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin — vancomycin is inferior for MSSA.
  4. Call it uncomplicated only if clearance, defervescence, no endocarditis/hardware, and no metastatic foci are all met — then 14 days from the first negative culture; otherwise 4–6 weeks.
  5. Hunt for metastatic foci (spine, joints, valves) and use TEE when risk is higher or bacteremia persists.
