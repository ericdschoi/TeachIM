Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia — The 5 Core Interventions

SAB is never a contaminant · ID consult + echo + repeat cultures + source control + right drug/duration · MSSA: cefazolin/antistaph penicillin (NOT vancomycin) · never treat as a simple line UTI

1 · Take it seriously — the 5 core interventions

A positive S. aureus blood culture is never a contaminant. Five evidence-based interventions reduce mortality — do all five, every time:

#Core intervention
1ID consultation — lowers mortality
2Echocardiography to evaluate for endocarditis
3Repeat blood cultures q48–72h to document clearance
4Source control — remove lines, drain foci
5Appropriate drug & duration — set by complicated vs uncomplicated
ID consultation independently improves survival in SAB — involve them in every case, not just the complicated ones.

2 · Right drug — MSSA vs MRSA

  • Start empirically with vancomycin while awaiting susceptibilities (covers MRSA).
  • MSSA: de-escalate to cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin (nafcillin/oxacillin) — both beat vancomycin for MSSA. Vancomycin is inferior for MSSA — do not 'leave it on' once you know it's methicillin-susceptible.
  • Cefazolin is convenient and well-tolerated; antistaph penicillins avoid the theoretical inoculum effect in deep infection.
  • MRSA: vancomycin (target AUC-guided) or daptomycin.
The classic error: a patient with MSSA bacteremia left on vancomycin for the whole course. Susceptible → switch to cefazolin/nafcillin.

3 · Complicated vs uncomplicated → duration

Duration hinges on this split. Uncomplicated SAB requires all of:

Uncomplicated needs ALLComplicated if ANY
Negative follow-up cultures (clearance)Persistent positive cultures
Defervescence within 72 hEndocarditis
No endocarditis / no prosthetic materialImplanted prosthesis/hardware
No metastatic fociMetastatic infection (bone, joint, abscess)
Uncomplicated SAB: ≥14 days IV from the first negative culture. Complicated SAB: 4–6 weeks (longer for endocarditis/osteomyelitis). Count the clock from clearance, not from day one.

Hunt for metastatic foci

  • S. aureus seeds widely: endocarditis, vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis, epidural abscess, septic arthritis, psoas/splenic abscess, septic emboli.
  • Ask about and exam for back pain (osteo/epidural — image with MRI), joint pain, new murmur, peripheral stigmata.
  • TEE is more sensitive than TTE for endocarditis; pursue TEE in higher-risk or persistent cases (prosthetic valve/device, persistent bacteremia, community-onset, no clear source).

Do/Avoid

  • DO consult ID, get an echo, repeat cultures, control the source, and set drug/duration on every SAB
  • DO de-escalate MSSA to cefazolin or an antistaph penicillin
  • DO time the duration from the first negative culture
  • AVOID calling S. aureus a contaminant or treating it like a simple line infection
  • AVOID keeping a confirmed MSSA on vancomycin
  • AVOID a short course without confirming uncomplicated criteria (clearance, no endocarditis, no foci)
