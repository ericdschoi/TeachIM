Learning Objectives

  1. Characterize the electrophysiology of the seven supraventricular tachycardias (automaticity vs. re-entry).
  2. Differentiate the SVTs using two features: regular vs. irregular rhythm and the presence/morphology of P waves.
  3. Identify the best acute management for each rhythm — and the AV-nodal-blocker pitfalls to avoid.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Every tachycardia comes from one of two mechanisms. Name them, and say which tends to be faster. Increased automaticity (a node or ectopic focus firing too fast) or a re-entry circuit (a self-sustaining loop). Re-entry circuits are generally faster. This single split organizes the whole differential.
Q2. You have a narrow-complex tachycardia. What two ECG features let you narrow the diagnosis fastest? Rhythm regularity (regular vs. irregular) and the P waves (present or absent, and their morphology — sinus, retrograde "retro-P," saw-tooth, multiple morphologies, or none). Those two axes separate the seven SVTs.
Q3. What does adenosine do to (a) AVNRT/AVRT, (b) atrial flutter, and (c) VT? (a) Terminates AVNRT/AVRT by breaking the re-entry circuit at the AV node. (b) Transiently blocks the AV node to unmask the flutter waves (diagnostic), but usually does not convert flutter. (c) Has no effect on true VT. So adenosine is both therapeutic and a diagnostic probe.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive presenter board (SVT_Optimized.pptx) and click each rhythm to reveal its EP diagram, ECG, and defining features, or rebuild the framework on a whiteboard. Use the learning board (SVT-interactive-board-for-learning.pptx) to prepare. Hand out the learner sheet so residents fill in the 7-rhythm grid as you go; finish with the Practice ECGs deck/handout. Talk ~15–20 min, practice ECGs ~15 min.

Hook (2 min)

"A narrow-complex tachycardia at 170 just hit your pager. Instead of memorizing a list, I'll give you two questions that sort all seven SVTs in about ten seconds — and tell you what to do about each." Then the framing: every tachycardia is either automaticity or re-entry, and two ECG features (regularity + P waves) carve the rest.

Objective 1 — Two mechanisms 4 min

ASK: "What are the only two ways a heart can go too fast?" — then introduce the key on the board (increased automaticity vs. re-entry circuit).

Automaticity = a pacemaker (the sinus node or an ectopic atrial focus) firing too fast, usually driven by an underlying process (fever, hypoxia, catecholamines, theophylline). Re-entry = a circular self-perpetuating loop (within the AV node, or using an accessory pathway), which tends to be faster and starts/stops abruptly. This mechanism split predicts the treatment: fix the driver for automaticity, break the loop for re-entry.

Objective 2 — Sort the seven by regularity + P waves 9 min

ASK: "Regular or irregular? Now look at the P waves — present, absent, retrograde, saw-tooth, or multiple? Place this rhythm." Click each rhythm to reveal.

Pearl — slow it down to see it: when the rate hides the P waves, a vagal maneuver or adenosine transiently slows AV conduction so you can read the atrial activity — saw-tooth flutter, retrograde P, or organized atrial tach — turning a diagnostic puzzle into an answer.

Objective 3 — Acute management & the three buckets 6 min

ASK: "Stable SVT — there are only three management strategies. What are they, and which rhythms go in each bucket?"

Stable SVT collapses into three actions: (1) address the underlying process (sinus tach, atrial tach, MAT); (2) interrupt the re-entry circuit with vagal maneuvers or adenosine (AVNRT, AVRT); (3) rate-control with AV-nodal blockers (AF, atrial flutter). Unstable at any point → synchronized cardioversion.

Pearl — the modified (REVERT) Valsalva: have the patient strain into a 10-mL syringe while semi-recumbent, then immediately lay them flat with a passive 45° leg raise. It converts roughly 43% of re-entrant SVT versus ~17% for the standard Valsalva — try it before adenosine.
Safety — pre-excited atrial fibrillation: an irregular, wide, very fast rhythm in a WPW patient is AF conducting down the accessory pathway. Avoid AV-nodal blockers (adenosine, calcium-channel blockers, β-blockers, digoxin): blocking the node forces conduction down the accessory pathway and can precipitate ventricular fibrillation. Use procainamide or synchronized cardioversion.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild the 2×2 (regular/irregular × P-wave pattern), place all seven rhythms, and state the management bucket for each. Then the safety one-liner: "Irregular + wide + fast = don't touch the node."

Practice ECGs 15 min — project SVT-Practice-ECGs.pptx / hand out SVTs-handout-1.pdf

After the framework, work the Practice ECGs deck one rhythm at a time. For each strip, have learners apply the two-question sort before revealing the answer. Sample teaching prompts:

A regular narrow-complex tachycardia at 180 with no visible P waves, abrupt onset/offset. Most likely rhythm and first move?AVNRT (the most common paroxysmal SVT). First move: vagal maneuvers (modified Valsalva or carotid sinus massage), then adenosine if that fails. Both are diagnostic and therapeutic.
A regular narrow tachycardia converts briefly with adenosine, revealing saw-tooth waves at an atrial rate of ~300 with 2:1 conduction. Diagnosis?Atrial flutter. Adenosine transiently blocks the AV node and unmasks the flutter waves; flutter is managed with rate control (and anticoagulation/rhythm strategy like AF), not adenosine conversion.
An irregularly irregular narrow-complex tachycardia with no discrete P waves. Diagnosis and management?Atrial fibrillation. Rate-control with AV-nodal blockers and assess stroke risk (CHA₂DS₂-VASc) for anticoagulation.
An irregular wide-complex tachycardia at 250 in a young patient with a delta wave on a prior ECG. What must you NOT give?Pre-excited AF (WPW). Do NOT give AV-nodal blockers (adenosine, CCB, β-blocker, digoxin) — they can accelerate accessory-pathway conduction and cause VF. Use procainamide or synchronized cardioversion.
An irregular narrow tachycardia with at least three different P-wave morphologies in a patient with a COPD exacerbation. Diagnosis?Multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAT). Treat the underlying pulmonary process; avoid non-selective β-blockers if there is active bronchospasm.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 24-year-old woman has sudden palpitations. ECG: regular narrow-complex tachycardia at 190 with no visible P waves. She is alert with a BP of 118/72. What is the most appropriate first step?

  1. Synchronized cardioversion
  2. Vagal maneuvers (modified Valsalva)
  3. IV metoprolol
  4. IV procainamide
Answer & explanationB. This is a stable, regular SVT (most likely AVNRT). Guidelines make vagal maneuvers the Class I first step; the modified (REVERT) Valsalva roughly doubles conversion over the standard technique. Cardioversion (A) is for instability. Metoprolol (C) is not first-line acute therapy here. Procainamide (D) is reserved for pre-excited/wide-complex scenarios.

2. A regular narrow-complex tachycardia transiently slows with adenosine, revealing saw-tooth deflections at an atrial rate near 300 with 2:1 block, then resumes. What is the diagnosis?

  1. AVNRT
  2. Atrial flutter
  3. Sinus tachycardia
  4. Multifocal atrial tachycardia
Answer & explanationB. Adenosine briefly increases AV block and unmasks the saw-tooth flutter waves (~300 bpm atrial rate). It usually does not terminate flutter (a macro-re-entrant atrial circuit), which separates it from AVNRT (A), where adenosine breaks the circuit and converts the rhythm. Sinus tach (C) has normal upright P waves; MAT (D) is irregular with multiple P morphologies.

3. A 30-year-old with known WPW presents with an irregular, wide-complex tachycardia at rates up to 260. Which agent is contraindicated?

  1. Procainamide
  2. Synchronized cardioversion
  3. Diltiazem
  4. None — any of these is fine
Answer & explanationC. This is pre-excited atrial fibrillation. AV-nodal blockers — diltiazem, and also adenosine, β-blockers, and digoxin — can enhance conduction down the accessory pathway and precipitate VF, so they are contraindicated. Procainamide (A) and synchronized cardioversion (B) are the appropriate treatments.

4. An 80-year-old with a COPD exacerbation has an irregular narrow-complex tachycardia; the ECG shows at least three distinct P-wave morphologies. What is the best initial management?

  1. Adenosine
  2. Synchronized cardioversion
  3. Treat the underlying pulmonary process
  4. Digoxin loading
Answer & explanationC. This is multifocal atrial tachycardia, an automaticity rhythm usually driven by pulmonary disease/hypoxia. It does not respond to adenosine (A) or cardioversion (B), and the cornerstone is treating the underlying process (and correcting electrolytes). Non-dihydropyridine CCBs can help rate; avoid non-selective β-blockers with active bronchospasm. Digoxin (D) is not first-line.

5. Which statement about adenosine in the diagnosis of a regular tachycardia is most accurate?

  1. It reliably converts ventricular tachycardia to sinus rhythm
  2. It terminates AVNRT/AVRT and can unmask atrial flutter, but does not affect VT
  3. It is contraindicated in all narrow-complex tachycardias
  4. It permanently rate-controls atrial fibrillation
Answer & explanationB. Adenosine breaks AV-nodal-dependent re-entry (AVNRT/AVRT), transiently blocks the node to reveal flutter/atrial-tachycardia P waves, and has essentially no effect on true VT — making it both therapeutic and a diagnostic probe. A and D misstate its effects; C is false (it is first-line for regular SVT after vagal maneuvers). Caution remains in pre-excited AF.

Attending Dotphrase NEW — EMR-ready A&P smart phrase

Dense assessment-and-plan template for the EMR. * = wildcard to edit at the bedside; brackets = pick-lists. 

#SVT: narrow-complex tachycardia, rate * , [STABLE / UNSTABLE]. mechanism [automaticity vs re-entry]; sort by regularity + P waves.
- UNSTABLE (hypotension/ischemia/AMS/shock) → synchronized cardioversion NOW (* J), sedate if able
- STABLE → place defib pads, 12-lead, continuous tele, IV access; read the atrial activity (slow w/ vagal or adenosine if P waves hidden)
- regular + sort:
   sinus tach (nl P) → treat driver [fever, hypovolemia, pain, hypoxia, anemia, PE, thyrotoxicosis, withdrawal]
   atrial tach (non-sinus/inverted P) → AV-nodal blocker [diltiazem/metoprolol] + treat driver
   AVNRT / orthodromic AVRT (no sinus P / retro-P, short R-P) → vagal (modified REVERT Valsalva) → adenosine 6 mg→12 mg→12 mg rapid push + flush
   atrial flutter (saw-tooth, atrial ~300, 2:1) → rate control [diltiazem/metoprolol]; adenosine unmasks not converts; anticoag per CHA2DS2-VASc; consider DCCV/rhythm
- irregular + sort:
   atrial fibrillation (no P) → rate control [diltiazem/metoprolol]; CHA2DS2-VASc → anticoag *
   MAT (≥3 P morphologies, COPD/hypoxia) → treat pulm process, correct K/Mg; non-DHP CCB for rate; AVOID nonselective BB if bronchospasm
- WIDE + irregular + very fast + known WPW/delta = pre-excited AF → *AVOID AV-nodal blockers (adenosine, CCB, BB, digoxin → VF risk); give procainamide or synchronized cardioversion
- VT clue if wide: extreme RAD / positive aVR favors VT; when in doubt treat as VT
- adenosine = diagnostic (unmasks flutter/atrial tach) AND therapeutic (terminates AVNRT/AVRT); no effect on VT
- workup: electrolytes (K/Mg/Ca), TSH, trop if ischemic sx, prior ECGs for delta wave; EP/cards referral for recurrent re-entry (ablation)

Take-Home Points

  1. Every tachycardia is either increased automaticity (a node/ectopic focus firing too fast) or a re-entry circuit — re-entry circuits are generally faster.
  2. There are only seven SVTs; sort them with two questions — regular vs. irregular, and what the P waves look like.
  3. Stable SVT has three management buckets: address the underlying process (sinus tach, atrial tach, MAT), interrupt the re-entry circuit with vagal maneuvers/adenosine (AVNRT, AVRT), or rate-control with AV-nodal blockers (AF, flutter).
  4. Adenosine reveals the rhythm — it resolves AVNRT/AVRT, unmasks atrial flutter/tach, and does not affect VT; the modified (REVERT) Valsalva is the best first vagal maneuver.
  5. Don't touch the node in pre-excited AF: an irregular, wide, very fast rhythm in WPW needs procainamide or cardioversion, not adenosine/CCB/β-blocker/digoxin. Extreme right-axis (positive aVR) is a quick clue for VT.
