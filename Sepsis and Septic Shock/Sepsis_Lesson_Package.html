Learning Objectives

  1. Define sepsis and septic shock with the Sepsis-3 framework and recognize organ dysfunction early.
  2. Execute the first-hour priorities: cultures, early broad-spectrum antibiotics, and balanced-crystalloid resuscitation with reassessment.
  3. Start norepinephrine first-line for fluid-refractory hypoperfusion and add vasopressin / steroids appropriately.
  4. Build in source control and antibiotic de-escalation, and avoid the bundle pitfalls flagged by the 2026 SSC.

Pre-work 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What separates septic shock from sepsis in the Sepsis-3 definitions?Septic shock = sepsis requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥ 65 mmHg AND a lactate > 2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation — a subset with circulatory and cellular/metabolic dysfunction and higher mortality.
Q2. Which crystalloid does current guidance favor for sepsis resuscitation, and why?Balanced crystalloids (lactated Ringer's, Plasma-Lyte) over 0.9% saline — they cause less hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis and are associated with faster recovery and a possible mortality benefit in large trials.
Q3. Your patient remains hypotensive after a weight-based fluid bolus. Do you give more fluid or start a pressor?Assess fluid responsiveness dynamically (passive leg raise, pulse-pressure/stroke-volume variation). If not fluid-responsive or MAP stays <65, start norepinephrine first-line rather than giving more fluid — over-resuscitation worsens outcomes.

Teaching Script timing & engagement cues

Hook (2 min)

"A 68-year-old comes in with a UTI and a heart rate of 112, and the intern writes 'urosepsis, admit.' Two hours later the lactate is 4.2, the pressure is 84/50, and she's confused. The difference between a clean save and an ICU transfer is what happens in the first hour — and which steps you do not put on autopilot." Set up recognize → resuscitate → reassess.

Step 1 — Recognize organ dysfunction 6 min

ASK: "Infection plus a fever isn't sepsis. What turns an infection into sepsis?"

Sepsis (Sepsis-3) = infection + life-threatening organ dysfunction from a dysregulated host response. Clinically: suspected infection plus new dysfunction — hypotension, rising lactate, oliguria/rising creatinine, altered mentation, hyperbilirubinemia, thrombocytopenia, new oxygen need. Septic shock adds vasopressor-dependence (MAP ≥65) plus lactate >2 despite fluids. Note the 2026 framing: qSOFA is no longer a screening tool — its sensitivity is too low to rule sepsis out; use a broader early-warning approach.

Pearl: Don't let a normal qSOFA reassure you out of a sepsis work-up — it misses too many.

Step 2 — The first hour 8 min

ASK: "Name the first-hour moves — and the one thing you must not let delay antibiotics."

Blood cultures ×2 before antibiotics — but never delay antibiotics to obtain them. Give broad-spectrum antibiotics quickly: within 1 hour for shock or high probability; the 2026 SSC permits a brief diagnostic window (up to ~3 h) only when sepsis is possible but not probable and there's no shock. Resuscitate with balanced crystalloid (~30 mL/kg for hypoperfusion), then reassess — fluid is a drug with a dose, not a reflex. Draw and trend lactate.

Pearl: Source control trumps everything — an undrained abscess or an infected line will defeat any antibiotic. Look for it on day one.

Step 3 — Pressors for the non-responder 7 min

ASK: "Pressure's still 82/48 after fluids. More fluid, or a pressor — and which one?"

Start norepinephrine first-line when MAP stays <65 despite (or alongside) fluids; it can be run through a good peripheral IV while central access is obtained. Add vasopressin to spare catecholamines, consider epinephrine as a second add-on, and add hydrocortisone when shock persists on escalating pressors. Judge fluid need dynamically (passive leg raise, pulse-pressure variation) — not by CVP.

Pearl: Pouring in a fourth and fifth liter when the patient isn't fluid-responsive causes edema and harm — reach for norepinephrine instead.

Step 4 — Reassess & de-escalate 2 min

Reassess perfusion repeatedly (MAP, lactate clearance, urine output, mentation, capillary refill). Plan source control and antibiotic de-escalation from the start; narrow on culture data, use procalcitonin trends to support stopping, and favor shorter courses once the source is controlled. The 2026 SSC recommends against routine antipyretics, beta-blockers, and probiotics as sepsis treatments.

Cases answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — recognition

68F with dysuria, T 38.6, HR 112, BP 96/58, confused, lactate 4.2, Cr up from 0.9 to 1.8.

Sepsis or just a UTI?Sepsis — infection plus organ dysfunction (hypotension, lactate 4.2, AKI, altered mentation). Not 'just a UTI.'
First-hour orders?Blood cultures ×2, urine culture; broad-spectrum antibiotics promptly; balanced crystalloid ~30 mL/kg with reassessment; trend lactate; look for obstruction (renal US/CT) as a source-control issue.

Case 2 — fluids vs pressor

Same patient after 30 mL/kg LR: BP 84/50, MAP 61, passive-leg-raise gives no stroke-volume rise, JVP now full, lactate still 4.

More fluid?No — she is not fluid-responsive and is showing congestion. Start norepinephrine to a MAP ≥65 rather than more crystalloid.
If still hypotensive on rising norepinephrine?Add vasopressin (catecholamine-sparing); consider hydrocortisone for refractory shock; reassess source control.

Case 3 — source control

55M with cholangitis: febrile, hypotensive, bilirubin 8, on appropriate antibiotics for 24 h but still in shock; CT shows a dilated, obstructed biliary tree.

Why isn't he improving?The source isn't controlled — an obstructed, infected biliary system needs decompression (ERCP/biliary drainage). Antibiotics alone cannot fix an undrained focus.

Board-style MCQs ABIM-format

1. A 70-year-old with pneumonia has BP 82/48 after 30 mL/kg of balanced crystalloid; a passive leg raise produces no change in stroke volume, and lactate remains 3.8. What is the best next step?

  1. Give another 2 L of normal saline
  2. Start norepinephrine titrated to MAP ≥ 65
  3. Start dopamine
  4. Transfuse packed red cells
  5. Begin broad fluid boluses guided by central venous pressure
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. The patient is fluid-unresponsive and hypotensive — norepinephrine is the first-line vasopressor to restore MAP. A: more fluid in a non-responder causes harm. C: dopamine is more arrhythmogenic and not preferred. D: no transfusion indication given. E: CVP poorly predicts fluid responsiveness — use dynamic measures.

2. Which statement about qSOFA reflects current (2026) guidance?

  1. qSOFA should be the primary screening tool to rule out sepsis
  2. qSOFA has low sensitivity and should not be used to exclude sepsis
  3. A qSOFA of 0 confirms the absence of organ dysfunction
  4. qSOFA replaces lactate measurement
  5. qSOFA defines septic shock
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. qSOFA's sensitivity is too low for screening; the SSC no longer endorses it to rule sepsis out — use broader early-warning systems. A/C: a low score does not exclude sepsis. D: lactate remains a key perfusion marker. E: septic shock is defined by vasopressor need + lactate >2, not qSOFA.

3. Which fluid is preferred for initial resuscitation in sepsis according to recent evidence?

  1. 0.9% normal saline
  2. Balanced crystalloid (lactated Ringer's or Plasma-Lyte)
  3. Hydroxyethyl starch
  4. 5% albumin as first-line
  5. Hypertonic saline
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Balanced crystalloids are favored — less hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, faster recovery, and a possible mortality signal. A: saline causes hyperchloremic acidosis. C: starches increase AKI/mortality and are contraindicated. D: albumin is an adjunct, not first-line. E: no role for hypertonic saline here.

4. A patient with septic shock from cholangitis remains hypotensive after 24 hours of appropriate antibiotics and vasopressors. CT shows persistent biliary obstruction. What is most important now?

  1. Broaden antibiotics further
  2. Source control with biliary decompression (ERCP/drainage)
  3. Add a third vasopressor and continue current management
  4. Start an antipyretic
  5. Give a probiotic
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. An undrained, obstructed focus defeats antibiotics — source control (biliary decompression) is the priority. A: broadening won't fix the mechanical source. C: escalating pressors without source control treats the symptom. D/E: 2026 SSC recommends against routine antipyretics and probiotics as sepsis therapy.

5. Regarding blood cultures and antibiotics in suspected septic shock, which is correct?

  1. Always wait for cultures to result before any antibiotic
  2. Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics, but never delay antibiotics to get them
  3. Antibiotics are unnecessary if cultures are pending
  4. Cultures should be drawn only after 24 hours
  5. Antibiotics should be withheld until imaging is complete
Answer + why each distractor is wrongB. Draw two sets of blood cultures before antibiotics for diagnostic yield, but do not let culture collection delay timely broad-spectrum therapy in shock. A/D/E: dangerous delays. C: empiric antibiotics are essential while cultures are pending.

Take-Home Points

  1. Sepsis is infection plus organ dysfunction; septic shock adds vasopressor-dependence with lactate >2 — and qSOFA is no longer a rule-out screen.
  2. First hour: cultures before antibiotics (without delaying them), early broad-spectrum antibiotics, and balanced-crystalloid resuscitation with reassessment.
  3. Favor balanced crystalloids over normal saline and dose fluid to dynamic responsiveness, not CVP.
  4. Start norepinephrine first-line for fluid-refractory hypotension; add vasopressin and steroids for refractory shock.
  5. Source control and early de-escalation are as important as the first dose — and skip routine antipyretics, beta-blockers, and probiotics.
