Sepsis & Septic Shock — Early Recognition & Resuscitation

Recognize organ dysfunction early → cultures + early antibiotics + balanced crystalloid → norepinephrine for persistent hypoperfusion · source control · reassess, don't autopilot the bundle

1 · Recognize — dysfunction, not just infection

Sepsis = life-threatening organ dysfunction from a dysregulated host response to infection (clinically: suspected infection + acute organ dysfunction). Septic shock = sepsis with vasopressors needed to keep MAP ≥ 65 and lactate > 2 despite adequate fluids.

ToolUse
SOFA / ΔSOFA ≥2Defines organ dysfunction (ICU/quantitative)
LactateMarker of hypoperfusion + resuscitation target
qSOFA (retired as screen)Low sensitivity; don't use to rule out — use NEWS-type screens
Organ dysfunction clues: new hypotension, ↑lactate, ↑creatinine/oliguria, ↑bilirubin, altered mentation, thrombocytopenia, new O2 requirement.

2 · The first hour — culture, antibiotic, fluid

  • Blood cultures ×2 before antibiotics — but never delay antibiotics to get them.
  • Antibiotics fast: broad-spectrum, ideally within 1 h for shock/high probability; the 2026 SSC allows a brief diagnostic window (up to ~3 h) when sepsis is possible but not probable and there is no shock.
  • Fluids: ~30 mL/kg balanced crystalloid (preferred over normal saline) for sepsis-induced hypoperfusion/lactate ≥4 or hypotension, then reassess — don't give it blind.
  • Draw a lactate; recheck if elevated to guide resuscitation.
Balanced crystalloids (LR, Plasma-Lyte) are now favored over normal saline (less hyperchloremic acidosis, possible mortality signal). Source control beats any antibiotic — find and drain it.

3 · Persistent hypoperfusion — pressors

If MAP stays < 65 after/with fluids: start norepinephrine first-line. Don't chase fluids endlessly into a non-responder.

AgentRole
NorepinephrineFirst-line vasopressor; titrate to MAP ≥65
VasopressinAdd-on to reduce norepi dose / catecholamine sparing
EpinephrineSecond-line add-on; consider if cardiac dysfunction
Steroids (hydrocortisone)If shock persists on escalating pressors
Start norepinephrine early — even peripherally through a good IV — rather than over-resuscitating with liters of fluid. Assess fluid responsiveness dynamically (passive leg raise, pulse-pressure variation), not by CVP.

Source & de-escalate

  • Find the source: exam, imaging, cultures; control it (drain abscess, remove infected line, relieve obstruction) as early as feasible.
  • De-escalate antibiotics on culture data; stop if infection excluded; use procalcitonin trends to support stopping.
  • Typical durations are shorter than tradition (often 5–7 days) for many sources once controlled.

Reassess · Do/Avoid

  • DO reassess perfusion repeatedly (MAP, lactate clearance, urine output, mentation, cap refill)
  • DO favor balanced crystalloid and start norepinephrine early for non-responders
  • DO plan source control and antibiotic de-escalation from day 1
  • AVOID using qSOFA to rule out sepsis (poor sensitivity)
  • AVOID reflexive fixed fluid boluses in a fluid-unresponsive or already-wet patient
  • AVOID routine antipyretics, beta-blockers, or probiotics as sepsis therapy (2026 SSC)
