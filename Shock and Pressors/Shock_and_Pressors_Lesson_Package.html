Learning Objectives

  1. Differentiate the four categories of shock by their hemodynamic profiles (preload/CVP, cardiac output, afterload/SVR).
  2. Match each shock category to its initial treatment lever — volume, inotrope, or vasopressor.
  3. Recognize the commonly used vasopressors and inotropes, their receptor effects, and the indication for each.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. A patient is hypotensive with warm extremities and a wide pulse pressure. Which category of shock does that hemodynamic picture point to, and why? Distributive (most often septic). Warm extremities and a wide pulse pressure reflect a low systemic vascular resistance (vasodilation) with a preserved or high cardiac output — the hallmark of distributive shock. Cold, clammy extremities with a narrow pulse pressure instead suggest a low-output state (cardiogenic or hypovolemic).
Q2. Why is there "no role for vasopressors if the tank is not full"? Vasopressors raise SVR by constricting an already under-filled vascular space; without adequate preload, squeezing empty vessels does little to restore cardiac output and risks end-organ and digital ischemia. Hypovolemic shock is corrected with volume (blood for hemorrhage) first; pressors are a bridge, not a substitute, for filling.
Q3. Name the first-line vasopressor for septic shock and the first-line agent for cardiogenic shock — and the single reason they differ. Norepinephrine for septic (distributive) shock; dobutamine (an inotrope) for cardiogenic shock. They differ because the broken lever differs: septic shock is primarily a vasodilation/low-SVR problem (so you vasoconstrict), whereas cardiogenic shock is primarily a pump/contractility problem (so you augment contractility).

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the interactive presenter board (Shock_and_Pressors_Optimized.pptx) and click through each cause/receptor/pressor, or rebuild the differential-diagnosis figure on a whiteboard. Every "Click each…" prompt on the board is a built-in ASK point — pose the question before revealing. The talk splits cleanly: pathophysiology + types of shock (~15 min) and vasopressors (~15 min). Three practice cases (~5 min each) are optional. Print the learner handout so residents fill in the pressor table as you go.

Hook (2 min)

"Three patients, all with a MAP of 50. One needs a liter of blood, one needs a pump drug, one needs a squeeze drug — and giving the wrong one can kill them. How do you tell them apart at the bedside in 30 seconds?" The answer — warm vs. cold, full vs. empty — is the entire framework, and it sets up the hemodynamic table.

Pearl — read the extremities and the pulse pressure first: warm + wide pulse pressure = low SVR = distributive; cold + narrow pulse pressure = low output = cardiogenic or hypovolemic. The exam sorts the patient before any catheter does.
ASK: "Your septic patient has underlying cardiomyopathy — how do you tell if there's a cardiogenic component dragging the output down?"
Pearl — SvO2 sorts mixed pictures: central/mixed venous O2 saturation is low in cardiogenic shock (tissues extract more from a low output) and typically high in established septic shock (impaired extraction), though it can be low in early sepsis. Interpret with caution — SvO2 is also affected by cardiac output, SaO2, and hemoglobin, so it can read falsely low with hypoxemia or anemia.

Objective 2 — Receptor physiology 6 min

ASK: "Map the receptors before the drugs: what does α1 do? β1? β2? V1?" — click each receptor type on the board.

Anchor the pharmacology in four receptors so learners can predict any agent's effect rather than memorize a list: α1 → vasoconstriction (↑SVR); β1 → ↑heart rate and ↑contractility (↑CO); β2 → vasodilation; V1 (vasopressin) → vasoconstriction through a non-adrenergic pathway (useful when acidosis blunts catecholamine response).

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild from memory: the four categories with their CVP/CO/SVR arrows, the first treatment lever for each, and the lead agent for septic vs. cardiogenic shock. Then the one-liner: "Fill the tank before you squeeze it; fix the pump when the pump is the problem."

Practice Cases 15 min — original board cases, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Sepsis on a failing heart

45-year-old man with ischemic cardiomyopathy (EF 40%) admitted with pneumonia and hypotension. T 39.2°C, RR 26, SpO2 90% on 5L NC, BP 87/32 (MAP 50). Exam: warm extremities, JVP ~10 cm, trace BLE edema. Labs: Cr 2.2 (baseline 1), WBC 18k, lactate 3.2. ECG: NSR, no new ischemia. BNP 500 (baseline 200–600).

What type of shock is this?Septic (distributive) shock — warm extremities, wide pulse pressure, and a clear source of infection. In a patient with underlying cardiac dysfunction it can be hard to know whether a cardiogenic component is also present; an SvO2 helps (normal 65–75%; low suggests a cardiogenic contribution, high is typical of septic shock).
How would you manage his shock?IVF resuscitation is central to septic shock, but because he is already mildly hypervolemic and hypoxic, give gentle volume (e.g., 500 mL) and reassess. Start norepinephrine at ~0.1 mcg/kg/min, titrate to MAP >65; add vasopressin if NE requirements escalate (rather than maxing out NE). Consider dobutamine if he stays hypotensive despite volume and pressors.

Case 2 — Pump failure

45-year-old man with dilated cardiomyopathy (EF 20%) admitted with hypoxia and altered mental status. Afebrile, RR 26, BP 72/45 (MAP 54), SpO2 90% on 5L NC. Exam: cold and clammy, JVP 12 cm, 2+ BLE edema. Cr 2.2 (baseline 1). ECG: sinus tachycardia, no ischemia. Troponin minimally elevated (0.05). BNP >1500 (baseline 200–500). CXR: cephalization, Kerley B lines, bilateral effusions.

What type of shock is this? How would you confirm?Cardiogenic shock — cardiomyopathy history plus exam signs of volume overload. SvO2 and TTE confirm: his SvO2 was 42% (low, consistent with cardiogenic shock even given hypoxemia) and TTE showed EF 15–20% with elevated RA pressure and no new wall-motion abnormality.
How would you manage his shock?Start dobutamine. Its mild SVR drop reduces afterload and can paradoxically improve BP by raising CO. Do NOT give IVF — his myocytes are already over-stretched past the Starling peak; diuresis (when BP allows, sometimes with low-dose NE support) improves contractility.

Case 3 — The empty tank

45-year-old man with cirrhosis, 1 day of hematochezia and orthostasis. Afebrile, RR 18, HR 140s, BP 72/45 (MAP 54), SpO2 92% on RA. Exam: dry mucous membranes, cold/clammy, JVP not appreciable. Cr 2.2 (baseline 1), plts 110k, Hct 24% (baseline 38%), INR 2.4 (baseline).

What type of shock is this?Hypovolemic shock from a GI bleed. Hematochezia with hemodynamic instability is concerning for a brisk upper GI source.
How would you manage his shock?Aggressive volume resuscitation is the cornerstone — no role for pressors if the tank is not full. Place two large-bore peripheral IVs or an introducer. Give packed RBCs (active bleeding — don't wait for Hct to fall further), correct coagulopathy, and IVF. Start octreotide (portal-hypertensive bleeding) and a PPI; consult GI for urgent endoscopy.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 60-year-old man with pneumonia has BP 80/40 (MAP 53) despite 30 mL/kg of crystalloid. He has warm extremities and a lactate of 4. Which is the most appropriate next vasoactive agent?

  1. Dopamine
  2. Phenylephrine
  3. Norepinephrine
  4. Dobutamine
Answer & explanationC. Norepinephrine is the first-line vasopressor for septic (distributive) shock — it raises SVR (α1) with modest inotropy and less tachyarrhythmia than dopamine. Dopamine (A) causes more arrhythmias and is no longer preferred. Phenylephrine (B) is a pure α1 agent reserved for when tachyarrhythmia precludes NE. Dobutamine (D) is an inotrope for cardiogenic shock and would worsen the vasodilatory hypotension here.

2. A 70-year-old with an EF of 15% presents cold, clammy, and hypotensive (BP 78/50) with pulmonary edema. After cautious management of his airway, which strategy best addresses his shock?

  1. 1–2 L crystalloid bolus to improve preload
  2. Dobutamine, with diuresis as BP allows
  3. Phenylephrine to raise the MAP
  4. Vasopressin monotherapy
Answer & explanationB. This is cardiogenic shock — a pump problem. Dobutamine augments contractility and its mild afterload reduction can improve output; diuresis (when BP permits) relieves the over-stretched ventricle. A fluid bolus (A) is harmful in cardiogenic shock with pulmonary edema. Pure vasoconstrictors (C, phenylephrine; D, vasopressin) raise afterload against a failing ventricle and do not address the low output.

3. A 45-year-old with cirrhosis and brisk hematochezia has HR 140, BP 72/45, flat neck veins, and Hct 24%. A trainee asks to start norepinephrine for the low MAP. What is the best response?

  1. Start norepinephrine and titrate to MAP >65
  2. Resuscitate with blood products and fluid first — pressors have no role until preload is restored
  3. Start vasopressin to spare splanchnic flow
  4. Start dobutamine for inotropic support
Answer & explanationB. This is hypovolemic (hemorrhagic) shock — "no role for vasopressors if the tank is not full." Volume and blood are the cornerstone; squeezing an empty vascular space (A, C) risks end-organ/digital ischemia without restoring output. Dobutamine (D) addresses contractility, which is not the problem.

4. A patient in septic shock requires escalating norepinephrine (now 0.4 mcg/kg/min) with a MAP still of 58. According to the 2021 Surviving Sepsis Campaign, what is the preferred next step?

  1. Continue increasing the norepinephrine dose without limit
  2. Add vasopressin
  3. Switch entirely to phenylephrine
  4. Add dobutamine
Answer & explanationB. The 2021 guideline suggests adding vasopressin (typically once NE is ~0.25–0.5 mcg/kg/min) rather than escalating norepinephrine indefinitely; the combination was associated with lower mortality. Unlimited NE escalation (A) increases ischemic risk. Phenylephrine (C) is not a preferred substitute for NE. Dobutamine (D) is added for a cardiogenic component or persistent hypoperfusion despite adequate MAP, not for refractory vasodilation per se.

5. A patient with septic shock-induced hypotension is enrolled in a discussion of fluid strategy. Based on the CLOVERS trial (2023), which statement is correct?

  1. A liberal fluid strategy significantly reduced 90-day mortality
  2. A restrictive-fluid/early-vasopressor strategy significantly reduced mortality
  3. There was no significant difference in mortality between restrictive and liberal strategies
  4. Early vasopressors significantly increased mortality
Answer & explanationC. CLOVERS found no significant difference in 90-day mortality (14.0% restrictive vs 14.9% liberal). Both an early-vasopressor/restrictive approach and a fluid-liberal approach are acceptable; neither is clearly superior, so the choice can be individualized. A, B, and D overstate the findings.

- bedside read: extremities [warm=low SVR/distributive | cold=low output]; pulse pressure [wide=distributive | narrow=cardiogenic/hypovolemic]; JVP/CVP *; UOP *
- hemodynamic profile (CVP / CO / SVR): hypovolemic ↓↓/↓/↑ · cardiogenic ↑/↓↓/↑ · obstructive ↑/↓/↑ · distributive ↓-nml/nml-↑/↓
- confirm/sort: lactate, ScvO2 [↓ cardiogenic | ↑ septic; falsely ↓ if anemic/hypoxic], POCUS/TTE [EF, IVC, effusion, RV], CVP if line
- TANK first: hypovolemic/hemorrhagic → blood + IVF, 2 large-bore IV/introducer; NO pressors until preload repleted. sepsis → balanced crystalloid (LR/Plasma-Lyte) *mL/kg, reassess (CLOVERS: restrictive vs liberal = no mortality diff)
- PUMP: cardiogenic → dobutamine *mcg/kg/min (mild ↓SVR can ↑BP via ↑CO) ± diurese when BP allows; NO bolus IVF. obstructive → relieve cause [thrombolyse/anticoag, needle/chest tube, pericardiocentesis]
- SQUEEZE: distributive/septic → norepinephrine *mcg/kg/min to MAP ≥65 (1st-line); start PERIPHERAL to avoid delay; ADD vasopressin 0.03 U/min when NE ~0.25-0.5 mcg/kg/min (not max-NE); epinephrine for anaphylaxis/refractory; phenylephrine if tachyarrhythmia limits NE; angiotensin II if catechol+vaso refractory
- AVOID: pressors in unfilled hypovolemia; IVF bolus in cardiogenic pulm edema; dopamine (arrhythmia) as routine 1st-line
- source-driven: sepsis → cultures + abx within 1h, source control; anaphylaxis → epi IM/IV + steroids/antihistamine; adrenal → hydrocortisone; neurogenic → vasopressor + ? atropine
- monitoring: MAP goal ≥65, a-line if gtt, ScvO2/lactate clearance, UOP, telemetry; central access for vasopressin/escalating NE

Take-Home Points

  1. Four categories of shock — hypovolemic, cardiogenic, obstructive, distributive — each with a distinct CVP/CO/SVR signature that names the first treatment lever.
  2. Sort at the bedside first: warm + wide pulse pressure = distributive (low SVR); cold + narrow pulse pressure = low-output (cardiogenic or hypovolemic).
  3. Norepinephrine is the vasopressor of choice for septic/distributive shock; dobutamine is the inotrope for cardiogenic shock; "no pressors if the tank is not full" — volume and blood come first in hypovolemia.
  4. SvO2 helps sort mixed pictures (low in cardiogenic, high in established sepsis) but is confounded by CO, SaO2, and hemoglobin.
  5. 2021 Surviving Sepsis Campaign updates: balanced crystalloids over saline, add vasopressin early rather than maxing norepinephrine, and start norepinephrine peripherally to avoid delay; CLOVERS showed no mortality difference between restrictive and liberal fluid strategies.
