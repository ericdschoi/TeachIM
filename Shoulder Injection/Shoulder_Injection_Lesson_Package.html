Learning Objectives

  1. Determine the appropriate indications and contraindications for a steroid injection of the subacromial space.
  2. Perform all critical steps of a subacromial steroid injection (except needle insertion) using high-fidelity simulation under direct preceptor observation.
  3. Perform all critical steps of a subacromial steroid injection in the clinical environment under direct preceptor observation.

Framework: Learn → See → Practice → Prove → Do → Maintain (Sawyer et al., Acad Med 2015). This lesson pairs a brief shoulder exam (likelihood-ratio–based) with the procedure — making the right diagnosis (and ruling out mimics like cervical radiculopathy) is the most-often-missed step.

Pre-work NEW — send the day before with the procedure video + quiz

Watch the procedural video, skim the clinical checklist, and answer these without looking anything up. (The full 9-item quiz, with and without answers, is included as files above.)

Q1. Which shoulder problems belong in the subacromial space — and which need an intra-articular (glenohumeral) injection instead? Subacromial: rotator cuff tendinopathy, subacromial bursitis, impingement. Intra-articular (glenohumeral): glenohumeral OA, adhesive capsulitis (a GH injection is more efficacious than subacromial here). Acromioclavicular (AC) OA needs an AC-joint injection. Matching the space to the diagnosis is the key teaching point.
Q2. Where do you mark the landmark and insert for the posterolateral subacromial approach? Mark the scapular spine and the lateral margin of the acromion; draw two intersecting lines to find the posterolateral point of the acromion, then insert ~1–2 cm below it in a soft recess. Aim the needle under the acromion, angled slightly cephalad toward the coracoid. The posterolateral and lateral approaches have similar outcomes.
Q3. What should you tell a patient about how much a subacromial steroid injection actually helps? It gives a short-term improvement in pain and function (better SPADI at ~8 weeks) but is no better than no injection at 6–12 months (GRASP, Lancet 2021). Progressive exercise is core therapy; a single PT session plus an injection was the best-value strategy, and patients did their exercises more after an injection. Subacromial decompression surgery has little benefit over conservative care for impingement (CSAW, Lancet 2018).

Simulation Teaching Script Restructured into Learn–See–Practice–Prove with timing & engagement cues

Run the simulation as a true clinical encounter (no-touch technique). Pair it with the shoulder-exam cases. Keep the clinical checklist in view during teaching, then remove it for the graded run. The cursor icons mark built-in ASK prompts. Target 45–70 min including the exam.

Hook & framing (2 min)

"A 70-year-old comes in 'for the shoulder shot.' Her empty-can and impingement tests are negative, but her biceps reflex is down and Spurling's is positive. Before you reach for the needle — where's the lesion, and would a subacromial injection do anything?" Hold the answer. Establish the two highest-yield skills: localizing the problem on exam (intrinsic shoulder vs cervical spine vs glenohumeral vs AC) and setting realistic expectations (injection = short-term; exercise = durable).

SEE — Supply prep (no-touch) & injection 14 min — instructor demonstrates

  1. Identify supplies (non-sterile gloves): 1% lidocaine (5 cc), triamcinolone 40 mg/mL (1 cc), alcohol swabs, 5–10 cc syringe, 18 g draw-up needle, 21–25 g 1.5" injection needle, marker, chlorhexidine, gauze, band-aid. Draw-up tips: clean vial tops; separate needles for drawing up vs injecting; inject air when withdrawing >3 cc; draw lidocaine first and add a little to the steroid vial.
  2. Mark the scapular spine and lateral acromion → posterolateral point of the acromion; insertion ~1–2 cm below it in a soft recess.
  3. Sterilize with a chlorhexidine scrub ×30 s.
  4. Position/stabilize the shoulder with the non-dominant hand; insert the needle under the acromion, angled slightly cephalad toward the coracoid, to ~3 cm.
  5. Aspirate (no blood), then inject the lidocaine/triamcinolone mix without resistance. Remove needle, band-aid.
ASK: "You feel firm resistance when you push the plunger. What does that mean and what do you do?"

Resistance = the tip is in tendon/soft tissue, not the bursa/subacromial space — injecting into the tendon risks rupture. Withdraw slightly and redirect under the acromion until the injection flows freely.

PRACTICE → PROVE — Mastery learning remaining time

Each learner takes a turn as proceduralist with a peer/standardized patient. In the simulation, the learner lists indications/contraindications and verbalizes needle insertion (insertion is not physically performed in sim). In the clinical setting, they must identify the specific indication for this patient and demonstrate the supporting history/exam finding. Repeat until the learner meets the passing standard on the clinical assessment tool (error-counting checklist + Global Skills + Entrustment).

Close (2 min)

Hide the guide. Ask the learner to rebuild: the exam findings that send you to the C-spine instead of the shoulder, which diagnoses get a subacromial vs glenohumeral vs AC injection, the posterolateral landmark and trajectory, what to tell the patient about durability, and when surgery beats PT.

Case Vignettes From the practice cases & quiz — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — The shoulder pain that isn't the shoulder

A 70-year-old woman has 2 months of left shoulder pain. Empty-can and external-rotation strength are 4/5 without pain; Neer's and Hawkin's are negative. Her biceps reflex is decreased on the left, Spurling's is positive, and neck rotation to the left is reduced. Six weeks of PT haven't helped.

Best next diagnostic step?MRI of the cervical spine (without contrast). Negative intrinsic shoulder maneuvers plus a positive Spurling's (+LR 4.5) and a decreased biceps reflex (+LR 9.1) point to cervical radiculopathy, not a subacromial problem. A subacromial injection would not help; image the neck.

Case 2 — Frozen shoulder, what next

A 60-year-old woman with T2DM has 7 months of left shoulder pain after a fall and limited active and passive ROM in all planes. You refer her to PT and order a shoulder x-ray. What's the best next treatment step?

Answer?This is adhesive capsulitis (global loss of active and passive ROM; diabetes is a risk factor). Best next step is an intra-articular glenohumeral injection (more efficacious than subacromial for frozen shoulder) alongside PT. The UK FROST trial found no clinically important difference between PT+injection, manipulation under anesthesia+injection, and capsular release — and the surgical arms had more serious adverse events, so start conservatively. The x-ray screens for glenohumeral OA, which can also limit passive ROM.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — 5 items, ABIM single-best-answer

1. A patient with shoulder pain has negative Neer's/Hawkin's, a decreased biceps reflex, and a positive Spurling's. What is the best next diagnostic step?

  1. MRI of the left shoulder
  2. Shoulder x-ray
  3. MRI of the cervical spine
  4. Subacromial steroid injection
Answer & explanationC. The picture is cervical radiculopathy (Spurling's +LR 4.5; decreased biceps reflex +LR 9.1), not intrinsic shoulder disease — image the C-spine. A shoulder MRI/x-ray or subacromial injection would miss the diagnosis.

2. Which diagnosis is an appropriate indication for a subacromial (rather than intra-articular) injection?

  1. Glenohumeral osteoarthritis
  2. Acromioclavicular osteoarthritis
  3. Rotator cuff tendinopathy
  4. Adhesive capsulitis
Answer & explanationC. Rotator cuff tendinopathy/impingement and subacromial bursitis are subacromial-space targets. Glenohumeral OA and adhesive capsulitis are treated with intra-articular glenohumeral injection (more efficacious for frozen shoulder), and AC OA with an AC-joint injection.

3. Which is the strongest reason to defer a subacromial corticosteroid injection?

  1. Well-controlled type 2 diabetes
  2. Prior shoulder surgery without intra-articular hardware
  3. Anticipated shoulder surgery within the next 3 months
  4. A subacromial injection 4 months ago
Answer & explanationC. Corticosteroid injection close to surgery (especially within ~4 weeks of arthroscopy) increases infection risk, so defer if surgery is anticipated within ~3 months. Well-controlled diabetes (A) is a relative consideration (counsel re: glucose), prior surgery without hardware (B) is not a contraindication, and an injection 4 months ago (D) is outside the "avoid within 3 months" window.

4. A 45-year-old with 4 months of impingement-pattern shoulder pain asks about a "quick fix." What is accurate counseling?

  1. A minimum of 6 weeks of PT is required before anything else
  2. A subacromial steroid injection can improve pain and function at ~8 weeks
  3. Arthroscopic subacromial decompression gives better long-term relief than conservative care
  4. An injection makes patients less likely to do their PT
Answer & explanationB. In GRASP (Lancet 2021), subacromial CSI improved SPADI at 8 weeks (no difference by 6–12 months), 6 PT sessions were not superior to 1, and patients actually did more of their exercises after an injection. Subacromial decompression has little benefit over conservative care (CSAW, Lancet 2018), making C wrong; A and D are contradicted by the evidence.

5. A 50-year-old has 3 months of difficulty lifting the arm overhead despite PT and NSAIDs; exam shows a positive drop-arm test. What is the next best step?

  1. Continue PT for 3 more months
  2. Refer to orthopedic surgery for rotator cuff repair
  3. Perform a subacromial corticosteroid injection
  4. Refer for an intra-articular glenohumeral injection
Answer & explanationB. A positive drop-arm test plus persistent symptoms after 3 months of PT suggests a full-thickness rotator cuff tear, for which surgery is significantly better than conservative care for pain and function at 2 years (Cederqvist, Ann Rheum Dis 2021). (Partial-thickness tears do equally well with PT.) An injection wouldn't address a full-thickness tear.

Take-Home Points

  1. Localize before you inject: negative intrinsic shoulder maneuvers + a positive Spurling's / decreased biceps reflex = cervical radiculopathy (image the C-spine, don't inject the shoulder).
  2. Match the space to the diagnosis: subacromial for RC tendinopathy/bursitis/impingement; glenohumeral for GH OA and adhesive capsulitis; AC joint for AC OA.
  3. Posterolateral landmark: ~1–2 cm below the posterolateral acromion, needle under the acromion angled slightly toward the coracoid; inject only without resistance (resistance = tendon, risks rupture).
  4. Set expectations: subacromial CSI helps short-term (~8 wk) but not at 6–12 months; progressive exercise is core therapy, and decompression surgery adds little for impingement.
  5. Know when surgery wins: a full-thickness RC tear (positive drop-arm) with persistent symptoms after ~3 months of PT does better with repair; partial-thickness tears do equally well with PT.
  6. Defer near surgery: avoid CSI if shoulder surgery is anticipated within ~3 months (infection risk). Well-controlled diabetes is only a relative caution.
