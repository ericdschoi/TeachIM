Learning Objectives

  1. Frame the meaningful clinical question in a patient with chest pain — for the outpatient with non-acute symptoms, "is this obstructive CAD?" (not "is this ACS?").
  2. Use a validated pretest-probability tool (CAD Consortium Clinical Score) to risk-stratify the likelihood of clinically significant CAD — as a check on gestalt, not a replacement for it.
  3. Build a framework for selecting the appropriate outpatient stress test and initial medical management based on risk category and the patient's ability to exercise.
  4. Apply the framework to six clinical cases.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. For an outpatient with weeks of exertional chest pressure, what is the clinical question you are trying to answer — and how is it different from the question in the ED? Outpatient, non-acute: "Is this symptom due to a flow-limiting (obstructive, >50%) coronary lesion?" — a diagnosis-of-CAD question answered with pretest probability and stress/anatomic testing. The ED question is "Is this acute coronary syndrome, and will aggressive inpatient management reduce a short-term major adverse cardiac event (MACE)?" — answered with serial ECGs, troponin, and tools like the HEART score.
Q2. Name the three pretest-probability risk bands the talk uses for obstructive CAD and the action each triggers. Low (<5%) → look for an alternative cause, no stress test. Intermediate (5–70%) → stress test to confirm or exclude obstructive CAD. High (>70%) → obtain ECG + imaging to exclude high-grade disease (left main, proximal LAD, 3-vessel) and start empiric medical therapy.
Q3. When can a patient do an exercise treadmill ECG (ETT) instead of an imaging stress test — and what disqualifies the ECG portion? ETT requires that the patient can exercise AND has an interpretable baseline ECG. Disqualifying ECG findings (reduce sensitivity → need imaging): LBBB, paced rhythm, pre-excitation (WPW), LVH with repolarization changes, or >1 mm resting ST depression. If the patient can exercise but the ECG is uninterpretable, use exercise imaging (echo or MPI); if they cannot exercise, use pharmacologic stress.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (Stress_Testing_CAD_Optimized.pptx) and click through — every rounded, shadowed box is an animated button — or reproduce the flow diagram on a whiteboard. Print the learner handout so residents annotate the decision tree as you build it. Framework talk: ~20 min. Six cases: +20–25 min.

Hook (2 min)

"Three patients, all with chest pain that comes on when they exert themselves and goes away with rest. One needs nothing, one needs a stress test, one needs to skip the stress test entirely. How do you decide before you order anything?" Tell them the whole talk is one decision tree — and that the tree starts not with a test but with a question.

Objective 1 — Frame the question 5 min

ASK: "Same symptom — chest pain — in clinic vs. in the ED. Are you asking the same question? What is each question really about?" Click Clinical Question after they answer.

Outpatient, non-acute chest pain asks: is this obstructive (>50%) CAD? The ED asks: is this ACS, and will aggressive inpatient care reduce short-term MACE? The two questions use different tools (pretest probability + stress/anatomic testing vs. serial troponin/ECG + HEART score) and this talk is exclusively the outpatient question. Naming the question keeps the workup from sprawling.

Pearl — describe the pain precisely: The 2021 AHA/ACC Chest Pain Guideline asks clinicians to retire "typical / atypical" and instead describe pain as cardiac, possibly cardiac, or noncardiac. "Atypical" was vague and tended to falsely reassure. The substance of the history (exertional, relieved by rest/nitroglycerin, reproducible) is unchanged — only the label.

Objective 2 — Pretest probability 7 min

ASK: "Before any test, what raises or lowers your suspicion that this chest pain is obstructive CAD?" Collect features, then click CAD Consortium Score.

The CAD Consortium Clinical Score estimates the pretest probability of obstructive CAD from age, sex, chest-pain character, and risk factors (diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia, smoking). It outperformed the older Diamond–Forrester model and predicts both obstructive disease and events (Bittencourt, Circulation 2016). The talk sorts patients into three bands:

Pretest probabilityInterpretationAction
Low (<5%)Obstructive CAD unlikelyLook for an alternative cause; no stress test
Intermediate (5–70%)Diagnostic uncertainty — testing changes managementStress test to confirm or exclude obstructive CAD
High (>70%)Obstructive CAD likely; the question is severityECG + imaging to exclude left main / proximal LAD / 3-vessel disease; start empiric medical therapy
ASK: "Did the score match your gut?" The teaching point: scores are a check on System-1 intuition, not a replacement. They will misclassify individual patients — use them to catch your own over- and under-calling, then decide.

Objective 3 — Choose the test and the management 8 min

Click Diagnosis and Management, then the flow diagram. "Risk" on the diagram is the pretest probability of obstructive CAD.

Low risk: evaluate for an alternative cause (MSK, GI, anxiety, pulmonary). High risk: obtain an ECG and an imaging stress test — the goal here is not to diagnose CAD (you already believe it's there) but to rule out high-grade disease (left main, proximal LAD, significant cardiomyopathy) that carries a sudden-death risk; if none, treat medically and defer cath/CCTA until medical therapy fails.

ASK: "Intermediate-risk patient in front of you — what's the very first branch point in choosing the stress test?" (Can the patient exercise?)

Intermediate risk walks the branch points:

Branch pointIf yesIf no
Can the patient exercise?Exercise test (functional info + reproduces the symptom)Pharmacologic stress (vasodilator MPI or dobutamine echo)
Is the baseline ECG interpretable? (no LBBB, paced rhythm, pre-excitation, LVH w/ strain, >1 mm ST-dep)Exercise treadmill ECG (ETT) — cheapest, preferredExercise imaging (stress echo or MPI)
Echo vs. nuclear (MPI/SPECT)?Institution-dependent; both give EF and ischemia. Echo adds valve assessment; nuclear may be easier with body habitus / poor windows.
ASK: "The stress test comes back positive. Cath now?" — not necessarily. Start medical therapy; reserve coronary anatomy (CCTA or left heart cath) for markedly positive tests or failure of medical management.

"Markedly positive" — go define the anatomy: ECG/exercise — significant ST-depression at low workload, ST-elevation, or exertional hypotension. Imaging — resting EF <35%, EF drop or LV dilation with stress, or ischemia in >2 vascular territories.

Empiric medical management of stable CAD (build it on the board):

BucketTherapy
Risk-factor modificationDiet/exercise, smoking cessation, A1c <7.0, BP <130/80
CardioprotectiveASA 81 mg daily; high-intensity statin; ACE-I/ARB if DM, CKD, or EF <40%
Anti-anginalSublingual nitroglycerin PRN; beta-blocker; add long-acting nitrate or CCB; consider ranolazine
Pearl — what's changed since 2020 (2023 Chronic Coronary Disease guideline): a beta-blocker is no longer required as the default anti-anginal in someone without prior MI or LVEF ≤50% and no other indication — pick the anti-anginal that fits the patient. High-intensity statin remains first-line; add an SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 RA when there is concomitant diabetes (SGLT2i if HF), and low-dose colchicine 0.5 mg daily is now a reasonable secondary-prevention add-on (Class 2a, LoDoCo2).

Objective 4 — Cases 20–25 min

Break into small teams. For each vignette: decide whether the pain is cardiac / possibly cardiac / noncardiac, assign a risk band, compare it to the CAD Consortium score, then choose a test and a disposition. The author built the stems deliberately incomplete — push the discussion, there is no single right answer. Full worked cases are in the Cases section below.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild the tree from memory: the question, the three risk bands and their actions, the exercise-vs-pharmacologic and ECG-vs-imaging branch points, and the rule that medical therapy comes before cath. Then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases 20–25 min — from the interactive board; answers click-to-reveal

The board's six vignettes are reproduced faithfully below. Pretest values and worked results on the board are discussion prompts, not answer keys — the author notes "there are no absolutely correct answers here." The reveals below give the framework-correct reasoning for each patient.

Case 1 — Can't exercise

45-year-old woman, remote smoker with well-controlled DM, with 2 months of sharp, intermittent central chest pain every few days while doing housework or grocery shopping, resolving on its own with rest. She cannot climb stairs or walk more than a block because of bilateral knee OA. Total chol 180, HDL 38, LDL 142; A1c 7.2.

Cardiac, possibly cardiac, or noncardiac? Risk band?Possibly cardiac — non-exertional quality but real risk factors (DM, prior smoking, LDL 142). The CAD Consortium score lands her in the intermediate range, where a stress test changes management. Note her score may differ from your gestalt — that discordance is the teaching point.
Which stress test?She cannot exercise (knee OA), so an exercise treadmill test is off the table → pharmacologic stress imaging (vasodilator MPI, e.g., regadenoside/dipyridamole, or dobutamine stress echo). Her baseline ECG is normal sinus rhythm, but exercise simply isn't possible.
If the result is non-diagnostic?Indeterminate septal changes during vasodilator MPI without symptoms or hemodynamic change are a common artifact, not ischemia. With no demonstrated obstructive CAD, the move is aggressive risk-factor modification (statin, A1c <7, BP <130/80) and reassurance — not catheterization.

Case 2 — Score vs. gestalt

73-year-old man, never-smoker with HTN, with one week of an ache and pressure just inferior to the sternum that comes on with minimal activity, usually in the evening or early morning, self-resolving after ~10 minutes. Still able to walk a few blocks with his dog. A1c 5.6.

Cardiac, possibly cardiac, or noncardiac? Risk band?The story sounds anginal (exertional, relieved by rest), so gestalt says cardiac. This is the deliberate gestalt-vs-score case: run the CAD Consortium score and discuss where it lands and why it might diverge from your gut. Whatever band you land on, an interpretable ECG and the ability to exercise keep your options open.
Which stress test?He can walk → he can likely exercise; if the baseline ECG is interpretable, an exercise treadmill ECG (ETT) is the cheapest first test and reproduces his symptom. If the ECG is uninterpretable, use exercise imaging.
Disposition by result?A normal high-workload test (good METs, target HR, no symptoms or ECG change) is reassuring → manage risk factors, no cath. A positive but not markedly positive test → start medical therapy for stable CAD. Markedly positive (ST-depression at low workload, exertional hypotension) → define the anatomy.

Case 3 — High pretest, uninterpretable ECG

75-year-old man, active smoker with HTN and COPD (GOLD 3), with one week of chest tightness when gardening or carrying in groceries that resolves after a few minutes of rest. Total chol 180, HDL 38, LDL 142 (on a statin); A1c 5.6. ECG: LVH with 1+ mm ST depression in V4–6.

Risk band and first move?Typical exertional angina in a 75-year-old active smoker = high pretest probability. The job now is severity, not diagnosis: obtain an ECG and an imaging stress test to exclude left main / proximal LAD / 3-vessel disease.
Why not an exercise treadmill ECG?His baseline ECG (LVH with ST depression) is uninterpretable for ischemia, so the ECG portion loses sensitivity → use stress imaging. He may still exercise (exercise echo or MPI) if able; COPD and deconditioning may push toward pharmacologic stress.
If imaging shows a small area of inducible ischemia with preserved EF?No high-grade/markedly-positive features → empiric medical management of stable CAD (per ISCHEMIA, an initial invasive strategy doesn't reduce death/MI vs. optimal medical therapy in stable moderate ischemia). Reserve cath for medical-therapy failure or high-risk findings.

Case 4 — Known CAD, new symptoms

62-year-old man, active smoker with COPD (GOLD 3), DM, and a prior drug-eluting stent in the RCA 3 years ago, now with shortness of breath and chest tightness every few days, usually with exertion but sometimes at rest, self-resolving. He cannot climb stairs due to hip pain. On ASA, beta-blocker, ACE-I, statin; A1c 7.9. Exam: expiratory wheezing. ECG: sinus with Q waves in II/III/aVF and incomplete RBBB.

How does known CAD change the framework?This is symptomatic known CAD despite guideline-directed medical therapy — at least intermediate risk. Stress imaging (MPI, echo, or CMR) is recommended to localize and quantify ischemia; he can't exercise (hip pain) so use pharmacologic stress. Some patients go straight to coronary anatomy (CCTA or LHC) given the symptom burden.
If MPI shows a large inducible anterior wall ischemia with EF dropping 55%→45%?That is a markedly positive, high-risk study (large territory + LV dysfunction with stress) → define the anatomy with left heart catheterization and intensify medical therapy (e.g., add an anti-anginal, optimize secondary prevention, address the persistent smoking and A1c 7.9).

Case 5 — The straightforward exerciser

65-year-old man, active smoker with HTN, with 2 days of chest pain whenever he climbs stairs or walks a few blocks with the dog. He feels diaphoretic and dizzy when it happens and needs to sit down; symptoms self-resolve after 10 minutes. Total chol 180, HDL 38, LDL 142 (on a statin); A1c 5.6. ECG: normal sinus rhythm.

Risk band and test?Exertional pain with diaphoresis in a 65-year-old smoker = intermediate-to-high pretest. He can exercise and the baseline ECG is interpretable → exercise treadmill ECG (ETT) first; it reproduces the symptom and adds functional/prognostic data (METs achieved).
He achieves 12 METs and 80% of max HR; test stopped for chest tightness without ECG changes or hypotension. Next?High functional capacity with a non-markedly-positive test → medical management of stable CAD and risk-factor control; cath is not mandated. Reassess if symptoms progress or become lifestyle-limiting.

Case 6 — When it's actually ACS

55-year-old woman, active smoker with well-controlled DM, with 2–3 weeks of chest tightness and shortness of breath when hiking, resolving with rest after 10–15 minutes. Total chol 180, HDL 38, LDL 142; A1c 7.2. ECG: LVH with 1+ mm ST depression and T-wave inversions V1–3; troponin negative. (Board pretest tag: "High — concerning for ACS.")

Why does this one leave the outpatient pathway?New dynamic-appearing ECG changes (ST depression, anterior TWI) with a concerning, accelerating story push this toward an acute evaluation — admit for ACS rule-out with serial ECGs and troponins, telemetry, and inpatient TTE rather than scheduling an outpatient stress test. The outpatient pretest framework applies to stable, non-acute chest pain; this is the boundary case that teaches when to switch questions.
If serial ECGs/troponins stay flat and there's no recurrence?Once ACS is excluded and the patient is stable, she rejoins the stable-CAD pathway: given the abnormal resting ECG, an imaging stress test (or CCTA) to evaluate for high-grade disease, then medical management vs. cath based on severity.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 58-year-old woman has 3 weeks of substernal pressure when climbing stairs, relieved by rest within 5 minutes. She has hypertension and hyperlipidemia and can walk on a treadmill. Resting ECG is normal. Pretest probability of obstructive CAD is intermediate. What is the most appropriate initial test?

  1. Coronary CT angiography
  2. Exercise treadmill ECG (ETT)
  3. Vasodilator myocardial perfusion imaging
  4. Invasive coronary angiography
Answer & explanationB. She can exercise and her baseline ECG is interpretable, so the exercise treadmill ECG is the preferred first test — inexpensive, provides functional capacity and symptom reproduction. Pharmacologic MPI (C) is for patients who cannot exercise. CCTA (A) is a reasonable Class 1 alternative in the 2021 guideline, especially if <65 and less obstructive disease is suspected, but among the options the functional ETT is the standard first step when exercise and ECG allow. Invasive angiography (D) is not a first-line diagnostic test in stable, intermediate-risk patients.

2. A 70-year-old man with diabetes and a left bundle branch block on his resting ECG has exertional angina and cannot walk more than half a block because of severe peripheral arterial disease. Which stress test is most appropriate?

  1. Exercise treadmill ECG
  2. Exercise stress echocardiography
  3. Vasodilator (pharmacologic) myocardial perfusion imaging
  4. No testing; treat empirically
Answer & explanationC. Two barriers: he cannot exercise (so any exercise-based test, A and B, is out) and he has LBBB (which makes the ECG uninterpretable and also causes false septal defects with exercise MPI — vasodilator stress is preferred over exercise or dobutamine in LBBB). Vasodilator MPI solves both. Empiric treatment alone (D) is reasonable in very high pretest probability but he still warrants assessment for high-grade disease given symptoms.

3. A 64-year-old man with stable exertional angina has a stress MPI showing a small, single-territory area of inducible ischemia with normal LV ejection fraction. He is started on a statin, aspirin, and an anti-anginal. Based on the ISCHEMIA trial, what is the expected benefit of proceeding directly to coronary angiography and revascularization rather than continuing optimal medical therapy?

  1. A significant reduction in death or myocardial infarction
  2. No reduction in death or MI, though angina may improve
  3. A significant reduction in stroke
  4. Elimination of the need for statin therapy
Answer & explanationB. ISCHEMIA (NEJM 2020) randomized stable patients with moderate-to-severe ischemia to an initial invasive strategy vs. optimal medical therapy and found no reduction in death or MI; an invasive approach improved angina-related quality of life in symptomatic patients. So in stable CAD without high-risk anatomy, medical therapy first is appropriate; revascularization is driven by refractory symptoms or high-risk features — not by the presence of moderate ischemia alone (A, C). Statins remain essential regardless (D).

4. According to the 2021 AHA/ACC Chest Pain Guideline, how should a patient's chest pain be characterized in documentation?

  1. Typical, atypical, or noncardiac
  2. Cardiac, possibly cardiac, or noncardiac
  3. Stable or unstable only
  4. By the Diamond–Forrester category
Answer & explanationB. The 2021 guideline recommends describing chest pain as cardiac, possibly cardiac, or noncardiac, and explicitly discourages "atypical," which was vague and falsely reassuring (A). "Stable vs. unstable" (C) describes the temporal pattern, not the likelihood of an ischemic cause. Diamond–Forrester (D) is an older pretest-probability model now superseded by CAD Consortium-type scores.

5. A 48-year-old woman with atypical chest pain has a low pretest probability of obstructive CAD. Per the 2021 Chest Pain Guideline, which test can be used as a first-line option to help exclude calcified atherosclerotic plaque and support deferring further testing?

  1. Coronary artery calcium (CAC) score
  2. Invasive coronary angiography
  3. Stress cardiac MRI
  4. Right heart catheterization
Answer & explanationA. In low-risk stable patients, a coronary artery calcium score of zero identifies a population at low risk of obstructive CAD and favorable prognosis, supporting deferral of further testing; the guideline endorses CAC as a useful first-line gatekeeper. Invasive angiography (B) and right heart cath (D) are inappropriate in this low-risk setting; stress CMR (C) is a functional imaging test reserved for intermediate-high risk or specific indications, not a first-line low-risk screen.

Guideline Update layered context — 2021 Chest Pain & 2023 Chronic Coronary Disease

The deck's standing diagnostic framework reflects the 2019 ESC chronic coronary syndromes guideline and remains a valid teaching structure. Two newer documents update the framing; surface them as context rather than replacing the original board.

2021 AHA/ACC/ASE/CHEST/SAEM/SCCT/SCMR Chest Pain Guideline (Gulati et al.)

  • Describe chest pain as cardiac / possibly cardiac / noncardiac; retire "typical/atypical."
  • Stable pain, no known CAD, low pretest probability → defer testing; coronary artery calcium (CAC) score is a useful first-line gatekeeper (CAC = 0 → low risk).
  • Intermediate–high risk → CCTA is Class 1 first-line (anatomic) alongside stress imaging (functional). CCTA favored if <65 y or less obstructive CAD suspected; stress favored if >65 y or more obstructive CAD suspected.
  • High-sensitivity troponin is preferred for the acute pathway.

2023 AHA/ACC/ACCP/ASPC/NLA/PCNA Chronic Coronary Disease Guideline (Virani et al.)

  • Beta-blockers are not required as anti-anginal therapy in patients without prior MI or LVEF ≤50% and no other indication — individualize the anti-anginal.
  • High-intensity statin first-line; add SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 RA when there is concomitant diabetes (SGLT2i if HF), regardless of glycemic control.
  • Colchicine 0.5 mg daily is a reasonable secondary-prevention add-on (Class 2a; LoDoCo2).
  • ISCHEMIA (2020): in stable CAD with moderate–severe ischemia, an initial invasive strategy did not reduce death/MI vs. optimal medical therapy — supports the board's "treat medically first, defer cath" teaching.

Take-Home Points

  1. Frame the question first: outpatient non-acute chest pain asks "is this obstructive CAD?" — a different question, and a different workup, than the ED's "is this ACS?"
  2. Risk-stratify by pretest probability (CAD Consortium): low <5% → seek an alternative cause; intermediate 5–70% → stress test; high >70% → ECG + imaging to exclude left main/proximal LAD/3-vessel disease, then empiric medical therapy.
  3. Choose the stress test by what the patient can do: exercise treadmill ECG when they can exercise and the baseline ECG is interpretable; otherwise imaging stress (echo or MPI), exercise or pharmacologic.
  4. Treat medically first; reserve CCTA/left heart cath for markedly positive testing or failure of medical management (ISCHEMIA).
  5. Scores support — never supersede — clinical judgment.
