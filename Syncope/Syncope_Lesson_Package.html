Learning Objectives

  1. Define syncope and distinguish it from other causes of transient loss of consciousness (TLOC).
  2. Describe the pathophysiology differentiating the 3 classifications of syncope: orthostatic hypotension, reflex, and cardiac.
  3. Select appropriate diagnostic tests based on history and exam — and know which tests are NOT routinely useful.
  4. Identify high-risk features that warrant inpatient evaluation.
  5. Bonus: Initiate first- and second-line management and know when to consult.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Define syncope. What separates it from the broader category of "transient loss of consciousness"? Syncope is an abrupt, transient loss of consciousness due to global cerebral hypoperfusion, with rapid spontaneous full recovery. TLOC is the umbrella (short duration, abnormal motor control, loss of responsiveness, amnesia for the event); syncope is the subset caused by cerebral hypoperfusion. A 6–8 second drop in cerebral blood flow is enough.
Q2. Name the three great mimics of syncope and one feature that distinguishes each. Seizure — prolonged convulsions and marked post-ictal confusion (convulsive syncope has brief myoclonus and no post-ictal state); tongue biting and incontinence also point to seizure. Vertebrobasilar TIA/stroke — LOC accompanied by focal findings (vertigo, dysarthria); posterior events don't cause isolated LOC. Hypoglycemia — impaired rather than abruptly lost consciousness, longer duration, and no spontaneous recovery without intervention.
Q3. What four things does EVERY syncope patient get, regardless of suspected cause? History, physical exam (cardiac focus), orthostatic vital signs, and a 12-lead ECG. Everything else is individualized.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the corrected interactive board (Syncope_Optimized.pptx) and click through, or reproduce the schematic on a whiteboard. Every cursor icon on the board is a built-in ASK prompt — pose the question before clicking. Talk without cases: ~20 min. Cases: +10–15 min. Print the learner handout (board set-up) so learners take notes as you go.

Hook (2 min)

"65-year-old watching TV goes unresponsive for 20 seconds with whole-body jerking, then wakes up clear-headed." Ask: syncope or seizure — and what one historical detail decides it? (No post-ictal confusion. Convulsive syncope is a thing.) This is Case 3; you're planting it early.

Objective 1 — Definition & mimics 4 min

ASK: "Define syncope" — then click to reveal. The definition is mechanistic: TLOC due to global cerebral hypoperfusion with spontaneous full recovery. ~60% of TLOC cases can be sorted by history and exam alone.

Mimics (click each mini-case): seizure (LOC + shaking + post-ictal state), vertebrobasilar TIA/CVA (LOC + vertigo/dysarthria — never isolated LOC), hypoglycemia (preceded by diaphoresis/tremulousness; recovery requires treating the glucose).

Pathophysiology review — optional 5 min

Build the cascade on the board; the rest of the talk reuses it: CPP = MAP − ICP; MAP = CO × SVR; CO = HR × SV; SV depends on preload, afterload, contractility. Then the baroreceptor reflex with standing: blood pools → ↓preload → ↓CO → ↓MAP → baroreceptors fire → ↑sympathetic tone → ↑CO and SVR → CPP preserved. Every syncope mechanism is a lesion somewhere in this loop.

Objective 2 — Three classifications 6 min

ASK: "Standing up drops your preload every time you do it. Why don't you faint every time — and what are the ways the rescue system can fail?"

Orthostatic hypotension — an insufficient autonomic response. Defined as a drop in SBP ≥20 or DBP ≥10 with postural change (ESC also accepts SBP <90 with symptoms — not in the ACC/AHA definition). Four failure points:

Failure pointMechanismDifferential
Sympathetic tone↓CO, HR, SVR → ↓MAP"Neurogenic OH": primary autonomic failure, Parkinson's/MSA; secondary — diabetes, amyloid, paraneoplastic, cord injury
SVR response↓SVR → ↓MAPDrugs (vasodilators, antidepressants) — drug-induced OH is the MOST common cause; adrenal insufficiency
HR response↓HR → ↓CO → ↓MAPBeta-blockers, calcium channel blockers
Preload↓SV → ↓CO → ↓MAPVolume loss, hemorrhage, diuretics; worse with exercise, meals, prolonged bedrest

Reflex (neurally mediated) — an inappropriate autonomic response to a trigger. Causes: vasovagal (orthostatic or emotional), situational (micturition, defecation, cough, post-exercise), carotid sinus hypersensitivity. Two hemodynamic subtypes — cardioinhibitory (vagal surge → bradycardia) and vasodepressive (sympathetic withdrawal → ↓SVR) — and most real patients are mixed.

Cardiac — low cardiac output, the classification that kills. Four mechanisms: ↑afterload (aortic stenosis, HOCM), ↓contractility (MI, ventricular arrhythmias), ↓HR (AV block, sinus node dysfunction, pacemaker malfunction), ↓preload (PE, pulmonary HTN, tamponade, dissection).

Pearl — the PE question: Prandoni (NEJM 2016) found PE in 17% of patients hospitalized for first syncope, but a 2018 study of >1.5 million patients across 4 countries found PE in <1% of all-comers with syncope and <3% of those hospitalized. PE is a real but uncommon cause — don't CT every fainter.

Objective 3 — Evaluation 5 min

ASK: "Three patients, three stories. Build the table with me: who faints, where, and with what warning?" — work the Who / Where-How / Prodrome grid for OH vs reflex vs cardiac before revealing.
OHReflexCardiac
WhoOlder, comorbid; autonomic neuropathy or parkinsonism; new/changed BP meds or diureticsAll ages, generally healthy; recurrent syncope often starting <40Structural heart disease or CAD; family history of sudden death at young age
Where / howWhile or just after standing; post-prandialProlonged standing, hot/crowded places; micturition/defecation/cough; emotional triggers; head turn, tight collar, shavingSupine, sitting, or at rest (arrhythmia); during exertion (structural)
ProdromeLightheadedness with postural changePallor, diaphoresis, nauseaNone ("unheralded"), or sudden palpitations; ± chest pain, dyspnea

Everyone gets: history, exam, orthostatic vitals, ECG.

ASK: "What testing is NOT routinely useful?" — head CT/MRI (unless head injury or new neuro findings), EEG (unless the story suggests seizure), carotid imaging (bilateral disease would be needed to cause syncope; essentially never occurs without other neuro symptoms).

Targeted tests by suspected type: OH — BMP/CBC (volume, occult bleed), ± autonomic evaluation (Valsalva without HR/BP response suggests autonomic failure; refer). Reflex — usually a history diagnosis; tilt-table if uncertain (a cardioinhibitory response predicts asystolic spontaneous syncope — treatment implications); carotid sinus massage in >40s with a suggestive story (contraindicated with bruit, known stenosis >70%, or TIA/stroke within 3 months). Cardiac — telemetry, TTE, ± troponin/BNP, stress test if exertional, then ambulatory monitoring matched to event frequency:

MonitorDurationBest for
Holter24–72 hSymptoms likely to recur within days
Event (loop) monitor2–6 weeksModerate frequency + prodrome (patient-activated)
Patch monitor~2 weeksModerate frequency; comfortable, single lead
Implantable loop recorderYearsInfrequent, unexplained syncope

Objective 4 — Disposition 4 min

ASK: "Which fainter goes home and which gets a bed?" — collect candidate features, then reveal the high-risk list.
Low risk → homeHigh risk → admit
PMHLong history of recurrent low-risk syncope; no structural heart diseaseSevere structural heart disease or CAD; family history of sudden cardiac death
EventTypical reflex prodrome; clear trigger; after prolonged standing or hot/crowded placesNew chest pain or dyspnea; exertional or supine onset; sudden palpitations; short/absent prodrome (<10 s) with structural disease
ExamNormalSBP <90; HR <40 awake; signs of GI bleed; undiagnosed systolic murmur
ECGNormalAcute ischemia; Mobitz II or 3rd-degree AV block; HR <40; pauses >3 s awake; BBB, Q waves, hypertrophy; VT (sustained or not); pre-excitation; device malfunction; QTc <340 or >460 ms; Brugada pattern; ARVC

Neither clearly high nor low risk → observe in the ED or hospital.

Objective 5 (bonus) — Management 3 min

OH: fluids, stop/adjust culprit meds, behavioral measures (slow position changes, salt, compression garments, counter-pressure maneuvers); midodrine or fludrocortisone for autonomic dysfunction. Reflex: reassurance and trigger avoidance; counter-pressure maneuvers if there's a usable prodrome; consider midodrine/fludrocortisone/salt; pacemaker in selected patients >40 with documented cardioinhibitory syncope that is frequent and unpredictable. Cardiac: consult cardiology — treatment is etiology-specific.

2026 context: Since this talk was written, the case for pacing in cardioinhibitory reflex syncope has strengthened — the 2021 ESC pacing guideline upgraded dual-chamber pacing (closed-loop stimulation) for patients >40 with severe, recurrent, unpredictable syncope and documented asystolic pauses, on the strength of the BIOSync RCT (77% relative risk reduction at 2 years). Cardioneuroablation is an emerging device-free alternative still under study. The teaching point stands: these patients go to cardiology/EP, not to empiric therapy.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Ask the group to rebuild: the definition, the three classifications, the four universal tests, and three high-risk features — then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases 10–15 min — from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — The healthy fainter

21-year-old presents after syncope. VS: 36.5°C, HR 82, BP 112/87. Exam: no murmurs, otherwise normal.

What history is important?Who: history of depression on escitalopram. Where/how: standing in line at the grocery store. Prodrome: clamminess, tunnel vision.
What initial tests?ECG: normal sinus rhythm, normal intervals, no hypertrophy or prior ischemia. Routine labs are not needed.
Diagnosis?Vasovagal (reflex) syncope — young, healthy, classic trigger and prodrome.
Disposition and treatment?Outpatient. Reassurance, trigger avoidance, counter-pressure maneuvers if prodrome recurs.

Case 2 — The dry fainter

56-year-old with HTN and chronic venous stasis presents with syncope. VS: HR 112, BP 112/87. Exam: no murmurs, dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor.

What history is important?Who: HTN on lisinopril; recent GI illness. Where/how: occurred on standing up. Prodrome: lightheadedness and dizziness.
What initial tests?Orthostatics: HR 112, BP 112/70 → HR 130, BP 85/40 standing. ECG: sinus tachycardia. CBC normal; Na 132, K 3.2, bicarb 18, Cr 1.8, lactate 2.5.
Diagnosis?Orthostatic hypotension from volume depletion (GI losses) on top of an ACE inhibitor.
Disposition and treatment?Urgent care or ED; consider observation depending on response. IV fluid resuscitation, hold BP medications.

Case 3 — The trap (convulsions ≠ seizure)

65-year-old with CAD s/p PCI, HFrEF (ischemic), HTN, T2DM presents with LOC + convulsions while watching TV; unresponsive ~20 s with witnessed whole-body jerks, then immediately lucid — no confusion. VS: HR 72, BP 112/87. Exam: occasional irregular beats, no murmurs, euvolemic.

What history is important?Who: significant cardiac history on furosemide, carvedilol, sacubitril-valsartan, spironolactone, empagliflozin, aspirin, metformin; 2 weeks of episodic palpitations and lightheadedness at rest. Where/how: at rest, supine/sitting. Prodrome: none. At-rest onset + no prodrome + cardiac history = high risk.
What initial tests?CBC, BMP, troponin and BNP, ECG, orthostatic vitals. Results: orthostatics HR 72, BP 112/70 → HR 85, BP 115/65 (unremarkable); BMP near-normal; hs-troponin I 140 → 100 ng/L (downtrending); ECG: NSR with anterolateral Q waves, frequent PVCs, no new ischemia. (Two corrections from the original board: "HR 850" → HR 85; troponin units ng/dL → ng/L.)
Most likely cause, and where is this patient managed?Cardiac syncope — admit to the hospital for telemetry and TTE.
Further workup returns: LVEF 30%, wall motion abnormalities, mild AS; telemetry shows frequent symptomatic NSVT. Next step?Consult cardiology/EP — etiology is most likely NSVT in ischemic cardiomyopathy. (This is also an ICD-evaluation conversation given EF ≤35%.)

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 24-year-old woman loses consciousness for 30 seconds while having blood drawn. Witnesses describe pallor, then a few seconds of limb jerking. She is immediately oriented on waking. Exam and ECG are normal. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. EEG and neurology referral
  2. Head CT
  3. Reassurance and counseling on counter-pressure maneuvers
  4. 48-hour Holter monitor
Answer & explanationC. Classic emotional vasovagal syncope with a textbook trigger and prodrome. Brief myoclonic jerks occur in convulsive syncope and do not indicate seizure when there's no post-ictal confusion (A). Imaging (B) has no role without head injury or focal findings. Monitoring (D) is for suspected arrhythmic syncope — nothing here suggests it. Reflex syncope is a history diagnosis; treatment starts with reassurance and counter-pressure maneuvers.

2. A 78-year-old man with Parkinson's disease faints two minutes after standing from dinner. Supine BP 142/84, HR 76; standing BP 108/70, HR 79. Which feature most specifically points to a neurogenic cause of his orthostatic hypotension?

  1. The 34-point drop in systolic pressure
  2. The near-absent compensatory heart rate increase
  3. His age
  4. The post-prandial timing
Answer & explanationB. The BP drop (A) defines OH (SBP ≥20 or DBP ≥10) but not its cause. In volume depletion or drug-induced OH, an intact autonomic system mounts a compensatory tachycardia; a blunted HR response despite significant hypotension indicates the sympathetic limb itself is failing — neurogenic OH (here, Parkinson's-related autonomic failure). Age (C) and post-prandial venous pooling (D) are nonspecific contributors. Valsalva without HR/BP response supports the same conclusion.

3. A 71-year-old man passes out while climbing stairs, without warning. He has a harsh late-peaking systolic murmur radiating to the carotids, not previously documented. ECG shows LVH. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. Discharge with an event monitor and outpatient TTE
  2. Admit; obtain TTE before any exertion or vasodilators
  3. Tilt-table testing
  4. Carotid sinus massage to evaluate for carotid hypersensitivity
Answer & explanationB. Exertional syncope + undiagnosed systolic murmur + LVH = presumed severe aortic stenosis until proven otherwise — two independent high-risk features mandating admission. Outpatient workup (A) risks sudden death. Tilt-table (C) evaluates reflex syncope. Carotid sinus massage (D) is for suspected carotid hypersensitivity in the right story and would add nothing; vasodilators and volume shifts are dangerous in critical AS.

4. A 58-year-old woman is admitted after syncope. Workup reveals a normal neuro exam and no head trauma. The admitting resident orders head CT, carotid Dopplers, and EEG "to be complete." Which statement is correct?

  1. All three are appropriate in unexplained syncope
  2. Head CT is indicated; the others are not
  3. None is routinely indicated without neurologic findings, head injury, or a seizure-suggestive history
  4. Carotid Doppler is the highest-yield test for syncope
Answer & explanationC. Routine neuroimaging, EEG, and carotid imaging are explicitly low-utility in syncope (ACC/AHA 2017). Syncope requires global hypoperfusion — unilateral carotid disease doesn't produce it, and bilateral critical disease essentially never presents as isolated syncope (D). EEG needs a seizure-suggestive story; CT needs trauma or focal findings. These three tests are among the most common sources of low-value spending in syncope evaluation.

5. A 66-year-old man has had three unexplained syncopal episodes over 18 months, each without prodrome. Holter ×48 h and a 2-week patch were unrevealing; TTE and ECG are normal. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Repeat 48-hour Holter
  2. Implantable loop recorder
  3. Empiric permanent pacemaker
  4. Tilt-table testing to rule in reflex syncope
Answer & explanationB. Match the monitor to event frequency: episodes months apart will never be caught on short-duration devices (A) — an implantable loop recorder monitors for years and has the highest diagnostic yield in infrequent, unexplained syncope. Pacing (C) requires a documented bradyarrhythmic correlate. Tilt-table (D) may be considered, but unheralded syncope in an older man is suspicious for arrhythmia, and capturing the rhythm during a real event is the priority.

CSRS = * [vlow ≤−2 / low −1–0 / med 1–3 / high 4–5 / vhigh ≥6]. HDS/HDUS.
- core w/u: H&P [prodrome, trigger, EXERTIONAL, palpitations pre-event, FHx SCD/<50yo, structural HD, QT/AVN/diuretic/antiHTN meds, lat tongue bite/post-ictal] + 12-lead ECG [ischemia, AVB, BBB, QTc, Brugada, WPW, epsilon/ARVC, LVH] + orthostatics [SBP ↓≥20 / DBP ↓≥10 w/in 3min]
- CSRS pts: vasovagal predispo −1 / hx heart dz (CAD/AF/CHF/valv) +1 / ED SBP <90 or >180 +2 / trop >99%ile +2 / abnl QRS axis (<−30 or >100) +1 / QRS >130ms +1 / QTc >480ms +2 / ED dx vasovagal −2 / cardiac +2 → 30d SAE: vlow 0.4% / low 0.7% / med ~8% / high ~20% / vhigh ~51% (low → d/c w/ RTC; high/vhigh → tele/admit)

Take-Home Points

  1. Syncope is defined by its mechanism — global cerebral hypoperfusion — which is what separates it from seizure, hypoglycemia, and TIA.
  2. Three classifications: orthostatic (insufficient autonomic response), reflex (inappropriate response to a trigger), cardiac (low output — the dangerous one).
  3. Who + where/how + prodrome + the med list sorts most patients; ~60% of TLOC is diagnosable on history and exam alone.
  4. Everyone gets history, exam, orthostatic vitals, and an ECG; further testing is individualized, and high-risk features buy a hospital bed.
