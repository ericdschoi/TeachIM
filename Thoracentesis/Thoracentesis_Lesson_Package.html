Learning Objectives

  1. Describe the appropriate indications and contraindications to performing a thoracentesis.
  2. Achieve a minimum passing score (MPS) on the validated clinical assessment checklist in a simulation setting.
  3. Perform a thoracentesis under full or partial supervision in the clinical setting until demonstrating the Unsupervised Practice Standard (UPS).

Framework: Learn → See → Practice → Prove → Do → Maintain (Sawyer et al., Acad Med 2015). The error-counting checklist cutoffs were set by an Angoff process among 25 procedural experts.

Pre-work NEW — send the day before with the procedure video + quiz

Watch the procedural video, skim the instructional guide and clinical checklist, and answer these without looking anything up. (The full 10-item quiz with answer key is included as a file above.)

Q1. What is the minimum safe fluid depth on ultrasound, and where should you insert relative to the rib, spine, and diaphragm? ≥2 cm of fluid (parietal-to-visceral pleura) in all dimensions. Insert over the upper margin of the rib (the neurovascular bundle runs under the rib's lower margin), as laterally as possible (>9–10 cm from the spine, where intercostal vessels are least tortuous), and at least one interspace above the diaphragm. Use the low-frequency transducer to map the pocket and the high-frequency linear probe with color Doppler to screen for an aberrant subcostal vessel.
Q2. Which is an absolute contraindication, and which findings are "consider increased risk" cautions? Absolute: overlying skin infection (and <2 cm fluid depth, or a multiloculated effusion needing a chest tube — diagnostic tap may still be indicated). Cautions (not absolute): full anticoagulation / INR >2 / platelets <50K (bleeding); mechanical ventilation (pneumothorax); signs of lung entrapment (re-expansion pulmonary edema). Notably, abnormal INR or platelet count alone has not been associated with more bleeding after ultrasound-guided thoracentesis (Patel, AJR 2011).
Q3. When do you stop draining fluid? Stop for chest pain, dyspnea, or severe coughing; for excessive resistance / pleural pressure more negative than about −20 cm H₂O (manually or by manometry); or when flow stops. Drain to symptoms and pleural pressure, not to a fixed volume (see the 2026 context pearl).

Simulation Teaching Script Restructured into Learn–See–Practice–Prove with timing & engagement cues

Run the simulation as a true clinical encounter with full sterile technique. Keep the Clinical Assessment Tool visible during teaching, then remove it for the graded run. The cursor icons below mark built-in ASK prompts — pose the question before you demonstrate. Target a 60–90 min session depending on experience.

Hook & framing (2 min)

"A 65-year-old with a large left effusion and worsening dyspnea needs a tap tonight. Your intern asks: how much fluid is safe to take off — a liter? a liter and a half? until it's dry?" Hold the answer; you'll return to it. Establish that the highest-yield safety levers are ultrasound site selection and knowing when to stop, not needle dexterity.

LEARN — Indications & contraindications 8 min

ASK: "Diagnostic vs therapeutic — sort these: undiagnosed effusion, large effusion with hypoxemia, an effusion at risk of becoming a complicated parapneumonic, an asymptomatic reaccumulated malignant effusion."
DiagnosticTherapeutic
Pleural effusion of unknown etiologyLarge effusion with dyspnea/hypoxemia; effusion at risk of complications if not drained (e.g., parapneumonic)
ASK: "Which contraindication is absolute, and which are 'increased-risk' cautions you weigh against urgency?"
Absolute / hard stopsIncreased-risk cautions (weigh & mitigate)
Overlying skin infection · <2 cm fluid in any dimension · multiloculated effusion needing a chest tube (diagnostic tap may still be reasonable)Full anticoagulation, INR >2, platelets <50K (bleeding) · mechanical ventilation (pneumothorax) · suspected lung entrapment (re-expansion edema)
Pearl — coags rarely block the tap: Ultrasound-guided thoracentesis is low-risk for bleeding, and abnormal INR/platelets alone have not been linked to more bleeding complications (Patel, AJR 2011; Ault, Thorax 2015). Correct only when clearly excessive, and never delay a needed diagnostic tap in sepsis to chase a mildly abnormal INR.

LEARN — Positioning & ultrasound site selection 12 min

Position for the largest accessible pocket: usually upright leaning forward over a table with a pillow; lateral decubitus or semi-recumbent if the patient can't sit up.

ASK: "Walk me through the scan. Which probe, what depth, and what are the four landmarks you must identify before you mark?"
  • Low-frequency curvilinear/phased-array probe, indicator cranial, depth >8 cm: scan mid-scapular to mid-axillary. Identify chest wall, parietal pleura, lung, diaphragm, and liver/spleen.
  • Choose the largest hypoechoic pocket ≥2 cm deep, >9–10 cm lateral to the spine (intercostal vessels are most tortuous medially), and ≥1 interspace above the diaphragm.
  • High-frequency linear probe + color/power Doppler: confirm a supracostal approach (insert over the upper margin of the rib) and screen for an aberrant subcostal vessel; measure skin-to-parietal-pleura depth.
  • Mark the site over the upper margin of the rib with a pen or skin indentation.
Safety anchor: The neurovascular bundle runs along the lower margin of each rib — always go over the top of the rib below the fluid. Don't mark and walk away — mark and proceed; patient position shifts move the pocket.

SEE — Sterile prep, anesthesia & catheter insertion 14 min — instructor demonstrates each step

  1. Time-out with the nurse (name, DOB, procedure, site, allergies) before opening sterile supplies; include the patient.
  2. Sterile prep: chlorhexidine scrub with friction ×30 s over an area larger than the drape aperture (chlorhexidine is scrubbed, unlike iodine which is applied and left to dry); apply the fenestrated drape.
  3. Anesthetize: dermal wheal with a 25 g needle, then advance a longer 21–22 g needle under continuous negative pressure, anesthetizing the tract until pleural fluid is aspirated; withdraw slightly and deposit ~2 cc just superficial to the pleura.
  4. Nick the skin with an #11 scalpel to ~½ the blade width.
  5. Insert catheter-over-needle: advance firmly through the nick just above the rib (if you hit rib, lift over it — don't pivot), applying negative pressure with the syringe hand and guiding with the other. When fluid returns, advance another 0.5–1 cm so the catheter (not just the needle) is in the pleural space, then anchor the needle hand and advance the catheter to the hub; remove the needle.
Pearl — catheter, not needle, in the space: Pneumothorax most often happens when the operator drains through a needle still in the chest. Once fluid returns, advance the soft catheter and pull the needle — and select the largest pocket you can, since a bigger fluid window reduces pneumothorax risk.

SEE — Drainage, sampling & closure 8 min

  • Drainage: connect IV tubing with two one-way valves; 60 cc syringe to the short arm, collection bag to the long arm. Pull to fill, push to empty; if no one-way valves, toggle the stopcock between pull (open to port) and push (closed). Stop for excess resistance / pressure < −20 cm H₂O, or for chest pain, dyspnea, or severe coughing.
  • Sampling (per clinical concern): all → cell count + differential, LDH, protein (≥10 cc); microbiology → Gram stain + reflex culture (≥20 cc); cytology → ≥30–60 cc; chylothorax → cholesterol + triglycerides; infection → consider pH (on ice, in a blood-gas syringe). Apply Light's criteria with paired serum LDH/protein.
  • Catheter extraction: turn the stopcock toward the patient, briskly remove the catheter, hold gauze pressure >1 min, then dress. If any concern for pneumothorax, rule it out with bedside lung ultrasound (anterior chest, supine) or CXR.
ASK: "pH 7.15, glucose 10, and Gram-positive cocci in the fluid of a parapneumonic effusion — antibiotics alone or something more?"

That's a complicated parapneumonic effusion / early empyema (pH <7.2, low glucose, positive Gram stain) — it needs drainage with a chest tube, not antibiotics alone. This is the single most important "fluid result changes management" teaching point.

PRACTICE → PROVE — Mastery learning remaining time

The learner rehearses each step, then signals readiness for a graded run. Quiz the non-technical steps with the checklist out of view, then run the full simulation with it visible, marking each mistake or required prompt. Repeat until the learner reaches the minimum passing standard.

Close (2 min)

Hide the guide. Ask the learner to rebuild: the minimum safe fluid depth, the three rules of site selection (above the rib, lateral to the spine, above the diaphragm), three reasons to stop draining, and which pleural-fluid pattern mandates a chest tube.

Case Vignettes From the quiz — answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Effusion after lung-cancer surgery

60-year-old with stage I NSCLC s/p local excision + mediastinal node dissection 1 month ago presents with dyspnea and cough. POCUS shows a moderate right effusion. Afebrile, HR 90, RR 22, SpO₂ 90% RA. A diagnostic/therapeutic thoracentesis is planned.

Which fluid studies are most appropriate?Cell count + differential, Gram stain + culture, LDH, total protein — plus triglycerides and cytology. The cancer history raises concern for malignant effusion (cytology), and mediastinal node dissection can injure the thoracic duct → chylothorax (triglycerides/cholesterol). Don't drain "to relieve symptoms" without sending diagnostics — the result changes management.

Case 2 — Persistent fever despite antibiotics

50-year-old admitted with sepsis from suspected CAP. After 2 days of broad-spectrum IV antibiotics the fever persists; repeat CXR shows a moderate effusion. Ultrasound-guided thoracentesis yields clear yellow fluid: total protein 6, LDH 1100, pH 7.15, glucose 10, Gram stain GPCs in pairs/chains.

Next best step?Continue antibiotics and place a chest tube for drainage. pH <7.2, low glucose, and a positive Gram stain define a complicated parapneumonic effusion / empyema, which will not resolve on antibiotics alone — it requires source control with tube drainage (BTS 2023). Monitoring, repeat imaging, or chasing other fever sources all delay definitive therapy.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — 5 items, ABIM single-best-answer

1. While selecting a thoracentesis site you want to avoid the intercostal neurovascular bundle and minimize bleeding. Which approach is best?

  1. Insert just below the rib at a medial (paraspinal) site
  2. Insert over the upper margin of the rib, as lateral as safely possible, after Doppler screening
  3. Choose the smallest accessible fluid pocket to limit lung injury
  4. Transfuse platelets to >100K before any tap
Answer & explanationB. The neurovascular bundle runs along the lower rib margin, so insert over the top of the rib; intercostal vessels are most tortuous medially, so go lateral (>9–10 cm from spine); Doppler screens for an aberrant subcostal vessel (A is exactly backwards). A small pocket (C) raises pneumothorax risk. Routine platelet transfusion (D) isn't required — abnormal coags alone don't increase bleeding after US-guided taps (Patel 2011).

2. What is the minimum safe fluid depth (parietal-to-visceral pleura) for a thoracentesis?

  1. 1 cm
  2. 2 cm
  3. 3 cm
  4. 5 cm
Answer & explanationB. A pocket ≥2 cm in all dimensions is the standard minimum safe depth; less than that markedly raises the risk of hitting lung. Larger pockets are safer but 2 cm is the accepted threshold.

3. A patient becomes dyspneic and coughs forcefully after ~1.2 L is removed during a therapeutic tap; you also feel increasing resistance on aspiration. What is the most appropriate action?

  1. Switch to wall suction to finish quickly
  2. Stop draining now
  3. Continue to a target of exactly 1.5 L
  4. Push the fluid back to relieve symptoms
Answer & explanationB. Stop for chest pain, dyspnea, severe coughing, or excessive resistance / pleural pressure < −20 cm H₂O — these signal lung non-expansion and risk of re-expansion edema. Drain to symptoms and pressure, not to a fixed volume; the traditional 1.5 L "limit" (C) is arbitrary and not evidence-based. Wall suction (A) does not make continued drainage safe here.

4. Which pleural-fluid pattern from a parapneumonic effusion most clearly mandates chest-tube drainage rather than antibiotics alone?

  1. pH 7.40, glucose 90, LDH 150, negative Gram stain
  2. pH 7.15, glucose 10, positive Gram stain
  3. Lymphocyte-predominant exudate, ADA low
  4. Transudate by Light's criteria
Answer & explanationB. pH <7.2, low glucose, and/or a positive Gram stain or frank pus define a complicated parapneumonic effusion/empyema needing drainage (BTS 2023). Option A is an uncomplicated parapneumonic effusion (antibiotics, observe). A lymphocytic exudate with low ADA (C) points elsewhere (e.g., malignancy), and a transudate (D) is managed by treating the underlying cause.

5. A 72-year-old on apixaban with an undiagnosed unilateral exudative effusion needs a diagnostic thoracentesis to evaluate for malignancy. INR 1.3, platelets 120K. What is the most appropriate approach?

  1. Proceed with ultrasound guidance; current evidence does not show increased bleeding from these parameters
  2. Refuse until apixaban is reversed with andexanet alfa
  3. Transfuse platelets to >150K first
  4. Cancel — anticoagulation is an absolute contraindication
Answer & explanationA. US-guided thoracentesis is low-risk; INR 1.3 and platelets 120K are well within safe ranges, and abnormal coags alone haven't increased bleeding in large series (Patel 2011; Ault 2015). For a non-urgent elective tap, holding a DOAC dose per local protocol is reasonable, but reversal (B), unnecessary transfusion (C), and cancellation (D) are not — anticoagulation is a relative caution, not an absolute contraindication.

Take-Home Points

  1. Ultrasound site selection is the core safety skill: ≥2 cm pocket, over the upper rib margin, >9–10 cm lateral to the spine, above the diaphragm; Doppler to dodge an aberrant subcostal vessel.
  2. The only true hard stops are overlying skin infection, <2 cm fluid, and an isolated multiloculated effusion; anticoagulation/INR/platelets are cautions, not absolutes — abnormal coags alone don't increase bleeding.
  3. Get the catheter, not just the needle, into the pleural space to avoid pneumothorax.
  4. Drain to symptoms and pleural pressure (stop at CP/SOB/cough, resistance, or < −20 cm H₂O) — not to an arbitrary 1.5 L limit.
  5. Send fluid for the studies your differential demands; a pH <7.2 / low glucose / positive Gram stain parapneumonic effusion needs a chest tube, not antibiotics alone.
  6. Prove competence by mastery learning: reach the MPS (<24 mistakes) in simulation, then the UPS (<7) under graded observation before unsupervised practice.
