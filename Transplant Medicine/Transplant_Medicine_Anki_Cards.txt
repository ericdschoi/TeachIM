What is the central trade-off in managing immunosuppression after solid organ transplant?	More immunosuppression lowers rejection risk but raises the risk of infection, malignancy, and drug toxicity — and vice versa.
Rank lung, liver, and heart/kidney transplants by the degree of immunosuppression required (allogenicity).	Lung > heart/kidney > liver. Lung transplants are most likely to reject (~50% at 5 yr); liver is least likely.
What is the difference between induction and maintenance immunosuppression?	Induction = intense, short-term immunosuppression given at the time of transplant (anti-lymphocyte/anti–IL-2 antibodies + high-dose steroids). Maintenance = lower-intensity long-term therapy, typically CNI + antimetabolite ± prednisone.
Name the three core maintenance immunosuppressant classes and an example of each.	Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine); antimetabolites (mycophenolate, azathioprine); steroids (prednisone). mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus) are an additional class.
How does allogenicity (rejection risk) change over time after transplant?	It is highest immediately/within the first month and decreases over time, allowing immunosuppression to be reduced accordingly.
When is a FIRST episode of acute rejection highly unlikely?	Beyond 6 months from transplant, assuming good medication adherence.
What are the two main types of acute rejection and how is each diagnosed?	Acute cellular (T-cell mediated; biopsy shows lymphocytic/inflammatory infiltrate) and acute humoral/antibody-mediated (biopsy shows C4d deposition, or detection of donor-specific antibodies).
What is the first-line treatment of acute cellular rejection?	Pulse-dose methylprednisolone (e.g., 1 g IV × 3 days), ± anti-lymphocyte/anti–IL-2 antibody for severe or steroid-refractory cases.
What infections predominate in the FIRST month after transplant?	Nosocomial infections (surgical-site, line, catheter, C. diff) and donor- or recipient-derived infections; opportunistic infections are uncommon this early.
What infections predominate 1–6 months after transplant?	Reactivation and opportunistic infections — CMV, BK, HSV/VZV, TB, HBV, plus PJP, Cryptococcus, Toxoplasma. Incidence is reduced by prophylaxis.
What infections predominate beyond 6 months after transplant?	Community-acquired infections (and late viral infections such as HSV, HBV, HCV, CMV); opportunistic infections become less common with reduced immunosuppression.
How much higher is malignancy risk in transplant recipients, and which cancers predominate?	About 2× the general population, especially virally mediated cancers — lymphoma/PTLD (EBV), skin and anogenital cancers (HPV), liver cancer.
What is PTLD and when does it typically occur?	Post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder — an EBV-driven lymphoid proliferation. It occurs early (typically the first year); EBV positivity and degree of immunosuppression are key risk factors.
What are the major categories of post-transplant complications?	Rejection, medication side effects, infection, malignancy, and surgical complications (leaks, kinks, fistulas, stenoses).
What are the key toxicities of calcineurin inhibitors?	Nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, and metabolic syndrome (hypertension, hyperlipidemia, hyperglycemia).
What is belatacept and a key contraindication?	A CD28/CD80-86 costimulation blocker used as CNI-sparing maintenance immunosuppression (FDA 2011); associated with better long-term GFR and fewer donor-specific antibodies. CONTRAINDICATED in EBV-seronegative recipients due to PTLD/PML risk.
Which CMV prophylaxis agent was FDA-approved in 2023 for high-risk kidney transplant, and why use it?	Letermovir (Prevymis), for high-risk (donor-positive/recipient-negative) kidney transplant recipients — an alternative to valganciclovir with less myelosuppression. Maribavir (2021) is used for refractory/resistant CMV.
What is the status of OKT3 (muromonab-CD3)?	Discontinued in 2010. Current T-cell–depleting induction agents are rabbit anti-thymocyte globulin (rATG) and alemtuzumab; basiliximab (anti–IL-2 receptor) is the non-depleting option.
