Learning Objectives

  1. Describe the constant balance of weighing the value of immunosuppression against the risks of rejection, infection, malignancy, and drug toxicity.
  2. Identify the common induction and maintenance immunosuppression regimens and how they change over time after solid organ transplant.
  3. Build a framework for the common post-transplant complications organized by time from transplant.

Pre-Work send the day before

Three retrieval questions to prime the session.

1. Which solid organ transplant is most likely to reject, and which is least? Lung is most likely (~50% experience rejection within 5 years); liver is least likely. Rejection risk (allogenicity) drives how much maintenance immunosuppression a patient needs long-term: lung > heart/kidney > liver.
2. What are the three core classes of maintenance immunosuppression? Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine), antimetabolites (mycophenolate, azathioprine), and steroids (prednisone). mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus) are an additional class; belatacept is a costimulation-blocker alternative.
3. A transplant patient develops a reactivation infection such as CMV. How many months out from transplant is this most likely? 1–6 months. Nosocomial/donor-derived infections dominate <1 month; reactivation and opportunistic infections (CMV, BK, PJP) cluster at 1–6 months; community-acquired infections dominate >6 months.

Teaching Script

Open the presenter board and reveal each animated element only after the group commits to an answer. The board has three slides: (1) the balance + allogenicity, (2) induction vs. maintenance medications, (3) the complications-by-time map.

Hook (2 min)

"Your patient is 4 months out from a kidney transplant and now has a fever and rising creatinine. Before you order a single test — what are the two competing diagnoses you're most worried about, and why does the timing matter?" (Answer surfaces by the end: infection vs. rejection, and 1–6 months is the reactivation/opportunistic window when both are plausible.)

Objective 1 — The Balance & Allogenicity (10 min)
ASK: "What is the price we pay for suppressing rejection?" Reveal the oversuppression (infection, cancer) and undersuppression (rejection) buttons.

Frame all of transplant immunology as a single dial: turn it up and rejection falls but infection, malignancy, and drug toxicity rise; turn it down and the reverse happens.

ASK: "Which transplant is most likely to reject? Least likely?" Then reveal the allogenicity order.

Allogenicity (rejection risk): lung > heart/kidney > liver. Lung transplants reject ~50% within 5 years and stay on all three drug classes for life; liver may be maintained on a calcineurin inhibitor alone.

Pearl: Allogenicity is highest immediately after transplant and falls over time. This single fact explains both the drug timeline (Objective 2) and the complication timeline (Objective 3).
Objective 2 — Induction vs. Maintenance (12 min)
ASK: "Why would a regimen on day 1 look completely different from the regimen at year 1?" Reveal the induction and maintenance buttons.
PhaseTimingAgentsRationale
InductionAt transplantAnti-lymphocyte antibody (rabbit ATG, alemtuzumab) or anti–IL-2 receptor antibody (basiliximab) + high-dose steroidsAllogenicity is at its peak; profound, brief suppression to get the graft through the highest-risk window
MaintenanceLong-termCNI + antimetabolite ± prednisoneLower-intensity therapy, de-escalated as rejection risk falls

Maintenance classes (know an example of each): CNI = tacrolimus, cyclosporine; antimetabolite = mycophenolate, azathioprine; steroid = prednisone. Additional: mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus).

Pearl (newer context): Belatacept (CD28 costimulation blocker, FDA 2011) is a CNI-sparing maintenance option with better long-term GFR and fewer donor-specific antibodies — but is contraindicated in EBV-seronegative recipients (PTLD/PML risk). OKT3/muromonab was discontinued in 2010; the current depleting induction agents are rabbit ATG and alemtuzumab.
Objective 3 — Complications by Time from Transplant (15 min)
ASK: "Name the five buckets of post-transplant complications." Reveal each on the board.

Categories: rejection, medication effects, infection, malignancy, surgical. The most teachable axis is time.

TimeInfectionRejection / Malignancy / Surgical
<1 monthNosocomial (surgical site, line, catheter, C. diff); donor- or recipient-derivedSurgical complications (leaks, kinks, fistulas, stenoses); hyperacute/early acute rejection
1–6 monthsReactivation & opportunistic: CMV, BK, HSV/VZV, TB, HBV; PJP, Cryptococcus, Toxoplasma (reduced by prophylaxis)Peak acute rejection window; PTLD (EBV) may begin
>6 monthsCommunity-acquired » opportunistic; late viral (HSV, HBV, HCV, CMV)Chronic rejection; late solid-organ malignancies
ASK: "If a well-adherent patient has a first episode of acute rejection at 14 months, what should that prompt?" (Reconsider the diagnosis or check adherence/drug levels — a first acute rejection beyond 6 months is highly unlikely.)

Rejection types: acute cellular (T-cell; biopsy lymphocytic infiltrate; treat with pulse methylprednisolone 1 g ×3 d ± anti-lymphocyte antibody) and acute humoral (antibody-mediated; biopsy C4d or donor-specific antibodies; treat with increased immunosuppression, IVIg, plasmapheresis, rituximab, bortezomib, eculizumab). Chronic rejection: lung = bronchiolitis obliterans; kidney = chronic allograft dysfunction.

Pearl: Malignancy risk is ~2× the general population, skewed toward virally driven cancers. PTLD (EBV) is the exception that occurs early (often first year); most others appear late.
Retrieval Close (3 min)

Close the boards and have learners rebuild the three take-home points from memory, then the complication timeline (<1 / 1–6 / >6 months).

Cases (apply the framework)

The original board is framework-based; these short application cases reinforce it. Reveal answers only after discussion.

Case 1 — Kidney transplant, 3 months out: fever, malaise, leukopenia, rising creatinine. Timing (1–6 months) puts CMV reactivation at the top of the infectious differential — but acute rejection must be excluded in parallel (it shares graft dysfunction and malaise). Check CMV PCR and a calcineurin-inhibitor trough; transplant nephrology will often biopsy. This is exactly the early-fever pitfall: infection AND rejection, not one or the other.
Case 2 — Lung transplant, 3 years out: progressive dyspnea, dry cough, obstructive PFTs with falling FEV1, normal CXR. Chronic rejection = bronchiolitis obliterans syndrome. Bronchoscopy to rule out stenosis/infection; biopsy shows small-airway fibrous scarring. Optimize immunosuppression; azithromycin 3×/week ± montelukast/sirolimus/photopheresis. Lung's high allogenicity is why these patients stay maximally immunosuppressed.
Case 3 — Heart transplant, 8 months out: a new, rapidly enlarging lymph node; EBV viremia. PTLD — EBV-driven, occurs early (typically first year), risk tied to EBV status and immunosuppression intensity. First step in management is reduction of immunosuppression; tissue diagnosis guides further therapy (rituximab-based).

Board-Style MCQs

1. A 52-year-old man is 2 weeks status post deceased-donor kidney transplant. He develops fever and a productive cough. Which infection category is MOST likely at this time point?
  1. CMV reactivation
  2. Nosocomial/donor-derived infection
  3. Community-acquired pneumonia
  4. PTLD
  5. Cryptococcal meningitis
Answer & explanationB. In the first month, nosocomial (surgical-site, line, catheter, hospital-acquired pneumonia, C. diff) and donor/recipient-derived infections dominate. A & E (reactivation/opportunistic) cluster at 1–6 months. C dominates >6 months. D (PTLD) is an early malignancy but presents as lymphoproliferation, not acute febrile pneumonia.
2. A patient is being counseled before kidney transplant about a CNI-sparing maintenance regimen with belatacept. Which finding is an absolute contraindication?
  1. CMV-seropositive donor
  2. EBV-seronegative recipient
  3. Prior cyclosporine intolerance
  4. Body mass index > 30
  5. History of well-controlled hypertension
Answer & explanationB. Belatacept is contraindicated in EBV-seronegative recipients because of markedly increased risk of PTLD (including CNS) and PML. A, C, D, E are not contraindications; indeed a CNI-sparing strategy may be chosen because of CNI intolerance (C).
3. A well-adherent liver transplant recipient with therapeutic tacrolimus levels presents with a first episode of biopsy-proven acute cellular rejection 18 months post-transplant. What does the timing most appropriately prompt?
  1. This is expected and requires no change
  2. Re-examination of the diagnosis and adherence/drug levels, since a first acute rejection beyond 6 months is unusual
  3. Immediate listing for re-transplant
  4. Permanent discontinuation of all immunosuppression
  5. Switch to induction-dose anti-thymocyte globulin as maintenance
Answer & explanationB. Assuming adherence, a first acute rejection beyond 6 months is highly unlikely, so confirm the diagnosis and verify adherence/levels. Treatment of true acute cellular rejection is pulse steroids ± anti-lymphocyte antibody (not maintenance ATG, E). C and D are inappropriate.
4. Which maintenance immunosuppressant class is most associated with nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, hyperkalemia, and hyperuricemia?
  1. Antimetabolites
  2. Calcineurin inhibitors
  3. Corticosteroids
  4. Anti–IL-2 receptor antibodies
  5. mTOR inhibitors
Answer & explanationB. Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine) cause nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, and metabolic syndrome. Antimetabolites (A) → marrow suppression and GI/colitis; steroids (C) → metabolic syndrome; D is an induction agent; mTOR inhibitors (E) cause proteinuria, poor wound healing, and cytopenias rather than this cluster.
5. A CMV-seronegative recipient receives a kidney from a CMV-seropositive donor (D+/R−). Which agent was FDA-approved in 2023 specifically for CMV prophylaxis in high-risk kidney transplant recipients, offering less myelosuppression than the standard?
  1. Valganciclovir
  2. Letermovir
  3. Acyclovir
  4. Maribavir
  5. Foscarnet
Answer & explanationB. Letermovir (Prevymis) gained FDA approval in June 2023 for prophylaxis in high-risk (D+/R−) kidney transplant recipients, with less myelosuppression than valganciclovir (A, the long-standing standard). Maribavir (D, FDA 2021) is for refractory/resistant CMV, not first-line prophylaxis. Acyclovir (C) is inadequate for CMV; foscarnet (E) is reserved for resistant disease and is nephrotoxic.

Take-Home Points

  1. Immunosuppression is a constant balance: lowering rejection risk costs you infection, malignancy, and drug toxicity.
  2. Allogenicity (rejection risk) is highest in the first month and falls over time; so does immunosuppression intensity. Lung > heart/kidney > liver.
  3. Assuming adherence, a first episode of acute rejection beyond 6 months is highly unlikely.
  4. Reactivation infections (CMV, BK, HSV/VZV, TB, HBV) cluster at 1–6 months; nosocomial/donor-derived <1 month; community-acquired >6 months.
  5. PTLD (EBV) occurs early; most other malignancies appear late. Overall cancer risk ~2× the general population.
