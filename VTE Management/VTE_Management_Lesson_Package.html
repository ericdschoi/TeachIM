Learning Objectives

  1. IF — determine whether a VTE warrants anticoagulation vs. further evaluation/surveillance.
  2. WHERE — decide whether to initiate anticoagulation inpatient or outpatient.
  3. WHICH — select the anticoagulant agent.
  4. HOW LONG — build a framework for the duration of anticoagulation.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. Does every venous clot get anticoagulated? No. An isolated distal (below-knee) DVT that is asymptomatic/low-risk can be surveilled with a repeat duplex in 1–2 weeks (treat only if it extends), and an isolated subsegmental PE without a proximal DVT in a low-risk patient may be surveilled. Proximal DVT and main/segmental PE are anticoagulated.
Q2. What score decides whether a PE patient can go home? The PESI (or simplified sPESI). An uncomplicated PE with PESI <85 (class I–II) or sPESI 0, without high bleeding risk, hemodynamic compromise, or social barriers, can be managed as an outpatient.
Q3. First-line agent for most stable VTE, including cancer-associated VTE? A DOAC. DOACs are first-line for stable VTE and are now preferred over LMWH for most cancer-associated VTE; LMWH or apixaban is favored for luminal GI cancers, and warfarin is used for antiphospholipid syndrome and severe renal impairment.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: draw the "Board Set-up" (the venous tree + the IF/WHERE/WHICH/HOW-LONG columns) on a whiteboard and fill it in as you go — or project VTE_Management_Optimized.pptx. Split learners into four groups, each working one case from the handout. Each of the four segments stands alone. Print the Teacher's Guide for yourself and the Learner Handout for the groups. Core 40–50 min.

Hook (2 min)

"Four patients, four clots — a calf DVT, a subsegmental PE, a big saddle PE, and a cancer patient with a proximal DVT. Not all four get the same treatment, and one might get none. Today we answer four questions for any clot: IF, WHERE, WHICH, HOW LONG."

Objective 1 — IF: should this clot be treated? 12 min

ASK: "Map the clot onto the venous tree — which locations are 'definitely treat' vs. 'potentially treat'?" Reveal: main/segmental PE and proximal LE DVT (popliteal and above) → definitely anticoagulate.
ScenarioDecision
Proximal DVT (popliteal/femoral/iliac), main or segmental PEAnticoagulate
Isolated distal (below-knee) DVTIf severe symptoms or high risk → treat; otherwise surveil with repeat duplex in 1–2 weeks, treat only if it extends
Isolated subsegmental PE, no proximal DVT, low recurrence riskLE duplex; surveillance is an option over anticoagulation (CHEST 2021)
Pearl: The decision to treat is about the clot's threat (location, burden) balanced against bleeding risk — not the mere presence of a clot. "Not everything that clots needs a blood thinner."

Objective 2 — WHERE: inpatient vs. outpatient? 8 min

ASK: "Your PE patient is stable — who still needs admission?" Reveal: risk-stratify with PESI. PESI <85 (class I–II) → consider outpatient; otherwise inpatient. Override to inpatient for high bleeding risk, unreliable disposition, or poor compliance (also hemodynamic instability/RV strain).
Pearl: Outpatient PE management is safe in carefully selected low-risk patients (HESTIA/PESI-based pathways) and avoids unnecessary admissions — but the social/logistic screen (reliable follow-up, ability to get and afford the drug) is as important as the score.

Objective 3 — WHICH: agent selection 10 min

AgentBridge?Role
LMWHNoBridge / pregnancy; option for luminal GI cancer; poor oral absorption
DOACNo (apixaban/rivaroxaban) — dabigatran & edoxaban need ~5 d lead-in heparinFirst-line for stable VTE, including most cancer VTE
WarfarinYes (overlap with heparin until INR therapeutic)Antiphospholipid syndrome, mechanical valves, CrCl <25–30, cost/access
2020–25 update: For cancer-associated VTE, DOACs (apixaban, edoxaban, rivaroxaban) are now first-line over LMWH (CARAVAGGIO 2020; ASH 2021/CHEST 2021) — but apixaban or LMWH is preferred for luminal GI malignancies because rivaroxaban/edoxaban raised GI bleeding. Avoid DOACs in antiphospholipid syndrome (use warfarin — TRAPS).

Objective 4 — HOW LONG: duration 10 min

ASK: "Plot thrombosis risk over time for four patients — who stops at 3 months and who goes indefinite?" Reveal the framework:
ScenarioDuration
Provoked by a reversible/transient cause (surgery, immobilization, estrogen)~3 months, then stop
Unprovoked but high bleeding risk~3 months, then reassess (risk/benefit may favor stopping)
Unprovoked, not high bleeding riskIndefinite anticoagulation
Provoked by an irreversible/persistent cause (active cancer, antiphospholipid syndrome)Indefinite (as long as the cause persists)
2020–25 update: For the extended phase (after the first 3–6 months), a reduced-dose DOAC — apixaban 2.5 mg BID (AMPLIFY-EXT) or rivaroxaban 10 mg daily (EINSTEIN-CHOICE) — maintains protection with less bleeding.

Close (3 min)

Hide the board. Have each group state their case's answer to all four questions (IF/WHERE/WHICH/HOW LONG) in one sentence, then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases from the Learner Handout — one per small group; answers click-to-reveal

The four original cases each anchor one decision (IF / WHERE / WHICH / HOW LONG). Representative versions below; use the handout for the full vignettes.

Case A — IF: isolated distal DVT

A patient has an isolated below-knee (calf/muscular vein) DVT found on duplex, minimal symptoms, no major risk factors, low bleeding risk.

Treat now or not?For a low-risk, minimally symptomatic isolated distal DVT, surveillance with a repeat duplex in 1–2 weeks is appropriate — anticoagulate if it extends toward the popliteal vein. Treat up front if severely symptomatic or high-risk for extension.

Case B — WHERE: segmental PE, stable

A hemodynamically stable patient with a segmental PE, normal vitals, no RV strain, PESI calculated at 70.

Inpatient or outpatient?PESI <85 (class I–II) without high bleeding risk or social barriers → outpatient management on a DOAC is reasonable, with close follow-up. Admit if PESI ≥85, bleeding risk, instability, or unreliable disposition.

Case C — WHICH: cancer-associated proximal DVT

A patient with active malignancy develops a proximal lower-extremity DVT.

Which agent?A DOAC (apixaban, edoxaban, or rivaroxaban) is now first-line over LMWH for cancer-associated VTE (CARAVAGGIO; ASH/CHEST). If the cancer is a luminal GI malignancy, prefer apixaban or LMWH because of GI bleeding risk with rivaroxaban/edoxaban.

Case D — HOW LONG: unprovoked proximal DVT

A patient with a first unprovoked proximal DVT, no identifiable transient trigger, average bleeding risk.

Duration?Indefinite anticoagulation (unprovoked, not high bleed risk), reassessing bleeding risk periodically. A reduced-dose DOAC is an option for the extended phase to lower bleeding.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 62-year-old woman with metastatic colon cancer develops a proximal femoral DVT. CrCl is normal; no active bleeding. Which is the most appropriate anticoagulant?

  1. Rivaroxaban
  2. Apixaban or LMWH
  3. Warfarin bridged with heparin
  4. Aspirin
Answer & explanationB. DOACs are first-line for cancer-associated VTE, but for a luminal GI malignancy (colon), apixaban or LMWH is preferred because rivaroxaban (A) and edoxaban carry higher GI bleeding in this group. Warfarin (C) is inferior to DOAC/LMWH for cancer VTE. Aspirin (D) is inadequate for acute VTE.

2. A 48-year-old man has a stable segmental PE, normal vitals, no RV dysfunction, PESI 65, reliable follow-up. What is the best disposition?

  1. Admit to the ICU for thrombolysis
  2. Admit to telemetry for 72 hours
  3. Outpatient anticoagulation with a DOAC and close follow-up
  4. IVC filter placement
Answer & explanationC. A PESI <85 (class I–II) uncomplicated PE without bleeding risk or social barriers can be managed as an outpatient on a DOAC. Thrombolysis (A) is for hemodynamically unstable (massive) PE. Routine admission (B) is unnecessary for low-risk PE. An IVC filter (D) is reserved for when anticoagulation is contraindicated.

3. A 30-year-old woman with triple-positive antiphospholipid syndrome has a recurrent unprovoked DVT. Which anticoagulation strategy is preferred long-term?

  1. Rivaroxaban
  2. Apixaban
  3. Warfarin (target INR 2–3)
  4. Low-dose aspirin
Answer & explanationC. In antiphospholipid syndrome, especially triple-positive disease, warfarin is preferred over DOACs — the TRAPS trial showed excess arterial thrombotic events with rivaroxaban. DOACs (A, B) are therefore avoided; aspirin alone (D) is inadequate for established VTE in APS.

4. A patient with a first unprovoked proximal DVT has completed 6 months of full-dose apixaban with no bleeding and average bleeding risk. What is the best next step?

  1. Stop anticoagulation entirely
  2. Continue extended anticoagulation, e.g., apixaban 2.5 mg twice daily
  3. Switch to warfarin indefinitely
  4. Place an IVC filter and stop the DOAC
Answer & explanationB. An unprovoked VTE without high bleeding risk warrants indefinite anticoagulation; for the extended phase a reduced-dose DOAC (apixaban 2.5 mg BID per AMPLIFY-EXT, or rivaroxaban 10 mg daily per EINSTEIN-CHOICE) maintains protection with less bleeding. Stopping (A) leaves high recurrence risk; warfarin (C) adds monitoring burden without benefit; an IVC filter (D) is not a substitute for anticoagulation here.

5. A healthy 55-year-old has an isolated subsegmental PE found incidentally on a staging CT; bilateral leg duplex shows no DVT and he has low recurrence risk. What is a guideline-supported option?

  1. Lifelong anticoagulation
  2. Clinical surveillance without anticoagulation
  3. Thrombolysis
  4. IVC filter
Answer & explanationB. For isolated subsegmental PE without a proximal DVT and with low recurrence risk, CHEST 2021 supports clinical surveillance over anticoagulation. Lifelong anticoagulation (A) is not warranted for this low-risk finding; thrombolysis (C) and IVC filter (D) have no role here.

Dense assessment-and-plan template for a new VTE. * = wildcard to edit at the bedside; brackets = pick-lists. 

#VTE: * yo w/ [proximal DVT / distal DVT / segmental PE / subsegmental PE / saddle PE], [provoked by * (transient/persistent) / unprovoked]. HDS [yes/no], RV strain [yes/no], bleeding risk [low/high]. CrCl *.
- IF treat: proximal DVT or main/segmental PE -> ANTICOAGULATE. isolated distal DVT low-risk -> *duplex in 1-2 wk, tx if extends*. isolated SSPE no proximal DVT + low risk -> LE duplex, surveillance option (CHEST 2021)
- WHERE: PE -> PESI/sPESI [PESI *]. PESI <85 (class I-II) + no high bleed risk/instability/social barrier -> OUTPATIENT; else INPATIENT. unstable/massive PE -> thrombolysis + ICU
- WHICH agent:
  - DOAC first-line (stable VTE incl most cancer VTE): apixaban [10 BID x7d -> 5 BID] or rivaroxaban [15 BID x21d -> 20 daily] (no lead-in); dabigatran/edoxaban need ~5d heparin lead-in
  - cancer VTE: DOAC > LMWH (CARAVAGGIO); *luminal GI cancer -> apixaban or LMWH (GI bleed)*
  - warfarin (bridge to INR 2-3): antiphospholipid syndrome (NOT DOAC - TRAPS), mech valve, CrCl <25-30
  - LMWH: pregnancy, poor PO absorption, periprocedural bridging
- HOW LONG: provoked/reversible -> ~3 mo. unprovoked + high bleed risk -> ~3 mo then reassess. unprovoked not-high-bleed -> indefinite. irreversible cause (active cancer, APS) -> indefinite
  - extended phase: reduced-dose DOAC [apixaban 2.5 BID / rivaroxaban 10 daily] to cut bleeding
- w/u as indicated: provoking factors, age-appropriate cancer screening if unprovoked, thrombophilia eval only if it changes mgmt; baseline CBC/renal/LFTs; counsel bleeding precautions, adherence, f/u *

Take-Home Points

  1. IF: not every clot is treated — an isolated distal DVT (low-risk) can be surveilled with a repeat duplex in 1–2 weeks, and an isolated subsegmental PE without proximal DVT may be surveilled; proximal DVT and main/segmental PE are anticoagulated.
  2. WHERE: an uncomplicated PE with PESI <85 can be managed as an outpatient if there's no high bleeding risk, instability, or social barrier.
  3. WHICH: DOACs are first-line for stable VTE, including most cancer-associated VTE (apixaban/LMWH preferred for luminal GI cancers; warfarin for antiphospholipid syndrome and severe renal impairment).
  4. HOW LONG: provoked by a reversible cause → ~3 months; unprovoked or an irreversible cause without high bleeding risk → indefinite (consider a reduced-dose DOAC for the extended phase).
