Learning Objectives

  1. Outline the pathophysiology of common causes of vaginitis.
  2. Differentiate causes of vaginitis based on history, physical examination, and diagnostic testing.
  3. Discuss risk reduction and treatment options for the causes of vaginitis.

Pre-work (send the day before)

1. Normal vaginal flora is dominated by which organism, and what pH does it maintain?Lactobacillus (70–90% of flora) produces lactic acid → normal pH 3.8–4.5. Estrogen promotes lactobacilli.
2. Which of the three common infectious vaginitides does NOT raise vaginal pH?Yeast (candidal vulvovaginitis) — Candida and lactobacilli coexist, so pH stays normal (4.0–4.5). BV and trichomonas both raise pH >4.5.
3. Why can't you diagnose the cause of vaginitis from symptoms alone?Symptoms (itching, burning, discharge, odor) are nonspecific and overlap across causes. All symptomatic patients should be offered an exam with pH + microscopy (KOH/wet prep) and/or DNA assay/NAAT, and STI cervicitis (GC/chlamydia) should be excluded.

Teaching Script (timed)

0:00–2:00 — Hook (Overview board)

Open with the four cardinal symptoms — itching, burning, abnormal discharge, abnormal odor — and the key framing: vaginitis splits into infectious (BV, yeast, trichomonas) and non-infectious (GSM/atrophic, irritant, allergic, inflammatory). The talk focuses on infectious causes, but the mimic that must not be missed is STI cervicitis (gonorrhea ± chlamydia).

ASK: "A patient says she 'has a yeast infection again.' Before you treat — what's your differential, and what one category can't you afford to miss?" (All three infectious causes plus non-infectious; don't miss GC/chlamydia cervicitis.)
2:00–9:00 — Objective 1: Pathophysiology (Causes boards)

Anchor everything on lactobacilli → lactic acid → pH. Click through each cause and have learners predict the pH:

Pearl: The single highest-yield discriminator is pH — yeast is the one that keeps a normal pH. That's the fastest bedside split between yeast and "everything else."

ASK: "Wet prep is negative but the patient clearly has symptoms and risk factors for trichomonas. Now what?" (Send NAAT/PCR — wet prep is only ~50–60% sensitive for trich; a negative microscopy does not rule it out.)
Pearl — Amsel criteria for BV (3 of 4): (1) thin homogeneous grayish-white discharge coating the walls, (2) pH >4.5, (3) positive whiff test, (4) clue cells (≥20% of epithelial cells) on saline wet mount. Highly sensitive; less specific than gram stain/DNA assay.
Pearl — co-existing etiologies: BV + yeast can coexist (elevated pH with both clue cells and hyphae). Incidental flora shifts, hyphae, or trichomonads on a Pap can be treated if symptomatic, monitored if not.
15:00–20:00 — Objective 3: Management (Management board)

Both topical and oral regimens are equally effective when used as directed — choose by patient preference, allergies, and pregnancy status.

Pearl: Trichomonas is an STI — treat the partner(s) (single 2 g metronidazole for men), avoid sex until both complete treatment, and re-test for reinfection at 3 months. Topical agents do not treat trich.

Risk reduction (emphasize for recurrent symptoms): fewer partners and barrier protection, avoid douching, properly clean sex devices, smoking cessation (BV/trich); glycemic control and avoid unnecessary antibiotics (yeast).

Retrieval close: board down — have learners rebuild the pH-by-cause table, name the test that confirms each, and give the first-line drug for each.

Newer Context (2021–2025) added

The deck's diagnosis and first-line treatment match the 2021 CDC STI Treatment Guidelines, which remain current. Worth knowing:

  • Trichomoniasis in women: multi-dose metronidazole 500 mg BID ×7 d is preferred over single 2 g (more effective; Kissinger RCT, reflected in 2021 CDC) — already on the deck. Single 2 g still used for partners/men.
  • Secnidazole (Solosec) 2 g oral granules ×1 — single-dose option, FDA-approved for BV (2017) and T. vaginalis (2021); the only single-dose oral agent covering both. Useful when adherence to a 7-day course is a concern.
  • Recurrent vulvovaginal candidiasis (RVVC): two newer oral agents — ibrexafungerp (Brexafemme, first-in-class triterpenoid; VVC 2021, RVVC 2022) and oteseconazole (Vivjoa, FDA 2022, first drug approved specifically for RVVC).
  • GSM/atrophic vaginitis: vaginal estrogen, moisturizers/lubricants; non-hormonal options for those avoiding estrogen.

Cases (from the original board — answers click-to-reveal)

Case 1 — 23-year-old woman, watery malodorous discharge; combined OCPs; LMP ~5 wk ago; 1 male partner; no douching/barrier/devices

1a. What additional history helps with diagnosis?Discharge consistency/color/odor; associated symptoms (pruritus, burning, dyspareunia, abdominal pain, nausea); sexual history; menstrual history. No barrier contraception ↑ STI risk; combined OCPs ↑ physiologic discharge.
1a. What is your differential for her symptoms?Infectious vaginitis (BV, candidal, trichomonas); cervicitis (gonorrhea ± chlamydia); non-infectious vaginitis (atrophic/GSM, allergic/irritant); erosive lichen planus.
1a. What is your next step in evaluation?Symptoms of vaginitis are nonspecific — perform a pelvic exam and send pregnancy test, wet mount, pH, KOH prep, ± STI testing; consider gram stain/culture/DNA assay/NAAT if available.
1a. What are her risk factors for vaginitis?No barrier contraception (↑ STI risk) and combined OCPs (↑ physiologic discharge). She has no specific risk factors for BV (douching, smoking, same-sex/multiple partners) or candida (high-sugar diet, DM, immunosuppression, recent antibiotics).
1b/c. Pelvic exam: moderate thin white discharge, faint fishy odor; cervix normal. Wet mount shows clue cells; pH 5.0; KOH+ fishy; pregnancy test positive. Most likely diagnosis?BV. She meets Amsel criteria (3/4): thin homogeneous white discharge, >20% clue cells on wet mount, positive whiff test, vaginal pH >4.5. (Clue cells = vaginal epithelial cells with ruffled borders from adherent bacteria.)
1c. How will you treat her (she is pregnant)?Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID ×7 d, OR metronidazole 0.75% gel 5 g intravaginal daily ×5 d. Both are acceptable in pregnancy for BV.

Case 2 — 24-year-old woman, 2 wk discharge; 1 new male partner ×1 mo, intermittent condoms; recently started douching

2. Exam: erythematous cervix, thick yellow discharge; wet prep clue cells; pH >5; whiff positive; pregnancy test negative. Next step in evaluation/management?Additional STI testing plus DNA assay/PCR for trichomonas (wet prep only 50–60% sensitive). Consider empiric treatment of BV and trichomonas with metronidazole 500 mg PO BID ×7 d — note topical agents will not treat trich.
2. DNA assay (VPIII) returns positive for BV and trichomonas; GC/chlamydia and HIV negative. What additional counseling?Treat the sexual partner (metronidazole 2 g PO ×1 for men), avoid sexual contact until symptoms resolve, test for reinfection at 3 months, encourage condom/barrier use, and avoid douching/vaginal cleansers to reduce recurrence.

Case 3 — 36-year-old woman, thick white discharge + vulvar itching/irritation; 1 female partner

3. Exam: vulvar erythema + thick white discharge, no cervical discharge/tenderness; pH 4; wet prep shows hyphae and budding yeast. Most likely diagnosis?Candidal vulvovaginitis. Normal pH (4) with hyphae/budding yeast on wet prep is characteristic.
3. Are same-sex partners at risk for vaginitis?Yes — ↑ risk of BV; trichomonas is possible with shared sex toys.
3. Next best step in evaluation/management?Empiric treatment: fluconazole 150 mg PO ×1 or vaginal suppositories. Additional testing for vaginitis (culture, DNA assay, whiff test). Screen for STIs (HIV), diabetes, and recent antibiotic use.

Board-Style MCQs added

1. A 28-year-old woman has a thin gray discharge with a fishy odor. Vaginal pH is 5.0, whiff test is positive, and clue cells are seen on wet mount. Which is the most appropriate first-line treatment?
  1. Fluconazole 150 mg PO ×1
  2. Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID ×7 d
  3. Tinidazole 2 g PO ×1
  4. Topical clotrimazole ×7 d
  5. Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM ×1
Answer & explanationsB. She meets Amsel criteria for BV (thin homogeneous discharge, pH >4.5, positive whiff, clue cells) — first-line is oral or topical metronidazole (or clindamycin). A/D — antifungals treat yeast (normal pH, hyphae). C — tinidazole is for trichomonas. E — ceftriaxone treats gonorrhea, not BV.
2. A 32-year-old woman has thick "cottage-cheese" discharge and vulvar itching. Vaginal pH is 4.2, whiff test is negative, and KOH prep shows hyphae. What is the diagnosis and pH explanation?
  1. BV; lactobacilli are depleted
  2. Trichomonas; protozoa raise pH
  3. Candidal vulvovaginitis; Candida and lactobacilli coexist, so pH is normal
  4. GSM; estrogen deficiency lowers pH
  5. Cervicitis; chlamydia lowers pH
Answer & explanationsC. Normal pH (4.0–4.5) with hyphae on KOH and a negative whiff is candidal vulvovaginitis — yeast and lactobacilli coexist, so pH is unchanged. A/B — both raise pH >4.5. D — GSM raises pH (>5). E — cervicitis does not characteristically lower pH and the KOH finding indicates yeast.
3. A 26-year-old woman with a new partner has frothy green-yellow discharge and a "strawberry cervix." Wet prep is negative for motile organisms. What is the best next step?
  1. Reassure — wet prep excludes trichomonas
  2. Send NAAT/PCR for Trichomonas vaginalis
  3. Treat empirically for yeast
  4. Prescribe topical metronidazole
  5. Repeat wet prep in 1 week
Answer & explanationsB. Wet prep is only ~50–60% sensitive for trichomonas — a negative microscopy does not exclude it, and the clinical picture (frothy green-yellow discharge, strawberry cervix, new partner) is classic. NAAT/PCR is the most sensitive/specific test. A/E — a negative wet prep can't rule out trich. C — wrong organism. D — topical metronidazole does not treat trichomonas (needs oral).
4. A patient is diagnosed with trichomoniasis. Which statement about management is correct?
  1. Topical metronidazole gel is adequate treatment
  2. Her male partner does not require treatment
  3. Preferred treatment is oral metronidazole 500 mg BID ×7 d, and partners should be treated
  4. Single-dose 2 g metronidazole is preferred over the 7-day course in women
  5. Re-testing is never indicated
Answer & explanationsC. Per 2021 CDC, multi-dose metronidazole 500 mg BID ×7 d is preferred for women, and sexual partners must be treated (men: 2 g ×1). A — topical agents don't treat trich. B — partner treatment is required (STI). D — the 7-day course is preferred over single-dose in women. E — re-test for reinfection at 3 months.
5. A 58-year-old postmenopausal woman reports vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, and recurrent UTIs. Exam shows thin, pale vaginal mucosa with scant discharge; pH is 5.5. Which is the most likely diagnosis and first-line treatment?
  1. BV; oral metronidazole
  2. Genitourinary syndrome of menopause; vaginal estrogen and moisturizers/lubricants
  3. Candidal vulvovaginitis; fluconazole
  4. Trichomonas; tinidazole
  5. Allergic vaginitis; topical steroids
Answer & explanationsB. Dryness, dyspareunia, recurrent UTIs, atrophic mucosa, and elevated pH in a postmenopausal woman are GSM (atrophic vaginitis) from estrogen deficiency — first-line is vaginal estrogen plus moisturizers/lubricants. A/C/D — infectious causes don't fit the atrophic exam and scant discharge. E — allergic vaginitis follows an exposure and isn't the best fit here.

Take-Home Points

  1. The common causes disrupt vaginal flora differently → predictable pH: BV and trichomonas raise pH >4.5; yeast keeps a normal pH. GSM raises it further (>5).
  2. Symptoms alone cannot determine the cause — offer exam with pH + microscopy (KOH/wet prep) and/or DNA assay/NAAT to all symptomatic patients, and exclude STI cervicitis.
  3. Treat by etiology and address risk reduction (especially for recurrence): BV → metronidazole/clindamycin; yeast → fluconazole or topical azole; trichomonas → oral metronidazole ×7 d AND treat partner(s).
