Learning Objectives

  1. Name and define the key outputs in pressure-targeted vs volume-targeted modes (what you set vs what you must monitor).
  2. Define resistance and compliance and predict how changes in each move peak, plateau, and driving pressure.
  3. Apply a structured diagnostic approach to a high peak-pressure alarm in a mechanically ventilated patient.

Pre-work send the day before

1. In a volume-targeted mode, which two pressures must you watch? In a pressure-targeted mode, which two volumes?

Targeting volume → monitor pressures (peak and plateau / driving). Targeting pressure → monitor volumes (tidal volume VT and minute ventilation VE = RR × VT). Simple principle: you watch the thing you don't control.

2. Write the bedside formulas for resistance and compliance and their normal cut-offs.

Resistance ∝ Ppeak − Pplateau (normal <10 cm H₂O). Compliance = VT / (Pplateau − PEEP) = VT / driving pressure; driving pressure for a 4–6 mL/kg breath should be <10 cm H₂O. The single maneuver that gives you the plateau is an inspiratory hold.

3. A vent alarms for high peak pressure. What is the very first thing you do — before any calculation?

Go to the bedside and establish urgency. Check vitals, VT/VE on the vent, and SpO₂. A high peak pressure plus falling SpO₂, falling VT/VE, rising PCO₂, or dropping BP = emergency → call for help and turn FiO₂ to 100% while you work through the steps.

Hook 2 min

"It's 2 a.m. RT pages you: 'Room 365's vent keeps alarming high peak pressures.' You don't need to know the patient's whole story to start — you need a plateau pressure and two subtractions. By the end of this talk you'll walk into that room with a three-step algorithm that works on any ventilator." Project the Key Outputs slide.

Objective 1 — Key outputs: set vs monitor 5 min Objective 1

ASK — "In a pressure-targeted mode, what are the key outputs? In a volume-targeted mode?" Reveal: pressure-targeted → VT and VE; volume-targeted → Ppeak and Pdriving.
TermDefinitionHow measured
Tidal volume (VT)Volume delivered each breathRead off vent (target it in VC)
Minute ventilation (VE)VT × RR (L/min)Read off vent; anchor adequacy to pH/PCO₂
Peak pressure (Ppeak)Total pressure to deliver VT during inspirationContinuously displayed
Plateau pressure (Pplateau)Pressure holding air in the lung at end-inspiration (no flow)Inspiratory hold maneuver
Driving pressure (Pdriving)Pressure difference to achieve VT = Pplateau − PEEPCompute after an inspiratory hold
Pearl. When you target volume, monitor pressures; when you target pressure, monitor volumes. The body of this talk is about volume-targeted modes, where peak and plateau are your dashboard.

Objective 2 — Resistance and compliance 6 min Objective 2

ASK — "Define resistance. Define compliance. Which one does the airway control, and which one does the lung/chest wall control?"

Resistance (R) = opposition to airflow through the whole circuit (ventilator tubing + ETT + airways). R ∝ Ppeak − Pplateau; normal <10 cm H₂O.

Compliance (C) = "stretchiness" of the respiratory system (lungs, pleura, chest wall, diaphragm, intra-abdominal pressure). C = ΔV/ΔP = VT / (Pplateau − PEEP) = VT / Pdriving. A stiffer system = lower compliance = higher driving pressure for the same breath.

OutputWhat moves it
PeakResistance and compliance, plus inspiratory flow rate/pattern and total PEEP (incl. auto-PEEP). It is the "everything" pressure.
PlateauMeasured with no airflow → resistance drops out. Reflects lung/chest-wall mechanics only. Keep <30 cm H₂O (barotrauma risk above).
DrivingSame drivers as plateau (compliance). Keep ≤14–15 cm H₂O in ARDS.
Pearl — the two subtractions. Ppeak − Pplateau isolates resistance. Pplateau − PEEP (driving pressure) isolates compliance. Almost everything in this talk reduces to those two subtractions.

Objective 3 — The high peak-pressure algorithm 7 min Objective 3

Step 1 — Establish urgency. At the bedside: full vitals + key vent outputs. High peak plus low SpO₂, low VT, VE down ~>50%, rising PCO₂, or dropping BP = emergency → call for help, FiO₂ to 100%, don't wait for an ABG.

Step 2 — Assess resistance. Inspiratory hold → plateau. Ppeak − Pplateau > 10 = resistance problem.

ASK — "Name the resistance differential and the matching action for each." Reveal: kinked tubing → inspect/uncompress; bitten ETT → bite block + analgesia/sedation; mucus plug / large-airway obstruction → deep suction (rarely bronchoscopy); bronchospasm → bronchodilators (esp. obstructive history); refractory kink/obstruction → tube exchange by airway-trained clinician.

Step 3 — Assess compliance. Pplateau > 30 or driving pressure > 10 = compliance problem.

ASK — "Sort the compliance differential into alveolar filling vs alveolar collapse vs extrapulmonary." Reveal: filling = cardiogenic edema, ARDS, pneumonia, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage; collapse = atelectasis, lobar collapse; extrapulmonary = pneumothorax, large pleural effusion (intrathoracic) or ascites, abdominal distention/compartment syndrome, pregnancy, stiff chest wall/burns (extrathoracic).
Pearl — tempo and exam localize the cause. A pneumothorax spikes peak over minutes; ARDS/fibrosis climbs over hours-to-days; severe COPD air-traps. Targeted exam + CXR/POCUS + ABG (and a bladder pressure if you suspect abdominal compartment syndrome) close the loop. Remember resistance and compliance problems can co-exist.

Retrieval close 2 min

Board off. Have a learner rebuild the algorithm from memory: Step 1 urgency → Step 2 peak−plateau for resistance → Step 3 plateau-30 / driving-10 for compliance → differential + action for each. Then have a second learner state the two subtractions and their normal cut-offs.

Cases click to reveal

Case 1 — Gradual rise, COVID ARDS

65-year-old intubated in the ICU for COVID pneumonia; gradually rising Ppeak from ~35 to consistently 40. AC/volume control, FiO₂ 70%, PEEP 12, RR 20, VT 5 mL/kg (450 mL), deeply sedated and paralyzed. Vitals: T 37, BP 110/57, HR 93, RR 24, SaO₂ 97%, VT 450 mL.

Step 1 — urgency?

Stable: oxygenation fine, VE acceptable, hemodynamics intact. Proceed methodically.

Next step?

Inspiratory hold for a plateau to separate resistance vs compliance. Plateau = 38 cm H₂O.

Resistance problem?

No. Ppeak − Pplateau = 40 − 38 = 2 (well under 10).

Compliance problem?

Yes. Driving pressure = Pplateau − PEEP = 38 − 12 = 26 cm H₂O (elevated) → low compliance. (Deck corrected here: the original printed "is 14"; 38 − 12 = 26.)

Differential and next steps?

Alveolar filling (worsening pneumonia/ARDS, pulmonary edema), atelectasis, restricted expansion from effusions; slow rise makes acute PTX/lobar collapse less likely. Get history, targeted exam, CXR/POCUS + ABG, CBC/BMP/BNP. Exam: soft abdomen, diffuse crackles; CXR: worsening bilateral infiltrates; rising leukocytosis; pH 7.34/45/82/24 on 70%. Most likely: worsening parenchymal disease — progressive ARDS ± superimposed bacterial infection.

Case 2 — Acute decompensation, status asthmaticus

25-year-old intubated for an asthma exacerbation; volume-targeted, FiO₂ 70%, PEEP 5, VT 350 mL (6 mL/kg), RR 24. One hour later: progressively hypotensive and hypoxemic — BP 100s/60s, RR 30, SaO₂ 92%, HR 120s; high peak pressures ~38, VT delivered 300 mL.

Urgency?

Oxygenation borderline-stable; proceed but stay alert — a falling BP can flip this to an emergency. Get an ABG.

First management step?

Inspiratory hold to measure plateau and split resistance vs compliance.

Resistance vs compliance? (early)

Plateau is low relative to peak → a resistance picture (bronchospasm/airway inflammation; consider dynamic hyperinflation/auto-PEEP). Action: escalate bronchodilators ± steroids, consider IV magnesium; slow the RR to lengthen expiration (as pH allows), listen for bilateral breath sounds, CXR/POCUS to rule out pneumothorax.

It evolves — BP now 80s/60s, peak still ~35, plateau risen to 30 (PEEP 12, RR 35)…

BP trending down → call for help. Now Pplateau − PEEP = 30 − 12 = 18 (compliance problem) while peak − plateau = 35 − 30 = 5 (no resistance problem) — concerning for breath-stacking / tension physiology. Disconnect from the vent to allow a long exhalation, confirm with POCUS, and if the patient arrests run a Code Blue with manual bagging at RR 8–10. (See changelog: the printed intermediate numbers in this case do not all reconcile — flagged for author review.)

Board-Style MCQs ABIM single-best-answer

1. A patient on AC/volume control alarms with peak 42, and an inspiratory hold shows plateau 22 (PEEP 8). Which is the best next step?

  1. Increase PEEP
  2. Suction the ETT and check for kinking/biting
  3. Start a diuretic
  4. Obtain a STAT chest CT
  5. Decrease tidal volume
Answer + explanation

B. Peak − plateau = 42 − 22 = 20 (>10) → a resistance problem. The first, reversible bedside causes are mucus plugging, a kinked or bitten tube — suction and inspect the circuit. A raises PEEP without addressing airway resistance. C targets compliance (edema), which is normal here (driving pressure 22 − 8 = 14). D is premature and moves an unstable patient. E doesn't fix resistance and may worsen ventilation.

2. Volume control, peak 40, plateau 36, PEEP 6. Which single value most specifically reflects a compliance problem?

  1. Peak − plateau = 4
  2. Peak pressure = 40
  3. Plateau − PEEP = 30
  4. PEEP = 6
  5. Peak − PEEP = 34
Answer + explanation

C. Driving pressure = plateau − PEEP = 36 − 6 = 30 — markedly elevated (>10) and the most specific marker of low compliance. A (peak − plateau = 4) reflects resistance, which is normal. B and E are lumped pressures influenced by both resistance and compliance. D is a set parameter, not a derived mechanic.

3. A ventilated COPD patient becomes acutely hypotensive with rising peak pressures shortly after the RR was increased. Plateau − PEEP is normal; peak − plateau is high. What is the most appropriate immediate action?

  1. Increase the respiratory rate further
  2. Give a fluid bolus and continue current settings
  3. Disconnect from the ventilator to allow full exhalation, then treat bronchospasm
  4. Increase PEEP to match auto-PEEP
  5. Emergent pericardiocentesis
Answer + explanation

C. The picture is dynamic hyperinflation / auto-PEEP (breath-stacking) causing obstruction physiology and hemodynamic collapse. Disconnecting the circuit lets the patient exhale and immediately relieves intrathoracic pressure; then treat the underlying bronchospasm (bronchodilators, slow the rate to lengthen expiration). A worsens stacking. B alone won't fix the mechanics. D can help matching once stabilized but is not the immediate life-saving move. E treats the wrong diagnosis.

4. Which finding accompanying a high peak-pressure alarm should most prompt you to call for help before completing your stepwise assessment?

  1. SaO₂ 96%
  2. Tidal volume at goal
  3. A drop in blood pressure with falling SpO₂
  4. pH 7.40
  5. Stable minute ventilation
Answer + explanation

C. Hemodynamic instability with desaturation marks an emergency (tension PTX, severe auto-PEEP, mucus plug occluding the airway). Call for backup, FiO₂ 100%, and proceed in parallel. The other options describe a stable patient in whom a methodical work-up is appropriate.

5. In a patient with ARDS on lung-protective settings (VT 6 mL/kg, plateau 28), which target is most strongly associated with improved survival and should guide further adjustment?

  1. Driving pressure ≤ 14–15 cm H₂O
  2. Peak pressure ≤ 50 cm H₂O
  3. FiO₂ ≤ 60% at all times
  4. Minute ventilation ≥ 10 L/min
  5. PEEP ≤ 5 cm H₂O
Answer + explanation

A. Driving pressure (plateau − PEEP) is the ventilatory variable most tightly linked to ARDS mortality; keeping ΔP ≤ 14–15 is associated with better survival even when VT and plateau are already protective (Amato, NEJM 2015). B peak pressure isn't the protective target. C/D are not survival targets and may be harmful (low VE may be acceptable with permissive hypercapnia). E low PEEP can worsen oxygenation/derecruitment.

Modern Framing — 2023–25 Context currency check

The core mechanics (resistance, compliance, peak/plateau/driving pressure, and the high peak-pressure algorithm) are timeless physiology and verified current as of June 2026. The items below add modern context; none change the lesson's standing teaching.

Driving pressure as the survival target. Driving pressure (ΔP = Pplateau − PEEP) is the ventilatory variable most tightly associated with ARDS mortality. Keep ΔP ≤ 14–15 cm H₂O — risk rises stepwise above that even when VT ≤6 mL/kg and plateau ≤30 (Amato, NEJM 2015; LUNG SAFE).

Lung-protective targets unchanged. VT 4–6 mL/kg ideal body weight, plateau <30, PEEP >5 (ARDSNet, NEJM 2000; ATS/ESICM/SCCM 2017; ESICM ARDS 2023).
ESICM ARDS 2023 / Global (new) ARDS definition 2024. SpO₂/FiO₂ ≤315 (when SpO₂ ≤97%) may define ARDS; lung ultrasound is an accepted imaging modality; high-flow nasal oxygen is recognized as a support category. Net effect: a broader population you'll be troubleshooting at the vent — the mechanics approach is unchanged.

Take-Home Points

  1. Peak and plateau are the key outputs in volume-targeted modes. Peak reflects resistance + compliance + PEEP + inspiratory flow; plateau (inspiratory hold) should be <30 cm H₂O.
  2. For a high peak-pressure alarm: (1) establish urgency, then (2) get a plateau and decide resistance vs compliance vs both.
  3. Resistance ∝ Ppeak − Pplateau; >10 = resistance problem (kink, bite, mucus plug, bronchospasm).
  4. Compliance ∝ 1/driving pressure (Pplateau − PEEP); driving >10 (or plateau >30) = compliance problem (alveolar filling/collapse, extrapulmonary). Keep ΔP ≤ 14–15.
  5. They can co-exist — and falling BP, SpO₂, or VE turns troubleshooting into an emergency: call for help and FiO₂ 100% first.
