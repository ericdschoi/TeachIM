Learning Objectives

  1. Determine the mode of mechanical ventilation from the settings and waveforms.
  2. Determine the relative level of oxygen support a ventilator is providing.
  3. Determine whether minute ventilation is appropriate.
  4. Identify problems with respiratory mechanics.

Pre-work send the day before

1. You glance at a vent: the pressure waveform is a square box, and there's a set RR of 18. What family of mode is this, and is it control or support?

Square wave in pressure → a pressure-targeted mode (PC, PRVC, or PS). A set/mandatory RR → a control mode (so PC or PRVC, not PS). Pressure support has no mandatory rate.

2. Write the minute-ventilation equation and the normal range.

VE = VT × RR. Normal 4–6 L/min (often higher with disease/metabolic acidosis).

3. In a volume-targeted mode the peak pressure alarms high. What single maneuver separates a resistance problem from a compliance problem?

An inspiratory hold to get the plateau pressure. Peak − Plateau > 3–5 = resistance (mucus plug, kinked tube, bronchospasm); elevated Plateau − PEEP (>10) = compliance (pneumothorax, edema, fibrosis, mainstem intubation, distended abdomen).

Teaching Script

The original interactive board drives the visuals; this script adds a hook, ASK pauses, pearls, and a retrieval close. Use the "Orientation → Mode → O₂ support → Ventilation → Mechanics → Cases" navigation on the board.

Hook 2 min

"You walk into a room and the vent is alarming. You don't need to memorize every brand's screen — you need four questions that work on any ventilator, any brand. Let's build them." Project the Orientation slide and point out: dials on the right = what the clinician set; numbers on the left = what the ventilator measured. They aren't always equal (a spontaneously breathing patient can exceed the set rate).

Orientation — reading the screen 3 min

ASK — "On the pressure waveform, what are PEEP, peak pressure, and ΔP?" Reveal: PEEP = pressure left in the circuit after exhalation; peak = max pressure during inspiration; ΔP = peak − PEEP (units cm H₂O). On the flow waveform, up = inspiratory flow, down = expiratory flow.
Pearl. Expiration on a ventilator is passive — expiratory flow depends only on patient mechanics (recoil + resistance), so it looks the same across all modes. Inspiration is where the mode shows itself.

ASK — Before revealing each mode card: "Where's the square wave? Is there a mandatory RR? What's targeted, and what must you monitor?"

Objective 3 — Is ventilation appropriate? 4 min Objective 3

Three steps: (1) check minute ventilation VE = VT × RR; (2) is it appropriate? — anchor to the pH (general rule keep pH 7.2–7.5), not the absolute VE; (3) vent- or patient-driven?

ASK — "Patient's measured RR exceeds the set rate and the pH is 7.5 — is the ventilator or the patient driving this?" If patient-triggered, look for patient causes of tachypnea: CNS injury, pain, salicylates, metabolic acidosis. If vent-driven, adjust settings.

Objective 4 — Respiratory mechanics 5 min Objective 4

Pressure-targeted modes: watch ΔP — good lungs need ΔP <10 cm H₂O to deliver ~6 mL/kg; a falling VT signals a mechanics problem. Volume-targeted modes: watch peak & plateau; low VT/VE points to a mechanics problem.

ASK — "Volume mode, ΔP > 10. What's your next move, and how do you localize the problem?"
Pearl — the inspiratory hold split. Do an inspiratory hold to get the plateau. Peak − Plateau > 3–5 = a resistance issue (mucus plug, kinked tube, bronchospasm). Elevated Plateau − PEEP (>10) = a compliance issue (pneumothorax, alveolar filling/edema, fibrosis, mainstem intubation, distended abdomen). (The deck's compliance box label was corrected from "Plat − Peak > 10" to "Plat − PEEP > 10" to match its own waveform annotation.)

Retrieval close 2 min

ASK — Cover the board. "List the four questions and, for each mode, the one square-wave clue and what you monitor." Then run the three cases as live practice.

Cases click to reveal

Case 1 — Square wave in pressure; set RR present; Pcontrol 20, PEEP 5, FiO₂ 21%. Peak 25, VT 345, RR 21, VE 7.2.

What is the mode?

Pressure control. Square wave in pressure = pressure mode; a mandatory RR = control mode.

What is the target, and what are the key outputs to monitor?

Target = Pcontrol. Monitor VT and minute ventilation.

How much O₂ support?

Low — minimal FiO₂ (21%) and PEEP (5).

Case 2 — Square wave in flow; set RR; VT targeted, PEEP 18, FiO₂ 100%. Peak 27, VT 355, RR 25, VE 8.9.

What is the mode?

Volume control. Square wave in flow = volume mode; mandatory RR = control mode.

Target, key output, and do you need an inspiratory hold?

Target = VT. Monitor peak (and plateau) pressure. ΔP = Peak − PEEP = 27 − 18 = 9 (<10) → inspiratory hold not strictly needed.

How much O₂ support?

Very high — maximum FiO₂ (100%) and PEEP (18).

Case 3 — Square wave in pressure with blunted, negative-deflection breaths; no mandatory RR. Psupport 10, PEEP 5, FiO₂ 30%. Peak 28, VT 355, RR 25, VE 8.9.

What is the mode?

Pressure support. Square wave in pressure = pressure mode; no mandatory RR = support mode. Blunted waves and negative deflections = patient-initiated breaths.

Target and key outputs?

Target = Psupport. Monitor VT, RR, and minute ventilation.

How much O₂ support?

Low — FiO₂ 30%, PEEP 5.

Board-Style MCQs single-best-answer

1. A ventilator shows a square waveform in the pressure tracing, a set respiratory rate of 16, and the clinician has set a target tidal volume; the screen also displays an "achieved VT." Which mode is this?

  1. Volume control (AC-VC)
  2. Pressure control (AC-PC)
  3. Pressure-regulated volume control (PRVC)
  4. Pressure support
Answer & explanation

C. Pressure waveform square (pressure-delivery) + a set tidal-volume target + an "achieved VT" readout = PRVC, the volume-targeted hybrid of pressure control.

A — classic volume control squares the flow wave and targets VT directly without an algorithm. B — pressure control targets pressure, not volume. D — pressure support has no mandatory rate.

2. A patient in AC-VC has a rising peak pressure. An inspiratory hold gives Peak 40, Plateau 22, PEEP 8. What is the problem?

  1. Compliance problem (e.g., pulmonary edema)
  2. Resistance problem (e.g., mucus plug or bronchospasm)
  3. Normal mechanics
  4. Auto-PEEP from inadequate expiratory time
Answer & explanation

B. Peak − Plateau = 40 − 22 = 18 (>3–5) with a near-normal Plateau − PEEP (22 − 8 = 14) points to high airway resistance: mucus plug, kinked tube, or bronchospasm.

A — a compliance problem elevates the plateau (Plateau − PEEP markedly >10) with a smaller Peak−Plateau gap. C — a large Peak−Plateau gap is not normal. D — auto-PEEP is diagnosed with an expiratory hold and a flow tracing that doesn't return to zero, not this pattern.

3. Which two ventilator settings primarily determine the level of oxygenation support, in every mode?

  1. Tidal volume and respiratory rate
  2. FiO₂ and PEEP
  3. Inspiratory time and flow rate
  4. Pressure support and trigger sensitivity
Answer & explanation

B. FiO₂ and PEEP set oxygenation regardless of mode; the ARDSNet ladder pairs them.

A — VT and RR drive ventilation (CO₂), not oxygenation. C/D — affect breath delivery/synchrony, not the primary oxygenation knobs.

4. A patient with ARDS is in AC-VC at 6 mL/kg PBW. Plateau is 24, PEEP 10. Per current evidence, which single parameter is most strongly associated with survival and should guide further adjustment?

  1. Peak inspiratory pressure
  2. Driving pressure (Plateau − PEEP)
  3. FiO₂
  4. Mean airway pressure
Answer & explanation

B. Driving pressure (here 24 − 10 = 14 cm H₂O) is the ventilator variable most strongly associated with survival in ARDS (Amato, NEJM 2015); target ≤14–15.

A — peak pressure includes a resistive component and is less predictive. C — FiO₂ tracks oxygenation, not VILI risk per se. D — mean airway pressure matters for oxygenation but is not the survival-linked lever here.

5. A ventilator shows no mandatory respiratory rate, a square wave in the pressure tracing with negative deflections preceding each breath, and the only set inspiratory parameter is a pressure above PEEP. What is the mode, and what is its most common use?

  1. Volume control — for ARDS lung protection
  2. Pressure control — for precise airway-pressure control
  3. Pressure support — for spontaneous breathing trials
  4. PRVC — for combined volume target with pressure comfort
Answer & explanation

C. No mandatory rate = a support mode; the set pressure above PEEP with patient-triggered (negative-deflection) breaths = pressure support, commonly used for SBTs.

A/B/D — all are control modes with a mandatory rate, which is absent here.

Modern Framing — Lung-Protective Ventilation 2015–24 evidence

The mode-reading framework taught here is timeless and unchanged. For learners who go on to manage ARDS, layer in the current lung-protective targets (also on the appended deck slide):

Low tidal volume 6 mL/kg predicted body weight (range 4–8) remains the foundation — the original ARDSNet mortality benefit (NEJM 2000) is still standard of care. Plateau pressure <30 cm H₂O (many target ≤28).
Driving pressure (ΔP = Plateau − PEEP) is the ventilator variable most strongly associated with survival in ARDS (Amato et al., NEJM 2015; mediation analysis of 3,562 patients). Target ≤14–15 cm H₂O where achievable. Note the deck's quick ΔP = Peak − PEEP is a bedside screen; true driving pressure uses the plateau from an inspiratory hold, not peak.

Sources: ARDS Network, NEJM 2000;342:1301. Amato MBP et al., NEJM 2015;372:747. Lung-protective targets unchanged through 2024.

Take-Home Points

  1. Read any ventilator with the same four questions: mode, O₂ support, ventilation, mechanics.
  2. Mode in two glances: square wave in pressure = pressure mode; square wave in flow = classic volume control. A mandatory RR = control mode; no mandatory RR = support (PS).
  3. Set pressure → monitor VT/VE; set volume → monitor peak/plateau. FiO₂ and PEEP set oxygenation in every mode.
  4. Mechanics: ΔP > 10 in a volume mode → inspiratory hold. Peak − Plateau gap = resistance; elevated Plateau − PEEP = compliance.
