Learning Objectives

  1. Construct a framework for the evaluation of anemia (underproduction vs. overproduction).
  2. Differentiate underproduction anemias using the MCV and iron studies.
  3. Differentiate immune-mediated from non-immune causes of hemolysis.
  4. Interpret the reticulocyte index, hemolysis labs, DAT, and peripheral smear to reach a diagnosis.

Pre-work NEW — 5 min, send the day before

Answer these before the session, without looking anything up.

Q1. What single test best separates a marrow problem from blood loss/hemolysis, and how do you read it? The reticulocyte index (corrected retic count). RI <2 = underproduction (the marrow isn't responding); RI ≥3 = appropriate response to blood loss, hemolysis, or sequestration. (Beware: a concurrent underproduction process can falsely lower the RI.)
Q2. Ferritin is 18 ng/mL in a patient with active rheumatoid arthritis. Does that confirm iron deficiency? Yes — a low ferritin (<30) is specific for iron deficiency even in inflammation, because inflammation only raises ferritin. The harder problem is the reverse: a normal/high ferritin does NOT exclude iron deficiency when inflammation is present (use TSAT <20%, sTfR, or a hepcidin/iron trial).
Q3. Schistocytes + thrombocytopenia on a smear — what must you not miss? A thrombotic microangiopathy (TTP/HUS) — a hematologic emergency. Send ADAMTS13 and start plasma exchange when TTP is likely; check coags/fibrinogen to separate DIC and consider HELLP/atypical HUS.

Teaching Script Restructured with timing & engagement cues

Format: project the interactive board (Work_up_of_Anemia_Optimized.pptx) and click the cursor icons in order (Overview → Underproduction → Iron labs → Overproduction → Non-immune → Cases), or rebuild the retic-index tree on a whiteboard. A "toolbox" of key tests appears as you click. Modular: Underproduction + Cases 1 & 4 (25 min) and Overproduction + Cases 2 & 3 (25 min) can be taught separately. Print the learner handout — page 1 is a blank schematic to fill in.

Hook (2 min)

Put a CBC on the board: Hgb 7.9, MCV 89, with the rest blank. "Same Hgb, same MCV — but two of these patients need a GI scope, one needs plasma exchange today, and one just needs IV fluids stopped. How do you tell them apart?" The retic index is the fork in the road.

Objective 1 — The framework: retic index first 8 min

ASK: "Before MCV, before iron studies — what's the first branch point in any anemia?" Reveal: underproduction (marrow problem, RI <2) vs. overproduction (RI ≥3: blood loss, hemolysis, sequestration). Hemodilution (aggressive IVF inpatient, hypervolemia/HF outpatient) drops the Hgb without deranging the RI.
Pearl: The retic index can be falsely low/normal if an underproduction process coexists with hemolysis or bleeding — don't let a "normal" retic talk you out of an obvious bleed.

Objective 2 — Underproduction: MCV + iron studies 12 min

ASK: "Sort underproduction by MCV." Build the three buckets (note overlaps):
MCVCauses
Macrocytic (>100)Megaloblastic (B12/folate; drugs — hydroxyurea, allopurinol, HIV meds), liver disease/ETOH, severe hypothyroidism, primary marrow pathology (MDS, esp. MCV >120)
Normocytic (80–100)Anemia of chronic inflammation, anemia of CKD (↓EPO), early iron deficiency, acute blood loss, primary marrow pathology (aplastic anemia, leukemia, pure RBC aplasia, myeloma)
Microcytic (<80)Iron deficiency, thalassemia, sideroblastic anemia (congenital; acquired — lead, Zn, INH/linezolid), anemia of chronic inflammation
ASK: "When other cell lines are also down, where does your differential shift?" → toward primary marrow pathology (MDS, aplastic anemia, leukemia, myeloma).
ASK: "Read the four iron studies — how do iron deficiency and ACD differ?" Reveal the toolbox:
Serum FeTIBC% satFerritin
Iron deficiencyLowHighLow (<20% suggestive, <10% very)Low (<30 ng/mL diagnostic)
ACD/inflammationLowLow–normalLow–normal (pseudo-normal if TIBC low)Normal–high (acute-phase)
ThalassemiaNormal–highNormal–highNormal–highNormal
Pearl: 20–85% of patients with ACD also have iron deficiency. A very low TSAT is the clue; sTfR, hepcidin, or a documented response to an iron trial settle it. Ferritin <30 is diagnostic of iron deficiency even in inflammation — inflammation only raises ferritin, never lowers it.

Objective 3 & 4 — Overproduction: confirm hemolysis, then DAT + smear 14 min

ASK: "RI is ≥3 — what are the three buckets?" Blood loss (GI/"floor", long bones, thorax, abdomen/retroperitoneum), hemolysis, splenic sequestration.
ASK: "Which labs confirm hemolysis?" ↑LDH, ↓haptoglobin, ↑indirect bilirubin, and the smear. Then the DAT (direct Coombs) splits immune from non-immune.
DATCategoryKey typesSmear clue
PositiveImmune (AIHA)Warm (IgG) — cancer/lymphoma, autoimmune (SLE), drugs; Cold (IgM) — lymphoma, mycoplasma, mono (also PNH, syphilis)Spherocytes
NegativeNon-immuneMAHA (fragmentation) vs non-MAHA (intrinsic/extrinsic to RBC)Schistocytes (MAHA)
ASK: "Non-immune, DAT-negative — how do you split it?" MAHA (schistocytes → if ↓platelets, think TMA: primary TTP/HUS, drug-induced; secondary DIC [↓fibrinogen], HELLP; other — malignant HTN, prosthetic valves/LVAD/TIPS, scleroderma renal crisis) vs non-MAHA (intrinsic — membrane [HS, elliptocytosis, stomatocytosis, PNH], Hb [sickle, thalassemia], metabolism [G6PD, PK deficiency]; extrinsic — liver disease/hypersplenism, infection [malaria/Babesia/Bartonella; Clostridium/leishmania toxins], oxidative-stress meds).
Emergency: Schistocytes + thrombocytopenia = possible TMA. Calculate the PLASMIC score, send ADAMTS13 before plasma exchange, and don't wait for the result if TTP is likely — confirmed TTP is treated with PEX + steroids + caplacizumab.

Close (2 min)

Hide the board. Have the group rebuild the algorithm from the top: retic index → (underproduction → MCV + iron studies) or (overproduction → hemolysis labs → DAT → smear). Then show the take-home slide.

Practice Cases from the interactive board, answers click-to-reveal

Case 1 — Fevers, menorrhagia, gum bleeding

33-year-old with 2 weeks of fevers/chills, nausea/vomiting, non-bloody diarrhea; + fatigue, menorrhagia, gum bleeding. Exam: blood blister on the lip. Hgb 7.9, MCV 89, platelets low, Cr 1.0, fibrinogen normal. Retic index 3.5. LDH 1508, haptoglobin <30, Tbili 1.0.

Interpret the smear and labs.Overproduction (RI 3.5) with hemolysis (↑LDH, ↓haptoglobin); smear shows schistocytes → MAHA. With concurrent thrombocytopenia and normal fibrinogen, this is a primary TMA.
Diagnosis and urgency?Primary TMA (e.g., TTP) given MAHA + low platelets — a hematologic emergency. Send ADAMTS13 and initiate plasma exchange; don't wait if TTP is likely.

Case 2 — Fall with femur fracture, confusion, constipation

56-year-old with a ground-level fall and femur fracture; weakness, fatigue, mild confusion, constipation, easy bruising. Cr 1.6, calcium 11.5. Retic index 1.1, MCV 89. Iron studies show ↓Fe, ↓%sat, ↓ferritin. LDH normal, smear shows rouleaux.

What does rouleaux + hypercalcemia + renal dysfunction suggest?Multiple myeloma — the classic CRAB tetrad (hyperCalcemia, Renal dysfunction, Anemia, Bony lesions). Rouleaux reflects the paraprotein.
How do you read the iron studies here?There is concurrent iron deficiency anemia (low Fe, %sat, and ferritin) on top of the marrow process — pursue both.

Case 3 — SLE, chest pain, jaundice

45-year-old with SLE, chest pain and dyspnea; + jaundice, joint pains, fatigue. Retic index 4.5, MCV 115. LDH 2504, haptoglobin <30, Tbili 4.6. DAT positive, IgG positive. Smear: spherocytes.

Diagnosis?Warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia (IgG, DAT-positive, spherocytes) associated with SLE. The high MCV reflects reticulocytosis; iron studies suggest concurrent anemia of chronic disease.

Case 4 — 6 months of progressive fatigue, no overt bleeding

73-year-old, minimal prior care, 6 months of fatigue and exertional dyspnea; normal diet, no overt blood loss. Retic index 1.1, MCV 68. Iron studies: ↓Fe, %sat 1%, TIBC normal, ferritin 150 ng/mL. LDH 254, smear: hypochromic RBCs.

Interpret the iron studies.Concurrent iron deficiency (very low % saturation) AND anemia of chronic disease (normal TIBC, elevated ferritin from inflammation masking the depleted stores).
Next step?Further GI evaluation for occult blood loss — new iron deficiency in an older adult is colon cancer until proven otherwise.

Board-Style MCQs NEW — post-session, ABIM format

1. A 68-year-old man with rheumatoid arthritis has Hgb 10.2, MCV 84. Ferritin 95 ng/mL, TSAT 9%, TIBC low-normal. Which is the best next step to resolve whether iron deficiency coexists with his anemia of chronic disease?

  1. Conclude pure anemia of chronic disease; no iron needed
  2. Soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR) or a trial of iron with repeat labs
  3. Bone marrow biopsy for iron stores
  4. Start an erythropoiesis-stimulating agent
Answer & explanationB. A TSAT of 9% is very low and suggests concurrent iron deficiency even though ferritin (an acute-phase reactant) is "normal" from his RA. sTfR (or sTfR/log-ferritin index), hepcidin, or an empiric iron trial with repeat CBC/iron studies is the practical next step. Calling it pure ACD (A) misses treatable iron deficiency; marrow biopsy (C) is invasive and rarely needed; an ESA (D) is premature before repleting iron.

2. A 28-year-old woman has Hgb 8.0, retic index 4, ↑LDH, ↓haptoglobin, platelets 22k, normal PT/PTT and fibrinogen, and schistocytes on smear. What is the most appropriate immediate action?

  1. Transfuse platelets to >50k before any procedure
  2. Send ADAMTS13 and start plasma exchange
  3. Start broad-spectrum antibiotics and observe
  4. Give vitamin K and fresh frozen plasma for DIC
Answer & explanationB. MAHA (schistocytes, hemolysis) + thrombocytopenia with normal coagulation points to a primary TMA, most urgently TTP. Send ADAMTS13 and begin plasma exchange without waiting for the result when TTP is likely (PLASMIC score); confirmed TTP gets PEX + steroids + caplacizumab. Platelet transfusion (A) is generally contraindicated in TTP (can worsen thrombosis). Normal fibrinogen/coags argue against DIC (D); this is not primarily an infection (C).

3. A 35-year-old develops back pain and dark urine 2 days after starting trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. Hgb fell from 14 to 9, retic index 5, ↑LDH, ↓haptoglobin, DAT negative, smear shows bite cells. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia
  2. G6PD deficiency
  3. Hereditary spherocytosis
  4. Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
Answer & explanationB. Oxidant-drug-triggered, DAT-negative intravascular hemolysis with bite cells (and Heinz bodies) is classic for G6PD deficiency — a defect in RBC metabolism. Warm AIHA (A) is DAT-positive with spherocytes; hereditary spherocytosis (C) is DAT-negative but chronic with spherocytes and a family history, not acute drug-triggered bite cells; MAHA (D) shows schistocytes, not bite cells.

4. A 22-year-old man of Mediterranean descent has Hgb 10.5, MCV 66, normal RDW, RBC count high-normal, and normal iron studies. Which is most likely?

  1. Iron deficiency anemia
  2. Thalassemia trait
  3. Anemia of chronic disease
  4. Sideroblastic anemia
Answer & explanationB. Marked microcytosis out of proportion to a mild anemia, a normal/high RBC count, a normal RDW, and normal iron studies are classic for thalassemia trait (confirm with hemoglobin electrophoresis). Iron deficiency (A) gives low iron studies and a high RDW; ACD (C) is usually normocytic with low TIBC; sideroblastic anemia (D) typically shows elevated ferritin/iron and ringed sideroblasts.

5. An inpatient's Hgb drops from 11 to 8.5 over 12 hours after aggressive IV fluid resuscitation. Retic index is 1.5, MCV normal, no bleeding source identified, LDH and haptoglobin normal. What best explains the drop?

  1. Occult GI hemorrhage
  2. Hemodilution from fluid resuscitation
  3. Acute hemolysis
  4. Anemia of chronic disease
Answer & explanationB. A rapid Hgb fall after large-volume IV fluids, with a non-elevated retic index and no hemolysis labs, is dilutional (hemodilution) — the red cell mass hasn't actually changed. A true bleed (A) or hemolysis (C) would typically drive a brisk reticulocytosis (RI ≥3) and, for hemolysis, ↑LDH/↓haptoglobin. ACD (D) develops over weeks, not hours.

  - micro (<80): Fe-def, thalassemia, sideroblastic [Pb/Zn/INH/linezolid], ACD -> iron studies, ?Hgb electrophoresis

- OVERPRODUCTION: confirm hemolysis [LDH high, hapto low, indirect bili high, smear] -> DAT:
  - DAT+ (immune/AIHA): warm IgG [cancer/lymphoma, SLE, drugs] / cold IgM [lymphoma, mycoplasma, mono; PNH, syphilis]; smear spherocytes -> heme c/s, steroids for warm, avoid cold exposure

Take-Home Points

  1. Start every anemia work-up with the reticulocyte index: <2 = underproduction (marrow problem), ≥3 = overproduction (blood loss, hemolysis, or sequestration).
  2. For underproduction, use the MCV (macro/normo/microcytic) plus iron studies; ferritin <30 ng/mL is diagnostic of iron deficiency, but a normal/high ferritin doesn't exclude it in inflammation (use TSAT, sTfR, or an iron trial).
  3. For overproduction, confirm hemolysis (↑LDH, ↓haptoglobin, ↑indirect bilirubin) then use the DAT + smear: DAT-positive = autoimmune (warm IgG / cold IgM, spherocytes); DAT-negative with schistocytes = MAHA.
  4. MAHA with thrombocytopenia is a hematologic emergency — think TMA (TTP/HUS), send ADAMTS13, and don't delay plasma exchange when TTP is likely.
  5. Concurrent iron deficiency + anemia of chronic disease is common, and new iron deficiency in an older adult warrants a GI work-up for occult blood loss.
