How is pre-renal AKI diagnosed at the bedside?	Suggestive history (volume loss, low EABV), a bland urinalysis, no hydronephrosis on US, and — if oliguric — a FeNa <1% (FeUrea <35% is more reliable if the patient is on diuretics).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What three features point toward an intra-renal etiology?	"Active" urine sediment (supports intra-renal), an elevated FeNa while oliguric (rules out pre-renal), and a normal renal ultrasound (rules out post-renal).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What are the causes of decreased effective arterial blood volume (pre-renal)?	True hypovolemia, sepsis (distributive), heart failure (cardiorenal), and cirrhosis (hepatorenal). Also altered vasculature: renal vein thrombosis, renal venous hypertension (CHF), and intra-abdominal hypertension.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What is "vasogenic" pre-renal AKI and its classic drug causes?	A change in arteriolar tone reducing GFR: afferent constriction from NSAIDs and calcineurin inhibitors; efferent dilation from ACE inhibitors and ARBs.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
List the three subcategories of intra-renal AKI.	Glomerular (glomerulonephritis), tubulointerstitial (acute tubular necrosis, acute interstitial nephritis, tubular obstruction), and vascular (MAHA/TTP-HUS, cholesterol emboli).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
How is acute glomerulonephritis recognized on urinalysis?	Dysmorphic RBCs and/or RBC casts, proteinuria, leukocyturia, ± hypertension. GN is grouped into immune-complex, pauci-immune, and anti-GBM; crescentic forms = RPGN.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What is the most common intra-renal cause of AKI, and its urine finding?	Acute tubular necrosis (ATN) — ischemic (prolonged hypoperfusion) or toxic (including iodinated contrast). Urine shows muddy-brown granular casts.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What drugs and findings suggest acute interstitial nephritis (AIN)?	Antimicrobials (esp. beta-lactams), NSAIDs, PPIs, immunotherapy; also infection and systemic disease (Sjögren's, sarcoidosis). UA: pyuria, WBC casts, proteinuria; eosinophils are insensitive/nonspecific.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What causes tubular obstruction within the kidney?	Pigment/cast nephropathy (myoglobin, hemoglobin, light chains/paraproteins) and crystal nephropathy (uric acid, acyclovir, ethylene glycol, methotrexate).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
How is post-renal AKI diagnosed and why look early?	Hydronephrosis on renal ultrasound. Rule it out early because obstruction is typically rapidly reversible once relieved.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
Does unilateral ureteral obstruction cause AKI?	No — not unless it obstructs a single functioning kidney. AKI from ureteral obstruction requires bilateral obstruction (or obstruction of the only working kidney).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
A patient on diuretics has a FeNa of 2% but seems volume-depleted. What test is more reliable?	Fractional excretion of urea (FeUrea); a value <35% supports a pre-renal state even when diuretics raise the FeNa.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
A rhabdomyolysis patient (CK 20,000) has dipstick 3+ blood but no RBCs on microscopy. Mechanism of AKI?	Pigment (myoglobin) cast nephropathy — heme-pigment tubular obstruction/toxicity. Dipstick detects myoglobin as "blood" without RBCs. Treat with aggressive IV fluids and stop nephrotoxins (e.g., NSAIDs).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What are the management principles by AKI category?	Pre-renal → restore perfusion (volume/hemodynamics). Intra-renal → treat the cause, remove nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, contrast, ACEi/ARB), supportive care. Post-renal → relieve the obstruction.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
In ATN that is now anuric, what fluid pitfall should you avoid?	Avoid overhydration — once anuric, aggressive fluids cause volume overload. Maintain euvolemia, monitor electrolytes, and avoid nephrotoxins.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
Three required criteria for ALF?	Acute liver injury (<26 wks, no chronic liver disease) + coagulopathy (INR >1.5) + hepatic encephalopathy.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
What separates ALF from acute liver injury?	Hepatic encephalopathy.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Exceptions allowing ALF despite preexisting liver disease?	HBV, autoimmune hepatitis, Wilson's disease.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Most common ALF cause in the US?	Acetaminophen (drugs/toxins ~50%).	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Two categories causing >50% of ALF?	Drugs/toxins and viral hepatitis.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Does alcohol cause ALF?	No — but it can worsen another insult (e.g., acetaminophen).	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
West Haven HE grades I–IV?	I altered sleep-wake/attention; II lethargy + asterixis; III stupor/gross disorientation; IV coma.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Empiric drug for all ALF?	N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — improves transplant-free survival even in non-APAP ALF.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
When is NAC benefit greatest in non-APAP ALF?	Early-stage disease with low-grade (I–II) hepatic encephalopathy.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Why avoid correcting INR in ALF?	INR is a key prognostic marker; reverse only for active bleeding (vitamin K is ok).	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Why minimize IV fluids in ALF?	Can precipitate/worsen cerebral edema (even with rising creatinine).	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Ammonia threshold predicting intracranial hypertension?	Arterial ammonia >150–200 µmol/L.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
HE grade prompting intubation?	Grade III–IV (airway protection).	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
King's College Criteria for acetaminophen ALF?	pH <7.3, OR all of: INR >6.5, Cr >3.3 mg/dL, grade III–IV HE.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
King's College Criteria for non-APAP ALF?	INR >6.5, OR ≥3 of: age <10/>40; HAV/HBV/drug; jaundice >7 d before HE; INR >3.5; Tbili >18.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Wilson's clue in acute ALF?	ALP:total-bilirubin ratio <4, young age, Coombs-negative hemolysis; ceruloplasmin may be normal acutely.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
Most important ALF disposition step?	Early transfer to a liver-transplant center before progression precludes safe transfer.	Hepatology::acute-liver-failure
How common is AML and who gets it?	~1% of new cancers (~20k/yr in the US); predominantly elderly (median age 68, ~59% are >65). Any age can be affected.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
AML prognosis with and without treatment?	Without treatment: survival is days to weeks. With treatment: only ~1 in 3 (≈30%) reach 5-year survival.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Where is the pathology in AML?	Overproliferation of myeloid stem cells OR myeloblasts. Because these are precursors to RBCs, WBCs, and platelets, any CBC pattern is possible.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Three CBC patterns AML can produce?	Isolated leukocytosis (myeloblast → ↑WBC), leukocytosis + cytopenias (blasts crowd out/disrupt other lines), and pancytopenia (myeloid stem cell affected → proportionate declines).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Diagnostic criteria for AML?	(1) ≥20% blasts in bone marrow aspirate OR peripheral blood, AND (2) cells of myeloid origin (Auer rods, MPO+, or myeloid immunophenotype). Certain defining cytogenetic abnormalities [t(6;9), CBF] are diagnostic regardless of blast count.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
How did WHO/ICC/ELN 2022 change AML diagnosis?	The ≥20% blast cutoff is no longer required when an AML-defining genetic abnormality is present (e.g., PML::RARA, core-binding factor). NPM1-mutated AML is a distinct entity needing ≥10% blasts. Greater emphasis on MRD by NGS/flow.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
What finding on smear is diagnostic of AML?	Auer rods (needle-like azurophilic cytoplasmic inclusions).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Physical exam findings of AML?	Gingival hyperplasia and leukemia cutis (leukemic infiltration); Sweet syndrome (acute febrile neutrophilic dermatosis); conjunctival/palmar pallor (anemia); petechiae/purpura (thrombocytopenia).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
The four critical complications of AML?	Leukostasis, tumor lysis syndrome (TLS), disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), and febrile neutropenia.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Leukostasis — mechanism, threshold, and treatment?	Hyperleukocytosis + cytokines → ↑viscosity → impaired perfusion (neuro, respiratory symptoms). Typically WBC >100k (can occur ≥50k with symptoms). Treat by reducing WBCs: cytoreductive chemo/hydroxyurea ± leukapheresis. Leukapheresis is CONTRAINDICATED in APL (worsens coagulopathy).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Tumor lysis syndrome — labs and treatment?	Hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, hyperphosphatemia, HYPOcalcemia; risks AKI (crystal nephropathy) and arrhythmias. Prevent/treat with IV fluids + allopurinol; rasburicase for severe/high uric acid.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
DIC in AML — labs and management?	↑PT/PTT/INR and ↑D-dimer with LOW fibrinogen (key); schistocytes on smear. Supportive transfusions temporize; definitive treatment is treating the leukemia. Strongly associated with APL.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Febrile neutropenia — definition and empiric antibiotics?	Single oral T >38.3°C (or ≥38.0°C sustained ≥1 h) with ANC <500 (or expected <500 within 48 h). Empiric anti-pseudomonal monotherapy: cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam. Add MRSA coverage (vancomycin) only for catheter/SSTI/PNA infection or instability.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Why avoid acetaminophen in hospitalized neutropenic patients?	It can mask fever, which may be the only early sign of impending sepsis.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
What is APL and why is it special?	Acute promyelocytic leukemia — distinct treatment with ATRA + arsenic trioxide; excellent long-term prognosis, but the acute phase (DIC/bleeding) is the deadliest period. Avoid leukapheresis.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
ELN risk stratification and prognostic spread?	Sorts patients by cytogenetics + NGS into favorable / intermediate / adverse. 5-year OS roughly 46% (favorable) vs 18% (intermediate) vs 4% (adverse).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
AML treatment framework?	Induction (treat detectable cancer: 7+3 = 7 days cytarabine + 3 days anthracycline; Ven/HMA for unfit/elderly; ATRA/arsenic for APL) → consolidation (allogeneic SCT or further chemo) → maintenance; salvage/targeted therapy or hospice for relapsed/refractory disease.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Why is the adjuvant/neoadjuvant framework not used in AML?	Hematologic malignancies are treated with systemic therapy only — there's no surgery/radiation backbone, so induction/consolidation/maintenance replaces adjuvant/neoadjuvant language.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Newer targeted/maintenance agents in AML (2022–23)?	Quizartinib added to 7+3 for newly diagnosed FLT3-ITD+ (QuANTUM-First, FDA 2023); ivosidenib+azacitidine for newly dx unfit IDH1 (AGILE); olutasidenib for R/R IDH1; oral azacitidine (Onureg) maintenance (QUAZAR); CPX-351 for t-AML/AML-MR.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Primary vs secondary vs tertiary AI — where is the lesion?	Primary = adrenal cortex; secondary = pituitary (ACTH); tertiary = hypothalamus (CRH).	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Why does primary AI cause adrenal crisis more readily than central AI?	Primary AI also loses aldosterone (mineralocorticoid) → salt wasting, hypovolemia, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia. Central AI spares the RAAS-driven aldosterone axis.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
ACTH and aldosterone in primary AI?	ACTH high (loss of cortisol feedback → also hyperpigmentation); aldosterone low, renin high.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
ACTH and aldosterone in central (secondary/tertiary) AI?	ACTH low/normal; aldosterone preserved (RAAS intact). Tertiary: ACTH stays low even after CRH.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Most common cause of AI overall?	Exogenous glucocorticoids (tertiary, HPA suppression).	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Most common cause of primary AI in resource-rich settings?	Autoimmune adrenalitis (check 21-hydroxylase antibodies).	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Drug causes of AI?	Steroids, azoles, etomidate, mitotane (synthesis); rifampin/phenytoin/phenobarbital (↑metabolism); checkpoint inhibitors (hypophysitis + adrenalitis).	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
First diagnostic test for AI and why that time?	8 AM serum cortisol — cortisol peaks in the morning, maximizing sensitivity.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
8 AM cortisol interpretation (older assay)?	<3 µg/dL rules in; <10 suspicious; >18 rules out. Indeterminate → ACTH stimulation test.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
How do modern cortisol assays change the cutoffs?	Specific monoclonal immunoassays and LC-MS/MS read ~20-30% lower, so rule-out / stim-test peak threshold is ~14-15 µg/dL, not 18. Use your lab's assay-specific value.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
How is the ACTH (cosyntropin) stimulation test performed?	250 µg cosyntropin IV/IM; cortisol at baseline and 30-60 min; a subnormal peak confirms AI. Then ACTH level localizes primary vs central.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Why can early central AI pass a cosyntropin stim test?	The adrenals haven't yet atrophied and can still respond acutely. If suspicion is high, retest or use another test and treat empirically.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Replacement in primary AI?	Hydrocortisone 20-25 mg/24h split (AM-weighted) + fludrocortisone 0.1 mg qAM (mineralocorticoid) ± DHEA 25-50 mg.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Replacement in central AI?	Hydrocortisone 15-20 mg/24h split; no fludrocortisone needed (RAAS intact).	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Adrenal crisis presentation and treatment?	Hypotension/shock ± fever, abdominal pain, nausea, fatigue. Treat empirically: hydrocortisone 100 mg IV then 200 mg/24h (or 50 mg q6h) + IV fluids — before confirmatory testing.	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Which steroid preserves a pending cortisol stim test?	Dexamethasone — it does not cross-react in the cortisol assay (but never delay treatment for testing).	Endocrine::adrenal-insufficiency
Four conditions that make a disease worth screening for?	(1) Common in the population; (2) a good test exists (acceptable sensitivity/specificity, low-cost, easy, low-harm); (3) a detectable asymptomatic period; (4) early treatment beats late. Bottom line: benefits must outweigh harms/cost.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
What patient factors modify the decision to screen?	Comorbidities and life expectancy, patient preference, and whether diagnosis/treatment would actually be pursued if positive. Individualize by age, sex, and risk factors.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Five domains of adult disease prevention?	Cancer, Infectious Disease, Cardiovascular, Psychosocial, and Family/Safety.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Which cancers have established screening?	Only five: breast, colon, cervical, lung, and prostate.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Breast cancer screening (USPSTF 2024)?	All average-risk women ages 40–74, biennial mammography (Grade B). Stop at >74 or life expectancy <10 years. Clinical/self breast exams no longer routine.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Colon cancer screening (USPSTF)?	Average-risk adults 45–79 (45–49 Grade B; 50–75 Grade A; 76–85 Grade C, selective). Options: colonoscopy q10y, FIT/gFOBT q1y, stool FIT-DNA q3y, CT colonography or sigmoidoscopy q5y. Colonoscopy is the gold standard (needed if any other test is positive).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Cervical cancer screening — USPSTF 2025 update?	Ages 21–29: cytology alone every 3 years. When can cervical cancer screening stop?	After 65 with adequate normal prior screening (within 10 years), or after hysterectomy with cervix removal and no history of high-grade lesion/cervical cancer.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Lung cancer screening criteria?	Adults 50–80 with ≥20 pack-year history who currently smoke or quit <15 years ago (Grade B). Annual low-dose CT. Stop if quit >15 years or limited life expectancy. How do you calculate pack-years?	Packs per day × years smoked (e.g., 1 ppd × 20 years = 20 pack-years).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Prostate cancer screening?	Individualized PSA-based screening in men 55–69 (Grade C, shared decision-making) — discuss harms (false positives, biopsy complications, overdiagnosis/overtreatment). Adult immunizations everyone should receive?	Influenza annually; COVID-19 (at least each fall); Tdap once then Td/Tdap every 10 years.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Pneumococcal vaccination in adults (2024 ACIP)?	PCV21 for all adults ≥50, and adults 19–49 with chronic conditions/immunocompromise. If PCV15 is used instead, follow with PPSV23 (~1 year later).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Hepatitis B and HPV vaccination in adults?	Hep B: universal for adults 19–59 (≥60 only if risk factors). HPV: ages 19–26 (preferred), or up to 45 with shared decision-making.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Shingles and RSV vaccination?	Recombinant zoster (Shingrix): age ≥50, 2 doses 2–6 months apart. RSV: one dose for adults ≥75 (and 60–74 at increased risk) via shared decision-making (high relative efficacy but NNT >700).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Infection screening recommended for all adults?	HIV once for ages 15–65; hepatitis C once for ages 18–79. Others (syphilis, gonorrhea/chlamydia, hepatitis B, latent TB) are risk-based.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1

Three psychosocial substance-use screens recommended for all adults annually?	Alcohol (USPSTF: all adults), tobacco (assess every visit, Grade A), and unhealthy drug use (all adults). Each is a question-based screen, not a lab test.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2

What is a myeloproliferative neoplasm (MPN)?	A clonal disease of the hematopoietic stem cell causing overproduction of MATURE myeloid cells. (Contrast with MDS = abnormal/ineffective maturation.)	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
The four most common MPNs?	Essential thrombocythemia (ET), polycythemia vera (PV), primary myelofibrosis (PMF), and chronic myeloid leukemia (CML).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Which MPNs share driver mutations, and which?	ET, PV, and PMF are grouped by three common driver mutations: JAK2, CALR, MPL. CML is defined separately by BCR-ABL1.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Essential thrombocythemia — definition and mutations?	Clonal overproduction of platelets; associated with JAK2, CALR, or MPL. 10–20% are "triple negative" (no identifiable mutation) and may need a marrow biopsy.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
ET — symptoms?	Often asymptomatic; vasomotor symptoms, TIAs, ocular migraines, 1st-trimester pregnancy loss; thrombosis or bleeding less common.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Differential for thrombocytosis?	Reactive (iron-deficiency anemia — commonly missed; infection/inflammation; post-splenectomy) vs primary (the 4 MPNs).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
ET/PV treatment principle and risk stratification?	Goal is to prevent thrombosis; platelet/Hgb magnitude does NOT drive thrombosis risk (don't treat reactive thrombocytosis). Low risk (<60 yo, no prior thrombosis): observation + low-dose aspirin (PV: phlebotomy to Hct <45%). High risk (>60 yo OR prior thrombosis): add cytoreduction; anticoagulate if prior venous clot, aspirin if arterial.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Polycythemia vera — hallmark and key mutation?	Erythrocytosis (often with leukocytosis/thrombocytosis too); >95% carry JAK2 V617F. Suspect with Hgb >16.5 (M) / >16.0 (F), a 25% rise in RBC mass, or arterial/venous thrombosis.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
WHO 2022 PV diagnostic thresholds?	Hgb >16.5 (M) / >16.0 (F) OR Hct >49% (M) / >48% (F), plus marrow panmyelosis and a JAK2 mutation; subnormal EPO is the minor criterion.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Classic thromboses that should raise suspicion for PV?	Budd-Chiari, portal/splenic/mesenteric vein thrombosis (splanchnic) — portal hypertension can mask the lab signs of PV.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
EPO level across causes of erythrocytosis?	Relative (hemoconcentration/dehydration): normal EPO. Secondary (hypoxia — COPD/OSA/altitude/RAS; or EPO-secreting tumor — HCC/RCC/pheo): elevated EPO. PV: suppressed EPO (but normal in ~15%).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
PV treatment?	Phlebotomy (goal Hct <45%) + low-dose aspirin; add a cytoreductive agent (hydroxyurea or interferon) if high risk. Ropeginterferon alfa-2b (Besremi) is now an NCCN-preferred first-line cytoreductive.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Primary myelofibrosis — pathology and findings?	Marrow replaced by fibrosis (→ extramedullary hematopoiesis); JAK2/CALR/MPL; can evolve from PV or ET. Classic: teardrop cells (dacrocytes) on smear and a "dry tap" with megakaryocyte hyperplasia.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
PMF — symptoms and treatment?	Hepatosplenomegaly, bone pain, cytopenias (anemia, bleeding, infections). Only cure = allogeneic HSCT (for poor-prognosis transplant candidates); otherwise active surveillance/supportive care or JAK inhibitors (ruxolitinib).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Newer JAK inhibitors for myelofibrosis?	Four total: ruxolitinib, fedratinib, pacritinib (Vonjo — for severe thrombocytopenia, plt <50k), and momelotinib (Ojjaara — for MF with anemia). Chosen by blood counts.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Chronic myeloid leukemia — definition and genetics?	Malignancy of mature granulocytes; BCR-ABL1 fusion (Philadelphia chromosome, t(9;22)) → ↑tyrosine kinase activity. Granulocytes are morphologically normal but cytochemically abnormal with a LOW leukocyte alkaline phosphatase (LAP) score.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Differential for leukocytosis?	Leukemoid reaction (infection, stress, medications, tobacco) vs malignancy. Suspicious for CML: neutrophilia with left shift, basophilia, eosinophilia, constitutional symptoms, abdominal fullness; WBC can exceed 100k.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Natural history and treatment of CML?	Chronic phase (85% at diagnosis) → accelerated phase → blast crisis. First-line = a tyrosine kinase inhibitor (imatinib was the first); switch TKIs for resistance (BCR-ABL1 testing); blast phase may need induction chemo + TKI. Asciminib (Scemblix, a STAMP inhibitor) is approved for ≥2 prior TKIs or T315I.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
AML vs CML vs ALL vs CLL — smear/marrow snapshot?	AML: myeloblasts/Auer rods, >20% marrow blasts (DIC esp. APL). CML: eosinophilia/basophilia, median WBC 100k, granulocytic hyperplasia <20% blasts, t(9;22). ALL: lymphoblasts, >20% marrow lymphoblasts, children, CNS infiltration (LP before tx). CLL: lymphocytosis + smudge cells, median age 70, Richter transformation, autoimmune cytopenias.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Why is leukostasis rare in CML despite high WBC?	CML (and CLL) tolerate higher WBC counts than acute leukemias; leukostasis/hyperleukocytosis is a hematologic emergency far more typical of AML/ALL.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms

Intrinsic non-immune (non-MAHA) hemolysis categories?	Membrane (HS, elliptocytosis, stomatocytosis, PNH), Hb (sickle, thalassemia), metabolism (G6PD, pyruvate kinase deficiency).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
New iron deficiency in an older adult -- next step?	GI evaluation for occult blood loss (colon cancer until proven otherwise).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
