Normal values: pH, PCO2, HCO3, PO2 on an ABG?	pH 7.40 ± 0.05 (acidemia <7.35, alkalemia >7.45); PCO2 40 ± 4; HCO3 24 ± 2; PO2 >80 mmHg on room air.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
"-emia" vs "-osis"?	-emia describes the blood pH (acidemia/alkalemia); -osis is a metabolic or respiratory PROCESS that would derange pH if unchecked — several -oses can coexist with any pH.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
What are the steps of ABG interpretation?	1) Acid-base status (pH). 2) Primary process (match pH against PCO2 and HCO3). 3) Check anion gap in everyone (+ delta-delta if AGMA). 4) Assess compensation.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Anion gap: formula and normal value?	AG = Na − (Cl + HCO3); normal ≈ 8–12, but EXPECTED AG ≈ 2.5 × albumin (g/dL) — hypoalbuminemia lowers the "normal" gap and can hide an AGMA.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Why calculate an anion gap in every patient regardless of primary process?	An AGMA can be present even when the primary disorder is respiratory or a metabolic alkalosis — and an elevated AG is never a compensatory change.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
How does the "residual HCO3" (delta-delta) method work?	Add (measured AG − expected AG) back to the measured HCO3. Result normal → pure AGMA; high (>26) → concurrent metabolic alkalosis; low (<22) → concurrent NAGMA.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Differential for anion gap metabolic acidosis?	MUDPILES: Methanol, Uremia, DKA/ketoacidosis, Paraldehyde, INH/Iron, Lactic acidosis, Ethylene glycol (+EtOH), Salicylates.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Differential for non-gap metabolic acidosis (NAGMA)?	HCO3 loss via gut (diarrhea, ileostomy) or kidney (RTA, early renal failure, hypoaldosteronism) + excess Cl (normal saline).	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
How does the urinary anion gap separate NAGMA causes?	UAG = Na + K − Cl in urine. "neGUTive" = GI HCO3 loss; positive = renal loss (RTA). Unreliable in renal dysfunction.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Differential for respiratory acidosis?	Decreased CO2 elimination (obstructive/intrinsic lung disease, neuromuscular weakness, decreased respiratory drive — opioids/benzos) or increased CO2 production (sepsis, overfeeding, hyperthyroidism).	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Differential for respiratory alkalosis?	Anxiety/pain, fever/sepsis, CNS disease, drugs (salicylates), liver disease, PE — all drive hyperventilation.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Metabolic alkalosis: chloride-responsive vs non-responsive causes?	IVF(Cl)-responsive: vomiting/NGT losses, diuretics (thiazide/loop), contraction alkalosis. Non-responsive: hyperaldosteronism, genetic disorders, milk-alkali, hypokalemia.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Two general rules of compensation?	PCO2 and HCO3 move in the SAME direction; respiratory compensation is fast (minutes), metabolic (renal) compensation slow (2–3 days); compensation never overshoots or fully normalizes the pH.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Winter's formula (and the bedside shortcut)?	Expected PCO2 = 1.5 × HCO3 + 8 (± 2) for metabolic acidosis. Thumb rule: PCO2 should ≈ the last two digits of the pH (e.g., pH 7.32 → PCO2 ~32).	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Expected PCO2 in metabolic alkalosis?	PCO2 ≈ 0.7 × HCO3 + 21 — hypoventilation raises PCO2, but rarely much above ~55.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Expected HCO3 change in respiratory acidosis (per 10 mmHg PCO2 rise)?	Acute: HCO3 ↑ ~1; chronic (2–3 d): HCO3 ↑ ~4 — a COPD patient with PCO2 60 and HCO3 32 is chronically compensated.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
Expected HCO3 change in respiratory alkalosis (per 10 mmHg PCO2 fall)?	Acute: HCO3 ↓ ~2; chronic: HCO3 ↓ ~5.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
What does a "base excess" on the ABG represent?	The number of H+ ions required to restore normal pH if PCO2 were normal: positive (> +2) → metabolic alkalosis; negative (< −2) → metabolic acidosis.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
What does measured PCO2 ≠ expected PCO2 (by Winter's) mean?	A second primary respiratory process: measured > expected → concurrent respiratory acidosis; measured < expected → concurrent respiratory alkalosis.	Electrolytes::acid-base-disorders
You're called about a critical K of 6.7. What are the first two steps?	Confirm the lab (expected? hemolysis? concurrent AKI?) AND obtain a STAT ECG — any K > 5.5 warrants an ECG. Confirmation must not delay treatment.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
What is the ECG progression of worsening hyperkalemia?	Early: peaked T waves + QT shortening → P-wave flattening/loss with PR prolongation → QRS widening → sine wave, ventricular arrhythmia, or asystole. (Also pseudo-RBBB / pseudo-STE in V1–V2.)	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Do ECG changes correlate with the serum potassium level?	Poorly. ECG changes predict poor outcomes better than the absolute K, and a rapid rise is more arrhythmogenic than a slow one — chronic CKD/ESRD patients tolerate higher K.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
What triggers IV calcium in hyperkalemia?	ECG changes OR K > 6.5 (treat K > 6.5 as emergent regardless of ECG).	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Compare the two IV calcium formulations.	Calcium gluconate 1–2 g IV — preferred, peripheral OK. Calcium chloride 0.5–1 g IV — more potent but caustic; give via central line.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
IV calcium: onset, duration, effect on K, and re-dosing rule?	Onset 3–5 min, duration 30–60 min. No effect on serum K — it stabilizes the myocardium and should normalize the ECG; re-dose every 5–10 min if ECG changes persist.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Insulin/dextrose for hyperkalemia: dose, renal adjustment, effect?	Regular insulin 10U IV + 1 amp D50 (5U if renal impairment; skip D50 if BG > 250). Onset 15–30 min, lasts 4–6 h (longer in AKI). Lowers K ~0.5–1.0 mEq/L. Monitor glucose afterward — hypoglycemia is the most common complication.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Albuterol for hyperkalemia: dose, effect, caveat?	10–20 mg nebulized in 4 mL saline — far above the 2.5 mg bronchodilator dose. Lowers K 0.5–1.5 mEq/L, onset 15–30 min; most effective combined with insulin. Use caution in active coronary disease (tachycardia/angina).	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
When is sodium bicarbonate a reasonable shifting agent?	Only with metabolic acidosis and NOT in ESRD (shown ineffective on HD). 50–100 mEq IV. It is the least effective shifting strategy.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Why is intracellular shifting only a temporizing measure?	It moves K into cells without removing it from the body — serum K rebounds as the effect wears off, so always pair shifting with elimination (diuresis, binder, or dialysis).	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
How is potassium normally eliminated from the body?	~90% renal, ~10% GI (the basis for diuretics/dialysis vs. gut binders).	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Role of loop diuretics in hyperkalemia?	First-line if hypervolemic (furosemide 40–160 mg IV); add isotonic fluids if euvolemic to avoid volume depletion worsening AKI; ineffective in anuria.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
When is urgent dialysis indicated for hyperkalemia?	Severe renal dysfunction (especially anuria/ESRD without access to K elimination) or hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy — HD is the intervention most likely to achieve normal K within 4 h.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC/Lokelma): dosing, onset, downsides?	10 g TID for up to 48–72 h; onset ~1 h (fastest binder); works in small + large intestine. Downsides: cost/availability, sodium load → edema. UKKA 2023 now lists it as an option alongside shifting agents in acute life-threatening hyperkalemia (K ≥ 6.5).	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Kayexalate (SPS): dosing and contraindications?	15–60 g PO q6h (or 30–50 g PR). Risk of bowel necrosis — avoid post-op, ileus/obstruction, IBD, infectious colitis; separate from other meds by 3 h; skip it if dialysis is planned.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
Patient on an ACE inhibitor develops hyperkalemia — stop the drug?	Per KDIGO 2024: prefer lowering the K (review K-raising drugs/diet, diuretic, binder, bicarbonate if acidotic) over stopping the RASi — discontinuation is associated with higher mortality and CV events in CKD.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
What monitoring follows treatment of hyperkalemia?	Recheck K in 1–4 h and repeat ECG every 30–60 min until abnormalities resolve; telemetry; repeat temporizing + elimination measures until K normalizes or dialysis occurs.	Electrolytes::inpatient-management-of-hyperkalemia
What are the 3 labs (and 3 questions) for diagnosing hyponatremia?	Serum osm — "is it real?"; urine osm — "is ADH on or off?"; urine Na — "are the kidneys seeing enough fluid?"	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
Why not classify hyponatremia by volume status at the bedside?	Physician assessment of volume status is classically unreliable; the 3-lab approach interrogates the physiology (osmolality, ADH, renal perfusion) directly.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
What defines TRUE hyponatremia?	Hypo-osmolar hyponatremia: serum osm < 290. Normal or high serum osm means something else is in the blood.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
What is pseudohyponatremia?	Normal serum osm + low measured Na: excess lipids (hypertriglyceridemia) or protein (multiple myeloma) dilute whole plasma while water-plasma [Na] is normal. Confirm with a whole-blood Na.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
What causes hyperosmolar hyponatremia, and how do you correct the Na?	An extra ACTIVE osmole (glucose, mannitol, sorbitol) pulls water out of cells, diluting Na. Corrected Na = measured Na + 1.6 × (glucose − 100)/100. (BUN and ethanol are INACTIVE osmoles — they raise measured osm but cross membranes and do not cause hyponatremia.)	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
What does a urine osm < 100 mean in hyponatremia?	ADH is OFF — the only causes are excess free-water intake (primary polydipsia) or inadequate solute (beer potomania, tea-and-toast diet).	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
What does a high urine osm (>100) mean in hyponatremia?	ADH is ON (appropriately or not) — the kidney is concentrating urine; next ask whether ADH secretion is appropriate using the urine Na.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
What triggers ADH release physiologically?	Low volume/effective circulating volume (baroreceptors) and high serum osm — ADH makes the kidney reclaim free water, concentrating the urine.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
ADH on + urine Na < 25: what does it mean?	RAAS is activated — the kidneys see low volume: true hypovolemia (GI losses) OR low effective circulating volume with high total body volume (heart failure, cirrhosis).	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
ADH on + urine Na > 40: what's the differential?	The kidneys see enough fluid → inappropriate ADH: SIADH, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency — or diuretic use (salt wasting falsely raises urine Na).	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
What is the exception that breaks the "low urine Na = low volume" rule?	Diuretics (loop, thiazide) — they waste salt, so urine Na stays high even in a hypovolemic patient.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
Common SIADH triggers?	Medications (antidepressants, antiepileptics, antipsychotics), surgery/pain, brain injury, lung disease, malignancy. SIADH patients are euvolemic.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
How does cerebral salt wasting differ from SIADH?	Both follow CNS insults (esp. SAH) with high urine Na, but cerebral salt wasting patients are HYPOVOLEMIC; SIADH patients are euvolemic.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
How do hypothyroidism and adrenal insufficiency cause hyponatremia?	Both drive excess ADH secretion; the hyponatremia corrects when the hormone deficiency is replaced — always check TSH and cortisol in "SIADH-pattern" labs.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
A patient with alcohol use disorder has Na 125, SOsm 270, UOsm 60, UNa 65. Diagnosis?	Beer potomania — true (hypo-osmolar) hyponatremia with ADH OFF (UOsm < 100): solute intake too low to excrete the ingested water.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
A patient with DM1 has glucose 500 and Na 125 with SOsm 310. Interpretation?	Hyperosmolar hyponatremia from glucose: corrected Na = 125 + 1.6 × 4 = ~131 — a mild true hyponatremia (here from hypovolemia) plus glucose effect.	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
Has the 3-lab diagnostic approach changed in recent guidelines?	No — the 2024 Austrian Society for Nephrology consensus confirms serum osm, urine osm, and urine Na identify most causes of hyponatremia. (This lesson covers diagnosis only, not treatment.)	Electrolytes::evaluation-of-hyponatremia
