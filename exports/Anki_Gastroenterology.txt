Transfusion-threshold exception?	~8 g/dL with acute coronary syndrome or significant cardiovascular disease.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Why is over-transfusion dangerous in variceal bleeding?	It raises portal pressure and worsens/rebleeds varices — keep restrictive, avoid excess plasma/platelets.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Glasgow-Blatchford 0–1 means?	Very low risk — consider discharge with outpatient endoscopy.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
BUN:Cr > 30 without renal disease suggests?	Upper GI source (absorbed nitrogen from digested blood).	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Unstable hematochezia — assume what?	Brisk upper GI bleed until proven otherwise (upper endoscopy).	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Pre-endoscopy meds for suspected ulcer bleed?	IV PPI; IV erythromycin 250 mg ~20–90 min before to clear the stomach.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Endoscopy timing for most UGIB?	Within 24 h after resuscitation; <12 h doesn't help and may harm.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Variceal-bleed bundle?	Octreotide + prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone) + endoscopic band ligation; TIPS/tamponade as rescue.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Which variceal drug reduces mortality?	Prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone) reduce mortality.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Does the initial Hb reflect blood loss?	No — it lags; equilibration takes hours. Resuscitate to hemodynamics.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
Common causes of lower GI bleed?	Diverticulosis, angioectasia, post-polypectomy, ischemic/IBD colitis, hemorrhoids.	Gastroenterology::acute-gi-bleeding
