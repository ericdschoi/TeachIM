How common is AML and who gets it?	~1% of new cancers (~20k/yr in the US); predominantly elderly (median age 68, ~59% are >65). Any age can be affected.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
AML prognosis with and without treatment?	Without treatment: survival is days to weeks. With treatment: only ~1 in 3 (≈30%) reach 5-year survival.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Where is the pathology in AML?	Overproliferation of myeloid stem cells OR myeloblasts. Because these are precursors to RBCs, WBCs, and platelets, any CBC pattern is possible.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Three CBC patterns AML can produce?	Isolated leukocytosis (myeloblast → ↑WBC), leukocytosis + cytopenias (blasts crowd out/disrupt other lines), and pancytopenia (myeloid stem cell affected → proportionate declines).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Diagnostic criteria for AML?	(1) ≥20% blasts in bone marrow aspirate OR peripheral blood, AND (2) cells of myeloid origin (Auer rods, MPO+, or myeloid immunophenotype). Certain defining cytogenetic abnormalities [t(6;9), CBF] are diagnostic regardless of blast count.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
How did WHO/ICC/ELN 2022 change AML diagnosis?	The ≥20% blast cutoff is no longer required when an AML-defining genetic abnormality is present (e.g., PML::RARA, core-binding factor). NPM1-mutated AML is a distinct entity needing ≥10% blasts. Greater emphasis on MRD by NGS/flow.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
What finding on smear is diagnostic of AML?	Auer rods (needle-like azurophilic cytoplasmic inclusions).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Physical exam findings of AML?	Gingival hyperplasia and leukemia cutis (leukemic infiltration); Sweet syndrome (acute febrile neutrophilic dermatosis); conjunctival/palmar pallor (anemia); petechiae/purpura (thrombocytopenia).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
The four critical complications of AML?	Leukostasis, tumor lysis syndrome (TLS), disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), and febrile neutropenia.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Leukostasis — mechanism, threshold, and treatment?	Hyperleukocytosis + cytokines → ↑viscosity → impaired perfusion (neuro, respiratory symptoms). Typically WBC >100k (can occur ≥50k with symptoms). Treat by reducing WBCs: cytoreductive chemo/hydroxyurea ± leukapheresis. Leukapheresis is CONTRAINDICATED in APL (worsens coagulopathy).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Tumor lysis syndrome — labs and treatment?	Hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, hyperphosphatemia, HYPOcalcemia; risks AKI (crystal nephropathy) and arrhythmias. Prevent/treat with IV fluids + allopurinol; rasburicase for severe/high uric acid.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
DIC in AML — labs and management?	↑PT/PTT/INR and ↑D-dimer with LOW fibrinogen (key); schistocytes on smear. Supportive transfusions temporize; definitive treatment is treating the leukemia. Strongly associated with APL.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Febrile neutropenia — definition and empiric antibiotics?	Single oral T >38.3°C (or ≥38.0°C sustained ≥1 h) with ANC <500 (or expected <500 within 48 h). Empiric anti-pseudomonal monotherapy: cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam. Add MRSA coverage (vancomycin) only for catheter/SSTI/PNA infection or instability.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Why avoid acetaminophen in hospitalized neutropenic patients?	It can mask fever, which may be the only early sign of impending sepsis.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
What is APL and why is it special?	Acute promyelocytic leukemia — distinct treatment with ATRA + arsenic trioxide; excellent long-term prognosis, but the acute phase (DIC/bleeding) is the deadliest period. Avoid leukapheresis.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
ELN risk stratification and prognostic spread?	Sorts patients by cytogenetics + NGS into favorable / intermediate / adverse. 5-year OS roughly 46% (favorable) vs 18% (intermediate) vs 4% (adverse).	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
AML treatment framework?	Induction (treat detectable cancer: 7+3 = 7 days cytarabine + 3 days anthracycline; Ven/HMA for unfit/elderly; ATRA/arsenic for APL) → consolidation (allogeneic SCT or further chemo) → maintenance; salvage/targeted therapy or hospice for relapsed/refractory disease.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Why is the adjuvant/neoadjuvant framework not used in AML?	Hematologic malignancies are treated with systemic therapy only — there's no surgery/radiation backbone, so induction/consolidation/maintenance replaces adjuvant/neoadjuvant language.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
Newer targeted/maintenance agents in AML (2022–23)?	Quizartinib added to 7+3 for newly diagnosed FLT3-ITD+ (QuANTUM-First, FDA 2023); ivosidenib+azacitidine for newly dx unfit IDH1 (AGILE); olutasidenib for R/R IDH1; oral azacitidine (Onureg) maintenance (QUAZAR); CPX-351 for t-AML/AML-MR.	Hematology::acute-myeloid-leukemia
What is a myeloproliferative neoplasm (MPN)?	A clonal disease of the hematopoietic stem cell causing overproduction of MATURE myeloid cells. (Contrast with MDS = abnormal/ineffective maturation.)	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
The four most common MPNs?	Essential thrombocythemia (ET), polycythemia vera (PV), primary myelofibrosis (PMF), and chronic myeloid leukemia (CML).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Which MPNs share driver mutations, and which?	ET, PV, and PMF are grouped by three common driver mutations: JAK2, CALR, MPL. CML is defined separately by BCR-ABL1.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Essential thrombocythemia — definition and mutations?	Clonal overproduction of platelets; associated with JAK2, CALR, or MPL. 10–20% are "triple negative" (no identifiable mutation) and may need a marrow biopsy.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
ET — symptoms?	Often asymptomatic; vasomotor symptoms, TIAs, ocular migraines, 1st-trimester pregnancy loss; thrombosis or bleeding less common.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Differential for thrombocytosis?	Reactive (iron-deficiency anemia — commonly missed; infection/inflammation; post-splenectomy) vs primary (the 4 MPNs).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
ET/PV treatment principle and risk stratification?	Goal is to prevent thrombosis; platelet/Hgb magnitude does NOT drive thrombosis risk (don't treat reactive thrombocytosis). Low risk (<60 yo, no prior thrombosis): observation + low-dose aspirin (PV: phlebotomy to Hct <45%). High risk (>60 yo OR prior thrombosis): add cytoreduction; anticoagulate if prior venous clot, aspirin if arterial.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Polycythemia vera — hallmark and key mutation?	Erythrocytosis (often with leukocytosis/thrombocytosis too); >95% carry JAK2 V617F. Suspect with Hgb >16.5 (M) / >16.0 (F), a 25% rise in RBC mass, or arterial/venous thrombosis.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
WHO 2022 PV diagnostic thresholds?	Hgb >16.5 (M) / >16.0 (F) OR Hct >49% (M) / >48% (F), plus marrow panmyelosis and a JAK2 mutation; subnormal EPO is the minor criterion.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Classic thromboses that should raise suspicion for PV?	Budd-Chiari, portal/splenic/mesenteric vein thrombosis (splanchnic) — portal hypertension can mask the lab signs of PV.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
EPO level across causes of erythrocytosis?	Relative (hemoconcentration/dehydration): normal EPO. Secondary (hypoxia — COPD/OSA/altitude/RAS; or EPO-secreting tumor — HCC/RCC/pheo): elevated EPO. PV: suppressed EPO (but normal in ~15%).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
PV treatment?	Phlebotomy (goal Hct <45%) + low-dose aspirin; add a cytoreductive agent (hydroxyurea or interferon) if high risk. Ropeginterferon alfa-2b (Besremi) is now an NCCN-preferred first-line cytoreductive.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Primary myelofibrosis — pathology and findings?	Marrow replaced by fibrosis (→ extramedullary hematopoiesis); JAK2/CALR/MPL; can evolve from PV or ET. Classic: teardrop cells (dacrocytes) on smear and a "dry tap" with megakaryocyte hyperplasia.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
PMF — symptoms and treatment?	Hepatosplenomegaly, bone pain, cytopenias (anemia, bleeding, infections). Only cure = allogeneic HSCT (for poor-prognosis transplant candidates); otherwise active surveillance/supportive care or JAK inhibitors (ruxolitinib).	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Newer JAK inhibitors for myelofibrosis?	Four total: ruxolitinib, fedratinib, pacritinib (Vonjo — for severe thrombocytopenia, plt <50k), and momelotinib (Ojjaara — for MF with anemia). Chosen by blood counts.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Chronic myeloid leukemia — definition and genetics?	Malignancy of mature granulocytes; BCR-ABL1 fusion (Philadelphia chromosome, t(9;22)) → ↑tyrosine kinase activity. Granulocytes are morphologically normal but cytochemically abnormal with a LOW leukocyte alkaline phosphatase (LAP) score.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Differential for leukocytosis?	Leukemoid reaction (infection, stress, medications, tobacco) vs malignancy. Suspicious for CML: neutrophilia with left shift, basophilia, eosinophilia, constitutional symptoms, abdominal fullness; WBC can exceed 100k.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Natural history and treatment of CML?	Chronic phase (85% at diagnosis) → accelerated phase → blast crisis. First-line = a tyrosine kinase inhibitor (imatinib was the first); switch TKIs for resistance (BCR-ABL1 testing); blast phase may need induction chemo + TKI. Asciminib (Scemblix, a STAMP inhibitor) is approved for ≥2 prior TKIs or T315I.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
AML vs CML vs ALL vs CLL — smear/marrow snapshot?	AML: myeloblasts/Auer rods, >20% marrow blasts (DIC esp. APL). CML: eosinophilia/basophilia, median WBC 100k, granulocytic hyperplasia <20% blasts, t(9;22). ALL: lymphoblasts, >20% marrow lymphoblasts, children, CNS infiltration (LP before tx). CLL: lymphocytosis + smudge cells, median age 70, Richter transformation, autoimmune cytopenias.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
Why is leukostasis rare in CML despite high WBC?	CML (and CLL) tolerate higher WBC counts than acute leukemias; leukostasis/hyperleukocytosis is a hematologic emergency far more typical of AML/ALL.	Hematology::myeloproliferative-neoplasms
What does CRABI stand for in plasma cell dyscrasias?	hyperCalcemia, Renal dysfunction, Anemia, Boney lytic lesions, Immunodeficiency — the end-organ damage that defines MM.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Three screening tests for a plasma cell dyscrasia?	SPEP + serum immunofixation + serum free light chains (FLC).	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Sensitivity: SPEP+IFX alone vs +UPEP vs +FLC?	~80% → ~95% (add UPEP) → ~99% (add serum FLC).	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Why can a urine dipstick miss myeloma?	Dipstick detects albumin only; it misses monoclonal free light chains (Bence Jones protein).	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
What % of myeloma is light-chain-only (missed by SPEP)?	Up to ~16% — caught by serum FLC.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Normal serum κ/λ free light chain ratio?	0.26–1.65. >1.65 = κ clone; <0.26 = λ clone.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Why is the FLC ratio unreliable in renal failure, and the workaround?	Reduced light-chain clearance raises the ratio (up to ~3); use stricter cutoffs (>4 or <0.25) to flag a true clone.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
MGUS diagnostic criteria?	M-protein <3 g/dL, <10% clonal marrow plasma cells, NO CRABI/end-organ damage.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Smoldering myeloma criteria?	M-protein ≥3 g/dL (or ≥500 mg/24h) OR 10–60% clonal marrow plasma cells, but NO CRABI.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
What turns SMM into MM?	CRABI end-organ damage OR a myeloma-defining biomarker (≥60% marrow, FLC ratio ≥100, >1 focal MRI lesion).	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Progression risk: MGUS vs SMM?	MGUS ~1%/yr; SMM ~10%/yr for the first 5 years (then ~5%/yr).	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Most sensitive imaging for myeloma bone disease?	PET/CT (gold standard); whole-body low-dose CT or MRI > plain whole-body X-ray, which misses small lytic lesions.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Labs to order at MM diagnosis for R-ISS staging?	β2-microglobulin (mg/L), albumin, LDH, and FISH cytogenetics.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
R-ISS stage III definition & median OS?	β2M ≥5.5 mg/L AND (elevated LDH OR high-risk FISH); median OS ~43 months.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
What does R2-ISS add to R-ISS (2022)?	Gain/amplification of 1q21 — a 4-tier additive score (D'Agostino, JCO 2022).	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Current standard front-line induction for transplant-eligible MM?	Quadruplet: anti-CD38 mAb (daratumumab) + VRd (D-VRd) — PERSEUS, NEJM 2024.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
autoSCT eligibility cutoffs in MM?	~age <75, ECOG <3, no cirrhosis/severe HF. Renal disease is NOT a contraindication.	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
Prophylaxis: bortezomib vs lenalidomide?	Bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor) → VZV/acyclovir prophylaxis; lenalidomide (IMiD) → VTE prophylaxis (ASA or anticoagulation).	Hematology::plasma-cell-dyscrasias
The four VTE-management questions?	IF (treat?), WHERE (inpatient vs outpatient), WHICH (agent), HOW LONG (duration).	Hematology::management-of-vte
Which clots are "definitely treat"?	Proximal LE DVT (popliteal and above) and main/segmental PE.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Isolated distal (below-knee) DVT -- approach?	If low-risk/asymptomatic, surveil with repeat duplex in 1-2 weeks; treat if it extends or is severely symptomatic/high-risk.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Isolated subsegmental PE without proximal DVT -- approach?	LE duplex; if low recurrence risk, surveillance is an option over anticoagulation (CHEST 2021).	Hematology::management-of-vte
What decides inpatient vs outpatient PE?	PESI/sPESI: PESI <85 (class I-II) -> outpatient if no high bleed risk, instability, or social barriers.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Overrides to admit a low-PESI PE?	High bleeding risk, unreliable disposition, poor compliance, hemodynamic instability/RV strain.	Hematology::management-of-vte
First-line agent for stable VTE?	A DOAC (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban).	Hematology::management-of-vte
Which DOACs need a heparin lead-in?	Dabigatran and edoxaban need ~5 days of parenteral heparin first; apixaban and rivaroxaban do not.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Cancer-associated VTE -- preferred agent?	A DOAC (apixaban/edoxaban/rivaroxaban) is now first-line over LMWH (CARAVAGGIO; ASH/CHEST 2021).	Hematology::management-of-vte
Cancer VTE caveat for luminal GI malignancy?	Prefer apixaban or LMWH (rivaroxaban/edoxaban raise GI bleeding).	Hematology::management-of-vte
Antiphospholipid syndrome -- agent?	Warfarin, NOT a DOAC (TRAPS: excess arterial events with rivaroxaban in triple-positive APS).	Hematology::management-of-vte
When is warfarin preferred over a DOAC?	Antiphospholipid syndrome, mechanical valves, CrCl <25-30, cost/access; needs heparin bridge until INR therapeutic.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Provoked VTE (reversible cause) -- duration?	~3 months, then stop.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Unprovoked VTE without high bleed risk -- duration?	Indefinite anticoagulation.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Provoked by an irreversible cause (active cancer, APS) -- duration?	Indefinite, as long as the cause persists.	Hematology::management-of-vte
Extended-phase DOAC dosing to reduce bleeding?	Apixaban 2.5 mg BID (AMPLIFY-EXT) or rivaroxaban 10 mg daily (EINSTEIN-CHOICE).	Hematology::management-of-vte
First branch point in any anemia work-up?	Reticulocyte index: <2 = underproduction (marrow); >=3 = overproduction (blood loss, hemolysis, sequestration).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
What drops Hgb without deranging the retic index?	Hemodilution -- aggressive IVF (inpatient) or hypervolemia/HF (outpatient).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Caveat to the retic index?	May be falsely low/normal if an underproduction process coexists with bleeding/hemolysis.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Macrocytic underproduction causes?	Megaloblastic (B12/folate, drugs: HU/allopurinol/HIV meds), liver/ETOH, severe hypothyroidism, primary marrow (MDS, esp. MCV >120).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Microcytic underproduction causes?	Iron deficiency, thalassemia, sideroblastic (lead, Zn, INH/linezolid), anemia of chronic inflammation.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
When do you shift toward primary marrow pathology?	When other cell lines are also affected (MDS, aplastic anemia, leukemia, myeloma).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Iron studies in iron deficiency?	Low Fe, high TIBC, low %sat (<20% suggestive, <10% very), low ferritin (<30 ng/mL diagnostic).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Iron studies in anemia of chronic disease?	Low Fe, low-normal TIBC, low-normal %sat, normal-high ferritin (acute-phase reactant).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Does a low ferritin confirm iron deficiency in inflammation?	Yes -- inflammation only raises ferritin; ferritin <30 is diagnostic even with active inflammation.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
ACD + concurrent iron deficiency -- how common and how to tell?	20-85% of ACD. Clue: very low TSAT; confirm with sTfR, hepcidin, or response to an iron trial.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Labs that confirm hemolysis?	High LDH, low haptoglobin, high indirect bilirubin, + peripheral smear.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
What does the DAT (direct Coombs) distinguish?	Immune (DAT-positive, antibodies on RBCs -> AIHA) vs non-immune (DAT-negative) hemolysis.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Warm vs cold AIHA?	Warm = IgG (cancer/lymphoma, SLE, drugs); Cold = IgM (lymphoma, mycoplasma, mono; also PNH, syphilis). Smear: spherocytes.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Smear clue for MAHA, and what it means with low platelets?	Schistocytes; with thrombocytopenia -> thrombotic microangiopathy (TTP/HUS) -- an emergency.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Primary vs secondary TMA?	Primary: TTP/HUS, drug-induced. Secondary/TMA-like: DIC (low fibrinogen), HELLP. Other: malignant HTN, prosthetic valve/LVAD/TIPS, scleroderma renal crisis.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Suspected TTP -- what to do?	PLASMIC score, send ADAMTS13 BEFORE plasma exchange, start PEX if TTP likely; treat confirmed TTP with PEX + steroids + caplacizumab.	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
Intrinsic non-immune (non-MAHA) hemolysis categories?	Membrane (HS, elliptocytosis, stomatocytosis, PNH), Hb (sickle, thalassemia), metabolism (G6PD, pyruvate kinase deficiency).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
New iron deficiency in an older adult -- next step?	GI evaluation for occult blood loss (colon cancer until proven otherwise).	Hematology::work-up-of-anemia
