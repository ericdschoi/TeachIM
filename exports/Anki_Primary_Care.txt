Four conditions that make a disease worth screening for?	(1) Common in the population; (2) a good test exists (acceptable sensitivity/specificity, low-cost, easy, low-harm); (3) a detectable asymptomatic period; (4) early treatment beats late. Bottom line: benefits must outweigh harms/cost.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
What patient factors modify the decision to screen?	Comorbidities and life expectancy, patient preference, and whether diagnosis/treatment would actually be pursued if positive. Individualize by age, sex, and risk factors.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Five domains of adult disease prevention?	Cancer, Infectious Disease, Cardiovascular, Psychosocial, and Family/Safety.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Which cancers have established screening?	Only five: breast, colon, cervical, lung, and prostate.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Breast cancer screening (USPSTF 2024)?	All average-risk women ages 40–74, biennial mammography (Grade B). Stop at >74 or life expectancy <10 years. Clinical/self breast exams no longer routine.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Colon cancer screening (USPSTF)?	Average-risk adults 45–79 (45–49 Grade B; 50–75 Grade A; 76–85 Grade C, selective). Options: colonoscopy q10y, FIT/gFOBT q1y, stool FIT-DNA q3y, CT colonography or sigmoidoscopy q5y. Colonoscopy is the gold standard (needed if any other test is positive).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Cervical cancer screening — USPSTF 2025 update?	Ages 21–29: cytology alone every 3 years. Ages 30–65: high-risk HPV PRIMARY screening every 5 years is now PREFERRED; self-collected (patient-collected) HPV samples are endorsed for the first time. Alternatives 30–65: cytology q3y or HPV+cytology co-test q5y. (ACS starts at 25.)	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
When can cervical cancer screening stop?	After 65 with adequate normal prior screening (within 10 years), or after hysterectomy with cervix removal and no history of high-grade lesion/cervical cancer.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Lung cancer screening criteria?	Adults 50–80 with ≥20 pack-year history who currently smoke or quit <15 years ago (Grade B). Annual low-dose CT. Stop if quit >15 years or limited life expectancy. Medicare requires a documented shared-decision-making visit before the first scan.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
How do you calculate pack-years?	Packs per day × years smoked (e.g., 1 ppd × 20 years = 20 pack-years).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Prostate cancer screening?	Individualized PSA-based screening in men 55–69 (Grade C, shared decision-making) — discuss harms (false positives, biopsy complications, overdiagnosis/overtreatment). Stop after 70. Digital rectal exam is NOT recommended for screening.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Adult immunizations everyone should receive?	Influenza annually; COVID-19 (at least each fall); Tdap once then Td/Tdap every 10 years.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Pneumococcal vaccination in adults (2024 ACIP)?	PCV21 for all adults ≥50, and adults 19–49 with chronic conditions/immunocompromise. If PCV15 is used instead, follow with PPSV23 (~1 year later).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Hepatitis B and HPV vaccination in adults?	Hep B: universal for adults 19–59 (≥60 only if risk factors). HPV: ages 19–26 (preferred), or up to 45 with shared decision-making.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Shingles and RSV vaccination?	Recombinant zoster (Shingrix): age ≥50, 2 doses 2–6 months apart. RSV: one dose for adults ≥75 (and 60–74 at increased risk) via shared decision-making (high relative efficacy but NNT >700).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Infection screening recommended for all adults?	HIV once for ages 15–65; hepatitis C once for ages 18–79. Others (syphilis, gonorrhea/chlamydia, hepatitis B, latent TB) are risk-based.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Gonorrhea/chlamydia screening — who?	All sexually active women <24 (Grade B); older women with risk factors (new/multiple partners, partner with an STI or concurrent partners, inconsistent barrier use).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Why does lung cancer screening uniquely require a shared-decision-making visit?	Medicare mandates a documented SDM conversation (eligibility, decision aid, counseling on radiation/overdiagnosis/false positives and on smoking cessation) before the first low-dose CT — a response to direct-to-consumer CT advertising.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-1
Three psychosocial substance-use screens recommended for all adults annually?	Alcohol (USPSTF: all adults), tobacco (assess every visit, Grade A), and unhealthy drug use (all adults). Each is a question-based screen, not a lab test.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Preferred alcohol screening tools?	Single Alcohol Screening Question (SASQ: "How many times in the past year have you had 5 [4 for women] or more drinks in a day?") or AUDIT-C. CAGE assesses dependence and is NOT for screening.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
NIAAA thresholds for unhealthy alcohol use?	Men 21–64: >4 drinks/day or >14/week. Men ≥65 and women of all ages: >3 drinks/day or >7/week.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Single-question drug-use screen?	"How many times in the past year have you used an illegal drug or used a prescription medication for non-medical reasons?"	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
USPSTF mental-health screening recommendations?	Screen all adults for depression; screen adults <65 for anxiety (no defined interval). Tools: PHQ-2/PHQ-4/PHQ-9 (depression), GAD-7 (anxiety).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Hypertension screening?	Office BP for all adults; annually if age ≥40 or increased risk. Confirm elevated readings with ambulatory or home BP before treating.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Obesity screening and intervention?	Measure BMI in all adults (Grade B). BMI ≥30 → refer to intensive multicomponent behavioral intervention. Classes: 1 (30–34.9), 2 (35–39.9), 3 (≥40).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Diabetes screening — ADA vs USPSTF?	ADA: screen all at age 35, or any age if overweight/obese (BMI ≥25, or ≥23 in Asian Americans) plus ≥1 risk factor. USPSTF: 35–70 if overweight/obese. A1c, fasting glucose, or OGTT; repeat ≥q3y (yearly if prediabetes).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Lipid disorder screening (USPSTF)?	Screen/treat ages 40–75 with ≥1 CV risk factor and 10-year CV risk >10% (Grade B); consider a statin at 7.5–10% risk. Insufficient evidence <40 or >75. Repeat ~q5y low-risk, q3y high-risk; need not be fasting for screening.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
AAA screening?	One-time abdominal ultrasound in men 65–75 who have ever smoked. Selective in never-smoking men in that range. Not recommended for women.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Physical activity recommendation for adults?	150–300 min/week moderate-intensity OR 75–150 min/week vigorous aerobic activity, plus muscle-strengthening ≥2 days/week.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Folic acid for those who may become pregnant?	0.4–0.8 mg (400–800 µg) daily for all women planning or capable of pregnancy (USPSTF).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Falls screening in older adults?	Screen adults >65 (or with risk factors) at least annually with 3 questions: feel unsteady? worry about falling? fallen in the past year? A "yes" to any = at risk → offer exercise interventions (CDC STEADI).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Osteoporosis screening?	DEXA in women ≥65, and postmenopausal women <65 at increased risk. USPSTF finds insufficient evidence to screen men (under review).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Intimate partner violence screening?	Screen all reproductive-age women, annually/since last visit, in private. Tools: HITS, HARK, PVS, WAST. (USPSTF has no recommendation for screening older/vulnerable adults for abuse — but be alert to it.)	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Advance care planning — who and when?	Consider for adults of ALL ages (more emphasis with multiple comorbidities or older age): durable medical power of attorney, living will, and orders for scope of treatment (e.g., MOST/POLST, varies by state).	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
2026 ACC/AHA dyslipidemia guideline — main change to CV risk estimation?	Use the PREVENT calculator (ages 30–79) instead of the Pooled Cohort Equations (which overestimated risk ~40–50%). Risk tiers: low <3%, borderline 3–<5%, intermediate 5–<10%, high ≥10%. Theme: "earlier and lower, for longer."	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
2026 ACC/AHA LDL-C targets and earlier-statin triggers?	Targets: very-high-risk <55, high-risk <70, intermediate on statin <100 mg/dL. Start statins earlier in familial hypercholesterolemia, those ≤30 with LDL ≥160, strong family ASCVD history, or high 30-year PREVENT risk.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Pneumococcal vaccination update (2024 ACIP)?	PCV21 is now an option for all adults ≥50 (and 19–49 with chronic disease), with the age lowered from 65; PCV20 remains valid. If PCV15 is used, follow with PPSV23.	Primary_Care::adult-disease-prevention-part-2
Four independent axes to distinguish in gender care?	Gender identity, gender expression, sex assigned at birth (AMAB/AFAB), and sexual orientation.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
The four patient-centered intake questions in gender care?	What name do you go by? / What pronouns do you use? / How would you describe your current gender identity? / What sex was assigned to you at birth?	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
What to do after misnaming or misgendering a patient?	Recognize, apologize, commit to change, move on (briefly; don't over-apologize).	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Who can diagnose gender dysphoria and start GAHT?	A generalist, using DSM-5 criteria and an informed-consent model. Gender incongruence is NOT a mental illness.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Informed-consent checklist before starting GAHT?	Capacity; screen contraindications; discuss expectations/changes/side effects; offer fertility counseling.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Two pillars of feminizing hormone therapy?	Estrogen (17-beta-estradiol) PLUS an anti-androgen (spironolactone, 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor, or GnRH agonist).	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Feminizing hormone targets?	Estradiol 100-200 pg/mL; suppress total testosterone <50 ng/dL.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Masculinizing therapy and its target?	Testosterone (single agent); goal trough total testosterone 400-700 ng/dL.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
GAHT monitoring schedule?	Levels plus exam at 3 and 6 months, then annually.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Feminizing labs to monitor?	Estradiol, total testosterone, LFTs, lipids; add potassium/creatinine if on spironolactone.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Masculinizing labs to monitor?	Total testosterone (plus or minus estradiol) and hematocrit/CBC (erythrocytosis).	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Irreversible feminizing change? Irreversible masculinizing changes?	Feminizing: breast growth. Masculinizing: voice deepening and clitoral enlargement.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Key adverse effect of each: estrogen, spironolactone, testosterone?	Estrogen -> VTE; spironolactone -> hyperkalemia (and orthostasis); testosterone -> erythrocytosis.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Lowest-VTE-risk estrogen formulation? Highest?	Lowest: transdermal (patch) estradiol. Highest: oral, especially ethinyl estradiol.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
DVT on estradiol - how to manage?	Patient-centered shared decision; if continuing, switch to transdermal plus therapeutic anticoagulation; stop only for symptomatic PE or instability.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
Hold GAHT when admitted or perioperatively?	No - do not hold, EXCEPT estradiol in a patient with VTE risk factors.	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
What drives cancer screening in transgender patients?	The organs that are present, not gender identity (e.g., retained cervix -> cervical screening).	Primary_Care::gender-affirming-care
What is the central trade-off in managing immunosuppression after solid organ transplant?	More immunosuppression lowers rejection risk but raises the risk of infection, malignancy, and drug toxicity — and vice versa.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
Rank lung, liver, and heart/kidney transplants by the degree of immunosuppression required (allogenicity).	Lung > heart/kidney > liver. Lung transplants are most likely to reject (~50% at 5 yr); liver is least likely.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What is the difference between induction and maintenance immunosuppression?	Induction = intense, short-term immunosuppression given at the time of transplant (anti-lymphocyte/anti–IL-2 antibodies + high-dose steroids). Maintenance = lower-intensity long-term therapy, typically CNI + antimetabolite ± prednisone.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
Name the three core maintenance immunosuppressant classes and an example of each.	Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine); antimetabolites (mycophenolate, azathioprine); steroids (prednisone). mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus) are an additional class.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
How does allogenicity (rejection risk) change over time after transplant?	It is highest immediately/within the first month and decreases over time, allowing immunosuppression to be reduced accordingly.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
When is a FIRST episode of acute rejection highly unlikely?	Beyond 6 months from transplant, assuming good medication adherence.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What are the two main types of acute rejection and how is each diagnosed?	Acute cellular (T-cell mediated; biopsy shows lymphocytic/inflammatory infiltrate) and acute humoral/antibody-mediated (biopsy shows C4d deposition, or detection of donor-specific antibodies).	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What is the first-line treatment of acute cellular rejection?	Pulse-dose methylprednisolone (e.g., 1 g IV × 3 days), ± anti-lymphocyte/anti–IL-2 antibody for severe or steroid-refractory cases.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What infections predominate in the FIRST month after transplant?	Nosocomial infections (surgical-site, line, catheter, C. diff) and donor- or recipient-derived infections; opportunistic infections are uncommon this early.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What infections predominate 1–6 months after transplant?	Reactivation and opportunistic infections — CMV, BK, HSV/VZV, TB, HBV, plus PJP, Cryptococcus, Toxoplasma. Incidence is reduced by prophylaxis.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What infections predominate beyond 6 months after transplant?	Community-acquired infections (and late viral infections such as HSV, HBV, HCV, CMV); opportunistic infections become less common with reduced immunosuppression.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
How much higher is malignancy risk in transplant recipients, and which cancers predominate?	About 2× the general population, especially virally mediated cancers — lymphoma/PTLD (EBV), skin and anogenital cancers (HPV), liver cancer.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What is PTLD and when does it typically occur?	Post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder — an EBV-driven lymphoid proliferation. It occurs early (typically the first year); EBV positivity and degree of immunosuppression are key risk factors.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What are the major categories of post-transplant complications?	Rejection, medication side effects, infection, malignancy, and surgical complications (leaks, kinks, fistulas, stenoses).	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What are the key toxicities of calcineurin inhibitors?	Nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, and metabolic syndrome (hypertension, hyperlipidemia, hyperglycemia).	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What is belatacept and a key contraindication?	A CD28/CD80-86 costimulation blocker used as CNI-sparing maintenance immunosuppression (FDA 2011); associated with better long-term GFR and fewer donor-specific antibodies. CONTRAINDICATED in EBV-seronegative recipients due to PTLD/PML risk.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
Which CMV prophylaxis agent was FDA-approved in 2023 for high-risk kidney transplant, and why use it?	Letermovir (Prevymis), for high-risk (donor-positive/recipient-negative) kidney transplant recipients — an alternative to valganciclovir with less myelosuppression. Maribavir (2021) is used for refractory/resistant CMV.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
What is the status of OKT3 (muromonab-CD3)?	Discontinued in 2010. Current T-cell–depleting induction agents are rabbit anti-thymocyte globulin (rATG) and alemtuzumab; basiliximab (anti–IL-2 receptor) is the non-depleting option.	Primary_Care::transplant-medicine
