Most common category of AKI in cirrhosis, and prevalence?	Pre-renal — 60–70%. Intrarenal 30–40%, post-renal <1%.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Two things you must always rule out in a cirrhotic with new AKI?	Infection (diagnostic paracentesis for SBP — can be painless) and GI bleeding.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
How much does AKI raise mortality in cirrhosis?	2–6 fold.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Physiologic definition of HRS?	Pre-renal AKI driven by systemic (splanchnic) vasodilation that does NOT respond to volume — no intrinsic renal disease.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
How is HRS confirmed at the bedside?	Stop diuretics + albumin challenge 1 g/kg/day (max 100 g/day) ×48 h: no Cr improvement, with no shock, no nephrotoxins, no structural kidney disease.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Modern HRS nomenclature (vs. old type 1/2)?	HRS-1 → HRS-AKI (ICA-AKI criteria: ↑Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 h or ≥50% in 7 d; old fixed Cr >2.5 dropped). HRS-2 → HRS-NAKI / HRS-CKD. (ICA 2015; AASLD 2021.)	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Core medical treatment of HRS-AKI?	Albumin to expand effective arterial volume + a vasoconstrictor to restore tone.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
US vasoconstrictor options for HRS-AKI in 2026?	Terlipressin (FDA-approved 2022) or norepinephrine (ICU). Midodrine + octreotide + albumin is the older, less effective ward regimen.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Terlipressin — when to avoid?	ACLF grade 3, pre-existing hypoxia, or SCr >5 mg/dL (respiratory-failure signal; benefit unlikely).	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Only definitive treatment for HRS?	Liver transplant (some need combined liver-kidney transplant). TIPS and HD are bridges.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Albumin regimen that reduces HRS and death in SBP?	1.5 g/kg on day 1 + 1 g/kg on day 3; greatest benefit if Tbili >4, Cr >1, or BUN >30 (Sort, NEJM 1999).	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Ascitic fluid threshold diagnosing SBP?	≥250 PMNs/µL → start empiric cefotaxime/ceftriaxone (fluoroquinolone if penicillin-allergic).	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Why use FeUrea instead of FeNa in cirrhosis?	Diuretics raise urinary Na and inflate FeNa; FeUrea <35% supports pre-renal AKI.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Why can urine Na be low even in ATN in advanced cirrhosis?	Persistent RAAS activation (aldosterone) reclaims urinary sodium; granular casts can also appear with severe hyperbilirubinemia — both unreliable for ATN here.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
Pathophysiology one-liner for decompensated cirrhosis → AKI?	↓Hepatic metabolism → splanchnic vasodilation → ↓effective arterial volume → RAAS/ADH/sympathetic activation → renal vasoconstriction + Na/water retention → hyponatremia + pre-renal AKI.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
AIN clue in a cirrhotic with AKI?	WBC casts + NSAID exposure (intrarenal cause); stop the offending drug.	Renal::aki-in-cirrhosis
What two complementary frameworks organize the differential for AKI in a kidney transplant recipient?	(1) Anatomic — pre-renal, intra-renal, post-renal; and (2) timing from transplant. Always keep BOTH transplant-specific and usual AKI causes on the list.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
Name the 5 transplant-specific causes of allograft dysfunction.	CNI (calcineurin-inhibitor) toxicity — acute and chronic; renal artery stenosis/thrombosis; viral infection (BK, CMV, adenovirus); acute rejection; ureteral stenosis.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
By what mechanism does ACUTE CNI toxicity cause AKI, and when does it typically occur?	Vasoconstriction of both afferent and efferent arterioles (a pre-renal/hemodynamic insult). Most common in the first 3 months post-transplant.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
What are the clinical and lab clues to acute CNI toxicity?	Neurotoxicity (headache, tremor, paresthesias, seizures), hypertension, rarely HUS; labs show ↑K and ↑uric acid; usually an elevated CNI trough level.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
Does a CNI trough within the goal range exclude chronic CNI toxicity?	No. Chronic CNI toxicity causes a gradual, asymptomatic rise in Cr and is diagnosed on biopsy (glomerulosclerosis, interstitial fibrosis, adventitial nodule — all nonspecific); a normal trough does NOT exclude it.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
When does hyperacute rejection occur and what mediates it?	Within the first 24 hours; antibody-mediated by pre-formed donor-specific antibodies (DSAs).	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
When does acute rejection most commonly occur, and what late trigger should you suspect?	Within the first 6 months; later episodes suggest medication non-adherence. Types: acute cellular (T-cell mediated) and acute humoral/antibody-mediated.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
How is acute rejection diagnosed?	Biopsy is the standard. Acute cellular → biopsy; acute antibody-mediated → biopsy + DSAs (± donor-derived cell-free DNA). Clues: graft pain, fever, hypertension, proteinuria (esp. >1 g/d), hematuria.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
What is the typical timing and progression of BK polyomavirus nephropathy?	Can occur anytime but peaks ~10–13 months post-transplant. Progression: BK viruria → viremia → BK nephropathy.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
What is the preferred test to screen for and diagnose BK, per the 2024 consensus?	Plasma (serum) BK PCR is the preferred screening/diagnostic test — screen monthly until ~9 months, then q3 months to 2 years. Urine BK PCR/decoy cells are a sensitive early screen; biopsy confirms nephropathy.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
Which CMV donor/recipient serostatus carries the highest risk of CMV disease?	CMV donor-positive / recipient-negative (D+/R−). CMV nephritis typically occurs after 3 months; clues: fever, malaise, arthralgias, ↓WBC and ↓platelets; diagnose with CMV PCR ± biopsy.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
When does renal artery stenosis typically present in a transplant, and how is it diagnosed?	Most common between 3 months and 2 years (atherosclerosis, trauma, ischemia; risk rises with CMV infection). Clues: hypertension, edema, ↓RBCs. Diagnosis: renal duplex ultrasound.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
When does vascular (renal artery/vein) thrombosis occur after transplant?	In the immediate post-transplant period; relatively rare. Artery: kinking or rejection. Vein: external compression or extension of a DVT. Diagnosis: renal duplex.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
Name the post-renal transplant-specific causes of AKI and the key test.	Ureteral stenosis (surgical anastomosis stenosing over time) and extrinsic compression from lymphocele, perinephric hematoma, or urinoma → obstructive uropathy. Diagnose with renal ultrasound (hydronephrosis).	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
What tests make up the "toolbox" for working up AKI in a transplant?	Renal duplex ultrasound, viral studies (plasma + urine BK/CMV PCR), CNI trough level, and allograft biopsy with DSAs (± donor-derived cell-free DNA). Involve transplant nephrology early.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
A transplant patient on tacrolimus develops AKI, ↑K, gout flare, and tremor after starting fluconazole. Mechanism?	Acute CNI toxicity. Azole antifungals (e.g., fluconazole) inhibit CYP3A4 and raise tacrolimus/cyclosporine levels, precipitating neurotoxicity, hypertension, ↑K and AKI. Always review for CNI drug interactions.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
What is the role of donor-derived cell-free DNA (dd-cfDNA) in 2024 practice?	A non-invasive adjunct that rises before creatinine; used to help exclude rejection (especially antibody-mediated) in acute allograft dysfunction or stable function (ASTS, Oct 2024). It supports, but does not replace, biopsy + DSAs.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
Why must "usual" AKI causes still be evaluated in a transplant recipient?	Transplant patients still develop pre-renal (volume depletion, NSAIDs/ACEi, sepsis, cardiorenal/hepatorenal), intra-renal (ATN, AIN, contrast, GN), and post-renal (calculi, clot, cancer, BPH) AKI — transplant-specific causes are ADDED to, not substituted for, the standard differential.	Renal::aki-in-a-renal-transplant
What is the KDIGO definition of AKI?	An abrupt drop in GFR, defined as ↑Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 h, OR ↑Cr ≥1.5× baseline within the prior 7 days, OR urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h. Characterize as non-oliguric, oliguric, or anuric.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
Why is eGFR not meaningful during an AKI?	eGFR equations assume a steady-state (stable) GFR. In AKI, GFR is changing rapidly, so the reported eGFR does not reflect true kidney function.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What are the three anatomic categories of AKI?	Pre-renal (decreased effective arterial blood volume), intra-renal/intrinsic (glomerular, tubulointerstitial, vascular), and post-renal (obstruction).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
How is pre-renal AKI diagnosed at the bedside?	Suggestive history (volume loss, low EABV), a bland urinalysis, no hydronephrosis on US, and — if oliguric — a FeNa <1% (FeUrea <35% is more reliable if the patient is on diuretics).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What three features point toward an intra-renal etiology?	"Active" urine sediment (supports intra-renal), an elevated FeNa while oliguric (rules out pre-renal), and a normal renal ultrasound (rules out post-renal).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What are the causes of decreased effective arterial blood volume (pre-renal)?	True hypovolemia, sepsis (distributive), heart failure (cardiorenal), and cirrhosis (hepatorenal). Also altered vasculature: renal vein thrombosis, renal venous hypertension (CHF), and intra-abdominal hypertension.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What is "vasogenic" pre-renal AKI and its classic drug causes?	A change in arteriolar tone reducing GFR: afferent constriction from NSAIDs and calcineurin inhibitors; efferent dilation from ACE inhibitors and ARBs.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
List the three subcategories of intra-renal AKI.	Glomerular (glomerulonephritis), tubulointerstitial (acute tubular necrosis, acute interstitial nephritis, tubular obstruction), and vascular (MAHA/TTP-HUS, cholesterol emboli).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
How is acute glomerulonephritis recognized on urinalysis?	Dysmorphic RBCs and/or RBC casts, proteinuria, leukocyturia, ± hypertension. GN is grouped into immune-complex, pauci-immune, and anti-GBM; crescentic forms = RPGN.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What is the most common intra-renal cause of AKI, and its urine finding?	Acute tubular necrosis (ATN) — ischemic (prolonged hypoperfusion) or toxic (including iodinated contrast). Urine shows muddy-brown granular casts.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What drugs and findings suggest acute interstitial nephritis (AIN)?	Antimicrobials (esp. beta-lactams), NSAIDs, PPIs, immunotherapy; also infection and systemic disease (Sjögren's, sarcoidosis). UA: pyuria, WBC casts, proteinuria; eosinophils are insensitive/nonspecific.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What causes tubular obstruction within the kidney?	Pigment/cast nephropathy (myoglobin, hemoglobin, light chains/paraproteins) and crystal nephropathy (uric acid, acyclovir, ethylene glycol, methotrexate).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
How is post-renal AKI diagnosed and why look early?	Hydronephrosis on renal ultrasound. Rule it out early because obstruction is typically rapidly reversible once relieved.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
Does unilateral ureteral obstruction cause AKI?	No — not unless it obstructs a single functioning kidney. AKI from ureteral obstruction requires bilateral obstruction (or obstruction of the only working kidney).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
A patient on diuretics has a FeNa of 2% but seems volume-depleted. What test is more reliable?	Fractional excretion of urea (FeUrea); a value <35% supports a pre-renal state even when diuretics raise the FeNa.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
A rhabdomyolysis patient (CK 20,000) has dipstick 3+ blood but no RBCs on microscopy. Mechanism of AKI?	Pigment (myoglobin) cast nephropathy — heme-pigment tubular obstruction/toxicity. Dipstick detects myoglobin as "blood" without RBCs. Treat with aggressive IV fluids and stop nephrotoxins (e.g., NSAIDs).	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
What are the management principles by AKI category?	Pre-renal → restore perfusion (volume/hemodynamics). Intra-renal → treat the cause, remove nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, contrast, ACEi/ARB), supportive care. Post-renal → relieve the obstruction.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
In ATN that is now anuric, what fluid pitfall should you avoid?	Avoid overhydration — once anuric, aggressive fluids cause volume overload. Maintain euvolemia, monitor electrolytes, and avoid nephrotoxins.	Renal::acute-kidney-injury
