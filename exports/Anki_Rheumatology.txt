What two features rapidly narrow the differential for inflammatory arthritis?	Time (acute vs chronic) and space (number of joints: mono = 1, oligo = 2–4, poly = 5+).	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
What is the default diagnosis for an acute hot, swollen, painful monoarthritis until proven otherwise?	Septic arthritis — a true emergency requiring synovial fluid analysis, empiric antibiotics, and usually urgent surgical washout.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Typical synovial fluid WBC count in septic arthritis?	Usually >50,000/µL with PMN predominance (use lower cutoffs in prosthetic joints or after partial antibiotic treatment).	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Empiric antibiotics for septic arthritis by Gram stain?	Gram-positive cocci → vancomycin; gram-negative organisms → ceftriaxone.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Synovial fluid finding diagnostic of acute gout?	Monosodium urate crystals (needle-shaped, negatively birefringent) under polarized microscopy.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Most common joint in acute gout?	First MTP (podagra, ~90%); lower-extremity predominant including the mid-foot, but any joint is possible.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
When should urate-lowering therapy be started in gout (ACR 2020)?	≥2 flares/year, tophi, or radiographic gouty erosions; consider after a single flare if CKD stage ≥3, serum urate >9 mg/dL, or urolithiasis. Allopurinol is first-line; goal urate <6 mg/dL.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Why continue colchicine when starting allopurinol?	Anti-inflammatory prophylaxis prevents mobilization flares while urate-lowering therapy is titrated (ACR 2020 — continue ~3–6 months).	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Synovial fluid and imaging findings in CPPD?	Calcium pyrophosphate crystals (rhomboid, weakly positively birefringent); radiographs may show chondrocalcinosis of knees, wrists, and fingers.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Two metabolic diseases to screen for in CPPD?	Hyperparathyroidism and hemochromatosis.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
What is Löfgren's syndrome?	Acute sarcoidosis triad: erythema nodosum + bilateral hilar adenopathy + acute arthritis (classically ankles).	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Classic triad of disseminated gonococcal infection?	Migratory polyarthralgia, tenosynovitis, and pustular dermatitis; treat with ceftriaxone (add doxycycline if chlamydia not excluded — CDC 2021).	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Key features of reactive arthritis?	Asymmetric lower-extremity oligoarthritis 1–6 weeks after a GI or GU infection, HLA-B27 association, negative synovial cultures; look for uveitis, urethritis, keratoderma blennorrhagicum.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Most common viral arthritis and its serology?	Parvovirus B19 — RA-like symmetric small-joint polyarthritis with positive IgM (IgG = prior exposure); HBV and HCV are next most common.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Presentation and treatment of Lyme arthritis?	Mono/oligoarthritis of a large joint (knee) months after tick exposure; synovial fluid >2K WBC, send Borrelia PCR; treat with a 4-week course of doxycycline (IDSA 2020).	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Joint pattern that distinguishes RA from psoriatic arthritis?	RA: symmetric wrists/MCPs/PIPs/ankles/MTPs, SPARES DIPs and the spine. PsA: asymmetric, can involve DIPs and the axial skeleton, with enthesitis, dactylitis, and nail pitting/onycholysis.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
Hallmark axial imaging findings of ankylosing spondylitis?	Sacroiliitis (SI involvement > rest of spine) with marginal erosions, syndesmophytes, and ankylosis; HLA-B27 associated.	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
X-ray hallmark of tophaceous gout?	Radiolucent tophi with punched-out erosions and overhanging edges, especially hands and feet; urate goal <6 mg/dL (may need <5 to shrink tophi).	Rheumatology::inflammatory-arthritis
What are the two major pathophysiologic pathways driving SLE manifestations?	(1) Antibody pathway — APC presents self-antigen → T-helper activates B cells → antinuclear antibodies (ANA) cause direct cellular destruction, immune-complex/tissue deposition, and a positive ANA test. (2) Inflammatory pathway — antigen binds TLRs on macrophages → cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, IFN-alpha) → inflammation.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Why are SLE patients especially susceptible to autoantibody formation after UV light?	UV light increases apoptosis; nuclear contents spilled by dying cells are taken up by antigen-presenting cells (macrophages) that then mount immune responses against self-antigens.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
How long can autoantibodies precede clinical SLE?	Years — in a VA cohort, 115/130 SLE patients had autoantibodies up to ~9.4 years before diagnosis, accumulating in a predictable order (benign → pathogenic autoimmunity → clinical illness).	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Which classification criteria underlie the SLE clinical-manifestation framework?	The EULAR/ACR 2019 Classification Criteria for SLE; each manifestation counts only if not better explained by another, more probable cause.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Describe the SLE malar rash.	Sun-sensitive facial rash that SPARES the nasolabial folds; usually rough and raised (vs rosacea, which is smooth and flat). Photosensitive rashes can occur elsewhere and notably spare the knuckles.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Which mucocutaneous findings are concerning for SLE?	Malar/photosensitive rashes, alopecia (scarring or non-scarring), painless or painful oral/nasal ulcers (active disease), Raynaud's, and sicca.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Which hematologic findings prompt an SLE workup?	Cytopenias (anemia — watch for hemolysis; leukopenia WBC <4, especially lympho/neutropenia, always get a diff; thrombocytopenia, though Plt <20 is more likely ITP) and arterial/venous VTE.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
What proportion of SLE patients have antiphospholipid antibodies?	30–40%; they raise the risk of vascular events and death. Revisit "provoked" VTEs; a clot is not required to diagnose antiphospholipid syndrome.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
What renal findings suggest lupus nephritis, and how common is it?	Proteinuria (>0.5 g) and/or hematuria; creatinine is often normal but a rising Cr demands urgent workup. Up to 50% of SLE patients develop lupus nephritis.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Most common musculoskeletal manifestation of SLE?	Synovitis — red, hot, objectively swollen joints (often MCP/PIP, wrists, knees); joints are the first sign in ~50%. Myositis presents as quadriceps weakness (dx with CK + EMG).	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Which serosal manifestations are common in SLE?	Pleural effusion (40–60%, often asymptomatic), pericardial effusion (>40%, can cause tamponade), and acute pericarditis (>50% of echos). Usually benign and atypical as an initial presentation.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Which "yellow-box" symptoms should NOT, by themselves, trigger an ANA?	Fatigue/weight change, hair loss in the shower, abdominal pain/chronic diarrhea/hematochezia (IBS/IBD), single/early miscarriage, iron-deficiency anemia, anxiety/depression/brain fog/headache, atypical chest pain, isolated dyspnea, pyuria, and generalized aches or pain without joint swelling.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
For diffuse pain without objective inflammation, what is the differential instead of SLE?	Fibromyalgia, thyroid disease, osteoarthritis, or another autoimmune disease (e.g., spondyloarthritis) — absence of a red/hot/swollen joint or elevated inflammatory markers argues against lupus.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
What is the recommended laboratory workup once SLE suspicion is high enough to send an ANA?	CBC w/ diff, CMP, C3/C4 (expect low), ESR (often elevated, CRP often normal — high CRP suggests RA overlap or infection), ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Smith, anti-RNP, anti-SSA/SSB, antiphospholipid antibodies, and UA.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Specificity/associations of the SLE autoantibodies?	Anti-dsDNA — specific, titer tracks activity (and nephritis); anti-Smith — specific, associated with lupus nephritis; anti-RNP — MCTD + Raynaud's; anti-SSA/SSB — Sjögren's and neonatal lupus. ANA must be positive to diagnose SLE.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Prevalence of a positive ANA in people WITHOUT SLE?	~32% at 1:40, ~13% at 1:80, ~5% at 1:160 — a low-positive ANA is common and non-specific.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
In a patient with known SLE and a high CRP, what should you think of first?	Infection — infections are a leading cause of mortality in SLE; both the disease and immunosuppressants raise risk, and ESR is usually elevated out of proportion to CRP in lupus activity (a high CRP points away from pure flare).	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
Beyond this diagnostic talk, what is the 2024 ACR treatment shift for active class III/IV lupus nephritis?	Early triple/combination therapy: hydroxychloroquine for all, plus pulse + oral glucocorticoids and one of — MPAA + belimumab, MPAA + a calcineurin inhibitor (voclosporin), or low-dose Euro-Lupus cyclophosphamide → MPAA + belimumab; lower-dose steroid taper to ≤5 mg/day by 6 months.	Rheumatology::is-it-lupus
What is the classic presentation of an acute gout flare?	Abrupt onset of a warm, red, excruciatingly painful, swollen peripheral joint, sometimes with fever; up to 90% of gout patients involve the first MTP at some point (~73% of flares are at the MTP).	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Why must arthrocentesis be done when gout is suspected with signs of infection?	Septic arthritis is a surgical emergency (rapid joint destruction) and can mimic — or coexist with — gout; crystals do NOT exclude infection.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Synovial fluid cell count ranges for non-inflammatory, inflammatory, and septic?	<2,000 = non-inflammatory; 2,000–75,000 = inflammatory; >75,000 = high risk for septic (overlaps exist — gout can exceed 100K; septic can be lower after antibiotics).	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Crystal appearance of gout vs pseudogout?	Gout = needle-shaped, negatively birefringent monosodium urate (yellow when parallel to the red compensator). Pseudogout (CPPD) = rhomboid, weakly positively birefringent calcium pyrophosphate.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Three first-line options for an acute gout flare?	NSAIDs, corticosteroids (systemic or intra-articular), or colchicine; choose by comorbidities.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
When are NSAIDs a poor choice for a gout flare?	History of GI bleeding, renal disease, or ischemic cardiac disease.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
When should colchicine be used for a flare, and key cautions?	Only effective within ~48 h of onset; renally dose in CKD; main side effect is diarrhea; caution with statins (myotoxicity) and CYP3A4 inhibitors.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
What agent treats a gout flare when NSAIDs, steroids, and colchicine are all contraindicated?	Anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist), off-label but far cheaper than canakinumab; with rheumatology referral; avoid in active infection.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Common medication precipitants of gout?	Loop and thiazide diuretics, aspirin, ACE inhibitors, ARBs (except losartan), ethambutol, pyrazinamide, niacin.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Which antihypertensives lower gout risk?	Losartan (uricosuric via URAT1 inhibition) and amlodipine — reasonable preferential BP agents in gout patients.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Dietary contributors to gout vs protective?	Increase risk: red meat, shellfish, alcohol (beer & spirits), fructose-rich drinks. Protective: higher dairy intake.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Indications to start urate-lowering therapy (ACR 2020)?	>=2 flares/year, tophi, or 1 flare + radiographic erosions; also consider after 1 flare if CKD stage >=3, serum urate >9 mg/dL, or urolithiasis.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
First-line urate-lowering therapy and the serum urate target?	Allopurinol (a xanthine oxidase inhibitor), treat-to-target serum urate <6 mg/dL (often <5 to dissolve tophi).	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
How is allopurinol started and titrated?	Start 100 mg daily (≤50 mg/day if CKD), up-titrate q2–4 weeks to target urate <6; max ~800 mg/day.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Why is prophylaxis given when starting ULT, and for how long?	Starting ULT without prophylaxis precipitates flares as urate falls; give low-dose colchicine, NSAID, or prednisone for 3–6 months until the target ULT dose is reached.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
What is allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome and who is at risk?	Cutaneous reaction, fever, eosinophilia, abnormal LFTs, and renal failure; risk markedly higher with HLA-B*5801 (highest in Han Chinese, Korean, Thai ~7.4%, and African American ~3.8%) — screen these groups and avoid allopurinol if positive.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Why is febuxostat second-line?	FDA boxed warning for increased cardiovascular and all-cause death (CARES, NEJM 2018); reserve for allopurinol intolerance or failure.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
Does starting ULT during an acute flare worsen it, and should chronic ULT be stopped during a flare?	No — starting ULT during a flare does not worsen or prolong it; patients already on ULT should continue it through flares.	Rheumatology::management-of-gout
